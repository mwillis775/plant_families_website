Aextoxicaceae

Aextoxicaceae Family

Aextoxicaceae is a monotypic family of flowering plants, containing only a single genus and species, Aextoxicon punctatum. This large evergreen tree, known locally as Olivillo or Aceitunillo, is endemic to the temperate rainforests of southern Chile and adjacent Argentina. It is characterized by opposite leaves covered in distinctive peltate scales underneath and small, olive-like drupe fruits.

Overview

The Aextoxicaceae family is unique in consisting of just one species, Aextoxicon punctatum. This tree is a significant component, often dominant, of the Valdivian and Magellanic temperate rainforests along the coast and Andean slopes of southern Chile and nearby areas of Argentina. It thrives in moist, cool environments.

Olivillo is a large, dioecious evergreen tree reaching heights of 15-25 meters or more. Its most striking vegetative feature is the dense covering of silvery or golden-brown peltate scales (small, shield-shaped hairs attached by a central stalk) on the underside of its leaves, young twigs, and buds, giving them a distinct metallic sheen. The upper leaf surface is dark green and often has small dark dots (punctations). The tree produces small, inconspicuous yellowish flowers in drooping racemes, followed by small, dark drupes.

Economically, Aextoxicon punctatum has limited major uses, though its wood is sometimes used locally for construction or fuel. Ecologically, it is a keystone species in its native forest ecosystems, contributing significantly to the forest structure and canopy cover. Its restricted distribution and unique phylogenetic position make it of considerable botanical interest.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Aextoxicaceae
  • Common Name: Aextoxicaceae family (Species: Olivillo, Aceitunillo)
  • Number of Genera: 1 (Aextoxicon)
  • Number of Species: 1 (Aextoxicon punctatum)
  • Distribution: Endemic to southern Chile and adjacent Argentina (Valdivian/Magellanic rainforests)
  • Evolutionary Group: Eudicots - Core Eudicots - Berberidopsidales

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

The family consists of a single species, Aextoxicon punctatum, which is a large, evergreen, dioecious tree (male and female flowers on separate trees).

Leaves

Leaves are opposite or subopposite, simple, with entire margins. They are petiolate and coriaceous (leathery) in texture. The upper surface is dark green, glabrous (smooth), and often marked with small, dark glandular dots (punctate). The lower surface is densely covered with characteristic silvery or coppery peltate scales. Stipules are absent.

Inflorescence

Flowers are arranged in pendulous (drooping) axillary racemes. These inflorescences bear numerous small flowers and are subtended by small bracts.

Flowers

Flowers are unisexual (plants dioecious), actinomorphic (radially symmetrical), small (about 5-6 mm diameter), and typically pale yellow.

  • Calyx: 5 sepals, slightly fused at the base, persistent.
  • Corolla: 5 petals, distinct, alternating with the sepals.
  • Staminate (Male) Flowers: Feature 5 stamens alternating with the petals; filaments are short. A small, non-functional pistil (pistillode) is present.
  • Pistillate (Female) Flowers: Feature 5 small, sterile stamens (staminodes) alternating with the petals. The ovary is superior, formed from 2 fused carpels, appearing unilocular (single chamber) due to an incomplete septum. It contains 2(-4) pendulous ovules. The style is short with two recurved stigmatic lobes.

Fruits and Seeds

The fruit is a small, indehiscent, fleshy drupe (stone fruit), resembling a small olive. It is typically globose, about 1 cm in diameter, and grayish to blackish-purple when ripe. It contains a single hard stone (endocarp) enclosing one, or rarely two, seeds.

The seed(s) have abundant, oily endosperm and a small, straight embryo.

Chemical Characteristics

The most prominent chemical-related feature is the presence of complex peltate scales (trichomes). The leaves and bark contain tannins. Other chemical constituents are not widely reported or characteristic compared to larger families.

Field Identification

Identifying Aextoxicaceae (i.e., Aextoxicon punctatum) is straightforward within its native range due to its unique combination of features:

Primary Identification Features

  • Location: Temperate rainforests of southern Chile and adjacent Argentina.
  • Habit: Large evergreen tree, often dominant.
  • Opposite leaves: Leaves arranged in pairs along the stem.
  • Peltate scales: Dense covering of silvery or coppery shield-shaped scales on the underside of leaves, buds, and young twigs (very distinctive).
  • Punctate upper leaf surface: Upper side of leaf dark green, often with visible dark dots.
  • Dioecious: Male and female flowers on separate trees.
  • Fruit: Small, dark, olive-like drupe.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Leathery leaf texture: Leaves are distinctly coriaceous.
  • Pendulous racemes: Inflorescences are drooping clusters of small, yellowish flowers.
  • Absence of stipules.

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Year-round: The evergreen nature, opposite leaves with peltate scales underneath, and tree habit allow identification at any time.
  • Flowering Season: Spring (austral spring: September-October). Look for the drooping racemes of small yellowish flowers.
  • Fruiting Season: Summer to Fall (austral summer/fall: December-March). Look for the characteristic small, dark drupes.

Common Confusion Points

Within its native range, Aextoxicon punctatum is quite distinctive due to the peltate scales on opposite leaves. However, some potential confusion might arise with:

  • Laureliopsis philippiana (Tepa): Another large evergreen tree in the same habitat (Atherospermataceae family). Tepa has opposite, serrated leaves (Aextoxicon has entire margins) that are aromatic when crushed and lack peltate scales.
  • Myrceugenia species (Arrayanes): Shrubs or trees in the Myrtaceae family, also common in Valdivian forests. They have opposite, simple, entire leaves but lack peltate scales, often have aromatic leaves when crushed, and have characteristic flowers with numerous stamens and different fruit types (berries).
  • Some Elaeocarpaceae: While the closely related family Berberidopsidaceae occurs in Chile (Berberidopsis corallina is a vine), some trees formerly in or related to Elaeocarpaceae might occur nearby, but leaf arrangement, presence/absence of scales, and flower/fruit details would differ.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Large evergreen tree (S Chile/Argentina)
  • Opposite, simple, entire, leathery leaves
  • Dense silvery/coppery peltate scales below
  • Punctate dots often visible above
  • Dioecious (separate M/F trees)
  • Small, yellowish flowers in drooping racemes
  • Small, dark, olive-like drupe

Key Variations:

  • (Monotypic family - essentially no variation at this level)
  • Scale color can vary (silvery to golden/coppery)
  • Punctations may be more or less obvious

Notable Examples

As Aextoxicaceae is a monotypic family, there is only one species to exemplify it:

Aextoxicon punctatum

Olivillo / Aceitunillo

The sole species representing the entire Aextoxicaceae family. This large evergreen tree is endemic to the Valdivian temperate rainforests of Chile and adjacent Argentina. It is easily recognized by its opposite, leathery leaves with entire margins, dark punctations above, and a dense layer of silvery-to-coppery peltate scales underneath. It plays a significant ecological role as a canopy dominant in these unique forest ecosystems.

Aextoxicon punctatum (Detail)

Olivillo Leaf Underside

A closer view highlighting the defining characteristic: the dense covering of peltate scales on the abaxial (lower) leaf surface. These shield-like structures give the underside a distinctive metallic sheen and texture, contrasting sharply with the smooth, dark green upper surface. This feature is key for field identification.

Aextoxicon punctatum (Fruit)

Olivillo Fruit

The fruit is a small (approx. 1 cm), globose drupe containing a single seed within a hard stone. It matures to a grayish or blackish-purple color, resembling a small olive, hence the common names Olivillo or Aceitunillo ('little olive'). The fruits develop from the inconspicuous yellowish flowers borne on drooping racemes.

Phylogeny and Classification

Aextoxicaceae is placed in the small order Berberidopsidales, within the core eudicot clade. This order contains only one other family, Berberidopsidaceae (which includes 2 genera, Berberidopsis and Streptothamnus, found in Chile and eastern Australia). The relationship between these two families as constituting the Berberidopsidales is strongly supported by molecular data.

The Berberidopsidales order itself holds an interesting phylogenetic position among core eudicots. Current phylogenetic analyses generally place Berberidopsidales as the sister group to a large clade containing the orders Santalales and Caryophyllales. This makes Aextoxicaceae part of an early-branching lineage within the core eudicots, separate from major groups like rosids and asterids, providing insights into the diversification of core eudicot lineages.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Clade: Angiosperms (Flowering plants)
  • Clade: Eudicots
  • Clade: Core Eudicots
  • Order: Berberidopsidales
  • Family: Aextoxicaceae

Related Families

The only other family in the order Berberidopsidales is:

  • Berberidopsidaceae: Contains woody vines (Berberidopsis) or shrubs (Streptothamnus) with alternate leaves, found in Chile and Australia.

The sister group to the entire Berberidopsidales order is generally considered to be the clade comprising:

  • Santalales: Includes parasitic plants like mistletoes (Viscaceae, Loranthaceae, Santalaceae) and some non-parasitic trees/shrubs.
  • Caryophyllales: A large and diverse order including cacti (Cactaceae), succulents (Aizoaceae), carnivorous plants (Nepenthaceae, Droseraceae), amaranths (Amaranthaceae), pinks (Caryophyllaceae), pokeweed (Phytolaccaceae), etc.

Evolutionary Significance

Aextoxicaceae, despite being monotypic, is significant for several evolutionary reasons:

  • Relictual Lineage: Represents a distinct, ancient lineage with a restricted geographical distribution, likely a relict from a potentially wider past distribution.
  • Phylogenetic Placement: Its position in Berberidopsidales, sister to the Santalales + Caryophyllales clade, helps clarify the relationships among major core eudicot lineages.
  • Biogeography: The order Berberidopsidales shows a classic Gondwanan distribution pattern (South America and Australia for Berberidopsidaceae, South America for Aextoxicaceae), providing clues about continental drift and plant migration history.
  • Unique Morphology: Features like the prominent peltate scales combined with opposite leaves and drupaceous fruit represent a unique combination of traits within the core eudicots.
