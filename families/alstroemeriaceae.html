Alstroemeriaceae

Peruvian Lily / Inca Lily Family

Alstroemeriaceae is a family of monocotyledonous flowering plants native exclusively to Central and South America. It includes perennial herbs and climbers, most famously the genus Alstroemeria, whose colourful hybrids are popular worldwide as cut flowers ('Peruvian Lilies'). A unique characteristic of the family is the resupinate leaves, which twist during development so the underside faces upwards.

Overview

The Alstroemeriaceae family belongs to the order Liliales and comprises 3 to 4 genera and about 250-260 species. Its natural distribution is restricted to the New World, ranging from Central America through the Andes down to southern South America. The family includes erect herbaceous perennials (Alstroemeria) and climbing or twining vines (Bomarea, Leontochir), often growing from rhizomes or tuberous roots adapted for storage.

These plants typically inhabit mountainous regions, grasslands, scrublands, and forest edges. They are characterized by their often showy, bilaterally symmetrical (zygomorphic) flowers with six distinct tepals, frequently marked with spots or streaks that act as nectar guides. A defining vegetative feature is the resupinate leaves, where the petiole twists 180°, orienting the morphological lower surface upwards.

Economically, the family is highly significant due to the genus Alstroemeria. Hybrid cultivars derived from Chilean species are among the most popular and long-lasting cut flowers globally, valued for their vibrant colours and intricate patterns. Some Bomarea species have edible tubers (B. edulis) used locally as food, while others are grown as ornamental climbers.

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Perennial herbs or climbing/twining vines, growing from rhizomes or tuberous roots. Roots are often fleshy and serve as storage organs.

Leaves

Leaves are alternate, simple, entire-margined, and typically linear, lanceolate, or ovate with parallel venation. The most distinctive feature is that they are resupinate: the petiole twists 180 degrees during development, so the morphological underside (abaxial surface) faces upwards and the upper side (adaxial surface) faces downwards.

Inflorescence

Flowers are borne in terminal inflorescences, typically simple or compound umbels (or pseudo-umbels), often subtended by an involucre of bracts. Sometimes reduced to few flowers.

Flowers

Flowers are usually large, showy, bisexual, and distinctly zygomorphic (bilaterally symmetrical). Parts are typically in threes (trimerous).

  • Perianth: Composed of 6 distinct (free) tepals arranged in two whorls of three. Tepals are petal-like, often brightly colored (red, orange, yellow, pink, purple, white), and frequently marked with spots or streaks, especially the upper two inner tepals which often function as nectar guides. The inner and outer whorls often differ in size and shape.
  • Androecium: 6 distinct stamens, often with filaments of unequal length. Anthers typically dehisce via longitudinal slits, releasing pollen outwards (extrorse).
  • Gynoecium: Ovary is inferior (positioned below the attachment point of the tepals and stamens), composed of 3 fused carpels forming a 3-locular ovary. Placentation is axile, with numerous ovules per locule. A single style terminates in a 3-lobed stigma.

Fruits and Seeds

The fruit is typically a loculicidal capsule, meaning it splits open along the midribs of the locules. Dehiscence is often elastic or explosive, forcibly ejecting the seeds.

Seeds are numerous, usually globose or angled. In Alstroemeria, they often have a reddish or brownish fleshy outer layer (sarcotesta). The endosperm is hard.

Chemical Characteristics

Members of the family contain specific types of saponins. Calcium oxalate crystals (raphides) are present in tissues. They characteristically lack chelidonic acid, which is found in some related families within the Liliales.

Field Identification

Identifying Alstroemeriaceae relies on recognizing their monocot features combined with unique characteristics like resupinate leaves and specific flower structures:

Primary Identification Features

  • Habit: Perennial herbs or climbing vines (not trees/shrubs).
  • Resupinate Leaves: Alternate, simple leaves that are twisted at the base so the underside faces up (critical feature).
  • Terminal Umbels: Flowers clustered in umbel-like inflorescences at the end of stems.
  • Zygomorphic Flowers: Flowers are bilaterally symmetrical, not radially symmetrical.
  • 6 Distinct Tepals: Showy, petal-like structures in two whorls of three, often spotted or streaked.
  • 6 Stamens.
  • Inferior Ovary: Ovary located below the tepals and stamens.
  • Capsule Fruit: Fruit is a capsule (often explosively dehiscent), not a berry or drupe.
  • New World Distribution: Native only to Central and South America.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Tuberous Roots/Rhizomes: Often possess fleshy storage roots or rhizomes.
  • Streaked/Spotted Tepals: Inner tepals frequently have intricate markings.
  • 3-Lobed Stigma: Style tip typically branches into three stigmatic lobes.

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Growing Season: Resupinate leaves are present; note herbaceous vs. climbing habit.
  • Flowering Season: Variable depending on species and altitude, often spring to summer. The showy, zygomorphic flowers are key identification features.
  • Fruiting Season: Following flowering. Look for the developing inferior ovary maturing into a capsule, which may dehisce later.

Common Confusion Points

  • Liliaceae (Lily family sensu stricto): True lilies (Lilium) have actinomorphic (radially symmetrical) flowers, usually a superior ovary, and lack resupinate leaves.
  • Iridaceae (Iris family): Irises have equitant leaves (not resupinate), typically 3 stamens (not 6), and an inferior ovary, but flowers are often actinomorphic or only slightly zygomorphic in a different way.
  • Orchidaceae (Orchid family): Orchids have highly zygomorphic flowers but are structurally very different, typically with a labellum (lip), fused stamen(s) and style (column), inferior ovary, and producing dust-like seeds in a capsule. Leaves are not resupinate.
  • Amaryllidaceae (Amaryllis family): Also monocots with inferior ovaries and often umbellate inflorescences (Amaryllis, Hippeastrum). Flowers are often actinomorphic or slightly zygomorphic, leaves are not resupinate, and may have a corona in the flower.
  • Commelinaceae (Spiderwort family): Herbs with alternate leaves, but leaves not resupinate, flowers often ephemeral, typically with 3 petals, 6 stamens (sometimes dissimilar), superior ovary.

The resupinate leaves are the most unique and reliable vegetative character for identifying Alstroemeriaceae.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Perennial herb or vine (New World)
  • Alternate, resupinate leaves
  • Terminal umbels
  • Zygomorphic flowers
  • 6 distinct, often spotted/streaked tepals
  • 6 stamens
  • Inferior ovary (3 carpels)
  • Capsule fruit (often explosive)

Key Variations:

  • Habit: Erect herb (Alstroemeria) vs. Climbing vine (Bomarea)
  • Flower color (diverse)
  • Degree of zygomorphy
  • Leaf shape (linear to ovate)

Notable Examples

The family is dominated by two main genera, one of which is extremely important horticulturally:

Alstroemeria hybrids

Peruvian Lily / Lily of the Incas

These popular garden perennials and cut flowers are complex hybrids primarily derived from Chilean species of Alstroemeria. They are prized for their long vase life and wide range of flower colors (pinks, purples, oranges, yellows, reds, whites), typically featuring distinctive streaks or spots on the inner tepals. They grow as erect herbs from tuberous roots.

Alstroemeria aurea

Golden Lily of the Incas / Yellow Alstroemeria

A species native to southern Chile and Argentina, known for its bright yellow or orange flowers. It is one of the parent species used in developing many commercial hybrids. Like other Alstroemeria, it is an erect perennial herb with resupinate leaves and grows from tuberous roots.

Bomarea sp.

Climbing Alstroemeria / Climbing Lily

This genus comprises numerous species of climbing or twining vines, predominantly found in the Andes. They have resupinate leaves similar to Alstroemeria but possess a vining habit. Flowers are often borne in dense, pendulous umbels and can be very showy, typically in shades of red, orange, or yellow. Some species are cultivated as ornamentals.

Bomarea edulis

(Edible Bomarea)

A widespread species of Bomarea ranging from Mexico to South America. It is notable for having starchy, tuberous roots that are sometimes harvested and eaten locally as a food source, similar to potatoes or yams.

Phylogeny and Classification

Alstroemeriaceae is classified within the order Liliales, a major group of monocots that also includes families like Liliaceae (true lilies), Colchicaceae (autumn crocus), Melanthiaceae (trilliums), and Smilacaceae (greenbriers). Liliales are characterized by often having showy flowers with six tepals, six stamens, and frequently nectar produced at the base of tepals or stamens.

Within the Liliales, molecular phylogenetic studies place Alstroemeriaceae in a well-supported core clade. Its closest relatives appear to be Colchicaceae and Liliaceae sensu stricto. The family itself is clearly monophyletic. The inclusion of Luzuriaga and Drymophila within Alstroemeriaceae in some older classifications is no longer supported; these genera now form the separate family Luzuriagaceae, also within Liliales but not immediately related to Alstroemeriaceae.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Clade: Angiosperms (Flowering plants)
  • Clade: Monocots
  • Order: Liliales
  • Family: Alstroemeriaceae

Related Families

Alstroemeriaceae shares the order Liliales with several other families, including:

  • Liliaceae (Lily family sensu stricto): Mostly bulbous herbs, actinomorphic flowers, superior ovary (Lilium, Tulipa, Fritillaria).
  • Colchicaceae (Autumn Crocus family): Often cormous herbs, actinomorphic flowers, superior ovary, source of colchicine (Colchicum, Gloriosa).
  • Melanthiaceae (Trillium/Wake Robin family): Rhizomatous herbs, flowers often actinomorphic, ovary superior or partly inferior (Trillium, Veratrum, Zigadenus).
  • Smilacaceae (Greenbrier family): Often woody vines with tendrils, small unisexual flowers in umbels, superior ovary, fruit a berry (Smilax).
  • Luzuriagaceae: Small family of vines/shrubs including Luzuriaga (formerly sometimes included in Alstroemeriaceae).
  • Campynemataceae, Corsiaceae, Petermanniaceae: Other smaller families within the order.

Evolutionary Significance

Alstroemeriaceae provides interesting evolutionary case studies:

  • Resupinate Leaves: The consistent 180-degree twisting of the leaf petiole is a rare and fascinating developmental modification among monocots, its adaptive significance debated but perhaps related to optimizing light capture or water relations.
  • Floral Zygomorphy: The evolution of bilateral symmetry in the flowers, particularly with specialized nectar guides on inner tepals, reflects adaptations for specific pollinators (often bees or hummingbirds).
  • Explosive Seed Dispersal: The elastic dehiscence of the capsule is an effective mechanism for short-distance seed dispersal.
  • New World Endemism: The family's restriction to Central and South America points to its origin and diversification within this region.
  • Horticultural Radiation: The genus Alstroemeria demonstrates rapid diversification potential, both naturally and through human-driven hybridization, leading to vast horticultural variety from relatively few wild species.
