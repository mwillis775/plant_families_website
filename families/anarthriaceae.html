Anarthriaceae

Southern Rush Family

Anarthriaceae is a small family of rush-like herbs in the order Poales, containing 3 genera (Anarthria, Hopkinsia, and Lyginia) with approximately 10 species. These perennial plants are characterized by their distichous leaves, spikelet-like inflorescences, and wind-pollinated flowers with six tepals. Endemic to southwestern Western Australia, they typically grow in winter-wet depressions or swampy ground. The family is notable for its unique combination of morphological features, including the presence of sclerenchymatous idioblasts in the mesophyll and chlorenchyma with 'peg cells', which distinguish it from the closely related Restionaceae.

Overview

The Anarthriaceae family comprises a small group of rush-like perennial herbs that are endemic to the southwestern corner of Western Australia. These plants are adapted to the Mediterranean climate of the region, with wet winters and dry summers, and typically grow in seasonally wet depressions, swamps, or along watercourses.

The family was originally classified as a subfamily (Anarthrioideae) within Restionaceae but was elevated to family status based on distinctive morphological and anatomical features. Molecular phylogenetic studies have supported this separation, confirming Anarthriaceae as a distinct lineage within the order Poales.

Anarthriaceae includes three genera: Anarthria (with about 5 species), Hopkinsia (with 2 species), and Lyginia (with 3 species). All members of the family are restricted to the southwestern corner of Western Australia, an area known for its exceptional plant diversity and high levels of endemism.

These plants play an important ecological role in their native habitats, contributing to the vegetation structure of seasonally wet areas and providing habitat for various organisms. They are adapted to the fire-prone ecosystems of southwestern Australia and can regenerate after fire from their rhizomatous bases.

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Members of Anarthriaceae are perennial rush-like herbs with distinctive growth forms:

  • Plants typically grow to heights of 0.2-0.6 meters
  • They form clumps or tussocks from rhizomatous bases
  • Some species (like Anarthria prolifera) have many-branched stems that curl below the inflorescences, while others have unbranched stems
  • Many species function as "switch-plants," with the principal photosynthesizing function transferred to the stems

These plants are adapted to seasonally wet habitats but often have a xerophytic appearance, with tough, wiry stems and reduced leaves that help them survive the dry summer months.

Leaves

The leaves of Anarthriaceae show considerable variation across genera and species:

  • Arrangement: Alternate and distinctively distichous (arranged in two rows)
  • Structure: Sheathing at the base, with the sheaths not forming tubes
  • Form: Varying from well-developed to much reduced or absent in some species
  • Shape: Can be acicular (needle-like), lanceolate, flat, folded, or terete (cylindrical)
  • Position: Mostly basal in many species, but not forming rosettes

In some species, particularly Anarthria prolifera, the interpretation of vegetative structures can be ambiguous, with flattened stems and peduncles appearing leaf-like, subtended by spathes and bracts that range from leaf-like with folded laminae to reduced to their sheaths.

Anatomical Features

Anarthriaceae possess several distinctive anatomical features that help distinguish them from related families:

  • Chlorenchyma: Contains specialized 'peg cells' that are characteristic of the family
  • Mesophyll: Contains sclerenchymatous idioblasts (isolated cells with thickened walls)
  • Stomata: Paracytic (with subsidiary cells parallel to the guard cells)
  • Guard cells: 'Grass type' in structure
  • Vessels: Present in the foliar tissues, with scalariform end-walls

Unlike some related families, Anarthriaceae plants do not contain silica bodies, and the mesophyll lacks crystals.

Reproductive Structures

The reproductive structures of Anarthriaceae are specialized for wind pollination:

  • Inflorescences: Flowers arranged in cymes or panicles, appearing as 'spikelets' due to the chaffy bracteoles and longer perianth; inflorescences can be narrow and spike-like or lax paniculate cymes, and are spatheate (with leaf-like spathes)
  • Flowers: Small, regular, 3-merous, and cyclic; bracteate and bi-bracteolate (with two unequal bracteoles much shorter than the perianth)
  • Sexual system: Plants can be monoecious (rarely), andromonoecious, gynomonoecious, or dioecious (usually)
  • Perianth: Consists of 6 tepals in 2 whorls, free, sepaloid (glume- or bract-like, folded), and similar in both whorls
  • Androecium: 3 stamens, free or with connate filaments, arranged in 1 whorl, with basifixed, latrorse, bilocular anthers
  • Gynoecium: Syncarpous with 3 carpels and 3 locules, superior, with 3 free, elongated styles that are stigmatic almost throughout
  • Ovules: 1 per locule, pendulous, orthotropous, with a hypostase near the chalazal end

Pollination is anemophilous (wind-pollinated), and floral nectaries are absent.

Fruits and Seeds

After pollination, each flower develops into a small, indehiscent nut:

  • Fruit type: Non-fleshy, indehiscent nut
  • Seeds: Endospermic with mealy endosperm containing starch
  • Testa: Without phytomelan (a black, carbonaceous substance found in the seed coats of many monocots)

The fruits are typically dispersed by wind or water, and the seeds germinate when conditions are favorable, usually after winter rains.

Distinguishing Features

Anarthriaceae can be distinguished from other families in the Poales by a combination of:

  • Rush-like habit with rhizomatous growth
  • Distichous leaf arrangement
  • Presence of 'peg cells' in the chlorenchyma
  • Sclerenchymatous idioblasts in the mesophyll
  • Six-tepaled flowers arranged in spikelet-like inflorescences
  • Three stamens and three carpels
  • Indehiscent nut fruits
  • Endemic distribution in southwestern Western Australia

Identification

Field Identification Tips

Identifying members of the Anarthriaceae family in the field can be challenging due to their superficial similarity to rushes and sedges. Look for:

  • Habitat: Winter-wet depressions, swamps, or along watercourses in southwestern Western Australia.
  • Growth form: Tufted, rush-like herbs forming clumps or tussocks from rhizomatous bases.
  • Leaves: Distichously arranged (in two rows), often reduced or mainly basal, with open sheaths.
  • Stems: Wiry, tough, and often functioning as the main photosynthetic organs.
  • Inflorescences: Spikelet-like structures arranged in narrow spikes or panicles, with leaf-like spathes.
  • Flowers: Small, with 6 tepals and 3 stamens in male flowers, wind-pollinated.
  • Geographic location: Restricted to the southwestern corner of Western Australia.

Similar Families

Anarthriaceae may be confused with several other families of rush-like plants:

  • Restionaceae: Very similar in appearance but typically lacks the distichous leaf arrangement and 'peg cells' in the chlorenchyma; often has silica bodies, which are absent in Anarthriaceae.
  • Cyperaceae (Sedge Family): Similar habitat preferences but typically has triangular stems and different floral structures, with flowers arranged in spikelets subtended by a single bract.
  • Juncaceae (Rush Family): Similar growth form but usually has cylindrical stems and flowers with 6 tepals in 2 distinct whorls, often with capsular fruits.
  • Ecdeiocoleaceae: Similar in appearance but has different anatomical features and is restricted to specific habitats in southwestern Australia.

Distinguishing Features

The most reliable features for distinguishing Anarthriaceae from similar families include:

  • Leaf arrangement: The distichous arrangement of leaves is distinctive.
  • Anatomical features: The presence of 'peg cells' in the chlorenchyma and sclerenchymatous idioblasts in the mesophyll are characteristic.
  • Floral structure: Six tepals in two whorls, three stamens, and three carpels with free styles.
  • Fruit type: Indehiscent nuts rather than capsules.
  • Geographic distribution: Endemic to southwestern Western Australia.

For definitive identification, examination of the anatomical features under magnification may be necessary, particularly to observe the 'peg cells' and sclerenchymatous idioblasts that are characteristic of the family.

Notable Examples

The Anarthriaceae family contains 3 genera with approximately 10 species. Here are some notable representatives:

Anarthria scabra

Rough Southern Rush

A perennial herb endemic to southwestern Western Australia, growing to about 0.5 meters in height. It forms tufts or tussocks from a rhizomatous base and has unbranched stems. The leaves are well-developed, flat, and arranged distichously (in two rows).

This species typically grows in winter-wet depressions or swampy ground and is adapted to the Mediterranean climate of the region, with wet winters and dry summers. The specific epithet "scabra" refers to the rough texture of the plant, particularly the leaves, which have a scabrous (rough to the touch) surface.

Anarthria prolifera

Branching Southern Rush

A distinctive species characterized by its many-branched stems that curl below the inflorescences, unlike the unbranched stems of other Anarthria species. It functions as a "switch-plant," with the principal photosynthesizing function transferred to the stems and peduncles.

The vegetative structure of A. prolifera is complex and can be difficult to interpret, with flattened stems and peduncles appearing leaf-like, subtended by spathes and bracts that range from leaf-like with folded laminae to reduced to their sheaths. This species is adapted to seasonally wet habitats in southwestern Western Australia.

Lyginia barbata

Bearded Southern Rush

A dioecious perennial herb endemic to southwestern Western Australia. It has wiry, unbranched stems arising from a rhizomatous base and reduced, scale-like leaves. The inflorescences are compact and spike-like, with the male and female flowers on separate plants.

The specific epithet "barbata" refers to the bearded or hairy appearance of certain parts of the plant, particularly the floral structures. This species typically grows in sandy soils in winter-wet depressions or along watercourses and is adapted to the fire-prone ecosystems of southwestern Australia, regenerating from its rhizomatous base after fire.

Phylogenetic Note

The phylogenetic position of Anarthriaceae has been clarified through molecular studies, which have confirmed its status as a distinct family within the order Poales. It belongs to a clade that includes Restionaceae, Centrolepidaceae, and Ecdeiocoleaceae, collectively known as the "restiid clade" within Poales.

Historically, the genera now placed in Anarthriaceae were classified as a subfamily (Anarthrioideae) within Restionaceae due to morphological similarities. However, distinctive anatomical features, particularly the presence of 'peg cells' in the chlorenchyma and sclerenchymatous idioblasts in the mesophyll, along with molecular evidence, supported the elevation of this group to family status.

The restiid clade, including Anarthriaceae, is sister to the "graminid clade" that includes the economically important families Poaceae (grasses), Cyperaceae (sedges), and Juncaceae (rushes). Together, these families form a well-supported monophyletic group within the commelinid monocots.

The endemic distribution of Anarthriaceae in southwestern Western Australia reflects the ancient Gondwanan origins of the family and the long isolation of the Australian continent. This region is known for its exceptional plant diversity and high levels of endemism, partly due to its stable geological history and the persistence of Mediterranean-type climate conditions over long periods.

Recent molecular studies have also provided insights into the relationships within Anarthriaceae, confirming the monophyly of the three genera (Anarthria, Hopkinsia, and Lyginia) and suggesting that the family diverged from its closest relatives during the Cretaceous period, approximately 100-66 million years ago.
