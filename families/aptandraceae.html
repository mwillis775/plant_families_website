Aptandraceae

Aptandra Family

Aptandraceae is a small family of trees and shrubs in the order Santalales, containing approximately 7 genera and 30 species. These woody plants are characterized by their simple, alternate leaves with entire margins, small flowers with distinctive staminal tubes in some genera, and drupes or berries often with a single seed. Distributed across tropical regions of Africa, Asia, and the Americas, they typically grow in rainforests and other humid habitats. The family is notable for its position in the Santalales order, its mostly non-parasitic habit (unlike many related families), and its division into two distinct clades: the Aptandra clade and the Anacolosa clade. Some members produce valuable timber and have traditional medicinal uses in their native ranges.

Overview

Aptandraceae is a small family of flowering plants in the order Santalales. The family comprises approximately 7 genera and 30 species of trees and shrubs distributed across tropical regions of Africa, Asia, and the Americas, where they typically grow in rainforests and other humid habitats.

The taxonomic history of Aptandraceae has been complex and subject to considerable revision. The family was first described by John Miers in the 19th century, but its circumscription has changed significantly over time. Various genera now included in Aptandraceae were previously placed in other families, particularly Olacaceae. Molecular phylogenetic studies in the early 21st century led to a major reorganization of the Santalales order, resulting in the current circumscription of Aptandraceae.

In the APG IV classification system (Angiosperm Phylogeny Group), Aptandraceae is recognized as a distinct family within Santalales. It occupies a position within the order that reflects its evolutionary relationship with other families, many of which include parasitic or hemiparasitic plants, though most Aptandraceae members are non-parasitic (autotrophic).

The family is divided into two well-supported clades: the Aptandra clade (including the genera Aptandra, Chaunochiton, Harmandia, Hondurodendron, and Ongokea) and the Anacolosa clade (including Anacolosa, Cathedra, and Phanerodiscus). These clades share certain morphological features, including simple, alternate leaves with entire margins, small flowers, and drupes or berries often with a single seed, but they also have distinctive characteristics that set them apart.

Some members of Aptandraceae have economic importance. For example, species of Ongokea produce valuable timber and oil-rich seeds that have been used for various purposes. Various species also have traditional medicinal uses in their native ranges, with parts of the plants being used to treat a range of conditions, from fevers to digestive disorders.

From an evolutionary perspective, Aptandraceae is particularly interesting due to its position in the Santalales order, which includes many parasitic and hemiparasitic plants. The mostly non-parasitic habit of Aptandraceae suggests that the ancestral condition in Santalales may have been non-parasitic, with parasitism evolving later in the order's history. The family thus provides important insights into the evolution of parasitism in plants, a trait that has evolved independently multiple times across the plant kingdom.

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Members of Aptandraceae are woody plants, including:

  • Trees, often reaching heights of 10-30 meters
  • Shrubs, typically multi-stemmed and bushy

They typically grow in tropical rainforests and other humid habitats across their range in Africa, Asia, and the Americas. Unlike many other families in the Santalales order, most Aptandraceae members are non-parasitic (autotrophic), obtaining nutrients through photosynthesis rather than parasitizing other plants.

Leaves

The leaves of Aptandraceae have several distinctive characteristics:

  • Arrangement: Alternate along the stems
  • Type: Simple, never compound
  • Margins: Entire (smooth, without teeth or lobes)
  • Shape: Typically elliptic to oblong or lanceolate
  • Texture: Often somewhat leathery (coriaceous)
  • Venation: Pinnate, with a prominent midrib and less conspicuous secondary veins
  • Stipules: Absent

The leaves are typically glabrous (smooth, without hairs) and often somewhat glossy on the upper surface. The simple, alternate leaves with entire margins are consistent across the family and help distinguish it from some related families with different leaf arrangements.

Flowers

The flowers of Aptandraceae provide key diagnostic features, though they vary somewhat between the two main clades:

  • Arrangement: In axillary or terminal inflorescences, often cymes or racemes
  • Size: Small, typically less than 1 cm in diameter
  • Symmetry: Actinomorphic (radially symmetrical)
  • Calyx: Small, cup-shaped, often with 4-5 lobes or teeth
  • Corolla: 4-5 petals, free or partially fused, often white, cream, or yellowish
  • Stamens: In the Aptandra clade, often with a distinctive staminal tube formed by the fusion of filaments; in the Anacolosa clade, typically free
  • Ovary: Superior to half-inferior, typically with 2-5 locules (chambers) at the base but becoming unilocular above
  • Style: Simple, with a capitate or lobed stigma

The floral structure, particularly the arrangement of stamens (with a distinctive staminal tube in some genera) and the partially locular ovary, is characteristic of the family and reflects its position in the Santalales order.

Fruits and Seeds

After pollination, the flowers develop into:

  • Fruit type: Typically a drupe or berry
  • Fruit shape: Often somewhat globose (spherical) to ovoid
  • Seeds: Usually 1 per fruit
  • Seed dispersal: Primarily by animals, particularly birds and mammals that consume the fleshy fruits and disperse the seeds

The drupes or berries with a single seed are consistent features of Aptandraceae and help distinguish it from some related families with different fruit types.

Wood and Bark

Aptandraceae has several notable wood and bark features:

  • Wood density: Often hard and dense, particularly in tree species
  • Wood color: Variable, often yellowish to reddish-brown
  • Grain: Typically fine and even
  • Bark: Variable, often smooth to slightly fissured

The wood of some Aptandraceae species, particularly in the genus Ongokea, is valued for its durability and is used for construction, furniture-making, and other purposes.

Anatomical Features

Aptandraceae has several notable anatomical features:

  • Vessel elements: Typically with simple perforation plates
  • Parenchyma: Often apotracheal (not associated with vessels)
  • Stomata: Typically paracytic (with two subsidiary cells parallel to the guard cells)
  • Special features: Some species have specialized cells or tissues containing various secondary compounds
  • Crystals: Druses (spherical aggregates of crystals) present in some genera, particularly Heisteria, Chaunochiton, Harmandia, and Aptandra

These anatomical features, particularly the wood anatomy and the presence of druses in certain genera, have been important in establishing the relationships between Aptandraceae and other families in the Santalales order.

Distinguishing Features

Aptandraceae can be distinguished from other plant families by a combination of:

  • Woody habit (trees or shrubs)
  • Simple, alternate leaves with entire margins
  • Small flowers, often with a distinctive staminal tube in the Aptandra clade
  • Drupes or berries with a single seed
  • Non-parasitic habit (unlike many related families in Santalales)

These features, particularly the floral structure and fruit characteristics, make Aptandraceae relatively easy to distinguish from unrelated families, though it can be more challenging to differentiate from closely related families in the Santalales order.

Identification

Field Identification Tips

Identifying members of the Aptandraceae family in the field can be facilitated by looking for:

  • Habitat: Trees or shrubs growing in tropical rainforests and other humid habitats in Africa, Asia, or the Americas.
  • Habit: Woody plants, often with a somewhat spreading crown (in tree species).
  • Leaves: Simple, alternate leaves with entire margins and a somewhat leathery texture.
  • Flowers: Small flowers, often with a distinctive staminal tube in the Aptandra clade.
  • Fruits: Drupes or berries with a single seed.

The combination of simple, alternate leaves, small flowers (often with a distinctive staminal tube in the Aptandra clade), and drupes or berries with a single seed is particularly useful for field identification.

Similar Families

Aptandraceae may be confused with several related families:

  • Olacaceae: Very similar and closely related, but with some differences in floral structure and fruit characteristics. The distinction between these families has been clarified by molecular studies.
  • Strombosiaceae: Also closely related and similar in appearance, but with some differences in floral structure and fruit development.
  • Erythropalaceae: Also in Santalales and with some similar features, but typically has different floral structures and fruit types.
  • Santalaceae: Some members have similar growth habits, but many are parasitic or hemiparasitic (unlike the mostly non-parasitic Aptandraceae) and often have different leaf arrangements and floral structures.

Distinguishing Features

The most reliable features for distinguishing Aptandraceae from similar families include:

  • Floral structure: The combination of small flowers, often with a distinctive staminal tube in the Aptandra clade, is characteristic.
  • Fruit characteristics: The drupes or berries with a single seed are distinctive.
  • Habit: The non-parasitic habit distinguishes Aptandraceae from many other families in Santalales, which include parasitic or hemiparasitic plants.
  • Anatomical features: Certain wood anatomical features, including vessel element structure and parenchyma distribution, can help distinguish Aptandraceae from related families.

For definitive identification, examination of the floral structure and fruits is often necessary, particularly to distinguish Aptandraceae from closely related families in the Santalales order.

Notable Examples

The Aptandraceae family contains approximately 7 genera and 30 species. Here are some notable representatives:

Aptandra tubicina

Tubular Aptandra

A tree or shrub native to tropical South America, particularly the Amazon Basin. It typically grows to about 5-15 meters tall and has simple, alternate leaves with entire margins.

The flowers are small and arranged in axillary or terminal inflorescences. They have a distinctive staminal tube formed by the fusion of filaments, a characteristic feature of the Aptandra clade. The fruits are drupes containing a single seed.

A. tubicina typically grows in lowland tropical rainforests. The specific epithet "tubicina" refers to the tubular structure formed by the fused stamens. The species has limited economic importance but is of botanical interest as a representative of the Aptandra clade within Aptandraceae.

Ongokea gore

Isano or Angueuk

A large tree native to tropical Africa, particularly Central and West Africa. It typically grows to about 20-30 meters tall and has simple, alternate leaves with entire margins.

The flowers are small, yellowish-green, and arranged in axillary clusters. The fruits are drupes containing a single large seed rich in oil.

O. gore typically grows in lowland tropical rainforests. The species has significant economic importance in its native range. The wood, known as angueuk or isano, is hard, dense, and durable, valued for construction, furniture-making, and other purposes. The seeds contain an oil (isano oil) that has been used traditionally for various purposes, including as a skin moisturizer and hair conditioner. The oil has also been investigated for potential industrial applications, including as a drying oil for paints and varnishes.

Anacolosa frutescens

Asian Anacolosa

A shrub or small tree native to tropical Asia, particularly Southeast Asia. It typically grows to about 3-10 meters tall and has simple, alternate leaves with entire margins.

The flowers are small, yellowish, and arranged in axillary clusters. The fruits are drupes containing a single seed.

A. frutescens typically grows in lowland tropical rainforests and other humid habitats. The species is a representative of the Anacolosa clade within Aptandraceae, which differs from the Aptandra clade in various morphological features, particularly in the structure of the flowers (lacking the distinctive staminal tube found in the Aptandra clade). The species has various traditional medicinal uses in its native range, with different parts of the plant being used to treat a range of conditions.

Phylogenetic Note

The phylogenetic position of Aptandraceae has been clarified through molecular phylogenetic studies in the early 21st century. These studies have led to a major reorganization of the Santalales order, with Aptandraceae now recognized as a distinct family within this order.

Aptandraceae is particularly interesting from an evolutionary perspective because it is positioned within an order that includes many parasitic and hemiparasitic plants, yet most of its members are non-parasitic (autotrophic). This suggests that the ancestral condition in Santalales may have been non-parasitic, with parasitism evolving later in the order's history. The family thus provides important insights into the evolution of parasitism in plants, a trait that has evolved independently multiple times across the plant kingdom.

The family is divided into two well-supported clades: the Aptandra clade (including the genera Aptandra, Chaunochiton, Harmandia, Hondurodendron, and Ongokea) and the Anacolosa clade (including Anacolosa, Cathedra, and Phanerodiscus). These clades share certain morphological features but also have distinctive characteristics that set them apart, particularly in the structure of the flowers.

The Aptandra clade is characterized by flowers with a distinctive staminal tube formed by the fusion of filaments, while the Anacolosa clade typically has free stamens. This difference in floral structure suggests different evolutionary trajectories within the family, possibly related to different pollination strategies.

The geographic distribution of Aptandraceae, spanning tropical regions of Africa, Asia, and the Americas, suggests an ancient origin for the family, possibly dating back to when the continents were connected or closer together. The current disjunct distribution may reflect vicariance (separation due to geological events) and/or long-distance dispersal events.

The family's position within Santalales, along with its division into two distinct clades, makes it particularly valuable for understanding the evolution and diversification of this order, which includes not only non-parasitic plants but also various types of parasitic plants, from root parasites to stem parasites to the extreme reduction seen in some mistletoes.
