Aulacomniaceae

Bog Moss Family / Groove Moss Family

Aulacomniaceae is a small family of mosses, primarily containing the genus Aulacomnium. These are typically medium-sized, acrocarpous mosses forming yellowish-green to brownish tufts, commonly found in wet habitats like bogs, fens, and wet meadows. The family is known for the characteristic ribbed or grooved (striate) capsules borne on long setae in most species, and the production of specialized asexual propagules (gemmae) on stalk-like structures in some common species.

Overview

The Aulacomniaceae family, with its main genus Aulacomnium comprising about 4-7 species, is widely distributed, especially in temperate and boreal regions of the Northern Hemisphere, though occurrences exist in the Southern Hemisphere as well. These mosses are characteristic inhabitants of nutrient-poor to moderately rich wetlands, including peat bogs, fens, swamps, wet tundra, and damp forests, occasionally growing on decaying wood or humus.

Members of this family often form conspicuous yellowish-green cushions or tufts. A particularly notable feature in species like Aulacomnium palustre is the frequent production of stalked clusters of gemmae, which allow for effective asexual reproduction and dispersal in their often waterlogged environments. When fertile, the capsules are typically cylindrical, inclined, and distinctly ribbed or furrowed when dry, giving rise to the less common name "Groove Moss".

Ecologically, Aulacomniaceae can be important components of peatland vegetation, contributing to biomass and peat formation. Their presence often indicates specific hydrological conditions. Phylogenetically, they belong to the large class Bryopsida and are usually placed within the order Rhizogoniales or sometimes Bryales.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Aulacomniaceae
  • Common Name: Bog Moss Family, Groove Moss Family
  • Number of Genera: 1 (Aulacomnium)
  • Number of Species: Approximately 4-7
  • Distribution: Widespread, primarily Northern Hemisphere temperate and boreal regions, in wet habitats.
  • Evolutionary Group: Bryophytes - Mosses (Class: Bryopsida, Order: Rhizogoniales / Bryales)

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Aulacomniaceae are acrocarpous mosses (sporophytes terminal on stems or main branches), although frequent branching via innovations below the reproductive structures can sometimes give a pleurocarpous appearance. They form loose to dense tufts or cushions, typically yellowish-green to brownish-green.

Stems

Stems are erect, often 2-10 cm tall, and frequently covered below with dense, reddish-brown rhizoids (tomentum).

Leaves

Leaves are typically crowded, erect-spreading to spreading when moist, and often strongly contorted, twisted, or crisped when dry. They are usually oblong-lanceolate to lanceolate in shape. The costa (midrib) is single and strong, usually ending near the leaf apex. Leaf margins are often recurved (rolled downwards) and may be entire or toothed near the apex. Leaf cells are typically small, rounded-quadrate to hexagonal, thick-walled, and usually bear a single papilla per cell (papillose), giving the leaf surface a dull appearance.

Asexual Reproduction

A highly characteristic feature of some species (notably A. palustre and A. androgynum) is the production of specialized asexual propagules called gemmae. These are typically fusiform (spindle-shaped) or clavate (club-shaped), multicellular structures borne in conspicuous clusters at the tips of specialized, often leafless, stalk-like extensions of the stem (sometimes referred to as pseudopodia, but structurally different from those in basal mosses).

Sporophytes (Reproductive Structures)

When produced, sporophytes are terminal on stems or branches. The capsule is elevated on a long, smooth seta. The capsule itself is typically cylindrical, inclined to horizontal, often curved, and characteristically striate or ribbed (longitudinally furrowed), especially when dry and empty. The peristome is double (diplolepidous), well-developed, and typical of the Bryalean type (with 16 outer teeth and 16 inner segments). The operculum (lid) is usually conic or shortly rostrate (beaked).

Habitat

Species are typically found in moist to wet, acidic to circumneutral habitats. Common environments include peat bogs, fens, swamps, wet meadows, wet tundra, seepage areas on slopes, and occasionally on rotting logs or humus-rich soil in damp forests.

Field Identification

Identifying Aulacomniaceae often relies on recognizing their typical habitat, growth form, leaf appearance (especially when dry), and the presence of characteristic asexual or sexual reproductive structures.

Primary Identification Features

  • Habitat: Look in wet environments like bogs, fens, swamps, and wet meadows.
  • Growth Habit: Medium-sized, yellowish-green to brownish tufts or cushions.
  • Leaves When Dry: Often noticeably contorted, twisted, or crisped.
  • Gemmae Stalks (Key for some species): Look for terminal clusters of small, spindle-shaped gemmae borne on specialized, often leafless stalks (especially prominent in A. palustre).
  • Capsules (if present): Cylindrical, inclined capsules on long setae, which become distinctly ribbed or furrowed when dry.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Leaf Cells (Microscopic): Small, thick-walled, and typically papillose.
  • Rhizoids: Stems often covered in dense reddish-brown rhizoids below.
  • Leaf Margins: Often recurved.

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Year-round: The vegetative gametophytes are present year-round. Gemmae stalks (in species that produce them) can often be found throughout the year.
  • Sporophyte Timing: Capsules typically mature in spring or summer in temperate regions. The ribbed appearance is most obvious on old, dry, empty capsules.

Common Confusion Points

Aulacomniaceae can be confused with other tufted mosses in similar wet habitats:

  • Sphagnum species: Grow in bogs but have a very different structure (fascicles of branches, unique leaf cell pattern).
  • Philonotis species (Bartramiaceae): Often yellowish-green in wet places, but leaves are frequently secund (curved to one side), cells are often prorate (papillae at cell ends), and capsules are globose and ribbed differently.
  • Meesiaceae (e.g., Meesia, Paludella): Also in fens/bogs. Meesia has very long setae and pyriform capsules; Paludella has distinctive squarrose leaves and dense tomentum.
  • Bryaceae / Mniaceae: Some species grow in damp places but generally lack papillose cells, gemmae stalks, and distinctly ribbed capsules of the Aulacomnium type.

The combination of wet habitat, yellowish tufts, often crisped dry leaves, stalked gemmae (in some), and ribbed capsules is characteristic of Aulacomniaceae.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Habitat: Bogs, fens, wet meadows
  • Yellowish-green / brownish tufts
  • Leaves crisped/contorted when dry
  • Stalked clusters of gemmae (esp. A. palustre)
  • Capsule on long seta
  • Capsule cylindrical, inclined, ribbed/striate when dry
  • Papillose leaf cells (microscope)

Key Variations:

  • Presence/absence of gemmae stalks
  • Leaf shape (oblong-lanceolate to lanceolate)
  • Degree of leaf contortion when dry

Notable Examples

All well-accepted species belong to the genus Aulacomnium:

Aulacomnium palustre

Ribbed Bog Moss / Marsh Groove-moss

A very common and widespread species in bogs, fens, and wet heaths across the Northern Hemisphere. Characterized by its yellowish-green tufts, leaves strongly crisped when dry, and the frequent presence of conspicuous stalks bearing terminal clusters of fusiform gemmae. Capsules are ribbed when dry.

Aulacomnium turgidum

Swollen Bog Moss / Mountain Groove-moss

Primarily found in arctic and alpine tundra environments. Forms dense, often brownish-yellow, turgid cushions. Leaves are broader, more concave, and less contorted when dry compared to A. palustre. It typically lacks the stalked gemmae clusters. Capsules are rare.

Aulacomnium androgynum

Bud-headed Groove-moss

Often found in somewhat drier habitats than the other species, such as on rotting wood, humus banks, or shaded soil, sometimes near streams. Generally smaller than A. palustre. Distinctive for producing spherical clusters of small, oval gemmae on terminal stalks. Capsules are ribbed.

Phylogeny and Classification

Aulacomniaceae belongs to the large and diverse class Bryopsida. Its precise phylogenetic placement has been debated, but it is often classified within the order Rhizogoniales, alongside families like Rhizogoniaceae and Calomniaceae. Some classifications place it within or near the order Bryales. Both Rhizogoniales and Bryales represent major lineages within the subclass Bryidae.

The family represents a distinct lineage characterized by features like papillose leaf cells, the tendency towards asexual reproduction via specialized gemmae, and the characteristic ribbed capsules. Its relationships to other families within the Bryidae subclass continue to be refined by molecular phylogenetic studies.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Division: Bryophyta (Mosses)
  • Class: Bryopsida
  • Subclass: Bryidae
  • Order: Rhizogoniales (or Bryales)
  • Family: Aulacomniaceae

Related Families/Groups

Depending on the classification system, families considered related to Aulacomniaceae include:

  • Within Rhizogoniales: Rhizogoniaceae, Calomniaceae, Orthodontiaceae.
  • Within or near Bryales: Bryaceae, Mniaceae, Leptostomataceae, Meesiaceae, Bartramiaceae.

These families share the general characteristics of the Bryidae subclass (e.g., acrocarpous habit, diplolepidous peristome) but differ in specific details of leaf structure, capsule shape, peristome details, and habitat preferences.

Evolutionary Significance

Aulacomniaceae demonstrates several interesting evolutionary aspects:

  • Adaptation to Wet Habitats: Shows successful colonization of challenging bog and fen environments.
  • Asexual Reproduction Strategies: The development of specialized stalked gemmae in some species highlights the importance of asexual reproduction for dispersal and persistence in certain environments.
  • Morphological Features: The combination of papillose cells and ribbed capsules represents a distinct character suite within the Bryidae.
  • Phylogenetic Placement: Its position within Rhizogoniales/Bryales contributes to understanding the diversification patterns within this large group of mosses.
