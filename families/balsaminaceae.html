Balsaminaceae

Touch-me-not Family / Balsam Family

Balsaminaceae is a family of flowering plants predominantly composed of the large genus Impatiens (over 1000 species), along with the monotypic aquatic genus Hydrocera. These are mostly succulent herbs known for their distinctive, strongly zygomorphic flowers featuring a nectar spur, and the explosive seed pods of Impatiens species. The family is widespread, especially in Old World tropics, and belongs to the order Ericales.

Overview

The Balsaminaceae family is renowned for the genus Impatiens, which includes popular horticultural plants like Busy Lizzie and Garden Balsam, as well as widespread native species like Jewelweed. These plants are typically succulent annual or perennial herbs with soft, often translucent stems that are frequently swollen at the nodes. They thrive in moist, often shaded habitats.

The family's flowers are highly specialized, exhibiting strong bilateral symmetry (zygomorphy) with a characteristic nectar-containing spur formed from a modified sepal, adapted for pollination primarily by bees, butterflies, moths, and birds. Perhaps the most famous feature is the fruit of Impatiens: a fleshy capsule that, when ripe, explosively dehisces upon the slightest touch, scattering seeds widely – the origin of the "touch-me-not" name.

Balsaminaceae has a broad distribution but centers of diversity are in tropical Africa, Madagascar, India, and Southeast Asia, with fewer species in temperate Asia, Europe, and North America. The family belongs to the large and diverse order Ericales within the Asterid clade of Eudicots.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Balsaminaceae
  • Common Name: Touch-me-not Family, Balsam Family, Impatiens Family
  • Number of Genera: 2 (Impatiens, Hydrocera)
  • Number of Species: Over 1000
  • Distribution: Widespread, especially Old World tropics and subtropics; also some temperate regions.
  • Evolutionary Group: Eudicots - Asterids - Ericales

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Most members are succulent annual or perennial herbs. Stems are typically soft, fleshy, often translucent, and commonly swollen at the nodes. Some can become somewhat subshrubby. The genus Hydrocera is distinct as an aquatic, emergent herb.

Leaves

Leaves are simple and arranged alternately, oppositely, or whorled along the stem (often varying on the same plant). Leaf margins are typically toothed (serrate or crenate), and frequently bear small glands (extrafloral nectaries) on the teeth or at the base of the petiole. Stipules are generally absent or reduced to nectariferous glands.

Flowers

Flowers are highly distinctive, bisexual, and strongly zygomorphic (bilaterally symmetrical). They occur solitary or in small axillary clusters.

  • Sepals: Typically 3 (sometimes appearing as 5 in development). The lowermost sepal is large, petal-like (petaloid), and drawn out backwards into a hollow nectar spur. The other two (upper/lateral) sepals are usually small and green, sometimes minute or absent.
  • Petals: Usually 5, but highly modified and appearing fewer. The uppermost petal (standard or banner) is usually large and positioned upright. The four lateral petals are fused in pairs, forming two large, wing-like structures, one on each side of the flower entrance.
  • Stamens: 5, with short, flattened filaments that are typically fused (connate) to form a cap-like structure over the ovary. The anthers are also joined (connate) around the stigma. This entire structure (the androecium) often detaches and falls off after pollen is shed.
  • Ovary: Superior, composed of 4 or 5 fused carpels with ovules arranged on axile placentas. A single short style with a 4- or 5-lobed stigma.

Fruits and Seeds

The fruit in the vast majority of the family (genus Impatiens) is a fleshy, elongated explosive dehiscent capsule. When mature and touched, internal pressures cause the capsule walls to split and coil back violently, flinging the seeds distances away (ballistic dispersal or ballochory). The single species of Hydrocera produces an indehiscent, fleshy, berry-like drupe adapted for water dispersal.

Chemical Characteristics

The characteristic succulence is due to high water content and mucilage within the plant tissues. Extrafloral nectaries secrete sugary solutions, potentially attracting ants for defense or distracting herbivores. Some Impatiens species contain compounds like 2-methoxy-1,4-naphthoquinone (giving orange color to Jewelweed stems) and possess mild anti-inflammatory or anti-itch properties, utilized in traditional remedies. Calcium oxalate crystals may be present in tissues.

Field Identification

Identifying Balsaminaceae often relies on recognizing their succulent habit combined with their unique flower structure and (for Impatiens) their explosive fruits:

Primary Identification Features

  • Habit: Look for succulent herbs, often with watery, translucent stems that may be swollen at the nodes. Usually found in moist areas.
  • Flowers: Highly irregular (zygomorphic) flowers are key. Look for the prominent backward-pointing nectar spur (part of a sepal) and the fused lateral petals that look like two large side "wings".
  • Fruit (Impatiens): Elongated, fleshy capsules that explode forcefully when touched or disturbed when ripe.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Leaves: Simple, usually toothed leaves, often bearing tiny glands (extrafloral nectaries) along the edges or on the petiole.
  • Stamens: The 5 stamens fused into a cap over the ovary are characteristic but may fall off early in the flower's life.
  • Hydrocera: If found as an aquatic plant with pinkish flowers and berry-like fruit in Southeast Asia, it is Hydrocera triflora.

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Flowering/Fruiting: Most easily identified during the flowering and fruiting season (typically summer and fall in temperate regions, extended periods in the tropics) when the distinctive flowers and/or explosive pods are present.
  • Vegetative State: The succulent stems and characteristic leaves (especially with glands) can help identify them vegetatively during the growing season. Many annual species die back in winter.

Common Confusion Points

  • Flower Shape: While zygomorphic flowers occur in many families (Orchidaceae, Lamiaceae, Fabaceae, Gesneriaceae), the specific combination of a large spurred lower sepal and paired fused lateral petals is unique to Balsaminaceae. Orchids have a highly complex structure involving a labellum and column. Lamiaceae have bilabiate (two-lipped) flowers and square stems. Fabaceae have papilionaceous (pea-like) flowers.
  • Succulent Habit: Other succulent herbs exist (e.g., some Portulacaceae, Crassulaceae), but their flower and fruit structures are very different.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Succulent herb with watery/translucent stems
  • Irregular (zygomorphic) flower
  • Prominent nectar spur pointing backwards
  • Fused lateral petals forming side "wings"
  • Explosive seed capsule (Impatiens)
  • Toothed leaves often with glands

Key Variations:

  • Flower color (highly variable: pink, purple, white, yellow, orange, red)
  • Spur length and curvature
  • Leaf arrangement (alternate, opposite, whorled)
  • Habit: Annual vs. perennial
  • Hydrocera: Aquatic habit, berry-like fruit

Notable Examples

The family is dominated by Impatiens, with Hydrocera being the only other genus.

Impatiens walleriana

Busy Lizzie, Balsam

Native to eastern Africa, this species is one of the most popular bedding plants worldwide, known for its prolific flowering in various colors (pinks, reds, oranges, whites, purples). It's a tender perennial grown as an annual in cooler climates. Suffered greatly from downy mildew in recent years.

Impatiens capensis

Jewelweed, Spotted Touch-me-not

A common native annual found in moist woodlands and wetlands across eastern and central North America. Recognizable by its orange, trumpet-shaped flowers with reddish spots and a recurved spur. The juice from its succulent stem is traditionally used to soothe skin irritations like poison ivy rash.

Impatiens balsamina

Garden Balsam, Rose Balsam

Native to South and Southeast Asia, this annual species has long been cultivated. Flowers are borne close to the main stem, often nestled between leaves, and come in shades of pink, red, purple, and white. Cultivars with double flowers are common.

Hydrocera triflora

Water Balsam

The single species in its genus, found in wetlands and slow-moving waters from Southeast Asia to northern Australia. It is an emergent aquatic herb with pentagonal stems, whorled leaves below and alternate above, pinkish flowers, and distinctive fleshy, berry-like fruits that are dispersed by water.

Phylogeny and Classification

Balsaminaceae is placed within the large and diverse order Ericales, belonging to the Asterid clade of Eudicots. Within Ericales, molecular studies suggest its closest relatives are the families Marcgraviaceae (tropical vines/shrubs with unique bracts) and Tetrameristaceae (small family of trees/shrubs).

The family itself consists of only two genera, but Impatiens is exceptionally species-rich (hyperdiverse), with over 1000 species described. This suggests rapid diversification, particularly in its centers of diversity like Africa, Madagascar, and the Sino-Himalayan region. Understanding the relationships within Impatiens is an active area of research.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Clade: Angiosperms (Flowering plants)
  • Clade: Eudicots
  • Clade: Asterids
  • Order: Ericales
  • Family: Balsaminaceae

Related Families

Balsaminaceae is part of the diverse Ericales order. Other major families in this order include:

  • Ericaceae: (Heath family) Includes blueberries, rhododendrons, heathers. Often associated with acidic soils.
  • Primulaceae: (Primrose family) Includes primroses, cyclamen, shooting stars. Mostly herbaceous.
  • Sapotaceae: Tropical trees, often producing latex (includes chicle, shea butter).
  • Ebenaceae: (Ebony family) Trees known for dark heartwood (ebony) and persimmons.
  • Polemoniaceae: (Phlox family) Mostly herbs, often with 5-merous flowers with fused petals.
  • Marcgraviaceae & Tetrameristaceae: Considered the closest relatives based on molecular data.

Evolutionary Significance

Balsaminaceae exhibits several features of evolutionary interest:

  • Specialized Pollination Syndromes: The highly modified zygomorphic flowers with nectar spurs are adapted to specific pollinators (bees, butterflies, moths, birds), driving diversification.
  • Explosive Seed Dispersal (Ballochory): The explosive capsules of Impatiens represent an effective mechanism for short-to-medium distance seed dispersal.
  • Rapid Speciation: The enormous size of the genus Impatiens points to rapid evolutionary radiation, likely linked to adaptation to diverse habitats (especially montane forests) and pollination specialization.
  • Adaptation to Moist Habitats: The prevalence of succulent tissues is an adaptation to the typically moist environments where these plants thrive.
