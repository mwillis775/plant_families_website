Blandfordiaceae

Christmas Bells

Blandfordiaceae is a small, monogeneric family comprising only the genus Blandfordia, with four species of perennial herbs endemic to eastern Australia. Commonly known as Christmas Bells, they are celebrated for their striking, large, bell-shaped or tubular flowers, typically red, orange, or yellow, borne on tall stalks above tufts of grass-like leaves. This monocot family belongs to the order Asparagales.

Overview

Blandfordiaceae is a distinctive family consisting of a single genus, Blandfordia, native exclusively to eastern Australia (Queensland, New South Wales, Tasmania). The four species are perennial herbs that grow from short rhizomes or corms, forming clumps of long, linear, grass-like leaves. They typically inhabit heathlands, woodlands, and swampy areas, often on nutrient-poor, sandy soils.

The common name, Christmas Bells, arises from their spectacular flowers, which bloom primarily in the Austral spring and summer (around Christmas). These large, nodding, brightly colored, bell-shaped flowers are borne in terminal clusters (racemes) on tall, erect stalks (scapes) that rise well above the foliage. They are adapted to pollination by birds, particularly honeyeaters, attracted to the abundant nectar produced within the flower tube.

These plants are fire-tolerant, often resprouting vigorously and flowering profusely after bushfires. Phylogenetically, Blandfordiaceae is placed within the large monocot order Asparagales, which includes orchids, irises, onions, and asparagus.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Blandfordiaceae
  • Common Name: Christmas Bells
  • Number of Genera: 1 (Blandfordia)
  • Number of Species: 4
  • Distribution: Endemic to Eastern Australia (Queensland, New South Wales, Tasmania).
  • Evolutionary Group: Monocots - Asparagales

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Perennial herbs forming tufts or clumps from short, often woody rhizomes or corms. They possess fibrous roots.

Leaves

Leaves are numerous, long, linear, and grass-like, arising from the base of the plant (basal). They are typically arranged in two ranks (distichous) or sometimes spirally. Leaves are often tough and fibrous, with parallel venation characteristic of monocots. Margins may be entire or finely toothed.

Flowers and Inflorescence

Flowers are borne in a terminal raceme (an unbranched cluster with stalked flowers) at the apex of a long, erect, leafless flowering stalk (scape) that emerges from the basal leaf tuft. Individual flowers are large, showy, pendulous or nodding, bisexual, and radially symmetrical (actinomorphic).

  • Perianth: Consists of 6 petal-like tepals that are fused for most of their length to form a conspicuous cylindrical or bell-shaped tube, flaring slightly into 6 short lobes at the mouth. The perianth is typically brightly colored in shades of red, orange, or yellow, often bi-colored with contrasting lobe tips (usually yellow).
  • Stamens: 6 stamens, with filaments attached to the inner surface of the perianth tube (epitepalous), positioned opposite the tepal lobes. Anthers are attached at their back (dorsifixed) and open via longitudinal slits.
  • Ovary: Superior, composed of 3 fused carpels forming 3 locules (chambers). Placentation is axile, with numerous ovules per locule. Septal nectaries (producing nectar) are located within the partitions (septa) of the ovary. A single, relatively long style arises from the top of the ovary, ending in a small, simple or slightly 3-lobed stigma.

Fruits and Seeds

The fruit is a dry, dehiscent capsule, typically elongated or oblong in shape. It splits open along the septa (septicidal) or sometimes along the center of each locule (loculicidal) into three valves to release the seeds.

Seeds are numerous, small, often flattened or angled, and may be slightly winged, hairy, or papillose (covered in small bumps). They contain endosperm.

Chemical Characteristics

Plants are known to contain steroidal saponins. Alkaloids may also be present. They are not typically used for food or major medicinal purposes, though Aboriginal peoples may have utilized them traditionally.

Field Identification

Identifying Blandfordiaceae relies on recognizing the combination of its grass-like leaves and highly distinctive flowers:

Primary Identification Features

  • Habit: Tufted perennial herb with linear, grass-like basal leaves.
  • Inflorescence: Flowers borne in a raceme atop a tall, erect scape.
  • Flowers: Large, showy, nodding, tubular or bell-shaped, typically bright red, orange, or yellow, often bi-colored.
  • Perianth: Composed of 6 tepals fused into a prominent tube with short lobes.
  • Ovary: Superior.
  • Location: Endemic to Eastern Australia (heathlands, woodlands, swamps).

Secondary Identification Features

  • Stamens: 6 stamens attached inside the perianth tube.
  • Fruit: A 3-valved capsule containing many small seeds.
  • Habitat: Often found in sandy, nutrient-poor soils; frequently flowers after fire.

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Flowering Season: Most easily identified during spring and summer (November to February in Australia) when the iconic "Christmas Bells" flowers are in bloom on their tall scapes.
  • Year-round: The dense tufts of grass-like leaves are present year-round but can be difficult to distinguish from other monocots when not in flower.
  • Post-Fire: Look for abundant flowering in habitats that have recently experienced bushfires.

Common Confusion Points

  • Other Grass-like Monocots: Vegetatively, can resemble grasses (Poaceae), sedges (Cyperaceae), rushes (Juncaceae), or other monocots with basal linear leaves (e.g., Xanthorrhoeaceae, some Iridaceae). However, the flower structure is entirely different and unmistakable.
  • Other Showy Monocots: While families like Liliaceae (sensu lato) or Iridaceae have showy flowers, they typically have free tepals or tepals fused only at the base (not forming a long distinct tube), different stamen arrangements, or inferior ovaries (Iridaceae). The combination of the tall scape, raceme of large, nodding, tubular/bell-shaped, brightly colored flowers with fused tepals and superior ovary is unique to Blandfordia.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Herb with basal tuft of linear, grass-like leaves
  • Tall scape bearing a raceme of flowers
  • Flowers large, nodding, bell or tube-shaped
  • Flowers bright red/orange/yellow (often bi-colored)
  • 6 tepals fused into long tube
  • Superior ovary
  • Capsule fruit
  • Endemic to E Australia

Key Variations:

  • Flower color combinations (e.g., all red, red with yellow tips)
  • Flower shape (narrow tube vs. wider bell)
  • Plant height/robustness

Notable Examples

All species belong to the genus Blandfordia and are known as Christmas Bells.

Blandfordia grandiflora

Christmas Bells

Found in coastal Queensland and New South Wales. Known for its particularly large, widely flared bell-shaped flowers, typically reddish-orange with yellow lobes. Often found in damp heathland and woodland edges.

Blandfordia nobilis

Christmas Bells

Occurs in New South Wales, typically in sandy heathlands near the coast. Flowers are generally narrower and more tubular than B. grandiflora, usually reddish with distinct yellow lobes. This was the first species described.

Phylogeny and Classification

Blandfordiaceae is classified within the large and diverse monocot order Asparagales. Its exact position within the order has been refined by molecular phylogenetic studies. It is generally considered part of the "lower" or non-core Asparagales group.

Molecular data suggest close relationships with other Southern Hemisphere families, particularly Asteliaceae (mostly tufted herbs, Southern Hemisphere) and potentially Lanariaceae (monotypic, South Africa) and Hypoxidaceae (Star Grass family, widespread). Blandfordiaceae represents a small, distinct lineage endemic to Australia within this major monocot order.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Clade: Angiosperms (Flowering plants)
  • Clade: Monocots
  • Order: Asparagales
  • Family: Blandfordiaceae

Related Families

Blandfordiaceae belongs to the Asparagales order. Closely related families based on molecular data often include:

  • Asteliaceae: Tufted perennial herbs, mostly Southern Hemisphere.
  • Hypoxidaceae: (Star Grass family) Herbs with corms/rhizomes, often yellow star-like flowers.
  • Lanariaceae: Monotypic family from South Africa (Lanaria lanata).

Other major families in the large Asparagales order (more distantly related) include:

  • Orchidaceae: (Orchid family) Immense family with highly specialized flowers.
  • Asparagaceae: (Asparagus family) Very diverse, includes Agave, Hyacinths, Yucca, etc.
  • Amaryllidaceae: (Amaryllis family) Includes onions, garlic, daffodils, amaryllis; often bulbous with inferior ovaries.
  • Iridaceae: (Iris family) Includes irises, crocuses, gladioli; usually 3 stamens and inferior ovaries.

Evolutionary Significance

Blandfordiaceae, though small, is significant for:

  • Australian Endemism: Represents a unique lineage entirely restricted to eastern Australia, contributing to the continent's distinct flora.
  • Monocot Diversity: Adds to the understanding of phylogenetic relationships and morphological diversity within the large Asparagales order.
  • Pollination Biology: The large, tubular, brightly colored flowers are a classic example of adaptation for pollination by nectar-feeding birds (ornithophily), specifically honeyeaters in Australia.
  • Fire Ecology: Demonstrates adaptations common in Australian flora, such as resprouting from underground organs and enhanced flowering following bushfires.
