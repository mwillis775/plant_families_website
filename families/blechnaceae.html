Blechnaceae

Chain Fern or Hard Fern Family

Blechnaceae is a family of ferns (order Polypodiales), commonly known as chain ferns or hard ferns. They are primarily terrestrial, sometimes epiphytic or climbing, with a near-cosmopolitan distribution but greatest diversity in the Southern Hemisphere. Key features include often pinnate and frequently dimorphic fronds, and distinctive linear sori (coenosori) running parallel to the costa (pinna midrib), typically covered by an indusium opening towards the costa.

Overview

The Blechnaceae family represents a well-defined lineage of ferns found in diverse habitats ranging from wet forests and swamps to rocky slopes and alpine regions. While distributed globally, the family shows a particular richness in the temperate and tropical regions of the Southern Hemisphere. Many species are terrestrial, growing from often stout, scaly rhizomes that can be creeping, ascending, or erect, sometimes forming short tree-like trunks in genera like Sadleria or some species formerly in Blechnum.

A striking feature in many Blechnaceae species is frond dimorphism: fertile fronds are often distinctly different from sterile fronds, typically being more erect, with narrower pinnae (segments) where the lamina is reduced to accommodate the large sori. The most diagnostic characteristic of the family is the arrangement of the sori: they form long, continuous or interrupted linear chains (coenosori) located on either side of and parallel to the costa (midrib) of the fertile pinnae or segments. These are typically protected by a linear indusium that opens inwards, facing the costa.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Blechnaceae Newman
  • Common Name: Chain Fern Family, Hard Fern Family
  • Number of Genera: ~24 (following recent classifications, e.g., PPG I)
  • Number of Species: ~250
  • Distribution: Near-cosmopolitan, but most diverse in the Southern Hemisphere and tropical mountains.
  • Evolutionary Group: Ferns (Polypodiopsida) - Polypodiales - Eupolypods II

Key Characteristics

Note: Blechnaceae are recognized by their often pinnate, frequently dimorphic fronds, and especially by the linear sori (coenosori) running parallel to the pinna midribs (costae), usually with an indusium opening towards the costa.

Sporophyte (Dominant Phase) Structure

The visible fern plant is the diploid sporophyte:

  • Rhizome: Creeping, ascending, or erect; often stout and woody, sometimes forming short trunks; typically covered with conspicuous scales (often large, brown to blackish, non-clathrate).
  • Fronds (Leaves):
    • Size & Form: Highly variable in size; frequently dimorphic (fertile fronds erect, narrower, with reduced lamina compared to the broader, often spreading sterile fronds), but sometimes monomorphic.
    • Stipe (Petiole): Base often scaly; vascular bundles are typically multiple (3+), round in cross-section, arranged in a U-shape or ring (similar to Dryopteridaceae, unlike the two strap-shaped bundles of Athyriaceae/Aspleniaceae).
    • Blade: Typically pinnatifid to 1-pinnate, less commonly simple, 2-pinnate, or decompound. Pinnae often articulate ( cleanly detaching) to the rachis in some groups (e.g., some Blechnum s.l.). Texture often leathery ('hard ferns').
    • Veins: Usually free, simple or forked. Sometimes anastomosing near the costa to form a costal areole from which the sorus arises.
    • Indument: Usually scaly, especially on rhizome and stipe base; blades may be glabrous or bear scales or hairs.

Reproductive Structures (Sori)

Sori contain the sporangia and are located on the underside of fertile fronds (or fertile pinnae/segments):

  • Shape & Position: Characteristically linear, forming continuous or interrupted chains (coenosori) on each side of and parallel to the costa (pinna midrib) or sometimes costules. They arise from a vascular commissure connecting the veins.
  • Indusium: Typically present (rarely absent), linear, attached along the outer edge of the sorus (further from the costa) and opening inwards (towards the costa). Texture often firm.

Sporangia and Spores

  • Sporangia: Typical leptosporangiate structure with a vertical annulus.
  • Spores: Monolete (bilateral symmetry, bean-shaped); surface varies (perispore ornamentation).

Gametophyte

The small, haploid gametophyte phase:

  • Form: Typically develops from the spore as a green, heart-shaped (cordate) or sometimes strap-shaped prothallus.

Field Identification

Identifying Blechnaceae ferns primarily involves examining the fertile fronds for the distinctive sorus arrangement and checking for frond dimorphism.

Primary Identification Features

  • Costal Coenosori: Look for long, linear sori running parallel to the midrib (costa) of the fertile pinnae/segments.
  • Indusium Opening Inwards: The linear indusium, if present, opens towards the costa.
  • Frond Dimorphism: Check if fertile fronds are distinctly different (usually narrower and more erect) than sterile fronds (common but not universal).
  • Stipe Vascular Bundles: A cross-section of the stipe base typically shows multiple (3+) round vascular bundles (requires cutting).
  • Pinnate/Pinnatifid Fronds: Most species have this structure, often with a leathery texture.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Stout Rhizome/Scales: Rhizomes are often robust and well-scaled. Some species form short trunks.
  • Habitat: Found in diverse habitats, often associated with moisture.

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Year-round: Sterile fronds (and rhizomes) are often present year-round (may be evergreen or deciduous).
  • Growing Season (Spring/Summer/Fall): Fertile fronds with mature sori are typically produced during the main growing season, making identification easiest then. Dimorphism is most apparent when both frond types are present.

Common Confusion Points

The unique sorus arrangement helps distinguish Blechnaceae:

  • Aspleniaceae (Spleenworts): Have linear sori, but they run along the secondary veins (not parallel to the costa) and the indusium opens outwards (away from the vein). Stipes have only two vascular bundles uniting upwards.
  • Pteridaceae (e.g., Pteris): Have linear sori located along the pinna margin, typically covered by the reflexed edge of the lamina (a false indusium).
  • Dryopteridaceae / Athyriaceae / Thelypteridaceae / Woodsiaceae: These families typically have sori that are round, J-shaped, U-shaped, or short-linear, not long costal coenosori. Dryopteridaceae share the multiple round stipe bundles, but soral characters differ greatly. The others typically have only two stipe bundles.
  • Polypodiaceae: Sori are typically round or oval, usually lack indusia, and are not arranged in costal chains.
  • Onocleaceae (Sensitive Fern Family): Also exhibits strong dimorphism, but fertile fronds are bead-like (sori enclosed in hardened pinna segments); sterile fronds are distinct. Shares multiple stipe bundles.

Key differentiator: The combination of linear sori parallel to the costa (costal coenosori) and an indusium opening towards the costa is highly diagnostic for Blechnaceae.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Sori: Linear chains parallel to costa (coenosori)
  • Indusia: Linear, opening towards costa
  • Fronds: Often dimorphic (fertile narrower)
  • Frond structure: Pinnatifid to 1-pinnate (usually)
  • Stipe Base: Multiple (3+) round vascular bundles
  • Rhizome: Often stout, scaly; sometimes trunk-forming

Key Distinctions:

  • vs. Aspleniaceae: Sori along secondary veins, indusium opens outwards, 2 stipe bundles.
  • vs. Pteridaceae (Pteris): Sori marginal, covered by reflexed margin.
  • vs. Dryopteridaceae etc.: Sori round/J-shaped etc., not costal chains (though Dryopt. shares multiple stipe bundles).
  • vs. Onocleaceae: Fertile fronds bead-like.

Notable Examples

Blechnaceae includes familiar ferns, particularly those known for dimorphism or distinctive soral chains.

Blechnum spicant (or Struthiopteris spicant)

Deer Fern, Hard Fern

Widespread in temperate Northern Hemisphere forests. Exhibits strong dimorphism: spreading, pinnatifid sterile fronds and very narrow, erect fertile fronds with continuous costal sori.

Woodwardia areolata (syn. Lorinseria areolata)

Netted Chain Fern

Found in eastern North America, often in wetlands. Shows dimorphism. Named for the chain-like appearance of the interrupted sori along the costa and the netted (areolate) venation near the costa on sterile fronds.

Sadleria cyatheoides

ʻAmaʻu Fern

A common tree fern endemic to Hawaii, forming short trunks. Fronds are large, 2-pinnate-pinnatifid. Sori are costal coenosori. Young fronds are often reddish.

Phylogeny and Classification

Blechnaceae is a well-supported monophyletic family within the Eupolypods II clade of the order Polypodiales. Molecular phylogenetic studies have solidified its position and led to significant recircumscription compared to older classifications. Notably, genera like Woodwardia (including Lorinseria and Anchistea) and the tree-fern genus Sadleria, sometimes placed in their own families or within Dryopteridaceae, are now firmly included within Blechnaceae based on molecular evidence.

The traditional large genus Blechnum was found to be polyphyletic and has been split into numerous smaller, more natural genera (e.g., Struthiopteris, Parablechnum, Austroblechnum, Blechnidium, etc.), although the name Blechnum is retained for a smaller core group. The family is characterized by the unique costal coenosorus, likely a key evolutionary innovation. Its relationships within Eupolypods II place it sister to or near families like Onocleaceae and Athyriaceae sensu lato.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Division: Pteridophyta (or Polypodiophyta)
  • Class: Polypodiopsida
  • Order: Polypodiales
  • Family: Blechnaceae
  • (Part of the Eupolypods II clade)

Related Families

Blechnaceae is part of the diverse Eupolypods II clade. Its closest relatives include:

  • Onocleaceae: Shares strong dimorphism and multiple stipe bundles but differs significantly in fertile frond structure (bead-like). Often considered sister to Blechnaceae.
  • Athyriaceae / Woodsiaceae / Aspleniaceae / Thelypteridaceae: Other major families within Eupolypods II, distinguished primarily by soral characteristics and stipe vascular bundle counts (Athyriaceae, Aspleniaceae, Thelypteridaceae, Woodsiaceae typically have 2 bundles uniting upwards).

Evolutionary Significance

Blechnaceae is significant for:

  • Unique Soral Arrangement: The evolution of the costal coenosorus with an inwardly opening indusium is a defining feature.
  • Prevalence of Frond Dimorphism: Represents a major evolutionary trend within the family, likely related to optimizing spore dispersal and photosynthesis separately.
  • Biogeography: Shows a strong signal of Southern Hemisphere diversification and Gondwanan origins for many lineages.
  • Morphological Diversity: Includes terrestrial, epiphytic, climbing, and even trunk-forming habits within a single, well-defined family.
