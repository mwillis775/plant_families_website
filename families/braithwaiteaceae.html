Braithwaiteaceae

Pleated Moss Family (Informal)

Braithwaiteaceae is a monotypic family of robust pleurocarpous mosses belonging to the order Ptychomniales. It contains the single genus Braithwaitea with its sole species B. sulcata. The family is defined by its distinctive, strongly plicate (longitudinally pleated) leaves and is found in Australasia.

Overview

The Braithwaiteaceae family, established relatively recently by McFarland, McDaniel & Goffinet based on molecular and morphological data, accommodates the single, unique moss genus Braithwaitea. This genus, and therefore the family, is monotypic, containing only the species Braithwaitea sulcata.

These are robust, pleurocarpous mosses, typically forming dense, often yellowish-green to brownish mats or wefts. They are most readily recognized by their leaves, which are deeply and conspicuously folded lengthwise (plicate or sulcate), a feature particularly evident when the plant is dry. Braithwaiteaceae are primarily epiphytes, growing on tree trunks and branches, but can also be found on rocks or logs in forests.

The family's distribution is restricted to Australasia, primarily eastern Australia and New Zealand, with possible occurrences in New Caledonia. It belongs to the small order Ptychomniales, closely related to the family Ptychomniaceae.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Braithwaiteaceae N.C.McFarland, J.R.McDaniel & B.Goffinet
  • Common Name: Pleated Moss Family (Informal)
  • Number of Genera: 1 (Braithwaitea Lindb.)
  • Number of Species: 1 (Braithwaitea sulcata (Hook.) A.Jaeger)
  • Distribution: Australasia (E. Australia, New Zealand), possibly New Caledonia
  • Evolutionary Group: Bryophytes - Mosses (Class Bryatae - Order Ptychomniales)

Key Characteristics (Moss Features)

Gametophyte (Leafy Plant)

The dominant stage. Plants are robust, pleurocarpous (creeping main stems with lateral branches and sporophytes), forming extensive mats.

  • Stems: Creeping, densely covered with leaves, irregularly or sub-pinnately branched. Branches often ascending.
  • Leaves (Phyllids): Crowded, erect-spreading to spreading, ovate-lanceolate, concave. The most striking feature is that they are strongly plicate (with multiple longitudinal folds or pleats), especially noticeable when dry. Leaf apex is usually acuminate (tapering to a point). Margins often recurved below and toothed above.
  • Costa (Midrib): Single and strong, extending well up the leaf but typically ending below the apex.
  • Leaf Cells: Elongated (linear to rhomboidal), usually smooth or sometimes slightly papillose (with small bumps). Alar cells (at basal corners) may be somewhat differentiated but not typically inflated or strongly colored.

Reproductive Structures (Gametophyte)

Antheridia and archegonia are produced in lateral buds along the stems.

Sporophyte (Spore-Producing Structure)

Arises laterally from the gametophyte stem. Consists of:

  • Seta: Relatively long, smooth, often reddish or brownish.
  • Capsule (Sporangium): Usually inclined to horizontal, ovoid to cylindrical, often slightly curved and asymmetrical. An operculum (lid) covers the mouth.

Peristome

Located beneath the operculum. The peristome is double and well-developed, typical of the Ptychomniales order. It consists of an outer ring of 16 lanceolate exostome teeth and an inner endostome with segments and cilia.

Field Identification

Identifying Braithwaiteaceae (Braithwaitea sulcata) relies heavily on recognizing its distinctive pleated leaves:

Primary Identification Features

  • Strongly Plicate Leaves: This is the most crucial feature. Look for robust moss leaves that have conspicuous longitudinal folds or pleats, making them appear ridged or furrowed, especially when dry.
  • Robust, Pleurocarpous Habit: Plants form dense mats or wefts with creeping stems and ascending branches.
  • Strong Single Costa: Leaves possess a prominent midrib extending most of their length.
  • Habitat and Location: Found primarily as epiphytes (or sometimes on rocks/logs) in forests within Australasia (E. Australia, New Zealand).

Secondary Identification Features

  • Inclined/Horizontal Capsule: If sporophytes are present, note the capsule orientation on a relatively long seta.
  • Color: Often yellowish-green to golden-brown.

Common Confusion Points

  • Ptychomniaceae: The most closely related family, also with plicate leaves. Distinguishing them may require examining sporophyte details or subtle gametophyte features (e.g., leaf shape, cell structure). Braithwaitea tends to be more robust with more strongly plicate leaves than many Ptychomniaceae.
  • Other Pleurocarpous Mosses: While other families like Hypnaceae or Brachytheciaceae contain robust pleurocarps, they generally lack the distinctly plicate leaves of Braithwaiteaceae.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Robust, mat-forming moss
  • Strongly pleated/plicate leaves (key!)
  • Strong single costa
  • Pleurocarpous growth habit
  • Epiphytic/epilithic in Australasia

Key Variations:

  • Color (yellow-green to brown)
  • Branching density
  • Presence/abundance of sporophytes
  • (Family is monotypic - variations are within B. sulcata)

Notable Examples

The family is monotypic, containing only one genus and species:

Braithwaitea sulcata

(No widely used common name)

The sole species in the family Braithwaiteaceae. It is a robust pleurocarpous moss easily recognized by its strongly plicate (pleated) leaves. It grows in forests in eastern Australia and New Zealand, typically as an epiphyte.

Phylogeny and Classification

Braithwaiteaceae is classified within the Class Bryatae (true mosses) and belongs to the relatively small order Ptychomniales. This order is characterized by pleurocarpous mosses often having plicate leaves and a particular type of double peristome.

Molecular phylogenetic studies have confirmed the placement of Braithwaitea within the Ptychomniales and supported its separation into its own family, distinct from the closely related Ptychomniaceae. Braithwaiteaceae represents a specialized lineage within this order, endemic to the Australasian region.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Division: Bryophyta (Mosses)
  • Class: Bryatae (or Bryopsida)
  • Order: Ptychomniales
  • Family: Braithwaiteaceae

Related Families (within Order Ptychomniales)

The primary related family within the Ptychomniales is:

  • Ptychomniaceae: Shares features like plicate leaves and a similar peristome structure. Includes genera like Ptychomnion and Cladomnion. Braithwaiteaceae is distinguished by a combination of morphological and molecular characteristics.

Evolutionary Significance

Braithwaiteaceae, as a monotypic family with a restricted distribution, is significant for understanding the evolution and biogeography of the Ptychomniales order. Its highly distinctive feature – the strongly plicate leaves – represents a notable morphological adaptation within mosses, possibly related to water retention or structural support. Studying this family helps clarify relationships and evolutionary patterns within its specific lineage of pleurocarpous mosses.
