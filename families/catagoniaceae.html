Catagoniaceae

Glossy Sickle Moss Family (Informal)

Catagoniaceae is a monotypic family of glossy, pleurocarpous mosses belonging to the order Hypnales. It contains the single genus Catagonium, typified by C. nitens. The family is characterized by its strongly complanate (flattened) and often falcate-secund (sickle-shaped) leaves that lack a costa (midrib), and its predominantly Southern Hemisphere distribution.

Overview

The Catagoniaceae family, established by Buck & Ireland, is defined by the single genus Catagonium, making it a monotypic family. The most prominent species is Catagonium nitens. These are distinctive, medium-sized to robust pleurocarpous mosses, readily recognized by their pronounced glossiness, often appearing golden-brown, reddish, or bright green.

Key morphological features include the strongly flattened shoots (complanate) and leaves that are typically curved to one side like a sickle (falcate-secund), especially near the tips of branches. Crucially, the leaves lack a midrib (costa) or possess only a very short, faint double one at the base. The plants form dense mats or tufts.

Catagoniaceae have a predominantly Southern Hemisphere distribution, being widespread in temperate and colder regions such as southern South America, South Africa, Australia, New Zealand, and subantarctic islands, sometimes extending into high-elevation tropical areas. They typically grow terrestrially on soil, humus, or rock ledges, often in open or semi-shaded montane or subalpine habitats. The family is placed within the large pleurocarpous order Hypnales.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Catagoniaceae W.R. Buck & Ireland
  • Common Name: Glossy Sickle Moss Family (Informal)
  • Number of Genera: 1 (Catagonium Müll. Hal. ex Broth.)
  • Number of Species: 1-2 (Primarily C. nitens)
  • Distribution: Primarily Southern Hemisphere temperate/cold regions, some high-elevation tropics.
  • Evolutionary Group: Bryophytes - Mosses (Class Bryatae - Order Hypnales)

Key Characteristics (Moss Features)

Gametophyte (Leafy Plant)

The dominant stage. Plants medium to robust, typically very glossy (golden, reddish, brownish, or green), pleurocarpous, forming dense mats or tufts.

  • Stems: Creeping or ascending, irregularly branched. Branches often somewhat flattened.
  • Leaves (Phyllids): Strongly complanate (flattened in one plane) and usually distinctly falcate-secund (curved to one side like a sickle). Leaves ovate-lanceolate to lanceolate, concave, tapering to an acute or acuminate apex. Margins usually plane and entire or finely serrulate near the tip.
  • Costa (Midrib): Characteristically absent, or very short and double and often faint at the extreme base.
  • Leaf Cells: Long and narrow (linear), smooth, often with thick walls, especially towards the leaf base. Alar cells at the basal corners are generally not or only weakly differentiated.

Reproductive Structures (Gametophyte)

Antheridia and archegonia are borne in lateral buds on the stems.

Sporophyte (Spore-Producing Structure)

Arises laterally from the gametophyte stem, often produced infrequently. Consists of:

  • Seta: Usually relatively long, smooth, often reddish.
  • Capsule (Sporangium): Typically inclined to horizontal, ovoid to cylindrical, often symmetric or slightly curved. Operculum usually conical or shortly beaked.

Peristome

Typically double and well-developed (hypnoid type). Consists of 16 outer exostome teeth and an inner endostome with segments and often cilia.

Field Identification

Identifying Catagoniaceae (Catagonium) relies on its glossy appearance and distinctive leaf arrangement:

Primary Identification Features

  • High Gloss: Plants are typically very shiny, often with golden or reddish tints.
  • Complanate & Falcate-Secund Leaves: Look for flattened shoots where the leaves are strongly curved to one side like sickles.
  • Costa Absent: The lack of a midrib (or presence of only a very short double one) is a key diagnostic feature (use hand lens).
  • Habit and Habitat: Dense mats or tufts, often terrestrial on soil or rock ledges in open montane/subalpine environments.
  • Southern Hemisphere Distribution: Primarily found in temperate/cold regions of the Southern Hemisphere.

Common Confusion Points

  • Plagiotheciaceae: Some genera (like Plagiothecium) are also complanate and lack a strong costa. They are often greener, less glossy, may have decurrent leaf bases (running down the stem), and different cell shapes or sporophyte orientations.
  • Hypnaceae: Many genera are pleurocarpous and some are glossy or have falcate leaves (e.g., Hypnum, Drepanocladus). However, they usually differ in the degree of complanation, often possess a short double costa, and may have different cell structures or habitats.
  • Sematophyllaceae: Some glossy pleurocarps, but often differ in leaf shape, presence of distinct alar cells, or peristome structure.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Very glossy moss (golden, reddish, green)
  • Dense mats/tufts
  • Pleurocarpous habit
  • Strongly complanate & falcate-secund leaves
  • Costa absent or very short/double (key!)
  • Linear leaf cells (microscope)
  • Terrestrial/epilithic in S. Hemisphere temperate/cold regions

Key Variations:

  • Color intensity
  • Degree of falcate curvature
  • Frequency of sporophytes
  • (Family is monotypic - variations are within C. nitens complex)

Notable Examples

The family is monotypic, represented by the genus Catagonium:

Catagonium nitens

(Sometimes called Shining Feather Moss, but ambiguous)

The primary, widespread species defining the family Catagoniaceae. It forms distinctive glossy mats with strongly flattened, sickle-shaped leaves that lack a costa. Found in temperate and cold regions of the Southern Hemisphere and some high mountains further north.

Phylogeny and Classification

Catagoniaceae is classified within the Class Bryatae (true mosses) and is placed within the large pleurocarpous order Hypnales. Its exact phylogenetic position within Hypnales has sometimes been debated, with relationships suggested near Plagiotheciaceae or within the broader Hypnaceae complex based on morphology or different molecular analyses.

The combination of strongly complanate, falcate-secund, ecostate leaves, linear cells, and specific sporophyte features defines it as a distinct lineage. Its predominantly Gondwanan distribution pattern is also of biogeographic interest, suggesting ancient origins in the Southern Hemisphere.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Division: Bryophyta (Mosses)
  • Class: Bryatae (or Bryopsida)
  • Order: Hypnales
  • Family: Catagoniaceae

Related Families (within Order Hypnales)

Catagoniaceae is related to other families within the diverse Hypnales order, potentially including:

  • Plagiotheciaceae: Shares complanate habit and often weak/absent costa but differs in glossiness, cell shape, and other details.
  • Hypnaceae: A large, core family of Hypnales, some members are glossy or falcate but differ in costa or complanation.
  • Sematophyllaceae: Diverse tropical/subtropical family, some members glossy but usually differ in alar cells and leaf shape.
  • Entodontaceae: Often glossy with complanate leaves, but typically have a short double costa and different peristome structure.

Evolutionary Significance

Catagoniaceae represents a morphologically distinct and successful lineage of pleurocarpous mosses, particularly well-adapted to temperate and subantarctic environments. Its characteristic features, including the high gloss, specific leaf arrangement, and lack of a costa, highlight the diverse evolutionary pathways within the Hypnales. Its Gondwanan distribution pattern provides valuable data for understanding bryophyte biogeography and the history of life in the Southern Hemisphere.
