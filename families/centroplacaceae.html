Centroplacaceae

Centroplacus Family

Centroplacaceae is a very small family of evergreen trees and shrubs belonging to the large order Malpighiales. It has a remarkably disjunct distribution, with one genus (Centroplacus) in West and Central Africa and the other (Bhesa) in Southeast Asia and Malesia. Key characteristics include alternate, simple leaves with prominent stipules (often intrapetiolar), small, typically unisexual flowers (plants dioecious), a superior ovary, and fruit in the form of a loculicidal capsule. The family was formally recognized relatively recently based on molecular phylogenetic data.

Overview

Centroplacaceae is placed within the order Malpighiales, a major lineage within the Fabid clade of Rosids. It is an exceptionally small family, containing only two genera – the monotypic Centroplacus from Africa and Bhesa with about 5-6 species in Asia – totaling fewer than 10 species worldwide. This striking Africa-Asia disjunct distribution makes the family biogeographically interesting.

Members are evergreen trees or shrubs of tropical forests. They are characterized vegetatively by their alternate, simple leaves which possess relatively large and often persistent stipules, frequently fused between the petiole and stem (intrapetiolar) and sometimes enclosing the terminal bud. The plants are typically dioecious, bearing small, inconspicuous, unisexual flowers arranged in axillary or terminal panicles. Florally, they feature a typically 5-merous structure, a superior ovary composed of 2-3 carpels, and usually 5 stamens in the male flowers. The fruit is consistently a loculicidal capsule.

The recognition of Centroplacaceae as a distinct family is relatively recent (early 21st century), resulting from molecular phylogenetic studies that showed a close relationship between the African Centroplacus and the Asian Bhesa. Bhesa had previously been problematic taxonomically, often placed within Celastraceae or sometimes Rhizophoraceae or other families, but molecular data strongly separate it from those groups and link it instead to Centroplacus. The family has no known major economic uses.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Centroplacaceae Doweld & Reveal
  • Common Name: Centroplacus family (no established English common name)
  • Number of Genera: 2 (Bhesa, Centroplacus)
  • Number of Species: Approximately 6-7
  • Distribution: Disjunct: West & Central Africa (Centroplacus) and SE Asia & Malesia (Bhesa)
  • Evolutionary Group: Eudicots - Rosids (Fabids / Eurosids I) - Order Malpighiales

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Evergreen trees or shrubs.

Leaves

Leaves are arranged alternately and spirally along the stem. They are simple, petiolate, with pinnate venation. Leaf margins are typically entire, but sometimes serrated or crenated, especially in young plants. A key feature is the presence of conspicuous stipules, which are often relatively large, sometimes fused between the petiole and the stem (intrapetiolar), often enclosing the terminal bud when young, and usually deciduous, leaving noticeable scars.

Inflorescence

Inflorescences are typically axillary, but sometimes terminal, usually branched structures like panicles or sometimes appearing raceme-like.

Flowers

Flowers are small, actinomorphic (radially symmetrical), and typically unisexual, with plants being dioecious (separate male and female individuals). Flowers are usually 5-merous.

  • Calyx: Consists of 5 small sepals, typically fused at the base into a cup, persistent.
  • Corolla: Consists of 5 free petals, often greenish, yellowish, or whitish, usually small.
  • Androecium (Male Flower): Typically 5 stamens, alternating with the petals. Filaments are distinct. Stamens are often inserted outside or on the margin of a nectar disk (when present). Anthers dehisce via longitudinal slits. A non-functional pistil (pistillode) is usually present.
  • Gynoecium (Female Flower): The ovary is superior, composed of 2 or 3 fused carpels, forming a 2- or 3-locular ovary. Placentation is axile, with typically 2 ovules per locule, pendulous from the apex. Styles are usually distinct or fused only at the base; stigmas are small. Non-functional stamens (staminodes) may be present. A nectar disk may surround the ovary base.

Fruits and Seeds

The fruit is a loculicidal capsule, meaning it splits open along the walls separating the locules (chambers). The capsule wall is often woody or leathery. It contains 1 to few seeds per locule (often only 1 develops fully). Seeds in Bhesa are reported to possess a fleshy, often reddish aril, while those of Centroplacus may lack a true aril. Endosperm presence/absence can vary. Seeds are often relatively large.

Chemical Characteristics

Detailed chemical studies across the family are limited. Woody plants in Malpighiales often contain compounds like tannins or triterpenoids, which may be present here. Specific chemical markers distinguishing the family are not widely established.

Field Identification

Identifying Centroplacaceae relies on recognizing the combination of tree/shrub habit, alternate simple leaves with prominent stipules, unisexual flowers (if seen), capsular fruit, and its distinctive disjunct tropical distribution.

Primary Identification Features

  • Habit: Tree or shrub.
  • Alternate, Simple Leaves: Leaves are undivided and arranged alternately.
  • Prominent Stipules: Often large, sometimes intrapetiolar, leaving distinct scars if deciduous.
  • Unisexual Flowers / Dioecious Plants: Male and female flowers occur on separate plants.
  • Superior Ovary: Positioned above sepals/petals.
  • Capsule Fruit: Fruit is a dry capsule that splits open.
  • Disjunct Distribution: Native only to specific regions of West/Central Africa OR Southeast Asia/Malesia.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Small, 5-merous flowers in panicles.
  • Pinnate leaf venation.
  • Seeds sometimes arillate (Bhesa).

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Vegetative Characters: Alternate simple leaves with prominent stipule scars are key year-round features. Habit is also constant.
  • Flowering/Fruiting: Likely occurs during specific tropical seasons. The capsular fruits, potentially showing arillate seeds in Bhesa upon dehiscence, are important diagnostic features when present.

Common Confusion Points

  • Former placement of Bhesa: Previously in Celastraceae, but Celastraceae usually have opposite leaves, prominent nectar disks within the stamens, and often different stipule types.
  • Other Malpighiales Families: Many families in this large order are tropical trees/shrubs with alternate simple leaves (e.g., Euphorbiaceae, Phyllanthaceae, Chrysobalanaceae, Putranjivaceae). Distinguishing requires careful attention to stipule details, flower sexuality, ovary structure (number of locules/ovules), presence/absence of latex (absent in Centroplacaceae), and fruit type (capsule here vs. drupes, schizocarps common elsewhere).
  • Families with Large Stipules: e.g., Rubiaceae (stipules interpetiolar, leaves opposite, ovary inferior), Rhizophoraceae (stipules often enclose bud, leaves opposite, flower/fruit different).

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Tree or shrub habit
  • Alternate simple leaves
  • Prominent stipules (often intrapetiolar)
  • Unisexual flowers (dioecious)
  • Small, 5-merous flowers
  • Superior ovary (2-3 locular)
  • Capsule fruit
  • Disjunct Africa/Asia range

Key Variations:

  • Genus (Centroplacus vs. Bhesa)
  • Stipule persistence
  • Presence/absence of seed aril
  • Leaf margin (entire vs. toothed)

Notable Examples

The family comprises only two distinct genera, separated geographically between Africa and Asia.

Centroplacus glaucinus

Centroplacus

The single species in this genus, representing the African component of the family. It is a tree found in the tropical forests of West and Central Africa. It has alternate, simple leaves with stipules, bears unisexual flowers (dioecious), and produces a 3-valved capsular fruit.

Bhesa species (e.g., B. robusta)

Bhesa

This genus contains about 5-6 species of trees and shrubs found in Southeast Asia and Malesia (e.g., India, Sri Lanka, Malaysia, Indonesia). They are characterized by alternate simple leaves with prominent, often persistent intrapetiolar stipules that enclose the buds. Flowers are unisexual (dioecious). The fruit is a 2-valved woody or leathery capsule, and the seeds typically possess a fleshy, often red aril. Bhesa was formerly often placed in Celastraceae.

Phylogeny and Classification

Centroplacaceae is classified within the large and diverse order Malpighiales, belonging to the Fabid clade of Rosids. Its recognition as a distinct family and its placement within Malpighiales are primarily based on molecular phylogenetic analyses conducted in the late 20th and early 21st centuries.

These studies revealed a surprising but strongly supported sister relationship between the monotypic African genus Centroplacus and the Asian genus Bhesa (which was often previously misplaced in Celastraceae or other families). This relationship necessitated the creation of the family Centroplacaceae to accommodate this distinct lineage. Its exact position within the complex phylogeny of Malpighiales is still under investigation, but it appears to be related to other small families like Pandaceae and Ctenolophonaceae, or potentially associated with clades containing families such as Linaceae, Ixonanthaceae, Chrysobalanaceae, or Euphorbiaceae.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Clade: Angiosperms (Flowering plants)
  • Clade: Eudicots
  • Clade: Rosids (Fabids / Eurosids I)
  • Order: Malpighiales
  • Family: Centroplacaceae

Related Families

As a member of the order Malpighiales, Centroplacaceae is distantly related to numerous diverse families. Some potentially closer relatives or notable members within the order include:

  • Pandaceae: Small family of trees/shrubs from Africa and SE Asia.
  • Ctenolophonaceae: Very small family (1 genus) of trees from W Africa and SE Asia/Malesia.
  • Linaceae (Flax family): Mostly herbs/shrubs, simple leaves, often 5-merous flowers with superior ovary.
  • Ixonanthaceae: Small family of tropical trees/shrubs.
  • Euphorbiaceae (Spurge family): Huge, diverse family, often with latex, unisexual flowers common, fruit often a schizocarp.
  • Phyllanthaceae: Trees, shrubs, herbs; alternate simple leaves common; unisexual flowers; superior ovary; fruit often schizocarp or drupe. (Segregated from Euphorbiaceae).
  • Chrysobalanaceae: Trees/shrubs, simple alternate leaves often with glands, zygomorphic flowers, drupe fruit.
  • Putranjivaceae: Trees/shrubs, simple alternate leaves often asymmetrical, dioecious, drupe fruit. (Segregated from Euphorbiaceae).

Evolutionary Significance

Centroplacaceae, though small, holds evolutionary interest:

  • Biogeography: Its striking Africa-Asia disjunct distribution provides a compelling case study for understanding long-distance dispersal or ancient vicariance events related to continental drift.
  • Phylogenetic Resolution: The establishment of the family demonstrates how molecular data can uncover unexpected relationships and resolve the placement of taxonomically problematic genera like Bhesa.
  • Malpighiales Diversification: Represents one of the many distinct lineages that diversified within the massive Malpighiales radiation, contributing to the overall understanding of this complex order's evolution.
  • Morphological Traits: Features like prominent intrapetiolar stipules and the potential presence/absence of arils provide characters for comparative studies within Malpighiales.
