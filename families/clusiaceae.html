Clusiaceae

Mangosteen Family (sensu stricto)

Clusiaceae sensu stricto (the Mangosteen family, formerly part of Guttiferae) is a family of primarily tropical trees, shrubs, and lianas belonging to the order Malpighiales. They are characterized by opposite, simple, entire leaves (usually leathery and exstipulate), the presence of canals producing colored latex or resin, actinomorphic flowers often with numerous stamens typically arranged in bundles, a superior ovary, and fruit that is most commonly a berry or drupe (like the Mangosteen, Garcinia mangostana). Modern classifications separate this family from the related Hypericaceae (St. John's Worts) and Calophyllaceae.

Overview

Clusiaceae, in its strict sense (sensu stricto) as recognized by the Angiosperm Phylogeny Group (APG IV), belongs to the large order Malpighiales within the Fabid clade of Rosids. This definition primarily includes genera like Clusia, Garcinia, Tovomita, and Chrysochlamys, separating them from the closely related families Calophyllaceae (Calophyllum, Mammea) and Hypericaceae (Hypericum), which were often included in older, broader concepts of Clusiaceae or Guttiferae. The family comprises about 14 genera and nearly 600 species of evergreen trees, shrubs, and lianas, distributed throughout the pantropical regions.

Members of Clusiaceae s.s. are readily identifiable by their vegetative features: typically opposite, simple, entire-margined, leathery leaves that lack stipules, and the presence of secretory canals producing clear, white, or often yellow latex or resin, visible when parts are broken. Flowers are actinomorphic, often unisexual (with plants being dioecious), and usually possess 4 or 5 sepals and petals. A key floral feature is the androecium, typically consisting of numerous stamens that are frequently grouped into distinct bundles (fascicles) opposite the petals. The ovary is superior, multi-locular, and develops into a fleshy fruit – usually a berry or drupe, though sometimes a capsule.

The family includes species of significant economic importance, most notably the Mangosteen (Garcinia mangostana) prized for its delicious fruit, and other Garcinia species used for fruits, timber, resins (like gamboge), or medicinal compounds (like hydroxycitric acid from G. gummi-gutta). The genus Clusia is ecologically interesting, containing many species that start as epiphytes or hemiepiphytes ('stranglers') and some exhibiting Crassulacean acid metabolism (CAM photosynthesis). Clusiaceae are tropical plants and not native to Oklahoma.

  • Scientific Name: Clusiaceae Lindl. (sensu stricto)

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Evergreen trees, shrubs, or lianas. Some species of Clusia are epiphytic or hemiepiphytic, starting life on other trees. Plants contain secretory canals or cavities producing clear, white, or (commonly) yellow latex or resin.

Leaves

Leaves are almost always arranged oppositely (rarely whorled), are simple, and usually have entire margins. They are typically petiolate, often thick and leathery (coriaceous). Venation is pinnate. Stipules are generally absent (exstipulate). Glandular dots or lines may be present.

Inflorescence

Inflorescences are variable, often terminal or axillary, typically cymose (determinate), sometimes appearing as fascicles (clusters), or flowers may be solitary.

Flowers

Flowers are usually actinomorphic (radially symmetrical), often showy, and frequently unisexual (plants then typically dioecious, but sometimes monoecious or polygamous), though bisexual flowers also occur. Flowers are typically 4- or 5-merous (sometimes more).

  • Calyx: Consists of typically 4 or 5 sepals (ranging from 2 to 10+), often arranged in decussate (opposite pairs) whorls. Sepals are distinct or slightly fused at the base, often thick, unequal, and persistent.
  • Corolla: Consists of typically 4 or 5 petals (rarely more or absent), which are free, often large and fleshy, typically white, yellow, or pinkish/reddish.
  • Androecium: Stamens are usually numerous (indefinite, >12 to hundreds). A highly characteristic feature is that they are often grouped into 4 or 5 distinct bundles (fascicles) located opposite the petals. Sometimes the stamens form a continuous ring, or are fewer in number. Filaments within bundles may be free or fused. Sterile stamens (staminodes) are frequently present, sometimes alternating with the fascicles or forming petal-like structures.
  • Gynoecium: The ovary is superior, composed of (2-)3-5(-many) fused carpels, forming a syncarpous ovary with usually the same number of locules. Placentation is typically axile, but sometimes basal or parietal. Each locule contains 1 to numerous ovules. Styles may be distinct, fused into a single column, or completely absent (stigmas sessile). Stigmas are often broad, thick, peltate (shield-shaped), or radiately lobed. A nectar disk is often absent.

Fruits and Seeds

The fruit is typically a fleshy berry (e.g., Garcinia) or a somewhat leathery drupe. Less commonly, it can be a septicidal capsule (e.g., Clusia). Fruits contain one to numerous seeds. Seeds are often relatively large and may possess a fleshy aril or a fleshy seed coat. Endosperm is usually absent or scanty.

Chemical Characteristics

The family is rich in secondary metabolites, particularly resins, polyisoprenoids (latex components), tannins, and various phenolic compounds including xanthones (often yellow pigments) and benzophenones. These compounds contribute to the colors, medicinal properties, and sometimes toxicity of various species.

Field Identification (Clusiaceae sensu stricto)

Identifying members of Clusiaceae s.s. in the tropics relies on the combination of opposite leathery leaves, colored latex, characteristic flowers with numerous stamens (often bundled), and usually fleshy fruits.

Primary Identification Features

  • Habit: Tree, shrub, or liana (often evergreen).
  • Opposite, Simple, Entire Leaves: Typically thick or leathery, usually lacking stipules.
  • Colored Latex/Resin: Exudes white, yellow, or clear resinous latex when cut or broken (a key feature).
  • Numerous Stamens, Often in Bundles: Flowers possess many stamens, frequently grouped into 4 or 5 distinct fascicles.
  • Superior Ovary: Ovary positioned above other floral parts.
  • Fruit Usually Berry or Drupe: Fleshy fruits are common (Garcinia), though capsules occur (Clusia).
  • Pantropical Distribution: Found only in tropical regions worldwide.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Flowers actinomorphic, often unisexual (dioecious common).
  • 4 or 5 sepals and petals common.
  • Stigmas often broad, peltate, or radiate.
  • Seeds sometimes arillate.

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Year-Round: Opposite, simple, entire, often leathery leaves and the presence of latex are constant vegetative clues.
  • Flowering/Fruiting: Flowers can be showy and seasonal; look for the numerous stamens in bundles. Mature fleshy fruits (berries/drupes) or capsules are key seasonal features.

Common Confusion Points

  • Calophyllaceae (Calophyllum, Mammea, etc.): Very closely related (formerly included). Also tropical trees/shrubs with opposite leaves and latex (often clear/white). Differs subtly: often have numerous fine parallel lateral leaf veins, stamens numerous but often free or indistinctly bundled, ovary typically with only 1 ovule per locule, fruit always a drupe.
  • Hypericaceae (Hypericum): Now separate family. Mostly herbs or small shrubs (few trees), leaves opposite but often with translucent pellucid dots, latex absent (may have dark glands), stamens numerous but typically only basally connate or in less defined groups, styles usually distinct, fruit usually a capsule. Temperate and tropical distribution.
  • Rhizophoraceae (Mangrove Family): Some have opposite, simple, leathery leaves and sometimes prop roots. Flowers differ (often 4-merous, fewer stamens, inferior/semi-inferior ovary), fruit often viviparous, latex absent. (Order Malpighiales).
  • Myrtaceae (Myrtle Family): Often opposite simple entire leaves, but usually possess pellucid dots (aromatic when crushed), flowers with numerous stamens (not usually bundled), ovary inferior, fruit a berry or capsule. (Order Myrtales).
  • Other tropical families with opposite leaves (Rubiaceae, Apocynaceae, etc.) differ significantly in stipule presence (Rubiaceae), latex type (often milky white in Apocynaceae), flower structure (fused corolla common in Apocynaceae/Rubiaceae, inferior ovary in Rubiaceae), stamen number/arrangement, and fruit types.

Notable Examples (Clusiaceae sensu stricto)

This family includes the commercially important mangosteen and many other tropical species known for fruits, resins, or ecological adaptations.

Garcinia species (e.g., G. mangostana)

Mangosteen, Gamboge, etc.

A very large pantropical genus (~260 species) of trees and shrubs, often dioecious, typically with yellow latex. Flowers usually 4- or 5-merous. Fruit is a berry with persistent sepals. Includes the highly prized G. mangostana (Mangosteen) fruit, G. gummi-gutta (source of hydroxycitric acid - HCA), and species yielding gamboge resin.

Clusia species (e.g., C. rosea)

Autograph Tree, Copey

A large Neotropical genus (~300-400 species) of shrubs, trees, and lianas, many starting as epiphytes or hemiepiphytes ('stranglers'). Characterized by very thick, leathery opposite leaves (can be easily written upon, hence "Autograph Tree"). Flowers often large, white or pink, typically unisexual (dioecious). Fruit is usually a fleshy capsule that splits open septicidally, revealing seeds often covered by a bright aril. Many exhibit CAM photosynthesis.

Colored Latex/Resin

Gums and Resins

A characteristic feature of Clusiaceae sensu stricto (and related families like Calophyllaceae) is the presence of canals producing latex or resin, which is often yellow (as in many Garcinia) but can also be white or clear. This exudate is visible when stems or leaves are cut.

Chrysochlamys & Tovomita spp.

Neotropical Clusiaceae

These are representative of other genera within Clusiaceae sensu stricto, primarily found in the Neotropics. They are trees or shrubs sharing the family characteristics of opposite leaves, latex, often unisexual flowers with numerous stamens (sometimes bundled), superior ovaries, and fleshy fruits (berries or drupes).

Phylogeny and Classification

Clusiaceae sensu stricto belongs to the large order Malpighiales, within the Fabid clade of Rosids. It is part of a well-supported subgroup within Malpighiales often termed the "clusioid clade". This clade represents a major radiation of tropical woody plants characterized by features like opposite leaves, often numerous stamens, and specific chemical compounds.

Modern molecular phylogenetics has led to a refinement of family boundaries within the clusioids. Clusiaceae s.s. (typified by Clusia and Garcinia) is now usually separated from Hypericaceae (Hypericum and relatives) and Calophyllaceae (Calophyllum, Mammea, Mesua, etc.), although Calophyllaceae remains its closest relative and is sometimes still included by some broader definitions. Other families within the clusioid clade include Bonnetiaceae and the highly specialized aquatic family Podostemaceae.

  • Clade: Angiosperms (Flowering plants)
  • Clade: Eudicots

Related Families

Within the Malpighiales order, Clusiaceae s.s. is most closely related to other families in the "clusioid clade":

  • Calophyllaceae: Tropical trees/shrubs, very similar to Clusiaceae s.s. but differ in subtle details (leaf venation, stamen bundling, ovule number). Includes Calophyllum, Mammea.
  • Hypericaceae (St. John's Wort family): Mostly herbs/subshrubs, often with pellucid leaf dots, distinct styles, usually capsular fruit.
  • Podostemaceae: Highly specialized aquatic herbs resembling algae or mosses, growing on rocks in fast water. Flowers extremely reduced. Molecular data place them surprisingly within the clusioids.
  • Bonnetiaceae: Small family of Neotropical shrubs/trees.

Other diverse families in the broader Malpighiales order include Euphorbiaceae, Passifloraceae, Salicaceae, Violaceae, Rhizophoraceae, Erythroxylaceae, Linaceae, Caryocaraceae, Chrysobalanaceae, etc.

Evolutionary Significance

Clusiaceae sensu stricto highlights several evolutionary patterns:

  • Tropical Woody Radiation: Represents a significant diversification of trees, shrubs, and lianas primarily in pantropical rainforests.
  • Chemical Diversity: Evolution of complex secondary metabolites like xanthones and resins, likely involved in defense and ecological interactions.
  • Floral Evolution: Shows trends towards unisexuality (dioecy) and modification of the androecium (numerous stamens, bundling).
  • Habit Specialization: Development of epiphytic and hemiepiphytic lifestyles in Clusia, including physiological adaptations like CAM photosynthesis.
  • Taxonomic Refinement: The ongoing clarification of family boundaries within the clusioid clade based on molecular data demonstrates the process of systematic botany.
