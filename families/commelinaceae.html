Commelinaceae

Spiderwort Family / Dayflower Family

Commelinaceae, the spiderwort or dayflower family, is a medium-sized family of monocots (order Commelinales) with about 40 genera and over 700 species. These plants are typically somewhat succulent herbs, easily recognized by their simple leaves with closed basal sheaths encircling the stem. Their flowers, often blue or purple and frequently lasting only a single day (ephemeral), usually have 3 distinct sepals and 3 delicate petals. Stamens are notable for often being unequal or bearing conspicuous hairs. The fruit is typically a capsule.

Overview

The Commelinaceae family is distributed worldwide, primarily in tropical and subtropical regions, but with significant representation in temperate zones. Common genera include Tradescantia (spiderworts), known for their radially symmetric flowers with often hairy stamens, and Commelina (dayflowers), characterized by zygomorphic flowers with two large upper petals and one reduced lower petal, often emerging from a folded spathe-like bract. Other notable genera are Murdannia, Callisia, and Cyanotis.

Many species are popular horticultural subjects, especially various Tradescantia species and cultivars grown as houseplants (like the popular 'Wandering Jew' or Inch Plant, T. zebrina) or as garden perennials (various spiderworts). Others, like some Commelina and Murdannia species, can be weedy in disturbed areas or agricultural fields. The flowers are typically ephemeral, opening in the morning and wilting by midday, sometimes deliquescing into a liquid blob. The often brightly colored hairs on the stamen filaments are thought to play a role in attracting pollinators, possibly by mimicking pollen or nectar.

Several species are native to Oklahoma (current location as of March 2025), including Ohio Spiderwort (Tradescantia ohiensis) found in prairies and open areas, and Erect Dayflower (Commelina erecta) common in sandy soils and disturbed sites.

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Mostly perennial or annual herbs, often somewhat succulent with fleshy or watery stems and leaves. Stems are typically terrestrial, sometimes creeping and rooting at the nodes, often appearing jointed due to the prominent leaf sheaths.

Leaves

Leaves are simple, usually with entire (smooth) margins, and arranged alternately (spirally or in two ranks) along the stem. Venation is parallel. A key diagnostic feature is the leaf base forming a distinct closed sheath that completely encircles the stem (unlike grasses where the sheath is usually open).

Inflorescence

Inflorescences are typically terminal or axillary clusters of flowers arranged in cymes. These cymes are often helicoid (coiled) and frequently emerge from, or are enclosed within, one or more folded, boat-shaped or leaf-like bracts called spathes (especially prominent in Commelina).

Flowers

Flowers are usually bisexual, actinomorphic (radially symmetric, e.g., Tradescantia) or distinctly zygomorphic (bilaterally symmetric, e.g., Commelina). They are characteristically ephemeral, often lasting only a few hours in the morning, and sometimes deliquescing (dissolving) into a jelly-like substance after blooming. Flowers are typically 3-merous.

  • Perianth: Clearly differentiated into calyx and corolla (unlike many other Liliales).
    • Calyx: Composed of 3 distinct (free) sepals, typically green and herbaceous, sometimes boat-shaped.
    • Corolla: Composed of 3 distinct (free) petals, which are usually delicate, thin-textured (membranous), and often narrowed into a claw at the base. Petal color is commonly blue or purple, but also pink, white, or rarely yellow. In zygomorphic flowers like Commelina, the petals are unequal in size, with two large upper/lateral petals and one much smaller, often inconspicuous lower petal.
  • Androecium: Stamens typically number 6, arranged in two whorls of 3, but they are often modified or unequal. It is common for some stamens to be sterile (staminodes), shorter, or otherwise different from the fertile ones. Filaments are frequently adorned with conspicuous, often brightly colored, moniliform (bead-like) hairs. Anthers dehisce via longitudinal slits or sometimes pores.
  • Gynoecium: Ovary is superior, composed of 3 fused carpels forming 3 (or sometimes 2) locules (chambers). Placentation is axile, with few ovules per locule. A single simple style terminates in a single small stigma, which may be capitate (head-like) or slightly 3-lobed.

Fruits and Seeds

The fruit is typically a thin-walled capsule that splits open along the partitions between the locules (loculicidal dehiscence) to release the seeds. Seeds are few per capsule, often brownish or black, and frequently have distinctive textured or patterned seed coats. A small, cap-like structure (operculum) often covers the embryo. The endosperm is abundant and mealy (starchy).

Chemical Characteristics

Plants often contain mucilage cells contributing to their somewhat succulent feel. Raphide crystals (needle-shaped bundles of calcium oxalate) are common in tissues. Blue and purple flower colors are due to anthocyanin pigments. The family generally lacks the potent alkaloids or glycosides found in some other monocot families.

Field Identification

Identifying Commelinaceae relies on recognizing the combination of herbaceous monocot features, especially the closed leaf sheaths, distinct 3+3 perianth, and often modified stamens.

Primary Identification Features

  • Herbaceous Habit: Plants are non-woody, often somewhat fleshy.
  • Closed Leaf Sheaths: Leaf bases wrap completely around the stem, forming a closed tube.
  • Alternate, Simple Leaves: Leaves arranged alternately, simple shape, parallel veins.
  • 3 Sepals + 3 Petals: Perianth clearly differentiated into 3 (usually green) sepals and 3 (usually colored, delicate) petals.
  • Often Modified Stamens: Typically 6 stamens (or staminodes), filaments frequently bear conspicuous hairs.
  • Superior Ovary: Ovary positioned above other floral parts.
  • Capsule Fruit: Dry fruit splitting open into locules.
  • Ephemeral Flowers: Flowers often last only part of a day.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Flower Symmetry: Radially symmetric (Tradescantia) vs. bilaterally symmetric (Commelina).
  • Unequal Petals: Two large + one small petal in Commelina.
  • Spathe Bracts: Inflorescence often emerging from folded, boat-shaped bracts (Commelina).
  • Jointed Stems: Stems often appear segmented at the nodes due to prominent sheaths.
  • Flower Color: Commonly blue, purple, or pink.

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Spring/Summer/Fall: Main growing and flowering season. Ephemeral flowers best observed in the morning.
  • Year-round (Indoors/Frost-free): Houseplant species (Tradescantia zebrina, Callisia) visible year-round.
  • (Current Time Context: Late March in Oklahoma - Too early for most native Commelinaceae like Tradescantia or Commelina to be flowering, but vegetative growth might start with warmer weather).

Common Confusion Points

  • Liliaceae / Colchicaceae / Melanthiaceae: Also monocots with 6 perianth parts, but these are usually undifferentiated tepals (not distinct sepals/petals). Leaf sheaths typically open or less prominent. Different stamen/style structures (e.g., usually single style in Liliaceae, 3 distinct styles in Colchicaceae).
  • Iridaceae (Iris Family): Have 6 tepals, but only 3 stamens and an inferior ovary.
  • Orchidaceae (Orchid Family): Highly specialized zygomorphic flowers, inferior ovary, pollen aggregated into pollinia.
  • Poaceae (Grasses) / Cyperaceae (Sedges) / Juncaceae (Rushes): Flowers highly reduced, wind-pollinated, lack showy petals. Grasses have open leaf sheaths; sedges often have triangular stems and closed sheaths; rushes have round stems and reduced flowers.

The combination of closed leaf sheath, 3 distinct sepals + 3 distinct petals, and often hairy/modified stamens is key for Commelinaceae.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Herbaceous (often succulent)
  • Alternate leaves with CLOSED sheaths
  • Flowers with 3 sepals + 3 petals
  • Petals often delicate, blue/purple
  • Stamens often 6, filaments often hairy
  • Superior ovary
  • Fruit a capsule
  • Flowers often ephemeral

Key Variations:

  • Flower symmetry (radial vs. bilateral)
  • Petal size (equal vs. unequal)
  • Presence of spathe bracts
  • Stamen modification (fertile vs. sterile, hair color)
  • Annual vs. perennial

Notable Examples

The Spiderwort family includes familiar houseplants, garden flowers, and common weeds.

Tradescantia ohiensis

Ohio Spiderwort / Bluejacket

A perennial herb native to central and eastern North America, including prairies and open woods in Oklahoma. Features smooth, glaucous (waxy blue-green) stems and leaves. Produces clusters of flowers with 3 equal blue to pink petals and 6 stamens with conspicuously hairy filaments. Flowers open in the morning and wilt by afternoon.

Commelina erecta

Erect Dayflower / Whitemouth Dayflower

A variable perennial herb native throughout much of the Americas, including Oklahoma, often found in sandy soils. Stems can be erect or sprawling. Flowers emerge one at a time from a folded, green spathe bract. Flowers are zygomorphic with 2 large, rounded blue upper petals and one tiny, inconspicuous lower petal. Stamens are modified.

Tradescantia zebrina

Inch Plant / Wandering Jew

A trailing perennial herb native to Mexico and Central America, extremely popular as a houseplant and sometimes used as groundcover in warm climates (can be weedy). Known for its attractive leaves, typically purple underneath and striped with silver/green/purple above. Produces small pinkish-purple flowers with 3 equal petals.

Murdannia nudiflora

Nakedstem Dewflower / Doveweed

An annual herb native to Asia, now an invasive weed in moist, disturbed areas, turf, and agriculture in warmer parts of the world, including the southeastern US. Forms low mats, rooting at nodes. Has small leaves and produces small purplish flowers with 3 equal petals. Stamens include both fertile and sterile types.

Callisia repens

Creeping Inch Plant / Turtle Vine

A low-growing, mat-forming perennial herb native to tropical America, often grown as a houseplant or groundcover. Has small, ovate leaves, often purplish beneath, densely covering creeping stems. Produces very small white flowers with 3 petals in axillary clusters.

Cyanotis somaliensis

Pussy Ears / Kitten Ears

A trailing perennial herb native to Somalia, grown as a succulent houseplant. Notable for its fleshy, lance-shaped leaves densely covered in soft white hairs, giving it a fuzzy appearance. Produces small purplish flowers with 3 petals and hairy stamens typical of the family.

Phylogeny and Classification

Commelinaceae is the largest family in the order Commelinales, which is part of the Commelinid clade – a major group within the monocots that also includes grasses (Poales), gingers (Zingiberales), and palms (Arecales). Commelinids are characterized by several features, including UV-fluorescent compounds in their cell walls (ferulic acid).

Within Commelinales, Commelinaceae is well-supported as monophyletic and is sister to the rest of the order, which includes smaller families like Pontederiaceae (Pickerel Weed family), Haemodoraceae (Bloodwort family), Philydraceae, and Hanguanaceae. Key evolutionary traits within Commelinaceae include the development of the closed leaf sheath, differentiation of the perianth into calyx and corolla, the striking diversity of stamen modifications (thought to be related to pollination strategies, potentially involving pollen deception or specialized rewards), and the evolution of ephemeral flowers.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Clade: Angiosperms (Flowering plants)
  • Clade: Monocots
  • Clade: Commelinids
  • Order: Commelinales
  • Family: Commelinaceae

Related Families

Within the order Commelinales, Commelinaceae is related to:

  • Pontederiaceae (Pickerel Weed / Water Hyacinth family): Aquatic herbs, often with showy blue/purple zygomorphic flowers, superior ovary.
  • Haemodoraceae (Bloodwort family): Herbs often with reddish rhizomes, 6 tepals, often an inferior ovary (Haemodorum) or superior (Anigozanthos - Kangaroo Paws).
  • Philydraceae: Herbs of Australia/Asia with unique flower structure.
  • Hanguanaceae: Robust herbs of SE Asia/Australia.

The Commelinales order is part of the Commelinid clade, related to other orders like Poales (Grasses, Sedges, Rushes), Zingiberales (Gingers, Bananas), and Arecales (Palms).

Evolutionary Significance

Commelinaceae represents a significant radiation of primarily herbaceous monocots, particularly diverse in tropical regions. The family showcases key monocot traits (parallel venation, 3-merous flowers) but is distinguished within its order by the closed leaf sheath and differentiated perianth. The evolution of diverse and often complex stamen morphology within the family is a subject of interest regarding pollination biology, potentially involving pollen or nectar imitation by sterile or hairy structures. The ephemeral nature of the flowers is another striking adaptation, possibly reducing resource expenditure or coordinating with specific pollinator activity periods.
