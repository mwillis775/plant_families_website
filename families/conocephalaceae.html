Conocephalaceae

Snakeskin Liverwort / Great Scented Liverwort

Conocephalaceae is a distinctive family (often considered monogeneric) of large complex thalloid liverworts in the order Marchantiales. It is readily identified by its large thallus marked with a conspicuous hexagonal pattern (like snakeskin), a strong aromatic scent when crushed, simple air pores, and characteristic cone-shaped female reproductive structures (archegoniophores).

Overview

The Conocephalaceae family primarily consists of the genus Conocephalum, with one widespread and well-known species, Conocephalum conicum, and sometimes a second species (C. salebrosum) recognized. These are among the largest thalloid liverworts, often forming extensive, robust, bright green mats in moist, shaded habitats such as stream banks, wet rocks, seeps, and forest floors.

The dorsal surface of the thallus has a striking pattern of hexagonal areas, each representing an underlying air chamber opening via a central simple pore. When crushed, the thallus releases a strong, pungent, somewhat mushroom-like aroma. Plants are strictly dioicous. Female plants produce distinctive cone-shaped archegoniophores on long stalks, while male plants develop clearly defined, raised, sessile pads containing the antheridia.

Conocephalum is common and easily recognizable in temperate regions of the Northern Hemisphere and also occurs elsewhere. Its large size, characteristic surface pattern, strong scent, and unique reproductive structures make it a well-known example of a complex thalloid liverwort.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Conocephalaceae
  • Common Name: Snakeskin Liverwort, Great Scented Liverwort
  • Number of Genera: 1 (Conocephalum)
  • Number of Species: 1-2 (primarily C. conicum)
  • Distribution: Widespread in temperate Northern Hemisphere; also scattered Southern Hemisphere locations. Found in moist, shaded habitats (stream banks, wet rocks, seeps).
  • Evolutionary Group: Bryophytes - Liverworts (Marchantiophyta) - Class Marchantiopsida - Order Marchantiales

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Plants are large, robust, thalloid gametophytes, often 1-2 cm wide and extending over 10 cm long, growing prostrate and branching dichotomously to form extensive mats. The thallus is somewhat fleshy and typically bright green to dark green.

Thallus Structure

The thallus exhibits complex internal differentiation. The dorsal surface is marked by a conspicuous hexagonal pattern outlining the large underlying air chambers. Each hexagonal area has a central, visible simple air pore (not barrel-shaped). Air chambers contain short photosynthetic filaments. The ventral surface bears numerous rhizoids (smooth and pegged) and typically small, inconspicuous ventral scales in two rows, often hidden by the dense rhizoids. The thallus releases a strong, distinctive aromatic scent when crushed.

Reproductive Structures

Plants are strictly dioicous. Antheridia (male organs) are embedded within distinct, raised, sessile, circular or oval dorsal pads (antheridial receptacles). Archegonia (female organs) are borne on specialized archegoniophores, which consist of a long, thick stalk and a characteristic cone-shaped or conical head (capitulum). Archegonia and subsequent sporophytes develop on the underside of this conical head, protected by involucres. Asexual reproduction via specialized gemmae is absent, though thallus fragmentation can occur.

Sporophyte and Spores

Multiple sporophytes develop on the underside of each mature archegoniophore. The sporophyte consists of a foot, a relatively long seta (which elongates rapidly at maturity, pushing the capsule out from the involucre), and a dark, ovoid capsule. The capsule wall is unistratose (one cell layer thick) and dehisces by splitting into 4-8 irregular valves or segments from the apex. Numerous long, yellowish-brown elaters with 2-3 spiral thickenings are mixed with the spores. Spores are numerous, small, greenish, and released singly.

Chemical Characteristics

Conocephalaceae is well-known for producing significant quantities of volatile aromatic compounds, primarily terpenoids and other secondary metabolites, responsible for its strong characteristic scent. These compounds may play roles in defense against herbivores or pathogens.

Field Identification

Conocephalum is one of the most easily identifiable complex thalloid liverworts due to its unique combination of features:

Primary Identification Features

  • Large thallus size: Significantly larger than many other thalloid liverworts.
  • Distinct hexagonal surface pattern: Looks like snakeskin or honeycomb.
  • Strong aromatic scent: Easily detected when the thallus is crushed or even just handled.
  • Cone-shaped archegoniophores: Female receptacles on long stalks are uniquely conical.
  • Raised antheridial pads: Male receptacles are distinct sessile discs/ovals on the dorsal surface.
  • Simple air pores: Visible as a small dot in the center of each hexagon (hand lens needed).

Secondary Identification Features

  • Habitat: Typically found in very moist, shaded places like stream banks, waterfalls, wet rocks, and damp soil in forests.
  • Dioicous: Male and female structures are on separate plants.
  • Inconspicuous ventral scales: Unlike the prominent scales of Aytoniaceae or Cleveaceae.

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Gametophyte: Perennial and usually visible year-round in suitable habitats.
  • Reproductive Structures: Antheridial pads and archegoniophores are seasonal, typically maturing in spring. The cone-shaped archegoniophores are very distinctive when present.

Common Confusion Points

  • Marchantiaceae (e.g., Marchantia): Distinguished by barrel-shaped pores, often having gemma cups, and different receptacle shapes (umbrella-like archegoniophores, flat-topped antheridiophores). Usually less aromatic.
  • Aytoniaceae / Cleveaceae: Have different archegoniophore shapes (lobed/star-shaped/hemispherical or sessile/subsessile), usually smaller thalli, often prominent ventral scales, and generally different or weaker scents.
  • Pellia / other simple thalloids: Lack the hexagonal surface pattern, air pores/chambers, strong scent, and specialized receptacles.
  • Hornworts (Anthocerotophyta): Lack the hexagonal pattern and complex internal structure; have distinctive horn-shaped sporophytes emerging directly from the thallus.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Large thallus
  • Hexagonal surface pattern
  • Strong aromatic scent
  • Simple pores (center of hexagons)
  • Cone-shaped female receptacle (stalked)
  • Raised dorsal male pad (sessile)

Key Distinctions:

  • Hexagonal pattern + scent + cone-receptacle is unique.
  • Simple pores distinguish from Marchantia (barrel pores).
  • Stalked cone distinguishes from Cleveaceae/Ricciaceae.
  • Pores/chambers distinguish from simple thalloids.

Notable Examples

The family is dominated by one widespread species:

Conocephalum conicum

Snakeskin Liverwort, Great Scented Liverwort

The most common and widespread species, exhibiting all the characteristic features of the family: large size, distinct hexagonal surface pattern, simple pores, strong aroma, dioicous reproduction with cone-shaped archegoniophores and dorsal antheridial pads. Found in moist, shaded habitats across the temperate Northern Hemisphere and elsewhere.

Phylogeny and Classification

Conocephalaceae represents a distinct lineage within the order Marchantiales (class Marchantiopsida). Although sharing the complex thalloid structure with families like Aytoniaceae and Marchantiaceae, its unique combination of features – particularly the simple pores coupled with highly specialized, cone-shaped archegoniophores and a strong chemical profile – sets it apart.

Molecular studies place Conocephalaceae within the core Marchantiales, related to other families possessing stalked reproductive structures. Its exact sister relationship might vary between studies but it consistently forms a well-supported, distinct clade. The evolution of its characteristic morphology, especially the conical archegoniophore, is a key feature of this lineage.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Clade: Embryophytes (Land plants)
  • Division: Marchantiophyta (Liverworts)
  • Class: Marchantiopsida
  • Order: Marchantiales
  • Family: Conocephalaceae

Related Families

Conocephalaceae is part of the order Marchantiales, which includes other families such as:

  • Aytoniaceae: Also has simple pores but differs in archegoniophore shape (lobed/star-shaped), generally smaller size, and often prominent ventral scales.
  • Marchantiaceae: Distinguished by barrel-shaped pores, often gemma cups, and different receptacle shapes (e.g., umbrella-like).
  • Cleveaceae: Has simple pores but lacks distinctly stalked archegoniophores (female structures are dorsal, sessile/subsessile).
  • Ricciaceae: Lacks stalked receptacles (sporophytes embedded); pore structure varies.

Evolutionary Significance

Conocephalaceae is evolutionarily significant due to:

  • Distinct Morphology: The combination of large size, hexagonal pattern, simple pores, and conical archegoniophore represents a unique evolutionary pathway within Marchantiales.
  • Chemical Profile: Its strong aromatic compounds likely play important ecological roles and are of biochemical interest.
  • Ecological Success: Represents a successful adaptation to moist, shaded, often streamside habitats in temperate regions.
  • Phylogenetic Marker: Its clear characteristics make it a useful reference point when studying the diversification of complex thalloid liverworts.
