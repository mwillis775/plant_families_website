Corrigiolaceae

Strapwort Family

Corrigiolaceae is a small family of flowering plants in the order Caryophyllales, comprising two genera (Corrigiola and Telephium) with approximately 11 species. These plants are characterized by their herbaceous growth habit, typically with prostrate or ascending stems. Members of this family have simple, alternate leaves with small stipules, and small, inconspicuous flowers with five sepals, five petals, and five stamens. The family has a primarily Mediterranean and European distribution, with some species extending into North Africa and western Asia, typically growing in sandy, gravelly, or disturbed habitats.

Overview

The Corrigiolaceae family comprises a small group of herbaceous flowering plants that were previously included in the Caryophyllaceae (pink family) or Illecebraceae families. The family name is derived from the genus Corrigiola, which comes from the Latin word "corrigia" meaning "strap" or "thong," referring to the strap-like stems of some species.

Corrigiolaceae has a primarily Mediterranean and European distribution, with species occurring in southern and western Europe, North Africa, and extending into western Asia. Some species have been introduced to other parts of the world. Members of this family typically grow in sandy, gravelly, or disturbed habitats, often in coastal areas, riverbanks, or seasonally wet places.

The family is of particular interest to botanists due to its position within the Caryophyllales order and its relationship to other families in this group. Recent molecular studies have supported its recognition as a distinct family closely related to Caryophyllaceae.

  • Evolutionary Group: Angiosperms - Eudicots - Caryophyllales

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Members of Corrigiolaceae are annual or perennial herbs with prostrate, ascending, or rarely erect stems. They typically have a spreading growth habit, forming mats or low cushions. The stems are often slender and can be somewhat woody at the base in perennial species.

Leaves

The leaves of Corrigiolaceae are simple, entire, and alternate (occasionally appearing opposite or whorled near the inflorescences). They are typically small, linear to oblong or spatulate in shape, and often somewhat fleshy or succulent. A distinctive feature of the family is the presence of small, scarious stipules at the base of the leaves, which are usually silvery or translucent.

Reproductive Structures

The flowers are small and inconspicuous, arranged in cymose inflorescences that are often clustered at the ends of branches or in leaf axils. Each flower has 5 sepals that are often keeled or hooded, 5 small petals (which may be reduced or absent in some species), 5 stamens, and a superior ovary with 3 styles or a 3-lobed style. The fruit is a small, one-seeded, indehiscent nutlet enclosed by the persistent sepals. The seed has a curved embryo surrounding the perisperm (a nutritive tissue).

Distinguishing Features

Corrigiolaceae can be distinguished from other plant families by a combination of:

  • Herbaceous habit with prostrate or ascending stems
  • Alternate leaves with scarious stipules
  • Small flowers with 5 sepals, 5 petals, and 5 stamens
  • One-seeded, indehiscent fruits
  • Curved embryo surrounding perisperm in the seed

Identification

Field Identification Tips

When trying to identify members of the Corrigiolaceae family in the field, look for:

  • Prostrate or ascending growth habit with slender stems
  • Alternate, simple leaves with small, scarious stipules
  • Small, inconspicuous flowers with 5 sepals and 5 petals
  • Clustered inflorescences at branch ends or in leaf axils
  • Preference for sandy, gravelly, or disturbed habitats, often in seasonally wet places

Similar Families

Corrigiolaceae may be confused with several other plant families:

  • Caryophyllaceae: Closely related family but typically with opposite leaves and lacking stipules
  • Illecebraceae: Previously included some Corrigiolaceae genera, but now recognized as part of Caryophyllaceae
  • Molluginaceae: Similar habit but with different floral structure and fruit type
  • Portulacaceae: Similar habit in some genera but with succulent leaves and different floral structure

Distinguishing Features

The most reliable features for distinguishing Corrigiolaceae from similar families include:

  • Leaf arrangement: Alternate leaves with scarious stipules (versus opposite leaves without stipules in Caryophyllaceae)
  • Floral structure: 5 sepals, 5 petals, 5 stamens, superior ovary with 3 styles
  • Fruit type: One-seeded, indehiscent nutlet
  • Seed structure: Curved embryo surrounding perisperm

For definitive identification, examination of the stipules and floral structure is often necessary, as these features are the most diagnostic for the family.

Notable Examples

The family Corrigiolaceae contains approximately 11 species across 2 genera. Here are some notable representatives:

Corrigiola litoralis

Strapwort

An annual herb native to western and southern Europe, North Africa, and western Asia. It has prostrate stems that radiate from a central taproot, forming mats on the ground. The leaves are small, linear-oblong, and bluish-green. The tiny flowers are white or pinkish, clustered at the ends of branches. This species typically grows in sandy or gravelly soils along riverbanks, lakeshores, and coastal areas. In some regions, it is considered rare or endangered due to habitat loss.

Telephium imperati

Orpine Strapwort

A perennial herb native to the Mediterranean region, particularly southern Europe and North Africa. It has ascending to erect stems that are somewhat woody at the base. The leaves are alternate, oval to oblong, and slightly fleshy. The flowers are small, white, and arranged in dense terminal clusters. This species typically grows in rocky, calcareous soils in dry, open habitats. It has been used in traditional medicine in some regions for its purported diuretic and wound-healing properties.

Corrigiola telephiifolia

Moroccan Strapwort

A perennial herb native to North Africa, particularly Morocco and Algeria. It has prostrate to ascending stems arising from a woody rootstock. The leaves are spatulate to oblong and somewhat fleshy. The flowers are small, white, and clustered at the ends of branches. This species typically grows in sandy and rocky soils in arid and semi-arid regions. It has cultural significance in North Africa, where the roots are used in traditional medicine and as a cosmetic ingredient in some regions.

Phylogenetic Note

Corrigiolaceae has undergone significant taxonomic revisions based on molecular phylogenetic studies. Previously included within Caryophyllaceae or recognized as part of Illecebraceae, it is now accepted as a distinct family within the Caryophyllales order. The distinctive morphological features of Corrigiolaceae, particularly its alternate leaves with stipules and one-seeded fruits, support its recognition as a separate family. Recent molecular studies place Corrigiolaceae as sister to Caryophyllaceae within the "core Caryophyllales" clade, suggesting a close evolutionary relationship between these families despite their morphological differences.
