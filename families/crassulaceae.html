Crassulaceae

Crassulaceae Family

Crassulaceae is a family of succulent plants, often found in arid regions. Known for their water-storing leaves and unique photosynthesis process (CAM), they include popular genera like Sedum and Echeveria.

Overview Characteristics Identification Examples Phylogeny

Overview

Crassulaceae is a major family within the order Saxifragales, belonging to the core eudicot lineage. It is a large family, containing about 35 genera and some 1400-1500 species, distributed nearly worldwide (cosmopolitan) but with significant centers of diversity in seasonally dry, arid, or rocky regions, particularly Southern Africa, Mexico, the Mediterranean, and Macaronesia. They are relatively uncommon in Australia and the Amazon basin.

The family is overwhelmingly characterized by its adaptation to dry environments through succulence – possessing fleshy leaves and/or stems capable of storing large amounts of water. Most members are perennial succulent herbs or subshrubs, often forming rosettes. Physiologically, many species utilize Crassulacean Acid Metabolism (CAM), a specialized photosynthetic pathway where stomata open primarily at night to take in CO2 (storing it as malic acid), thus minimizing water loss during the day. Flowers are typically radially symmetrical, 4- or 5-merous, often with twice as many stamens as petals. A highly distinctive feature is the gynoecium, which is usually apocarpous, consisting of separate (or only slightly basally fused) carpels, typically equal in number to the petals, each usually subtended by a small nectar scale. The fruit is usually a cluster of dry follicles.

Crassulaceae holds immense horticultural importance, with numerous genera and species (Sedum, Sempervivum, Echeveria, Crassula, Kalanchoe, Aeonium, etc.) widely cultivated globally as houseplants, rock garden subjects, green roof plants, and collector's succulents. Several species of Sedum (Stonecrops) are native to Oklahoma, typically found in rocky glades, prairies, and open woods.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Crassulaceae J.St.-Hil.
  • Common Name: Stonecrop family (or Orpine family)
  • Number of Genera: Approximately 35
  • Number of Species: Approximately 1,400-1,500
  • Distribution: Cosmopolitan, especially arid/semi-arid regions (S Africa, Mexico, Medit.).
  • Evolutionary Group: Eudicots - Core Eudicots - Order Saxifragales

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Predominantly perennial succulent herbs or subshrubs, often with fleshy stems. Some annuals, few aquatics (Crassula helmsii), very few true shrubs exist. Many species form compact basal or terminal rosettes of leaves. Physiologically adapted to arid conditions, frequently utilizing Crassulacean Acid Metabolism (CAM) photosynthesis.

Leaves

Leaves are almost always fleshy and succulent, storing water. Arrangement is highly variable: opposite, whorled, alternate, or crowded into basal or terminal rosettes. Leaves are typically simple (rarely pinnate in Kalanchoe/Bryophyllum) and usually have entire margins (sometimes crenate or slightly toothed). Stipules are absent (exstipulate). Leaf shape is extremely diverse: flattened, terete (cylindrical), globose, scale-like, etc. Surfaces may be smooth, glaucous (waxy), hairy, or papillose. Some (Kalanchoe/Bryophyllum) produce vegetative plantlets along leaf margins.

Inflorescence

Inflorescences are usually terminal (sometimes axillary), typically determinate cymes. These cymes are often branched and arranged in flat-topped (corymbose) or pyramidal (paniculate) clusters. Less commonly, flowers are solitary or in spikes or racemes. Bracts are usually present.

Flowers

Flowers are usually bisexual (perfect), typically actinomorphic (radially symmetrical), often brightly colored (white, yellow, pink, red, orange), and typically 4- or 5-merous (ranging from 3 to over 30 parts per whorl in some Sempervivum).

  • Calyx: Consists of usually 4 or 5 sepals (same number as petals), which are free or fused only at the base, often fleshy, and persistent.
  • Corolla: Consists of usually 4 or 5 petals (same number as sepals), which are usually free (distinct) but sometimes fused at the base into a short tube (Kalanchoe, Cotyledon). Petals are often persistent after withering.
  • Androecium: Stamens typically number as many as the petals (haplostemonous) or, more characteristically, twice the number of petals (diplostemonous), arranged in two distinct whorls (5+5 or 4+4). Filaments are free, inserted on the receptacle or at the base of the corolla tube (if present). Anthers usually basifixed or dorsifixed, dehiscing via longitudinal slits.
  • Gynoecium: Highly distinctive and characteristic: usually apocarpous or nearly so. It consists of usually 4 or 5 (same number as petals) distinct (free) or only slightly fused at the very base carpels arranged in a whorl. Each carpel is superior and typically bears a small scale-like nectar gland (nectary) on its outer surface at the base. Each carpel contains few to numerous ovules, usually on marginal placentas, and tapers into a short style with a small terminal stigma.

Fruits and Seeds

The fruit is characteristically an aggregate (cluster) of dry, dehiscent units called follicles, with one follicle developing from each free carpel of the flower. Each follicle typically opens along its inner (adaxial) suture to release the seeds. Less commonly, the fruit may be a capsule (if carpels are more fused). Seeds are usually numerous and very small, often dust-like, sometimes winged or ribbed. Endosperm is typically sparse or absent; the embryo is straight.

Chemical Characteristics

Widespread use of CAM photosynthesis is a key physiological trait, involving nocturnal CO2 uptake and daytime fixation, linked to high concentrations of malic acid in vacuoles at night. Contain various organic acids, tannins, mucilage, and flavonoids. Some genera possess alkaloids or cyanogenic glycosides and can be toxic if ingested in quantity. High cellular water content defines succulence.

Field Identification

Crassulaceae are generally easy to recognize at the family level due to their succulent habit and distinctive floral structure, especially the separate carpels.

Primary Identification Features

  • Succulent Habit: Plants possess fleshy leaves and/or stems.
  • Leaf Form/Arrangement: Leaves usually simple, entire, often opposite, whorled, or in rosettes.
  • Actinomorphic Flowers: Radially symmetrical, usually 4- or 5-merous.
  • Stamens Often 2x Petals: Commonly 8 or 10 stamens in 5-petaled flowers.
  • Apocarpous/Nearly Apocarpous Gynoecium: Carpels (usually 4 or 5) are distinct/free or only fused at the base, each usually with a basal nectar scale.
  • Fruit an Aggregate of Follicles: Cluster of dry pods, each opening along one seam.
  • Habitat: Often found in dry, rocky, sunny locations or as cultivated succulents. Native Sedum in OK found in glades/rock outcrops.

Secondary Identification Features

  • CAM Photosynthesis (physiological trait).
  • Exstipulate leaves.
  • Small, numerous seeds.
  • Flowers often in terminal cymose inflorescences.

Seasonal Identification Tips (relevant to Oklahoma / cultivation)

  • Year-Round: Succulent leaves and habit (rosettes, fleshy stems) are visible year-round.
  • Spring/Summer: Main flowering season for many species, including native Oklahoma Sedum. Observe flower parts (petal/sepal/stamen counts, separate carpels).
  • Summer/Fall: Follicle fruits develop and dehisce. Vegetative propagation (offsets in Sempervivum, leaf plantlets in Kalanchoe) may be evident.
  • Cultivation: Many popular houseplants (Crassula, Kalanchoe, Echeveria) can flower at various times indoors or in greenhouses.

Common Confusion Points

  • Aizoaceae (Ice Plant Family): Also succulents, often opposite leaves. Distinguished by flowers typically having numerous petal-like staminodes (appearing to have many petals), often an inferior or half-inferior syncarpous ovary, and fruit usually a complex capsule opening by valves or hygroscopic keels. (Order Caryophyllales).
  • Cactaceae (Cactus Family): Stem succulents, usually leafless (or leaves modified to spines), possess areoles, flowers with numerous tepals and stamens, inferior ovary, fruit a berry. (Order Caryophyllales).
  • Portulacaceae (Purslane Family): Succulent herbs. Distinguished by usually only 2 sepals, ephemeral petals, superior syncarpous ovary, fruit a circumscissile capsule (lid pops off). (Order Caryophyllales).
  • Saxifragaceae (Saxifrage Family): Related (Saxifragales). Some form rosettes (Saxifraga), but leaves usually less fleshy. Flowers often have a hypanthium, gynoecium variable (can be apocarpous but usually fewer carpels or more fused), lack nectar scales at carpel bases typically.
  • The combination of succulence + 4/5-merous flowers + often 2x stamens + distinct/basally fused carpels + follicle aggregate fruit is diagnostic for Crassulaceae.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Succulent Herb or Subshrub
  • Fleshy Leaves (Opp/Whorl/Alt/Rosette)
  • Flowers Actinomorphic, 4-5 merous (usu.)
  • Stamens often 2x Petal number (e.g., 8 or 10)
  • Gynoecium Apocarpous (4-5 free/basally fused carpels)
  • Nectar scale at base of each carpel
  • Fruit an aggregate of Follicles
  • CAM Photosynthesis common

Key Variations:

  • Leaf arrangement and shape
  • Rosette vs. Caulescent habit
  • Flower part number (4 vs 5 vs more)
  • Stamen number (1x vs 2x petals)
  • Petal fusion (Free vs. Basally fused)
  • Flower color

Notable Examples

Crassulaceae is a favorite among horticulturalists and includes many well-known succulent genera, as well as native stonecrops.

Sedum species

Stonecrop, Orpine

A very large (~450 spp.), cosmopolitan genus of succulent herbs and subshrubs with diverse leaf shapes (flat, terete) and arrangements (alt, opp, whorled). Flowers typically 5-merous, often yellow or white (also pink/red), usually with 10 stamens and 5 separate carpels. Several species are native to Oklahoma rocky areas. Widely grown in gardens.

Sempervivum species

Houseleek, Hens-and-Chicks

Native to mountains of Europe and the Mediterranean. Form tight evergreen rosettes that reproduce vegetatively via offsets ("chicks"). Flowers (pink/red/yellow) borne on a terminal stalk, often with >5 parts (6-16 common); main rosette dies after flowering (monocarpic). Popular hardy succulents.

Crassula species (e.g., C. ovata)

Jade Plant, Crassula

A large genus (~200 spp.) centered in Southern Africa, extremely diverse in form (shrubs, herbs, aquatics). Leaves usually opposite. Flowers small, 5-merous, often white/pink. Includes the popular C. ovata (Jade Plant) houseplant and many other cultivated succulents with unique forms.

Echeveria species

Echeveria

Genus (~150 spp.) primarily from Mexico, forming attractive succulent rosettes often with colorful leaves (sometimes glaucous or hairy). Flowers typically 5-merous, often urn-shaped or bell-shaped, reddish/orange/yellow, borne on arching stalks (cincinni). Extremely popular in succulent cultivation, many hybrids.

Kalanchoe / Bryophyllum spp.

Kalanchoe

Large genus (~125 spp.) mostly from Old World tropics (esp. Madagascar, Africa). Succulent herbs or shrubs. Flowers often 4-merous, brightly colored, petals usually fused into a distinct tube. Many species formerly in Bryophyllum reproduce vegetatively via plantlets on leaf margins. Popular houseplants (e.g., Florist Kalanchoe).

Apocarpous Gynoecium

Flower Center Detail

A hallmark of Crassulaceae flowers is the gynoecium composed of multiple carpels (typically 4 or 5) that are either completely separate (apocarpous) or fused only slightly at the base. Each carpel has its own style/stigma and usually a small nectar scale at its base, developing into a separate follicle fruit.

Phylogeny and Classification

Crassulaceae is a major family within the diverse order Saxifragales, which sits near the base of the core eudicot lineage. Saxifragales includes a wide array of growth forms, from tiny herbs to large trees, including families like Saxifragaceae, Grossulariaceae (currants), Paeoniaceae (peonies), Hamamelidaceae (witch hazels), and Altingiaceae (sweetgums).

Within Saxifragales, Crassulaceae represents the primary lineage that diversified extensively into succulent forms adapted to arid environments. It belongs to the "core Saxifragales" clade. Its exact sister relationship is still debated among researchers, but potential close relatives based on molecular data include families like Saxifragaceae, Grossulariaceae, Iteaceae, and Paeoniaceae. Crassulaceae itself is generally divided into several subfamilies based on molecular and morphological data (e.g., Crassuloideae, Kalanchoideae, Sempervivoideae).

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Clade: Angiosperms (Flowering plants)
  • Clade: Eudicots
  • Clade: Core Eudicots
  • Order: Saxifragales
  • Family: Crassulaceae

Related Families

As a member of the order Saxifragales, Crassulaceae is related to other diverse families within this group:

  • Saxifragaceae (Saxifrage family): Mostly herbs, often with basal rosettes, variable flowers usually 5-merous, ovary superior to inferior, fruit usually a capsule or follicles.
  • Grossulariaceae (Gooseberry family): Shrubs (genus Ribes), often spiny, palmately lobed leaves, flowers 5-merous with hypanthium, inferior ovary, fruit a berry.
  • Paeoniaceae (Peony family): Herbs or shrubs, large compound leaves, large showy flowers with numerous stamens, apocarpous gynoecium (like Crassulaceae but fewer carpels), fruit follicles.
  • Iteaceae (Virginia Sweetspire family): Shrubs/small trees, simple alternate serrated leaves, small flowers in racemes, fruit a capsule.
  • Hamamelidaceae (Witch Hazel family): Trees or shrubs, simple alternate often asymmetric leaves, flowers often 4-merous with strap-like petals, fruit a woody capsule.
  • Altingiaceae (Sweetgum family): Trees (Liquidambar), palmately lobed alternate leaves, flowers unisexual in dense heads, fruit an aggregate capsule.
  • Cercidiphyllaceae (Katsura family): Deciduous trees, simple cordate leaves with shoot dimorphism, apetalous flowers, fruit follicles.

Evolutionary Significance

Crassulaceae is highly significant from several evolutionary perspectives:

  • Major Succulent Radiation: Represents one of the largest and most successful diversifications of succulent plants among angiosperms, primarily driven by adaptations to water-limited environments.
  • Evolution of CAM Photosynthesis: The family is a classic model system for studying the evolution, physiology, and ecological significance of Crassulacean Acid Metabolism (CAM), a key water-conserving photosynthetic pathway that has evolved convergently in several unrelated succulent lineages.
  • Retention of Apocarpy: The common presence of separate or nearly separate carpels is often considered an ancestral trait within eudicots, making Crassulaceae interesting for studies of gynoecium evolution.
  • Vegetative Propagation: Many species exhibit highly efficient vegetative reproduction (e.g., via offsets, fragmentation, or leaf-borne plantlets), contributing to their success in harsh or disturbed habitats and horticulture.
  • Horticultural Importance: Represents a major source of ornamental succulent plants cultivated worldwide.
