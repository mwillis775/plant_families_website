Crossosomataceae

Rockflower Family

Crossosomataceae, the Rockflower family, is a small family of deciduous or evergreen xerophytic shrubs native to arid regions of the southwestern United States and adjacent Mexico. Its phylogenetic position is somewhat uncertain, often placed in its own order Crossosomatales near the base of the Rosids. Members typically have small, simple, often alternate leaves, relatively showy solitary white or pink flowers with 4-5 petals and numerous stamens arising from a distinct hypanthium, a superior gynoecium composed of 1-9 separate or basally fused carpels, and fruit consisting of an aggregate of dry follicles. Seeds often possess a characteristic fringed aril.

Overview

Crossosomataceae is a small but distinct family comprising only 4 genera (Apacheria, Crossosoma, Glossopetalon, Velascoa) and about 8-10 species in total. These plants are specialized shrubs adapted to dry, rocky environments (xerophytes) found in the arid and semi-arid regions of the southwestern USA (California, Arizona, Nevada, Utah, Colorado, New Mexico, Texas) and adjacent Mexico. They are not native to Oklahoma, occurring in drier regions to the west.

Morphologically, they are typically stiffly branched shrubs, sometimes thorny, with small, simple leaves that are usually alternate. Their flowers, though often solitary, can be relatively showy, featuring 4 or 5 white or pinkish petals, numerous stamens, and arising from a noticeable floral cup (hypanthium). A key characteristic is the gynoecium, which consists of one to several carpels that are either completely separate (apocarpous) or fused only at the base, positioned superiorly within the hypanthium. This develops into an aggregate fruit composed of dry follicles. Seeds often have a unique, fringed or Ciliate aril.

The phylogenetic placement of Crossosomataceae has been historically debated. Modern molecular studies place it within the Rosids, often as part of a small, distinct order named Crossosomatales, which may also include families like Staphyleaceae, Stachyuraceae, and Guamatelaceae. This order represents an early branching lineage within the Rosid clade, separate from the major orders like Malvales, Fabales, or Rosales. The family has little direct economic importance but is of botanical interest due to its specialized adaptations and phylogenetic position.

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Deciduous or evergreen shrubs, typically xerophytic (adapted to dry conditions). Stems often rigid, intricately branched, sometimes ending in spines (Glossopetalon).

Leaves

Leaves are usually arranged alternately (sometimes opposite in Apacheria). They are typically small, simple, and usually have entire margins (rarely lobed). Leaves are often somewhat leathery or reduced in size, with short petioles or nearly sessile. Small stipules are usually present, sometimes appearing as glands or small spines, often falling early (deciduous).

Inflorescence

Flowers are typically borne solitary (or sometimes in clusters of 2-3) at the ends of short lateral branches, often appearing axillary along the main stems.

Flowers

Flowers are usually relatively conspicuous and showy compared to the small leaves. They are actinomorphic (radially symmetrical), bisexual (perfect), and typically 4- or 5-merous. A distinct feature is the presence of a well-developed, cup-shaped or saucer-shaped hypanthium (floral cup), making the flower perigynous.

  • Calyx: Consists of 4 or 5 sepals, fused below forming the hypanthium tube, with free lobes at the rim. Persistent in fruit.
  • Corolla: Consists of 4 or 5 petals, which are free (distinct), inserted on the hypanthium rim, usually white or pinkish. Petals are often clawed (narrowed at the base) and spreading, typically larger than the sepal lobes. They overlap or are twisted in bud (imbricate or contorted).
  • Androecium: Stamens are numerous (typically 15 to 50 or more), arranged in several series. They arise from the inner rim of the hypanthium or from a nectar disk lining the hypanthium. Filaments are distinct and slender; anthers are small, 2-thecate, opening via longitudinal slits.
  • Gynoecium: Distinctively consists of 1 to 9 (commonly 2-5) carpels that are either entirely distinct (apocarpous) or fused only slightly at their base (basally connate). The carpels are superior, situated on the receptacle within the hypanthium. Each carpel contains few to numerous ovules (usually attached marginally) and typically has its own short to elongated style and a simple or capitate stigma.

Fruits and Seeds

The fruit is characteristically an aggregate (cluster) of dry, dehiscent follicles, with one follicle developing from each successful carpel of the flower. Each follicle is typically leathery or somewhat papery and opens along one suture (usually the ventral/adaxial side) to release the seeds. Seeds are few to numerous per follicle, often kidney-shaped (reniform), and typically possess a unique, conspicuous, whitish, fringed or Ciliate (hair-like) aril that partially or wholly surrounds the seed. Endosperm may be present or absent; the embryo is often curved.

Chemical Characteristics

Plants contain tannins. Other chemical constituents are not widely characterized or used for identification. They lack the glucosinolates, iridoids, or colored latex found in some other potentially confusing or related orders.

Field Identification

Identifying Crossosomataceae requires recognizing the xerophytic shrub habit combined with specific floral and fruit characteristics, within its restricted geographic range in the arid southwestern US and Mexico.

Primary Identification Features

  • Habit: Xerophytic shrub (often twiggy, sometimes spiny).
  • Small, Simple Leaves: Usually alternate, often entire.
  • Solitary Flowers (Usually): Relatively showy white or pink flowers borne singly on short shoots.
  • Hypanthium Present: Flowers have a distinct floral cup.
  • Numerous Stamens: Many stamens inserted on hypanthium.
  • Apocarpous/Basally Connate Gynoecium: 1-9 carpels, free or fused only at base, superior within hypanthium.
  • Fruit an Aggregate of Follicles: Cluster of dry pods opening along one seam.
  • Fringed Aril on Seeds (Often): Seeds possess a characteristic Ciliate/fringed aril (may require magnification or fallen seeds).
  • Distribution: Arid/Semi-arid SW USA and adjacent Mexico.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Flowers 4- or 5-merous.
  • Petals free, often clawed.
  • Small stipules often present (may be deciduous or modified).

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Spring: Typically the main flowering season, often following winter/spring rains. Look for the solitary white/pink flowers on short branches.
  • Summer/Fall: Follicle fruits develop and mature. Dehisced follicles releasing seeds with arils might be observable.
  • Year-Round: The shrubby, often twiggy or spiny habit and small leaves are persistent features. Habitat (rocky slopes, canyons, deserts) and location within the known range are crucial clues.

Common Confusion Points

  • Rosaceae (Rose Family): Many shrubs in arid regions belong to Rosaceae (e.g., Purshia, Cercocarpus, some Prunus). They often have 5 petals, numerous stamens, and a hypanthium. Key differences: Rosaceae usually have clearly serrated or lobed leaves (sometimes compound), prominent persistent stipules, and fruit types vary widely (achenes, drupes, pomes, follicles) but rarely match the specific follicle aggregate + fringed aril combination of Crossosomataceae. Gynoecium structure often differs (e.g., single carpel in Prunus, inferior ovary in pomes).
  • Saxifragaceae sensu lato (incl. Hydrangeaceae): Some shrubs in related orders (Fendlera, Jamesia, Philadelphus) might occur in similar habitats and have opposite leaves and white flowers. They differ in having fewer stamens (e.g., 8 or 10-many but different arrangement), typically a syncarpous (fully fused) ovary (often semi-inferior or inferior), and fruit usually a capsule (not an aggregate of follicles).
  • Rhamnaceae (Buckthorn Family): Xerophytic shrubs often present. Usually have small, less showy flowers with fewer stamens (4-5), a prominent nectar disk inside the stamens, and fruit typically a drupe or capsule.
  • The combination of characters including the apocarpous/basally connate gynoecium, follicle aggregate, and fringed aril is particularly diagnostic for Crossosomataceae when combined with other features.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Xerophytic Shrub (often twiggy/spiny)
  • Small simple leaves (usu. Alternate)
  • Flowers usually solitary, white/pink
  • 4-5 petals, Numerous Stamens
  • Hypanthium present
  • 1-9 Free or Basally Connate Carpels (Superior within hypanthium)
  • Fruit an aggregate of Follicles
  • Seeds often with Fringed Aril
  • SW USA / Mexico distribution

Key Variations:

  • Leaf arrangement (Alt. vs. Opp. in Apacheria)
  • Presence/absence of spines (Glossopetalon)
  • Flower part number (4 vs. 5)
  • Carpel number (1 to 9)
  • Petal size/shape

Notable Examples

The four small genera of Crossosomataceae are restricted to arid environments in western North America.

Crossosoma species (e.g., C. bigelovii)

Rockflower

The type genus, comprising 2-3 species of shrubs found on rocky slopes and canyons in California, Arizona, Nevada, and Baja California. Leaves alternate, somewhat fleshy. Flowers relatively large, white, 5-petaled, with numerous stamens (20-50+). Typically 2-9 separate carpels develop into follicles with distinctly fringed (Ciliate) arillate seeds.

Glossopetalon species (e.g., G. spinescens)

Greasebush, Spiny Greasebush

Genus of about 4 species (formerly Forsellesia) found across the western US interior. These are intricately branched, often thorny shrubs of very dry habitats. Leaves small, alternate, quickly deciduous. Flowers smaller than Crossosoma, usually 4-merous, white, with fewer stamens (10-20?). Typically only 1 carpel develops into a follicle. Aril present.

Crossosomataceae Flower

Flower Structure

Flowers typically have 4 or 5 separate petals (often white/pink) and numerous stamens, all attached to the rim of a distinct floral cup (hypanthium). Inside the hypanthium sit the 1-9 superior carpels, which are free or only fused at the base.

Follicle Fruit & Arillate Seed

Fruit and Seed Detail

The fruit is an aggregate of follicles. Each follicle splits along one seam to release seeds. A characteristic feature is the often prominent aril surrounding the seed, which is typically fringed or Ciliate (hairy) along its margin.

Apacheria & Velascoa

Other Genera

Apacheria chiricahuensis is a rare shrub from the Chiricahua Mountains (AZ/NM border) notable for having mostly opposite leaves. Velascoa Calderoniae is another monotypic genus known only from Mexico (Querétaro). Both share the basic family characteristics.

Phylogeny and Classification

The phylogenetic placement of Crossosomataceae has historically been uncertain due to its somewhat unique combination of morphological features (e.g., apocarpy/near-apocarpy, numerous stamens, hypanthium). It has previously been associated with families like Rosaceae, Dilleniaceae, or Saxifragaceae.

Current molecular phylogenetic studies strongly suggest that Crossosomataceae belongs within the Rosid clade, likely near the base of the Fabids (Eurosids I) or Malvids (Eurosids II). It is often placed in its own small order, Crossosomatales. This order is phylogenetically somewhat isolated and may include several other small families like Staphyleaceae (Bladdernut family), Stachyuraceae, Guamatelaceae, Aphloiaceae, Geissolomataceae, and Strasburgeriaceae, although the exact composition and relationships within this group are still areas of active research. Crossosomatales appears to represent an early diverging lineage within the Rosid complex, separate from the larger orders like Malvales, Brassicales, Fabales, or Rosales.

Related Families

Crossosomataceae is likely part of the small order Crossosomatales. Other families sometimes included in or considered related to this order are:

  • Staphyleaceae (Bladdernut family): Shrubs/trees, opposite pinnately compound leaves with stipules, 5-merous flowers, superior ovary (2-3 fused carpels), fruit an inflated capsule or berry.
  • Stachyuraceae: Small family (1 genus Stachyurus) of shrubs/trees from E Asia, alternate simple serrated leaves, small 4-merous flowers in pendant racemes before leaves, superior ovary, fruit a berry.
  • Guamatelaceae: Monotypic family (Guamatela) from Central America. Shrub with opposite simple leaves, apocarpous gynoecium, fruit follicles (superficially similar gynoecium/fruit to Crossosomataceae but unrelated order placement sometimes suggested).
  • Aphloiaceae, Geissolomataceae, Strasburgeriaceae: Other small, often geographically restricted families sometimes associated with Crossosomatales.

More distantly, Crossosomatales is related to other major Rosid orders like Malvales, Brassicales, Sapindales, Huerteales, etc.

Evolutionary Significance

Crossosomataceae, though small, is evolutionarily significant:

  • Isolated Rosid Lineage: Represents a distinct and relatively isolated evolutionary branch, likely diverging early within the Rosid clade, making it important for understanding Rosid phylogeny.
  • Adaptation to Aridity: Showcases morphological and potentially physiological adaptations to survive in the arid environments of southwestern North America.
  • Retention of Apocarpy: The presence of separate or nearly separate carpels (apocarpy or near-apocarpy) is relatively uncommon among Rosids (compared to basal eudicots) and offers insights into gynoecium evolution.
  • Unique Aril Structure: The fringed or Ciliate aril found on the seeds is a distinctive feature potentially related to specialized dispersal mechanisms.
  • Phylogenetic Puzzle: Its uncertain exact placement highlights ongoing research and challenges in resolving deep relationships within major angiosperm clades like the Rosids.
