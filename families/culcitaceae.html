Culcitaceae

(No common name; often Culcita Family)

Culcitaceae is a small family of large, terrestrial ferns in the order Cyatheales, containing the single genus Culcita. These ferns possess stout, creeping or ascending rhizomes (sometimes forming short trunks) densely covered in soft hairs, and large, highly divided fronds. They are characterized by marginal sori protected by two-valved indusia. The family exhibits a disjunct distribution, with one species in Macaronesia/Iberia and another in the Neotropics.

Overview

Culcitaceae, represented by the genus Culcita, is another family belonging to the early diverging lineages within the tree fern order Cyatheales. These are large ferns, but instead of tall trunks, they typically have stout, creeping, ascending, or shortly erect rhizomes densely clothed in characteristic long, soft, golden-brown or reddish-brown hairs.

The family comprises only two species with a remarkably disjunct distribution. Culcita macrocarpa is found in the Macaronesian islands (Azores, Madeira, Canaries) and locally on the Iberian Peninsula (Spain and Portugal), often in humid laurel forests or heathlands. Culcita coniifolia occurs in the American tropics, from southern Mexico and the Greater Antilles south through the Andes to Bolivia, typically in montane cloud forests.

These ferns are recognized by their large, multi-pinnate, somewhat leathery fronds and the dense covering of soft hairs on the rhizome and stipe bases. Their reproductive structures are also distinctive: marginal sori located near the sinuses of the ultimate segments, each protected by a two-valved indusium, similar in structure to those found in Dicksoniaceae. Culcitaceae ferns are homosporous.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Culcitaceae
  • Common Name: No widely accepted common name; Culcita Family.
  • Number of Genera: 1 (Culcita)
  • Number of Species: 2 (C. macrocarpa, C. coniifolia)
  • Distribution: Disjunct: Macaronesia & Iberia; Neotropics (Mexico to Bolivia, Antilles).
  • Key Features: Terrestrial, stout rhizome/short trunk, dense soft hairs, large compound fronds, marginal sori with bivalvate indusia, homosporous.
  • Evolutionary Group: Polypodiopsida (Leptosporangiate Ferns) - Cyatheales

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Large terrestrial ferns with stout, creeping, ascending, or shortly erect rhizomes/trunks, often decumbent. Rhizomes are densely covered in hairs.

Fronds (Leaves)

Fronds are large (up to 2-3 meters long), arising from the rhizome apex.

  • Structure: Pinnately compound, typically 3-pinnate to 5-pinnate, broadly triangular in outline.
  • Texture: Somewhat leathery or coriaceous.
  • Vernation: Young fronds show circinate vernation.
  • Petioles (Stipes): Stout, densely covered at the base with characteristic soft hairs.

Fronds are generally monomorphic.

Hairs (Indument)

Rhizomes and petiole bases are characteristically covered in dense, long, soft, multicellular hairs, typically golden-brown or reddish-brown. Scales are absent.

Sori and Indusial Characters

Culcitaceae are homosporous. Spores are produced in sori located on the frond margins.

  • Sorus Position: Sori are marginal, typically situated in the sinus (notch) between ultimate lobes of the frond segments.
  • Indusium: Each sorus is protected by a distinctive two-valved (bivalvate) indusium. The outer valve is formed by the reflexed, modified tooth or lobe of the leaf margin, while the inner valve is a true indusium flap arising from the receptacle. The two valves form a cup or purse-like structure around the sporangia.
  • Receptacle: Sporangia are attached to a slightly elevated receptacle within the indusium.

Spores

Spores are trilete, globose-tetrahedral, and typically have a somewhat granular or ridged surface.

Field Identification

Identifying Culcitaceae relies on the combination of its stout, hairy rhizome/base, large divided fronds, and the characteristic marginal sori with two-valved indusia.

Primary Identification Features

  • Habit: Large terrestrial fern with a stout rhizome or short trunk (not tall and slender).
  • Indument: Dense covering of soft, long, reddish-brown or golden hairs on rhizome/stipe bases.
  • Marginal Sori: Sori located at the edge of the frond segments, often in notches.
  • Bivalvate Indusium: Sori protected by two distinct flaps (one from the margin, one inner flap).
  • Large, Highly Divided Fronds: Fronds typically 3-5 times pinnate.

Common Confusion Points

  • Dicksoniaceae: Also have marginal sori with bivalvate indusia and are often hairy. However, Dicksoniaceae often form taller, more defined trunks, and details of the indument and frond cutting may differ.
  • Dennstaedtiaceae (e.g., Dennstaedtia, Pteridium): Can be large, hairy ferns with creeping rhizomes, but their sori, while sometimes marginal and indusiate, typically have indusia formed differently (e.g., cup-shaped from the margin, or margin plus a less distinct inner flap).
  • Loxsomataceae: Also has marginal sori, but the indusium is urn-shaped (not two-valved) and the indument consists of stiffer bristles.
  • Cyatheaceae: Typically have scales (not just hairs) and sori that are usually round and located away from the margin (not marginal), with different indusial types (cup-shaped, globose, scale-like, or absent).

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For (Culcita):

  • Stout rhizome/short trunk
  • Dense, soft, long hairs (golden/reddish)
  • Large (3-5 pinnate) fronds
  • Marginal sori (often in notches)
  • Two-valved indusium
  • Habitat moist forests

Distinguish From:

  • Dicksoniaceae (often taller trunk)
  • Dennstaedtiaceae (soral details)
  • Loxsomataceae (bristles, urn-shaped indusia)
  • Cyatheaceae (scales, non-marginal sori)

Notable Examples

The family contains a single genus, Culcita, with two species.

Culcita

(No common name)

The sole genus, characterized by stout, hairy rhizomes/bases, large fronds, and marginal sori with bivalvate indusia. Includes C. macrocarpa (Macaronesia/Iberia), notable for its dense golden hairs and presence in European flora, and C. coniifolia (Neotropics), which is similar but found in American cloud forests.

Phylogeny and Classification

Culcitaceae is classified within the order Cyatheales. It is considered one of the relatively early diverging lineages within the order, along with families such as Loxsomataceae, Plagiogyriaceae, and Thyrsopteridaceae. Its exact relationship to these other families is still being refined by molecular studies, but it consistently falls outside the core clade containing the largest tree fern families, Cyatheaceae and Dicksoniaceae.

The disjunct distribution of its two species across the Atlantic Ocean (Macaronesia/Iberia vs. Neotropics) is a significant biogeographic pattern, likely reflecting an ancient origin and vicariance (separation by continental drift) or long-distance dispersal events in the past.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Clade: Tracheophytes (Vascular plants)
  • Class: Polypodiopsida (Leptosporangiate ferns)
  • Order: Cyatheales
  • Family: Culcitaceae

Related Families (within Cyatheales)

As an early diverging lineage in Cyatheales, it is related to other families in the order, particularly:

  • Loxsomataceae: Contains Loxsoma and Loxsomopsis, with marginal urn-shaped indusia.
  • Plagiogyriaceae: Contains Plagiogyria, lacking scales/hairs and having distinct pneumatophores.
  • Thyrsopteridaceae: Monotypic family with extreme frond dimorphism.
  • (Also related to Lophosoriaceae, Cyatheaceae, Dicksoniaceae, etc.)

Evolutionary Significance

Culcitaceae is important for:

  • Understanding Cyatheales Evolution: Represents another piece in the puzzle of early divergences within the tree fern order.
  • Biogeography: Its trans-Atlantic disjunction provides insights into historical plant distributions.
  • Morphological Traits: Shows similarities (bivalvate indusia) with Dicksoniaceae but differs in habit and indument, illustrating evolutionary variations on themes within the order.
