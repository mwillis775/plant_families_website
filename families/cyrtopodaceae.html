Cyrtopodaceae

(No widely recognized common name)

Cyrtopodaceae is a small family of primarily pleurocarpous mosses, though often appearing tufted or bushy. They are typically found as epiphytes on trees or growing on rocks (rupestral) in forests, mainly in the Southern Hemisphere. Key characteristics include leaves that are strongly crisped or contorted when dry, and erect, symmetric capsules that are often ribbed and borne on short to moderately long setae, clearly exserted beyond the perichaetial leaves (unlike Cryphaeaceae).

Overview

The Cyrtopodaceae family is small, containing only one or two genera (Cyrtopus and sometimes Bescherellia) and about five species. Its distribution is centered in the Southern Hemisphere, particularly Australasia (Australia, New Zealand, New Caledonia), with occurrences also in South America and southern Africa. These mosses typically inhabit moist forests, growing on tree trunks, branches, or sometimes on rocks.

Plants often form yellowish-green to brownish, somewhat glossy tufts or mats. Although fundamentally pleurocarpous (with creeping primary stems), the erect or ascending secondary stems often give them a tufted or bushy appearance. The leaves are highly responsive to moisture, becoming strongly crisped and contorted when dry, but spreading when moist. Unlike the previous family (Cryphaeaceae), the sporophytes in Cyrtopodaceae have distinct setae (short to moderately long) that clearly elevate the capsules above the surrounding leaves. The capsules themselves are typically erect, symmetric, and often ribbed.

The classification of Cyrtopodaceae is somewhat unstable. It is often considered very closely related to, or sometimes even included within, the family Ptychomitriaceae. Its ordinal placement within the Bryopsida is also debated, with suggestions including near Orthotrichales, Ptychomniales, or in its own order, Cyrtopodales.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Cyrtopodaceae
  • Common Name: (No widely recognized common name)
  • Number of Genera: 1-2 (Cyrtopus, Bescherellia)
  • Number of Species: Approximately 5
  • Distribution: Primarily Southern Hemisphere (esp. Australasia), S. America, S. Africa; Epiphytic or rupestral.
  • Habitat: Tree bark and rocks in moist forests.
  • Evolutionary Group: Bryophytes - Mosses (Class: Bryopsida, Order: Uncertain - Cyrtopodales / Orthotrichales / Ptychomniales)

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Plants are fundamentally pleurocarpous, with creeping primary stems, but often dominated by erect or ascending secondary stems, forming loose to dense tufts or mats that can appear bushy or sub-dendroid. Typically yellowish-green to brownish, sometimes glossy.

Stems

Primary stems creeping; secondary stems erect/ascending, often irregularly branched. Rhizoids may be present.

Leaves

Leaves are crowded, erect-spreading to spreading when moist, but characteristically strongly crisped, curled, or contorted when dry. Shape is typically ovate-lanceolate to lanceolate, usually tapering to an acute or acuminate apex. The costa (midrib) is single and strong, usually ending near the apex or extending slightly beyond (percurrent to shortly excurrent). Leaf margins are often recurved below and frequently toothed towards the apex. Leaf cells are generally small, rounded-quadrate to shortly rectangular, often thick-walled, and smooth or slightly papillose. Alar cells are usually not strongly differentiated.

Asexual Reproduction

Specialized asexual propagules are not typically reported; fragmentation may occur.

Sporophytes (Reproductive Structures)

Sporophytes arise laterally on secondary stems or branches. The capsule is elevated on a distinct seta, which varies from short to moderately long but clearly exserts the capsule beyond the perichaetial leaves. The capsule is typically erect, symmetric, ovoid to cylindric, and often (especially in Cyrtopus) distinctly ribbed or striate when dry. The peristome is typically double (diplolepidous), though sometimes reduced; the teeth are often papillose. The operculum (lid) is usually rostrate (beaked). The calyptra (remnant of the archegonium covering the capsule) is often large, mitrate (cap-like) or cucullate (hood-like), and sometimes hairy.

Habitat

Primarily epiphytic on tree trunks and branches, but also commonly found growing on rocks (rupestral), typically within moist forests or rainforests.

Field Identification

Identifying Cyrtopodaceae involves noting the habitat, the growth habit, the strongly crisped dry leaves, and the characteristics of the exserted capsule.

Primary Identification Features

  • Habitat: Epiphytic on trees or growing on rocks (rupestral) in moist forests.
  • Leaves When Dry (Key Feature): Strongly crisped, curled, or contorted.
  • Growth Habit: Pleurocarpous, but often forming erect/ascending tufts or bushy mats.
  • Capsule (Key Feature): Erect, symmetric, ovoid to cylindric capsule borne on a distinct seta (short to moderate length), clearly exserted. Capsule often ribbed/striate when dry.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Leaves When Moist: Spreading, ovate-lanceolate to lanceolate, costa strong.
  • Leaf Cells (Microscopic): Small, often thick-walled, quadrate/shortly rectangular.

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Year-round: The vegetative plants with their characteristic crisped dry leaves are visible year-round.
  • Sporophyte Timing: Sporophytes may be found at various times depending on the species and location. The erect, often ribbed capsules on distinct setae are key when present.

Common Confusion Points

Cyrtopodaceae can be confused with several other moss families:

  • Ptychomitriaceae: Very closely related (sometimes merged). Ptychomitrium itself is acrocarpous, but related pleurocarpous genera share similarities. Often distinguished by technical peristome details or gametophyte features.
  • Orthotrichaceae: Also often epiphytic with erect, ribbed capsules on short/moderate setae, but they are strictly acrocarpous and differ in peristome structure and leaf cell patterns.
  • Cryphaeaceae / Leucodontaceae: Also epiphytic, but have capsules immersed or barely emergent on very short/absent setae.
  • Neckeraceae: Some are epiphytic/rupestral, but often have flattened shoots and different capsule orientation/structure.
  • Racopilaceae: Have distinctly dimorphic stem and branch leaves.

Look for the combination of epiphytic/rupestral habit, pleurocarpous (but often tufted) growth, strongly crisped dry leaves, and erect, often ribbed capsules on distinct setae.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Habitat: Epiphytic or Rupestral (forests)
  • Pleurocarpous (often tufted/bushy)
  • Leaves strongly crisped/contorted when dry
  • Capsule on distinct seta (short to moderate)
  • Capsule erect, symmetric
  • Capsule often ribbed/striate

Key Variations:

  • Seta length
  • Degree of capsule ribbing
  • Presence/absence of hair-points on calyptra

Notable Examples

The family is small, primarily represented by the genus Cyrtopus:

Cyrtopus setosus

(No standard common name)

The most well-known species, found in New Zealand, Australia, and surrounding islands. Grows on tree trunks, branches, and rocks in forests. Forms yellowish-green to brownish tufts with strongly crisped leaves when dry. Capsules are erect, cylindric, and distinctly ribbed, borne on short to moderate setae. The calyptra is often hairy.

Phylogeny and Classification

Cyrtopodaceae belongs to the class Bryopsida. Its classification at the ordinal level remains uncertain and reflects ongoing research into moss phylogeny. Traditionally, due to similarities in the erect, often ribbed capsule and peristome structure, it has been considered close to the order Orthotrichales or sometimes placed in its own order, Cyrtopodales.

More recently, molecular and morphological studies suggest a very close relationship with the family Ptychomitriaceae (which contains both acrocarpous and pleurocarpous genera). Consequently, Cyrtopodaceae is often now included within Ptychomitriaceae or placed alongside it within the order Ptychomniales. The Ptychomniales themselves are part of the large subclass Bryidae, but their exact relationship to other major orders like Hypnales or Bryales is still being refined.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Division: Bryophyta (Mosses)
  • Class: Bryopsida
  • Subclass: Bryidae
  • Order: Ptychomniales / Orthotrichales / Cyrtopodales (Uncertain)
  • Family: Cyrtopodaceae (or within Ptychomitriaceae)

Related Families/Groups

Based on current understanding, the most closely related family is:

  • Ptychomitriaceae: Shares many features; Cyrtopodaceae is sometimes merged into this family. Contains both acrocarpous (Ptychomitrium) and pleurocarpous genera.

Other potentially related families, depending on ordinal placement, might include:

  • Orthotrichaceae: Shares erect, often ribbed capsules and epiphytic habit, but differs in being acrocarpous and peristome details.
  • Racopilaceae: Sometimes placed near Ptychomniales, differs greatly in having dimorphic leaves.

Evolutionary Significance

Cyrtopodaceae, though small, is interesting for:

  • Gametophyte/Sporophyte Combination: Exhibits a pleurocarpous gametophyte architecture combined with an erect, symmetric, often ribbed capsule more reminiscent of some acrocarpous groups (like Orthotrichaceae).
  • Biogeography: Its predominantly Southern Hemisphere distribution contributes to understanding Gondwanan biogeographic patterns in mosses.
  • Phylogenetic Placement: Its uncertain classification highlights areas where moss phylogeny is still being actively researched, particularly concerning the relationships between major pleurocarpous and acrocarpous lineages within Bryidae.
  • Relationship with Ptychomitriaceae: The close link between Cyrtopodaceae and Ptychomitriaceae provides insights into the evolution of growth forms (pleurocarpy vs. acrocarpy) within closely related lineages.
