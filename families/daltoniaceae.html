Daltoniaceae

Daltonia Moss Family

Daltoniaceae is a family of primarily pleurocarpous mosses found predominantly in tropical and subtropical regions worldwide. They are often delicate plants forming loose, frequently glossy or iridescent mats, typically growing as epiphytes on bark and twigs or notably as epiphylls on the surface of living leaves in humid forests. Key characteristics often include leaves with a distinct border of narrow cells, variable costa (single, double, or absent), and characteristically large, lax, smooth leaf cells.

Overview

The Daltoniaceae family comprises around 5-10 genera and approximately 100 species, though the exact circumscription relative to the closely related Hookeriaceae is sometimes debated. These mosses thrive in the shaded, humid conditions of tropical, subtropical, and sometimes temperate rainforests. Their ability to grow on the surfaces of living leaves (epiphyllism) is a notable ecological specialization shared with relatively few other bryophyte groups.

Plants typically form thin, creeping mats or patches, often exhibiting a sheen or iridescence when moist. The leaves are diverse in shape but frequently possess a border of elongated cells, and the main cells of the leaf blade (laminal cells) are typically large, thin-walled, and smooth, giving the plants a soft texture. The costa structure is variable, providing a key character for distinguishing genera within the family and related groups (e.g., Daltonia usually has a single costa, while related Hookeriaceae often have double or absent costas).

Sporophytes are borne laterally and feature capsules elevated on setae of varying lengths, often erect or inclined. Daltoniaceae belongs to the order Hookeriales, a distinct lineage within the Bryopsida known for its large leaf cells and often intricate peristome structures.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Daltoniaceae
  • Common Name: Daltonia Moss Family
  • Number of Genera: Approximately 5-10
  • Number of Species: Approximately 100
  • Distribution: Pantropical and subtropical, extending to temperate regions (esp. Southern Hemisphere).
  • Habitat: Humid, shaded forests; typically epiphytic (bark, twigs) or epiphyllous (leaves), also on rock/logs.
  • Evolutionary Group: Bryophytes - Mosses (Class: Bryopsida, Order: Hookeriales)

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Plants are pleurocarpous, usually forming loose, delicate, often glossy or iridescent mats or patches. Stems creeping, often slender, sometimes pendent.

Stems

Stems creeping or pendent, typically irregularly or pinnately branched. Central strand may be present or absent.

Leaves

Leaves are variable in shape, often ovate, oblong, lingulate (tongue-shaped), or lanceolate, sometimes asymmetric. They may be complanate (flattened in one plane) or arranged radially. Apex is usually acute, obtuse, or apiculate. A key feature in many genera is a distinct leaf border of narrow, elongated, often thick-walled cells. The costa is variable: single and ending below the apex (e.g., Daltonia), short and double, or absent. Leaf cells are typically large, lax, smooth, thin-walled (parenchymatous), and often hexagonal or rhomboidal. Alar cells are generally not differentiated.

Asexual Reproduction

Asexual reproduction via specialized propagules (gemmae or deciduous leaves/branches) occurs in some species.

Sporophytes (Reproductive Structures)

Sporophytes arise laterally. The capsule is elevated on a seta of variable length (short to long), which is typically smooth. The capsule is usually erect or inclined, symmetric, and ovoid to cylindric. The peristome is double (diplolepidous), often well-developed with 16 outer teeth and 16 inner segments (Hookerioid type), but can be specialized or reduced in some genera. The operculum (lid) is typically rostrate (beaked). The calyptra (capsule covering) is often mitrate (cap-like) and frequently fringed or hairy at the base.

Habitat

Typically found in very humid, shaded conditions in tropical and subtropical forests. Predominantly epiphytic (on bark, twigs, tree ferns) or epiphyllous (growing on the surface of living leaves), but also occurs on rocks, soil banks, and decaying logs.

Field Identification

Identifying Daltoniaceae often involves recognizing the delicate pleurocarpous habit in humid tropical/subtropical forest habitats, combined with leaf characteristics like borders and large cells.

Primary Identification Features

  • Habitat (Key Feature): Humid, shaded forests; typically epiphytic or epiphyllous.
  • Growth Habit: Delicate, pleurocarpous mats or patches, often glossy or iridescent.
  • Leaf Border (Common Feature): Presence of a distinct border of narrow cells along the leaf margin (use hand lens).
  • Leaf Cells (Key Feature): Cells appear large, lax, and smooth (requires hand lens, best with microscope).
  • Costa: Variable – check if single, double/short, or absent.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Sporophytes (if present): Capsules typically erect or inclined on setae of various lengths. Calyptra often fringed/hairy at base.
  • Leaf Shape: Often ovate, oblong, or lingulate.

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Year-round: The delicate gametophytes are typically visible year-round in their humid habitats.
  • Sporophyte Timing: Sporophyte production varies; capsules may be found at different times depending on species and location.

Common Confusion Points

Daltoniaceae can be confused with other delicate pleurocarpous mosses in similar habitats, especially other families within Hookeriales:

  • Hookeriaceae: Very closely related (sometimes merged). Often differ in costa structure (Hookeriaceae frequently have double or absent costas, while Daltonia has a single costa). Both share large cells and often bordered leaves.
  • Pilotrichaceae: Also Hookeriales, often more robust, frequently complanate, sometimes aquatic/semi-aquatic.
  • Leucomiaceae: Also Hookeriales, typically very pale whitish-green, delicate, ecostate (lacking costa).
  • Neckeraceae: Often larger, complanate, leaf cells usually smaller and firmer, peristome different.
  • Plagiotheciaceae: Often complanate, but leaf cells typically narrower and costa short/double or absent.
  • Sematophyllaceae / Hypnaceae: Leaf cells usually much smaller and narrower, often have differentiated alar cells.

Look for the combination of humid tropical/subtropical habitat (esp. epiphytic/epiphyllous), delicate pleurocarpous mats, often bordered leaves, and large, lax, smooth leaf cells.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Habitat: Humid forests, epiphytic/epiphyllous
  • Delicate pleurocarpous mats (often glossy)
  • Leaves often with distinct border
  • Leaf cells large, lax, smooth
  • Costa variable (single, double/short, absent)
  • Capsule erect/inclined on seta

Key Variations:

  • Presence/absence and type of costa
  • Leaf shape (ovate, lingulate, etc.)
  • Presence/absence of leaf border
  • Seta length

Notable Examples

The family includes several genera characteristic of tropical and subtropical regions:

Daltonia spp.

(Daltonia Mosses)

The type genus for the family, widespread in tropical and temperate regions. Characterized by typically bordered leaves with large cells and a single costa ending below the apex. Often found on twigs and branches.

Distichophyllum spp.

(Distichophyllum Mosses)

A large genus often included in Daltoniaceae (sometimes Hookeriaceae). Plants are frequently complanate (flattened). Leaves are strongly bordered, often asymmetric, and the costa is variable (single, short/double, or absent). Common in wet, shaded habitats on various substrates.

Phylogeny and Classification

Daltoniaceae belongs to the class Bryopsida and is a core member of the order Hookeriales. This order is distinct from other major pleurocarpous orders like Hypnales and is characterized by features such as typically large, lax leaf cells, often bordered leaves, and diverse, often elaborate peristome structures.

Within Hookeriales, Daltoniaceae is very closely related to Hookeriaceae. The boundary between these two families is often considered artificial or unclear, based primarily on costa characters (single in Daltoniaceae vs. double/absent in Hookeriaceae) which may not reflect true evolutionary relationships consistently. Some classifications merge the two families, while others maintain them as distinct but closely allied sister groups. Other families within Hookeriales include Pilotrichaceae and Leucomiaceae.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Division: Bryophyta (Mosses)
  • Class: Bryopsida
  • Subclass: Bryidae
  • Order: Hookeriales
  • Family: Daltoniaceae (closely related to/sometimes merged with Hookeriaceae)

Related Families/Groups

Daltoniaceae is most closely related to other families within the order Hookeriales:

  • Hookeriaceae: Sister group or family into which Daltoniaceae is sometimes merged. Differs primarily in costa characters.
  • Pilotrichaceae: Often more robust, complanate plants.
  • Leucomiaceae: Very pale, delicate, ecostate plants.
  • Saulomataceae: Small family within the order.

The order Hookeriales itself is distinct from other major pleurocarpous orders like Hypnales.

Evolutionary Significance

Daltoniaceae is significant in bryophyte evolution for:

  • Hookeriales Diversity: Represents a key component of the Hookeriales order, showcasing adaptations within this lineage.
  • Epiphytism and Epiphyllism: Shows remarkable specialization for growing on bark and, notably, on living leaves in humid environments.
  • Large Cell Morphology: The characteristic large, lax leaf cells represent a different cellular strategy compared to the smaller, often thicker-walled cells of many other moss groups.
  • Taxonomic Relationships: The close and debated relationship with Hookeriaceae highlights ongoing research into family delimitation based on molecular data versus traditional morphological characters like the costa.
