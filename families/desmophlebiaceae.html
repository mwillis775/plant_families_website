Desmophlebiaceae

(No widely accepted common name)

Desmophlebiaceae is a monotypic family of ferns (order Polypodiales), containing only the genus Desmophlebium from Southeast Asia (e.g., Borneo, Philippines). It is distinguished by its highly unique venation pattern of parallel main veins connected by perpendicular cross-veins, forming rectangular areoles, and round, exindusiate sori borne on these cross-veins. Its phylogenetic position remains highly uncertain.

Overview

Desmophlebiaceae is one of the most enigmatic and poorly understood families within the Polypodiales order. It contains only one genus, Desmophlebium, with one or possibly two species restricted to montane rainforests in parts of Southeast Asia, such as Borneo and the Philippines. These terrestrial ferns are medium-sized, growing from short-creeping rhizomes.

The family's most striking feature, and the primary reason for its taxonomic isolation, is its unique venation. The main lateral veins within each pinna run parallel to each other towards the margin, and they are interconnected by numerous, regularly spaced, perpendicular cross-veins (commissures). This creates a highly distinctive grid-like pattern of rectangular areoles unlike that seen in almost any other fern group. The round sori, lacking indusia, are borne directly on these perpendicular cross-veins. Due to its unique morphology and rarity, its evolutionary relationships have been difficult to determine and remain uncertain.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Desmophlebiaceae M.R.Schomb.
  • Common Name: (None widely accepted)
  • Number of Genera: 1 (Desmophlebium)
  • Number of Species: 1-2 (D. leuchlorum, D. nepalense - status debated)
  • Distribution: Southeast Asia (e.g., Borneo, Philippines, possibly Nepal).
  • Evolutionary Group: Ferns (Polypodiopsida) - Polypodiales - Position highly uncertain within the order.

Key Characteristics

Note: Desmophlebiaceae is defined by its unique venation (parallel main veins connected by perpendicular cross-veins forming rectangular areoles) and round, exindusiate sori borne on the cross-veins.

Sporophyte (Dominant Phase) Structure

The visible fern plant is the diploid sporophyte:

  • Rhizome: Short-creeping to suberect; scaly (scales likely brown, non-clathrate, details limited).
  • Fronds (Leaves):
    • Size & Form: Medium-sized; monomorphic.
    • Stipe (Petiole): Scaly at base; vascular bundle structure uncertain, likely multiple based on general Polypodiales anatomy but specifics rarely documented.
    • Blade: 1-pinnate; pinnae simple, lanceolate, margins entire or slightly crenate/toothed.
    • Veins: Highly characteristic and diagnostic: Main lateral veins run mostly parallel towards the margin. These are connected by numerous, regularly spaced, perpendicular cross-veins (commissures). This creates a distinctive pattern of rectangular areoles. Free included veinlets within the areoles are absent or few and simple.
    • Indument: Blades likely glabrous or nearly so.

Reproductive Structures (Sori)

Sori contain the sporangia and are located on the underside of fertile fronds:

  • Shape & Position: Sori are round to slightly elongated, relatively small. Crucially, they are borne directly on the perpendicular cross-veins (commissural veins), typically forming one or more rows between the main parallel lateral veins.
  • Indusium: Absent (sori are exindusiate).

Sporangia and Spores

  • Sporangia: Typical leptosporangiate structure.
  • Spores: Reportedly monolete (bilateral, bean-shaped), surface details likely require SEM and may be variable or poorly documented.

Gametophyte

The small, haploid gametophyte phase:

  • Form: Unknown or poorly documented, presumably the typical heart-shaped fern prothallus.

Field Identification

Identifying Desmophlebiaceae relies almost entirely on recognizing its unique venation pattern, combined with the sorus characteristics.

Primary Identification Features

  • Rectangular Venation Pattern: This is the key feature. Look for parallel main lateral veins connected by numerous, regularly spaced, perpendicular cross-veins, forming a grid of rectangles.
  • Sori on Cross-Veins: Observe the underside; the small, round sori are located directly on these perpendicular cross-veins.
  • Exindusiate Sori: No indusium (protective covering) is present over the sori.
  • 1-Pinnate Fronds: Fronds are simply pinnate with undivided pinnae.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Habitat and Location: Found in montane rainforests in specific parts of Southeast Asia (Borneo, Philippines, etc.).

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Year-round: As a tropical montane fern, fronds are likely present year-round. Mature sori are needed for confirmation.

Common Confusion Points

The unique venation makes confusion unlikely if observed carefully, but comparison with other ferns having netted veins or round exindusiate sori might arise:

  • Polypodiaceae: Many have round, exindusiate sori and sometimes netted venation. However, the venation rarely forms such a regular rectangular grid, and sori are typically borne at the ends of free veins within areoles or at vein junctions, not specifically on perpendicular commissures linking parallel main veins.
  • Thelypteridaceae (some genera): May have netted venation and round sori, but the venation pattern is different (less rigidly rectangular), sori are usually indusiate (though indusia can be small or fall early), and sori are borne on the regular veins, not specifically commissures.
  • Pteridaceae (some genera): Highly diverse; some might have netted veins or exindusiate sori, but none share the specific combination of Desmophlebiaceae's venation and soral placement.
  • Other families with netted venation (e.g., Lomariopsidaceae): Differ significantly in soral characteristics (e.g., acrostichoid sporangia in Lomagramma).

Key differentiator: The highly regular, rectangular venation pattern formed by parallel main veins and perpendicular cross-veins, with round, exindusiate sori borne on the cross-veins, is unique to Desmophlebiaceae.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Veins: Parallel main veins + perpendicular cross-veins = Rectangular areoles (Unique grid pattern)
  • Sori: Round, borne on cross-veins
  • Indusium: Absent (Exindusiate)
  • Fronds: 1-pinnate
  • Habitat: SE Asian montane forests

Key Distinctions:

  • vs. Polypodiaceae: Venation pattern differs, sori usually on free vein ends/junctions.
  • vs. Thelypteridaceae: Venation pattern differs, sori usually indusiate, not on commissures.
  • vs. Pteridaceae: Lacks this specific combination of features.

Desmophlebium leuchlorum

(No common name)

The primary species representing the family, found in locations like Borneo and the Philippines. It exhibits the characteristic 1-pinnate fronds and the unique rectangular venation pattern with sori on the cross-veins.

Phylogeny and Classification

The phylogenetic position of Desmophlebiaceae within the order Polypodiales is highly uncertain and represents a significant gap in our understanding of fern evolution. Its unique combination of morphological features, particularly the venation, makes it difficult to place based on morphology alone. Historical classifications placed it tentatively near families like Thelypteridaceae or Dryopteridaceae, but these were largely speculative.

Molecular phylogenetic studies have also yielded ambiguous or conflicting results, often hampered by the rarity of the plant and difficulty in obtaining suitable material. Some studies suggest it might be an isolated lineage within the Eupolypods I clade (which includes Pteridaceae, Aspleniaceae etc.), while others place it uncertainly within Eupolypods II (including Polypodiaceae, Dryopteridaceae, Athyriaceae etc.), or even outside both major clades. The Pteridophyte Phylogeny Group classification (PPG I) places it tentatively within Eupolypods I but acknowledges the profound uncertainty. Further research with broader taxon sampling and more genetic data is required to resolve its evolutionary relationships.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Division: Pteridophyta (or Polypodiophyta)
  • Class: Polypodiopsida
  • Order: Polypodiales
  • Family: Desmophlebiaceae
  • Phylogenetic position highly uncertain within Polypodiales (tentatively placed in Eupolypods I by PPG I, but requires confirmation).

Related Families

Defining close relatives is currently not possible due to the high phylogenetic uncertainty. Potential distant relationships suggested by different studies or classifications might include (but are not confirmed):

  • Pteridaceae: Sometimes suggested as a possible relative within Eupolypods I.
  • Polypodiaceae: Shares exindusiate sori, but differs greatly otherwise; relationship unclear.
  • Thelypteridaceae / Dryopteridaceae / Athyriaceae: Historical placements, now considered less likely but reflect some superficial similarities.
  • It may represent a very isolated, ancient lineage with no close extant relatives.

Evolutionary Significance

Desmophlebiaceae is significant for:

  • Unique Morphology: The highly specialized rectangular venation pattern is almost unparalleled among ferns.
  • Taxonomic Isolation: Represents a major uncertainty in the fern tree of life, highlighting gaps in our knowledge.
  • Biogeography: Its restricted distribution in Southeast Asia may provide clues about past geological or climatic conditions, once its relationships are better understood.
  • Conservation Concern: As a rare plant with a limited distribution, it may be vulnerable to habitat loss.
