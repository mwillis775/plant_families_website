Dicranellaceae

Dicranella Moss Family

Dicranellaceae is a family of small to medium-sized mosses, commonly found forming silky tufts on soil or occasionally rock. They are characterized by narrow, lanceolate to subulate leaves that are often strongly curved to one side (secund), a strong costa, and typically inclined capsules borne on elongated setae, often with a swelling at the base (struma).

Overview

The Dicranellaceae family comprises a group of relatively common mosses found across the globe in various climates, though particularly diverse in temperate regions. These mosses typically grow on disturbed mineral soil, such as banks, roadsides, ditches, and forest floors, sometimes colonizing sandstone or other soft rocks. They often form characteristic silky, yellowish-green to green tufts.

Key features include their narrow leaves, which are frequently secund (all pointing in the same direction, like teeth on a comb), giving the shoots a distinctive curved appearance. The sporophytes, when present, usually feature capsules held on slender stalks (setae), often nodding or inclined, and sometimes possessing a noticeable swelling (struma) at the base where the capsule joins the seta. The peristome teeth are typically of the "dicranoid" type, deeply split into two forks.

Dicranellaceae is closely related to the larger family Dicranaceae, and the distinction between them (or the inclusion of Dicranellaceae within Dicranaceae) varies among classification systems. Generally, Dicranellaceae includes smaller plants with less differentiated alar cells compared to core Dicranaceae genera like Dicranum. Both families belong to the large order Dicranales.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Dicranellaceae
  • Common Name: Dicranella Moss Family
  • Number of Genera: ~5-10 (e.g., Dicranella, Anisothecium, Microdus)
  • Number of Species: Approximately 100-200
  • Distribution: Cosmopolitan, especially common in temperate zones; typically on soil.
  • Evolutionary Group: Bryophytes - Bryopsida (True Mosses) - Dicranales

Key Characteristics

Dicranellaceae mosses share features common to the Dicranales, with some specific traits.

Gametophyte Form and Habit

The gametophyte generation forms tufts on soil:

  • Habit: Plants typically grow in loose to dense tufts, often with a silky lustre.
  • Size: Small to medium-sized mosses, stems usually 0.5-3 cm high.
  • Color: Commonly yellowish-green, light green, or dark green.
  • Stems: Erect, usually simple or sparsely branched. Often with rhizoids at the base.
  • Substrate: Predominantly found on bare mineral soil, clay, sand, or humus; sometimes on soft rock.

Leaves (Phyllids)

Leaves are typically narrow and often curved to one side:

  • Arrangement & Shape: Usually erect-spreading to strongly secund (curved/pointing to one side), especially near the stem apex. Leaves often lanceolate to linear-lanceolate, typically tapering gradually to a narrow, subulate (awl-shaped) point from a broader, sometimes sheathing base.
  • Costa (Midrib): Strong, single, usually filling the subula and ending in the apex (percurrent) or extending slightly beyond it (shortly excurrent).
  • Margins: Mostly plane or sometimes recurved below, usually entire or finely serrulate (toothed) near the apex.
  • Cells: Upper laminal cells typically rectangular, quadrate, or shortly linear, smooth. Basal cells usually longer and more rectangular. Alar cells (at basal corners) generally not strongly differentiated or inflated (a key difference from many Dicranaceae).

Reproductive Structures

  • Sexual Condition: Variable, autoicous or dioicous depending on the species.
  • Archegonia & Antheridia: Usually terminal on main stems or short branches. Perichaetial leaves often larger than vegetative leaves and sheathing the base of the seta.

Sporophyte

Sporophytes are frequently produced and have typical dicranoid features:

  • Seta: Usually elongated, slender, smooth, often yellowish, reddish, or brownish, typically straight or somewhat curved (flexuose).
  • Capsule: Typically inclined to horizontal (cernuous), sometimes erect or curved. Shape usually ovate to cylindrical, often asymmetric. Frequently strumose (possessing a distinct swelling or goiter at the base/neck). Capsule wall usually smooth, sometimes becoming ribbed or furrowed when dry and empty.
  • Operculum: Typically long-rostrate (with a long, slender beak).
  • Peristome: Single (haplolepidous type). Consists of 16 reddish-brown teeth that are typically deeply divided about halfway down into 32 filiform (thread-like) segments. Teeth are characteristically vertically pitted-striolate on the outer surface (fine lines and pits).
  • Calyptra: Typically cucullate (hood-shaped, split on one side), smooth, usually falling early.

Spores

Spores are typically small, spherical, and smooth or finely papillose.

Chemical Characteristics

No specific chemical characteristics are widely noted beyond those typical for Bryopsida mosses.

Field Identification

Identifying Dicranellaceae often involves recognizing the combination of small size, silky tufts, secund leaves, and inclined, often strumose capsules on soil.

Primary Identification Features (Hand Lens Level)

  • Secund Leaves: Look for the characteristic curving of the narrow leaves to one side of the stem, especially noticeable near the shoot tips.
  • Habitat: Predominantly found on bare, often disturbed mineral soil (clay, sand, silt) on banks, roadsides, trails, or fields.
  • Silky Tufts: Plants often form yellowish-green or green tufts with a somewhat silky appearance.
  • Inclined, Strumose Capsules: If sporophytes are present, look for capsules nodding or inclined on slender setae, often with a distinct swelling (struma) at the base.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Narrow Leaf Shape: Leaves tapering to a fine point (subulate).
  • Strong Costa: Midrib usually prominent and reaching near or into the leaf tip.
  • Capsule Shape: Ovate to cylindrical, sometimes ribbed when dry.
  • Long-Beaked Operculum: The lid of the capsule has a long, pointed beak.

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Year-Round: Gametophytes are perennial and can be identified by their habit and leaf characteristics.
  • Spring / Summer / Autumn (Varies): Sporophytes are common in many species and are most useful for identification during their maturation period.

Common Confusion Points

Distinguishing Dicranellaceae from similar mosses:

  • Dicranaceae (Dicranum, etc.): Often larger plants, may have more strongly differentiated alar cells (inflated or colored basal corner cells). Capsule orientation and struma presence can vary. Distinction requires careful comparison, sometimes microscopic features.
  • Ditrichaceae (Ditrichum, Ceratodon): Grow in similar habitats. Leaves are usually straight or only slightly curved, not strongly secund. Capsules often erect and symmetric, peristome teeth may differ (e.g., less deeply divided in Ditrichum).
  • Bryaceae (small species like Pohlia): Leaves usually broader with different cell shapes (rhomboidal). Possess a double peristome (endostome + exostome), though this requires magnification.
  • Campylopus (Leucobryaceae or Dicranaceae): Often have distinctive hyaline hair points on leaves or different alar cell development.
  • Oncophoraceae (Oncophorus): Similar habit and strumose capsules, but typically grow on wood or rock and have different leaf base shapes.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Habitat: Bare soil, disturbed ground
  • Small, silky tufts (often yellowish-green)
  • Narrow leaves, often strongly secund (curved to one side)
  • Capsules often inclined/nodding on slender setae
  • Capsules frequently strumose (swollen base)

Key Distinctions:

  • vs. Dicranaceae: Often smaller size, less differentiated alar cells.
  • vs. Ditrichaceae: Leaf posture (secund vs. mostly straight), capsule orientation/shape.
  • vs. Bryaceae: Leaf shape/cells, peristome type (single vs. double).

Notable Examples

The family's main genus is Dicranella.

Dicranella heteromalla

(Silky Forklet Moss)

A very common and widespread species found on acidic soil banks, roadsides, and disturbed ground. Forms characteristic silky, bright green or yellowish-green tufts. Leaves are strongly secund, linear-subulate. Capsules are inclined, ovate, smooth (not ribbed), and typically possess a distinct struma at the base.

Phylogeny and Classification

Dicranellaceae is classified within the class Bryopsida and belongs to the large order Dicranales. This order encompasses mosses generally characterized by lanceolate to subulate leaves, often a strong costa, and typically a single peristome with 16 teeth often divided into 32 segments (haplolepidous-dicranoid type).

The family Dicranellaceae is very closely related to Dicranaceae. Historically and in some current classifications, the genera of Dicranellaceae (like Dicranella) are treated as a subfamily (Dicranelloideae) within a broadly defined Dicranaceae. However, other classifications elevate it to family rank based on consistent morphological differences (smaller size, less differentiated alar cells) and molecular phylogenetic analyses that often show Dicranellaceae as a distinct clade sister to or nested near parts of Dicranaceae sensu stricto. Its precise relationship within the Dicranales complex, which also includes families like Ditrichaceae, Rhabdoweisiaceae, and Leucobryaceae, remains an area of active bryological research.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Clade: Embryophyta (Land Plants)
  • Division: Bryophyta (Mosses)
  • Class: Bryopsida
  • Order: Dicranales
  • Family: Dicranellaceae (sometimes included in Dicranaceae)

Related Families (Order Dicranales)

Dicranellaceae is closely allied with other families in the Dicranales:

  • Dicranaceae: The most closely related family, often larger plants with more differentiated alar cells.
  • Ditrichaceae: Small mosses often on soil, usually with straight leaves and erect capsules.
  • Rhabdoweisiaceae: Small tufted mosses, usually on rock, with different peristome features.
  • Leucobryaceae: Characterized by whitish appearance due to multi-layered hyaline cells (leucocysts), often placed within Dicranales.
  • Oncophoraceae: Often with strumose capsules but typically on wood or rock.

Evolutionary Significance

Dicranellaceae contributes to understanding moss evolution:

  • Colonization of Disturbed Habitats: Many species are pioneers on bare soil, showing adaptations for rapid colonization.
  • Relationship within Dicranales: Helps clarify the boundaries and relationships between major lineages like Dicranaceae and Ditrichaceae.
  • Morphological Variation: Shows variation in capsule orientation, struma development, and leaf posture within a relatively constrained set of features.
  • Common Ancestry: Shares the characteristic dicranoid peristome structure with many related families, indicating common ancestry and evolutionary pathways for spore dispersal mechanisms.
