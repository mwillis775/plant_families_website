Dilleniales

An Order of Flowering Plants

Dilleniales is an order of flowering plants containing the single family Dilleniaceae. This group comprises trees, shrubs, lianas, and occasionally herbs, found predominantly in tropical and subtropical regions, particularly diverse in Australia. They are known for often having conspicuous flowers with numerous stamens and fruits that are frequently follicles or berry-like structures sometimes enclosed by persistent sepals.

Overview

The order Dilleniales, represented solely by the family Dilleniaceae, occupies a unique position within the eudicot lineage. These plants are primarily woody, inhabiting tropical and subtropical environments across the globe, with a significant center of diversity in Australasia (especially the genus Hibbertia). While not as speciose as some other major orders, Dilleniales exhibits interesting floral and vegetative characteristics.

Members are recognized by features such as simple, often alternate leaves (sometimes opposite or whorled), frequently prominent stipules or winged petioles, and flowers typically bearing numerous stamens. The carpels are generally free or only slightly fused at the base. Economically, some species like Dillenia indica (Elephant Apple) are used locally for fruit or timber, and many Hibbertia species are cultivated as ornamentals ('Guinea Flowers').

Their phylogenetic placement as sister to the large Superrosid clade makes them significant for understanding the early evolution of core eudicots. The family Dilleniaceae itself shows considerable variation in growth form and fruit type across its genera.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Dilleniales (Order)
  • Family Included: Dilleniaceae
  • Common Name: (Order level); Dilleniaceae sometimes called the Dillenia family or Guinea Flower family
  • Number of Genera: Approximately 11-13 (in Dilleniaceae)
  • Number of Species: Approximately 300-400 (in Dilleniaceae)
  • Distribution: Pantropical and subtropical, centers of diversity in Australasia, Southeast Asia, and the Neotropics
  • Evolutionary Group: Eudicots - Sister to Superrosids

Key Characteristics (of Dilleniaceae)

Growth Form and Habit

Most members of Dilleniaceae are woody plants, including trees, shrubs, and lianas (woody vines). Some herbaceous species exist, particularly within the large genus Hibbertia. Stems are typically solid, and lianas often exhibit anomalous secondary growth.

Leaves

Leaves are usually simple and alternate, though opposite or whorled arrangements occur in some Hibbertia. They are often leathery (coriaceous) and may have serrated or entire margins. Some species (Dillenia, Curatella) have strongly parallel, prominent secondary veins giving a corrugated appearance, or a rough, sandpapery texture due to silica deposits (Curatella). Stipules are often present, sometimes winged and fused to the petiole, or absent.

Inflorescence

Flowers can be solitary or arranged in various types of inflorescences, such as cymes, racemes, or panicles. These are often terminal or axillary. Bracts are typically present below the flowers.

Flowers

Flowers are typically bisexual, actinomorphic (radially symmetrical), and often showy, commonly yellow or white.

  • Sepals: Usually 5 (sometimes fewer or more), free, often persistent and sometimes becoming fleshy and enclosing the fruit (Dillenia). They are typically imbricate (overlapping in bud).
  • Petals: Usually 5 (sometimes fewer or absent), free, often crumpled in bud and falling quickly (caducous). Typically yellow or white.
  • Androecium: Stamens are numerous (often >20, can be fewer), typically free, arising centrifugally (from center outwards) or centripetally. Filaments are usually slender; anthers typically open via pores or short slits. Staminodes (sterile stamens) are sometimes present.
  • Gynoecium: Composed of (1-) 2-20 carpels, which are usually free (apocarpous) or only slightly fused at the base. Each carpel contains one to many ovules. The ovary is superior. Styles are typically free, one per carpel.

Fruits and Seeds

The fruit type is variable. A common type is a follicle, a dry fruit that dehisces along one suture to release seeds. Sometimes multiple follicles develop from a single flower (an etaerio of follicles). In some genera, like Dillenia, the fruit is indehiscent and berry-like or drupe-like, often enclosed by the persistent, fleshy sepals. Seeds can be arillate (possessing an aril, a fleshy appendage) which aids in dispersal, often by birds or ants.

Chemical Characteristics

Plants in this order often accumulate aluminum. Ellagic acid and tannins are commonly present. Some species contain saponins or alkaloids, but they are not defining chemical features for the group as a whole.

Field Identification (of Dilleniaceae)

Identifying plants belonging to Dilleniales (Dilleniaceae) involves observing a combination of vegetative and floral features. Since it's the only family in the order, identifying Dilleniaceae confirms the order.

Primary Identification Features

  • Numerous Stamens: Flowers often have many conspicuous stamens, sometimes bent in bud.
  • Free or Nearly Free Carpels: The gynoecium usually consists of multiple carpels that are separate or only slightly joined at the base (visible in flower or fruit).
  • Persistent Sepals: Sepals often remain attached to the developing fruit, sometimes becoming enlarged or fleshy.
  • Simple, Alternate Leaves: This is the most common leaf arrangement, often combined with a leathery texture.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Leaf Texture/Venation: Look for strongly parallel secondary veins (e.g., Dillenia) or a rough, sandpapery feel (Curatella).
  • Stipules/Petioles: Check for the presence of stipules, which might be fused to the petiole forming wings.
  • Flower Color: Yellow and white are common petal colors.
  • Fruit Type: Look for dehiscent follicles or fruits enclosed by persistent sepals. Arillate seeds might be visible if fruits are open.
  • Habit: Primarily woody (trees, shrubs, lianas) in tropical/subtropical areas.

Common Confusion Points

  • Ranunculaceae (Buttercup Family): Some Ranunculaceae also have numerous stamens and free carpels, but they are mostly herbaceous, often have compound or lobed leaves, and lack persistent, fleshy sepals enclosing the fruit.
  • Rosaceae (Rose Family): Many Rosaceae have numerous stamens, but they often have a hypanthium (floral cup), stipules are usually present and distinct, and carpels can be free or fused within the hypanthium. Leaf shape and fruit types (pomes, drupes, achenes) also differ.
  • Hypericaceae (St. John's Wort Family): Can have numerous stamens, often fused into bundles (fascicles), and typically opposite leaves often with translucent dots. Fruit is usually a capsule.

Field Guide Quick Reference (Dilleniaceae)

Look For:

  • Woody habit (mostly)
  • Simple, alternate leaves (mostly)
  • Flowers with numerous stamens
  • Carpels mostly free
  • Persistent sepals (often)
  • Follicular fruits common

Key Variations:

  • Rough/sandpapery leaves (Curatella)
  • Corrugated leaves (Dillenia)
  • Yellow flowers common (Hibbertia, Dillenia)
  • Fruit enclosed by fleshy sepals (Dillenia)
  • Liana habit (Tetracera, some Hibbertia)

Notable Examples (from Dilleniaceae)

Here are some representative examples from the family Dilleniaceae, which constitutes the order Dilleniales:

Dillenia indica

Elephant Apple

A medium-sized tree native to Southeast Asia, known for its large, white, fragrant flowers and distinctive fruit. The fruit is an aggregate of carpels enclosed by thick, fleshy sepals, making it look like a large, green, hard ball. The leaves are large with prominent parallel veins. The acidic fruit pulp is used in curries, jams, and jellies in some regions.

Hibbertia scandens

Snake Vine, Guinea Gold Vine

A vigorous climbing or scrambling shrub native to eastern Australia. It is widely cultivated for its large, bright yellow, open flowers with numerous stamens. The leaves are simple, elliptical, and glossy green. It thrives in coastal areas and is often used as a groundcover or screen plant.

Curatella americana

Sandpaper Tree

A small, fire-resistant tree found in savannas and grasslands of the Neotropics. Its most distinctive feature is its large, ovate leaves that are extremely rough on both surfaces due to silica deposits, hence the common name. The rough leaves have traditional uses for polishing or sanding. It produces clusters of small white flowers followed by small capsules.

Tetracera species

Water Vines

This genus consists mainly of lianas found in tropical regions worldwide. Many species are known as 'water vines' because cutting the thick stems yields potable water. They often have rough leaves similar to Curatella and produce panicles of small white or yellowish flowers, typically followed by dehiscent follicles with arillate seeds.

Phylogeny and Classification

Dilleniales is an order of flowering plants within the eudicot clade. According to the Angiosperm Phylogeny Group system (APG IV, 2016), Dilleniales occupies a significant position as the sister group to the large and diverse Superrosids clade. This placement suggests it represents an early-diverging lineage within the core eudicots, after the separation of groups like Ranunculales, Proteales, Trochodendrales, and Buxales.

The order contains only one family, Dilleniaceae. Historically, the placement of Dilleniaceae has been debated, sometimes associated with Theales or Malvales, or considered part of a basal eudicot grade. However, molecular data strongly supports its isolated position as sister to the Superrosids. This relationship highlights certain primitive eudicot features found in Dilleniaceae (like apocarpy) alongside more derived traits.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Clade: Angiosperms (Flowering plants)
  • Clade: Eudicots
  • Clade: Core Eudicots
  • Order: Dilleniales
  • Family: Dilleniaceae

Sister Group Relationship

Dilleniales is the sister order to the Superrosids. The Superrosids include major orders such as:

  • Rosales (Rose, Elm, Fig families)
  • Fabales (Pea family)
  • Fagales (Beech, Oak families)
  • Malpighiales (Euphorbia, Willow, Violet families)
  • Brassicales (Mustard family)
  • Malvales (Mallow, Hibiscus families)
  • And many others...

Understanding Dilleniales helps provide context for the diversification of this massive Superrosid clade.

Evolutionary Significance

The Dilleniales order is evolutionarily important due to:

  • Phylogenetic Position: Its status as sister to the Superrosids makes it crucial for understanding trait evolution at the base of this major eudicot radiation.
  • Mix of Traits: It retains some potentially ancestral eudicot features like numerous stamens and often free carpels, while also showing specialized features within the family.
  • Biogeography: Its pantropical distribution with high diversity in Australasia provides insights into historical plant movements and diversification patterns.
  • Morphological Diversity: Despite being a single family, Dilleniaceae shows considerable variation in habit (trees, shrubs, lianas, herbs) and fruit morphology (follicles, capsules, fleshy fruits).
