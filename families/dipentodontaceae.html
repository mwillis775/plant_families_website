Dipentodontaceae

Dipentodon Family

Dipentodontaceae is a small family of shrubs and small trees comprising two genera, Dipentodon (East Asia) and Perrottetia (pantropical disjunct). They typically have simple, alternate, serrated leaves with stipules, and bear small, inconspicuous flowers in axillary clusters (cymes). The family belongs to the small rosid order Huerteales.

Overview

The Dipentodontaceae family includes only two genera, Dipentodon (monotypic, found in southern China and adjacent regions) and Perrottetia (about 15-20 species with a scattered pantropical distribution). These plants are typically understory shrubs or small trees found in moist forests, often at higher elevations.

Key characteristics include their alternate, simple leaves that usually have toothed margins and small stipules (often falling early). Flowers are small, typically 5-parted, greenish or yellowish, and borne in axillary cymes. A prominent nectar disc is usually present within the flower. The fruit is either a small capsule (Dipentodon) or a berry (Perrottetia). Phylogenetically, Dipentodontaceae is placed in the order Huerteales, a small order within the Fabid (Malvid) clade of Rosids, whose exact relationship to other major Rosid orders is still being refined.

The family is not of major economic importance but is significant for understanding phylogenetic relationships among the Rosid lineages.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Dipentodontaceae Merr.
  • Common Name: Dipentodon Family
  • Number of Genera: 2 (Dipentodon, Perrottetia)
  • Number of Species: Approximately 16-20
  • Distribution: Disjunct: E Asia (Dipentodon); Pantropical (Perrottetia - Americas, SE Asia, Malesia, Australasia, Pacific).
  • Evolutionary Group: Angiosperms - Eudicots - Rosids - Fabids - Huerteales
  • Habit: Shrubs or small trees.

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Plants are shrubs or small trees, typically evergreen or semi-deciduous.

Leaves

Leaves are arranged alternately (spirally) along the stem. They are simple, with margins usually serrated or crenated (toothed), rarely entire. Venation is pinnate. Small stipules are typically present at the base of the petiole, but they often fall off early (caducous), leaving small scars.

Inflorescence

Flowers are borne in axillary cymes, which are often branched and may appear panicle-like or umbel-like.

Flowers

Flowers are small, inconspicuous (often greenish, yellowish, or reddish), usually bisexual or sometimes unisexual (plants then dioecious or polygamo-dioecious). They are radially symmetrical (actinomorphic) and typically (4-)5-merous.

  • Calyx: Consists of (4-)5 sepals, fused at the base into a short tube, with persistent lobes.
  • Corolla: Consists of (4-)5 free petals, usually smaller than or equal in size to the sepals. Petals are sometimes absent (apetalous in Dipentodon).
  • Androecium: Contains (4-)5 fertile stamens that alternate with the petals (if present). Stamens arise from the rim of, or are inserted upon, a prominent, fleshy intrastaminal nectar disc. Filaments are short. Anthers open via longitudinal slits.
  • Gynoecium: Features a superior ovary (usually; sometimes half-inferior), composed of 2-3(-5) fused carpels. The ovary is typically unilocular (single chamber) with parietal placentation (ovules on the ovary wall), or sometimes partly 2-3 locular at the base with axile placentation. Styles are short or absent, with small capitate or lobed stigmas. The prominent nectar disc typically surrounds the base of the ovary. Each carpel usually has 1-2 ovules.

Fruits and Seeds

The fruit is either a small, ellipsoid, one-seeded capsule that opens by a single slit (Dipentodon), or a small, globose berry containing 1-4 seeds (Perrottetia). Seeds contain fleshy endosperm and a straight embryo.

Chemical Characteristics

Plants are known to contain tannins and ellagic acid.

Field Identification

Identifying Dipentodontaceae requires careful observation of vegetative and floral details, combined with geographic location:

Primary Identification Features

  • Shrub or small tree habit.
  • Alternate, simple, serrated/crenated leaves: Check leaf margins for teeth.
  • Small stipules or stipule scars: Look closely at the base of the petiole.
  • Small, (4-)5-merous flowers in axillary cymes: Inconspicuous flowers clustered in leaf axils.
  • Prominent nectar disc: Often visible within the small flower surrounding the ovary base.
  • Superior ovary (usually): Ovary positioned above sepal/petal/stamen attachment.
  • Fruit a small capsule or berry: Depending on the genus.
  • Specific geographic distribution: Either E Asia (Dipentodon) or pantropical disjunct (Perrottetia).

Secondary Identification Features

  • Petals absent (Dipentodon) or present (Perrottetia).
  • Flowers often greenish or yellowish.

Seasonal Identification Tips

Vegetative features are useful year-round:

  • Year-round: Habit, leaf arrangement (alternate), shape (simple), and margin (serrated) are key vegetative clues. Stipule scars may be visible.
  • Flowering Season (Varies): Small flowers in axillary cymes; look for the 5-merous pattern, nectar disc, and petal presence/absence.
  • Fruiting Season: Look for the small capsules (Dipentodon) or berries (Perrottetia).

Common Confusion Points

Distinguishing Dipentodontaceae from families with similar simple, alternate, serrated leaves:

  • Celastraceae (Bittersweet family): Often have opposite leaves (though some alternate), flowers usually have a prominent disc but stamens are often inserted on it or alternate with lobes, ovary structure differs, fruit often a capsule with brightly colored arillate seeds. Perrottetia was formerly placed here.
  • Rhamnaceae (Buckthorn family): Shrubs/trees/vines with often serrated leaves and stipules. Flowers small, 4-5 merous, but stamens are characteristically opposite the often hooded petals. Fruit usually a drupe or capsule.
  • Salicaceae (Willow family - broader sense): Includes many tropical trees/shrubs (formerly Flacourtiaceae p.p.) with alternate, simple, often serrated leaves and stipules. Flower structure varies greatly but often differs (e.g., unisexual, reduced perianth, different ovary structure).
  • Rosaceae (Rose family): Huge family, many shrubs/trees with alternate simple serrated leaves and stipules, but flowers typically have numerous stamens and often a hypanthium. Fruit types vary greatly.
  • Focus on the combination: Shrub/Tree + Alternate Simple Serrated Leaves + Stipules/Scars + Axillary Cymes + Small (4-)5-merous Flowers + Prominent Disc + Stamens Alternating with Petals (if present) + Superior Ovary + Capsule/Berry = Dipentodontaceae.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Shrub or small tree
  • Leaves: Alternate, Simple, Serrated/Crenated
  • Stipules present (or scars)
  • Inflorescence: Axillary cymes
  • Flowers: Small, (4-)5-merous
  • Petals: Present or Absent (Dipentodon)
  • Stamens: (4-)5, alternating with petals
  • Prominent nectar disc
  • Ovary: Superior (usually), 2-3(-5) carpels
  • Fruit: Capsule (Dipentodon) or Berry (Perrottetia)

Key Variations:

  • Petals present (Perrottetia) vs. absent (Dipentodon)
  • Fruit type (capsule vs. berry)
  • Distribution (E Asia vs. Pantropical)

Notable Examples

The family comprises only two genera:

Dipentodon sinicus

(No common name)

The sole species in its genus, found in southern China, NE India, N Myanmar, and N Vietnam. A shrub or small tree characterized by lacking petals and having a 1-seeded capsule fruit. Represents one part of the family's disjunct distribution.

Perrottetia sandwicensis

Olomea

Endemic to Hawaii, this species represents the pantropical genus Perrottetia. It is a shrub or small tree with serrated leaves, small flowers possessing petals, and small berry fruits. Other Perrottetia species are found in the Americas, Asia, and Australasia.

Phylogeny and Classification

Dipentodontaceae belongs to the small order Huerteales, which is placed within the Fabid (or Malvid) clade of the Rosids (Eudicots). The recognition of Huerteales as a distinct order is relatively recent, based primarily on molecular phylogenetic evidence that grouped Dipentodontaceae with several other small, previously misplaced families.

The order Huerteales includes only four families: Dipentodontaceae, Gerrardinaceae (Africa), Petenaeaceae (Central America), and Tapisciaceae (Asia, Neotropics). The exact phylogenetic position of Huerteales relative to other major Malvid orders (like Malvales, Brassicales, Sapindales, or the COM clade - Celastrales, Oxalidales, Malpighiales, or the Nitrogen-fixing clade - Rosales, Fagales, Cucurbitales, Fabales) is still an area of active research, but it represents a distinct evolutionary lineage within this large group.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Clade: Angiosperms (Flowering plants)
  • Clade: Eudicots
  • Clade: Rosids
  • Clade: Fabids (Malvids)
  • Order: Huerteales
  • Family: Dipentodontaceae

Related Families (Order Huerteales)

Dipentodontaceae belongs to the small Huerteales order along with only three other families:

  • Gerrardinaceae: Contains one genus (Gerrardina) with two species of shrubs or small trees in eastern and southern Africa.
  • Petenaeaceae: Contains one genus (Petenaea) with one species, a shrub or small tree from Central America (Mexico, Guatemala, Belize).
  • Tapisciaceae: Contains two genera (Huertea, Tapiscia) with about five species of trees found in Asia and the Neotropics.

Huerteales itself is related to other orders within the Fabid/Malvid clade of Rosids.

Evolutionary Significance

Dipentodontaceae and the order Huerteales are significant for several reasons:

  • Phylogenetic Relationships: Helps resolve the complex relationships among the major lineages within the Rosid clade, particularly the Malvids/Fabids.
  • Biogeography: The highly disjunct distributions of genera within Dipentodontaceae (Dipentodon in Asia, Perrottetia pantropical) and the order Huerteales as a whole point to ancient origins and complex dispersal or vicariance histories, likely dating back to Gondwanan or Laurasian connections.
  • Relictual Lineages: The small size and often restricted distributions of families within Huerteales suggest they may be relictual groups representing older evolutionary branches.
