Diplaziopsidaceae

(No widely accepted common name)

Diplaziopsidaceae is a small family of terrestrial ferns (order Polypodiales), primarily found in tropical Asia. Recently recognized as distinct, it includes medium to large ferns characterized by 1-pinnate fronds, netted venation, and elongated linear sori often paired back-to-back (diplazioid), distinguishing them from close relatives like Athyriaceae by stipe anatomy and venation patterns.

Overview

Diplaziopsidaceae is a small but distinct family of ferns, segregated relatively recently from the large Athyriaceae family based on molecular evidence and subtle but consistent morphological differences. The family primarily comprises the genus Diplaziopsis, found in moist, shaded forests and along streambanks in tropical and subtropical Asia. Some classifications also include the monotypic North American genus Homalosorus.

These ferns are typically medium to large, growing from stout, creeping rhizomes covered in prominent scales. Their fronds are characteristically 1-pinnate, bearing large, simple pinnae. While the sori arrangement—long, linear, and often paired back-to-back along veins (diplazioid)—closely resembles that of the genus Diplazium (Athyriaceae), Diplaziopsidaceae is distinguished by its consistently netted (anastomosing) vein pattern and a unique stipe structure featuring two large vascular bundles that do not unite upwards.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Diplaziopsidaceae X.C.Zhang & Christenh.
  • Common Name: (None widely accepted)
  • Number of Genera: 1-2 (Diplaziopsis, sometimes Homalosorus)
  • Number of Species: ~4-5
  • Distribution: Primarily tropical and subtropical Asia; Homalosorus in eastern North America.
  • Evolutionary Group: Ferns (Polypodiopsida) - Polypodiales - Eupolypods II

Key Characteristics

Note: Diplaziopsidaceae are characterized by 1-pinnate fronds with large pinnae, netted venation, diplazioid sori, and two large vascular bundles in the stipe.

Sporophyte (Dominant Phase) Structure

The visible fern plant is the diploid sporophyte:

  • Rhizome: Stout, creeping (short to long); densely covered with large, brown, non-clathrate scales.
  • Fronds (Leaves):
    • Size & Form: Medium to large; monomorphic.
    • Stipe (Petiole): Stout, often long, scaly at the base; vascular bundles are characteristically two, large, elongated (hippocampiform or C-shaped in cross-section) at the base, running parallel and not uniting distally (unlike Athyriaceae/Aspleniaceae).
    • Blade: Typically 1-pinnate; pinnae are large, simple (entire or shallowly lobed/toothed), lanceolate to oblong, often tapering to a point. Terminal pinna usually similar to lateral pinnae. Texture herbaceous to somewhat leathery.
    • Veins: Distinctively netted (anastomosing), especially near the costa and margin, forming regular rows of areoles. Main lateral veins usually distinct and running towards the margin.
    • Indument: Blades usually glabrous; scales confined to rhizome and stipe base.

Reproductive Structures (Sori)

Sori contain the sporangia and are located on the underside of fertile fronds:

  • Shape & Position: Sori are linear and elongated, borne along the smaller connecting veins within the areoles. They are frequently paired back-to-back along the same vein segment (diplazioid arrangement), appearing as double sori.
  • Indusium: Present, linear, conforming to the sorus shape, attached along the vein. Typically thin and membranous, sometimes fragile. Opens outwards (away from the vein it is attached to).

Sporangia and Spores

  • Sporangia: Typical leptosporangiate structure with a vertical annulus.
  • Spores: Monolete (bilateral, bean-shaped); perispore (outer layer) is often prominently winged or folded.

Gametophyte

The small, haploid gametophyte phase:

  • Form: Presumably develops as a typical green, heart-shaped prothallus (details may be limited).

Field Identification

Identifying Diplaziopsidaceae relies on recognizing the combination of 1-pinnate fronds with large pinnae, netted venation, and diplazioid sori. Stipe structure is also key but requires sectioning.

Primary Identification Features

  • Netted Venation: Carefully examine the underside of a pinna; veins clearly form a network (anastomose), especially near the edges.
  • Diplazioid Sori: Look for long, linear sori often arranged in back-to-back pairs along the veins.
  • 1-Pinnate Fronds with Large Pinnae: Fronds are simply pinnate with relatively large, undivided or shallowly lobed segments.
  • Stipe Vascular Bundles: A cross-section of the stipe base shows two large, C-shaped vascular bundles (requires cutting).

Secondary Identification Features

  • Stout Creeping Rhizome: Rhizome is robust and covered in large scales.
  • Habitat: Found in moist tropical/subtropical Asian forests (Diplaziopsis) or eastern North American woodlands (Homalosorus).

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Year-round: As largely tropical/subtropical ferns (or temperate deciduous for Homalosorus), fronds with sori are typically visible during the growing season.

Common Confusion Points

The main confusion is with the genus Diplazium in the Athyriaceae family:

  • Athyriaceae (Diplazium): Shares the diplazioid sorus arrangement. However, Diplazium species typically have free veins (not netted) and two smaller strap-like vascular bundles in the stipe that unite upwards into a U or V shape. Fronds in Diplazium are often more dissected (2-pinnate or more).
  • Aspleniaceae: Have linear sori, but usually single (not paired), veins are free, and stipe bundles are two smaller ones uniting upwards.
  • Thelypteridaceae: Some genera (like Thelypteris subg. Cyclosorus) have netted veins and sometimes elongated sori, but sori are not typically diplazioid, indument is often hairy, and stipe bundles are two smaller ones uniting upwards.
  • Dryopteridaceae / Blechnaceae: Differ greatly in sorus shape (round, costal chains), venation (usually free), and stipe bundles (multiple round).

Key differentiator: The combination of netted veins, diplazioid sori, 1-pinnate fronds, and two large, non-uniting stipe bundles distinguishes Diplaziopsidaceae, especially from Diplazium.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Veins: Netted / Anastomosing
  • Sori: Linear, often paired back-to-back (Diplazioid)
  • Fronds: 1-pinnate with large pinnae
  • Stipe Base: Two large C-shaped vascular bundles (non-uniting)
  • Rhizome: Stout, creeping, large scales
  • Habitat: Moist forests (Asia / E. North America)

Key Distinctions:

  • vs. Athyriaceae (Diplazium): Diplazium has free veins and two smaller stipe bundles uniting upwards.
  • vs. Aspleniaceae: Veins free, sori single linear, 2 smaller uniting stipe bundles.
  • vs. Thelypteridaceae: Often hairy, stipe bundles smaller/uniting, sorus details differ.
  • vs. Dryopteridaceae/Blechnaceae: Different sorus shape, venation, stipe bundles.

Notable Examples

This small family has few species, primarily in the genus Diplaziopsis.

Diplaziopsis javanica

(No common name)

A representative species from tropical Asia, showcasing the large 1-pinnate fronds, netted venation, and characteristic diplazioid sori of the family.

Homalosorus pycnocarpos

Glade Fern, Narrow-leaved Spleenwort

Found in eastern North America. Sometimes included in Diplaziopsidaceae due to molecular data and stipe structure, though traditionally placed in Athyriaceae or Aspleniaceae. It has 1-pinnate fronds and linear sori (not typically diplazioid) and free veins, making its placement complex.

Phylogeny and Classification

Diplaziopsidaceae is a recently established family within the Eupolypods II clade of the order Polypodiales. Its recognition stems from molecular phylogenetic analyses that showed the genus Diplaziopsis, previously placed within Athyriaceae (often near Diplazium due to similar sori), formed a distinct lineage. This molecular separation is supported by morphological characters like the unique stipe anatomy (two large, non-uniting vascular bundles) and the combination of netted venation with diplazioid sori.

The family is positioned within the large group of closely related families including Aspleniaceae, Athyriaceae, Thelypteridaceae, Cystopteridaceae, Woodsiaceae, and others. The North American genus Homalosorus is sometimes included based on some molecular analyses suggesting a close relationship, although it lacks the netted venation and consistently diplazioid sori of Diplaziopsis, making its placement tentative. The establishment of Diplaziopsidaceae exemplifies the ongoing refinement of fern classification based on integrating molecular and morphological data.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Division: Pteridophyta (or Polypodiophyta)
  • Class: Polypodiopsida
  • Order: Polypodiales
  • Family: Diplaziopsidaceae
  • (Part of the Eupolypods II clade)

Related Families

Diplaziopsidaceae is closely related to other families in the Eupolypods II clade, particularly:

  • Athyriaceae: The family from which Diplaziopsis was segregated. Differs mainly in venation (usually free) and stipe anatomy (two smaller bundles uniting upwards).
  • Aspleniaceae: Shares linear sori (though usually single) and is phylogenetically close, but differs in venation and stipe anatomy.
  • Rhachidosoraceae: Another small family recently segregated in this group.
  • Cystopteridaceae & Woodsiaceae: Also part of this related complex, but differ significantly in sorus shape, indusium type, and often habit.

Evolutionary Significance

Diplaziopsidaceae is significant for:

  • Phylogenetic Refinement: Represents a case study in using molecular data to resolve relationships within large fern families like Athyriaceae sensu lato.
  • Unique Character Combination: Showcases an unusual combination of diplazioid sori (like Diplazium), netted venation (unlike most Diplazium), and distinct stipe anatomy.
  • Biogeographic Pattern: Primarily an Asian lineage, potentially with a disjunct member in North America (Homalosorus), raising interesting biogeographical questions.
