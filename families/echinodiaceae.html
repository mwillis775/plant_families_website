Echinodiaceae

Prickly Moss Family (Informal)

Echinodiaceae is a family of generally robust, pleurocarpous mosses belonging to the order Hypnales. The core genus is Echinodium. These mosses are often characterized by their somewhat stiff, pinnately branched habit, strong single costa, papillose leaf cells, and leaves that can appear wrinkled (rugose) when dry. The family is primarily distributed in temperate Southern Hemisphere regions and Macaronesia.

Overview

The Echinodiaceae family, established by Brotherus, primarily includes the genus Echinodium (the inclusion of Sciadocladus is debated, often placed elsewhere). These are typically robust, somewhat stiff pleurocarpous mosses that form loose tufts or mats, often appearing dull green to yellowish-brown. The name Echinodium refers to a hedgehog, perhaps alluding to the stiff appearance or papillose leaves of some species.

Plants usually exhibit creeping primary stems from which erect or ascending secondary stems arise, these being often regularly pinnately or bipinnately branched, sometimes appearing somewhat frondose. Key microscopic features include the typically papillose leaf cells (usually with one papilla per cell, though variable) and a strong single costa. The leaves often become distinctively wrinkled or folded (rugose) when dry.

Echinodiaceae are predominantly found in humid, shaded habitats, often growing on rocks, cliffs, soil banks, or tree bases, frequently near streams or waterfalls. Their distribution is mainly in temperate regions of the Southern Hemisphere (Australasia, southern South America, South Africa) and also notably in Macaronesia (Atlantic islands). The family is placed within the large pleurocarpous order Hypnales.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Echinodiaceae Broth.
  • Common Name: Prickly Moss Family (Informal)
  • Number of Genera: Primarily 1 (Echinodium Jur.), others sometimes included.
  • Number of Species: Several species in Echinodium.
  • Distribution: Primarily Southern Hemisphere temperate regions and Macaronesia.
  • Evolutionary Group: Bryophytes - Mosses (Class Bryatae - Order Hypnales)

Key Characteristics (Moss Features)

Gametophyte (Leafy Plant)

The dominant stage. Plants robust, somewhat stiff, typically dull green to yellowish-brown, pleurocarpous, forming loose tufts or mats.

  • Stems: Creeping primary stems with erect or ascending, often stiff secondary stems. Secondary stems usually regularly pinnately or bipinnately branched, sometimes appearing frondose.
  • Leaves (Phyllids): Crowded, spreading when moist, often appressed and appearing wrinkled or folded (rugose) when dry. Typically ovate to ovate-lanceolate, concave. Apex usually acute to acuminate. Margins generally plane or slightly recurved, often serrated towards the apex.
  • Costa (Midrib): Single and strong, well-defined, usually ending well below the leaf apex.
  • Leaf Cells: Small, typically isodiametric to shortly rectangular or rhomboidal, usually thick-walled. Cells are characteristically papillose (bearing papillae, usually single over the cell lumen, but sometimes multipapillose or smooth in certain species/parts), contributing to the dull appearance. Alar cells generally not strongly differentiated.

Reproductive Structures (Gametophyte)

Antheridia and archegonia are borne in lateral buds on the stems or branches.

Sporophyte (Spore-Producing Structure)

Arises laterally from the gametophyte stem. Consists of:

  • Seta: Usually relatively long, smooth or sometimes papillose, often reddish or brownish.
  • Capsule (Sporangium): Typically inclined to horizontal, ovoid to cylindrical, often symmetric or slightly curved. Operculum usually conical or rostrate.

Peristome

Typically double and well-developed (hypnoid type). Consists of 16 outer exostome teeth and an inner endostome with segments and often cilia.

Field Identification

Identifying Echinodiaceae involves recognizing their robust, somewhat stiff habit and key leaf features:

Primary Identification Features

  • Robust, Stiff Habit: Plants often form somewhat rigid tufts or mats, typically pinnately branched.
  • Rugose Leaves (when dry): Leaves often appear distinctly wrinkled or transversely folded when dry.
  • Papillose Cells (Dull Look): Plants usually lack glossiness due to papillose leaf cells (microscope needed for confirmation, but contributes to dull appearance).
  • Strong Single Costa: Leaves have a prominent midrib ending below the apex (use hand lens).
  • Habitat and Location: Found in shaded, humid places, often on rocks or soil near water, primarily in Southern Hemisphere temperate regions and Macaronesia.

Common Confusion Points

  • Lembophyllaceae: Some genera are robust pleurocarps in similar regions, but often have smooth cells or different branching patterns.
  • Neckeraceae: Often grow on rocks/trees but are typically strongly complanate (flattened) and may have different costa or cell structures.
  • Brachytheciaceae: Includes many robust pleurocarps, but they usually have smooth leaf cells and often different leaf shapes or costa lengths. Some may be slightly plicate but usually not distinctly rugose like Echinodium.
  • Thuidiaceae/Leskeaceae: Also have papillose cells but typically differ in branching habit (often more regularly pinnate/fern-like in Thuidiaceae) and leaf shape.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Robust, somewhat stiff moss
  • Often pinnately branched
  • Leaves often rugose (wrinkled) when dry
  • Dull appearance (papillose cells)
  • Strong single costa ending below apex
  • Habitat: Shaded rocks/soil near water
  • S. Hemisphere / Macaronesia distribution

Key Variations:

  • Degree of branching and stiffness
  • Intensity of leaf rugosity
  • Papillosity details (uni- vs multi-)
  • (Variations exist among Echinodium species)

Notable Examples

The family is primarily represented by the genus Echinodium:

Echinodium hispidum

(No widely used common name)

A species found in New Zealand and possibly Australia. It showcases the family's robust, often pinnately branched habit, papillose cells, strong costa, and tendency for leaves to be rugose when dry. Often grows on shaded rock faces or tree bases.

Echinodium prolixum

(No widely used common name)

Another species from Australasia, similar in general appearance and habitat preference to E. hispidum, differing in subtle morphological details.

Echinodium renauldii

(No widely used common name)

A species primarily known from Macaronesia (e.g., Madeira, Azores). Its presence highlights the disjunct distribution pattern often seen in bryophytes, linking these Atlantic islands with Southern Hemisphere regions.

Phylogeny and Classification

Echinodiaceae is classified within the Class Bryatae (true mosses) and is placed in the large pleurocarpous order Hypnales. Its exact phylogenetic relationships within Hypnales have been subject to discussion, sometimes being associated with the Lembophyllaceae or Neckeraceae based on morphological similarities or specific molecular analyses.

The family represents a distinct lineage characterized by its robust habit, papillose cells, strong costa, and often rugose leaves. Its predominantly Southern Hemisphere and Macaronesian distribution suggests an interesting biogeographical history, potentially reflecting ancient connections or long-distance dispersal events.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Division: Bryophyta (Mosses)
  • Class: Bryatae (or Bryopsida)
  • Order: Hypnales
  • Family: Echinodiaceae

Related Families (within Order Hypnales)

Echinodiaceae shares ancestry with other families within the diverse Hypnales order, possibly including:

  • Lembophyllaceae: A family primarily of the Southern Hemisphere, containing robust pleurocarps, some superficially similar but often differing in cell structure (usually smooth).
  • Neckeraceae: Often epiphytic or epilithic with complanate leaves, but typically differ in costa and peristome details.
  • Brachytheciaceae: A large family, some members robust, but usually with smooth cells and different leaf shapes.
  • Ptychomniaceae / Braithwaiteaceae: Also robust pleurocarps, but distinguished by strongly plicate (not rugose) leaves.

Evolutionary Significance

Echinodiaceae represents a distinct lineage within the Hypnales, showcasing adaptations to humid, shaded environments primarily in temperate Southern Hemisphere regions and Macaronesia. Features like papillose cells and rugose leaves likely play roles in water management and survival in these habitats. Studying the family contributes to understanding the diversification of pleurocarpous mosses and their biogeographical patterns across oceanic and southern landmasses.
