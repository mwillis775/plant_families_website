Elaeagnaceae

Oleaster Family

The Elaeagnaceae, or Oleaster family, is a family of flowering plants in the order Rosales. It includes shrubs and small trees characterized by distinctive silvery or brown scales or stellate hairs covering their leaves and stems. Many species are notable for their ability to fix atmospheric nitrogen via root nodules containing actinobacteria (Frankia).

Overview

The Elaeagnaceae family comprises about 3 genera and 60-100 species, primarily distributed in temperate regions of the Northern Hemisphere, with extensions into tropical Asia and northern Australia. Members are often adapted to dry or coastal environments and are frequently thorny. The family is well-known for species like Russian olive (Elaeagnus angustifolia) and sea buckthorn (Hippophae rhamnoides).

A key ecological feature of many Elaeagnaceae is their symbiotic relationship with nitrogen-fixing bacteria (Frankia) in root nodules. This allows them to thrive in nutrient-poor soils and act as pioneer species in disturbed habitats. The characteristic silvery appearance is due to dense coverings of peltate (shield-shaped) or stellate (star-shaped) scales or hairs, which likely help reduce water loss and deter herbivores.

Several species are cultivated for ornamental purposes, erosion control, wildlife habitat, or their edible fruits, which are often rich in vitamins (e.g., sea buckthorn berries). However, some species, like Russian olive, have become invasive outside their native ranges.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Elaeagnaceae Juss.
  • Common Name: Oleaster Family
  • Number of Genera: Approximately 3 (Elaeagnus, Hippophae, Shepherdia)
  • Number of Species: Approximately 60-100
  • Distribution: Primarily temperate Northern Hemisphere, also tropical Asia, Australia
  • Evolutionary Group: Eudicots - Rosids - Rosales
  • Key Feature: Presence of silvery/brown peltate or stellate scales/hairs; nitrogen fixation via root nodules.

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Members of Elaeagnaceae are typically deciduous or evergreen shrubs or small trees. Many species are armed with thorns (modified branches). They exhibit actinorhizal symbiosis, forming root nodules with nitrogen-fixing Frankia bacteria.

Leaves

Leaves are simple, entire (untoothed margins), and arranged alternately or oppositely (e.g., in Shepherdia). They lack stipules. The most striking feature is the presence of dense silvery or brownish scales (peltate or stellate trichomes) covering the leaf surfaces, especially the underside, and often young stems and buds as well.

Inflorescence

Flowers are borne solitary or in small clusters (fascicles, racemes, or umbels) in the leaf axils. Inflorescences are generally inconspicuous.

Flowers

Flowers are actinomorphic (radially symmetrical), usually bisexual or unisexual (plants then often dioecious, e.g., Hippophae, Shepherdia). They are distinct in lacking petals (apetalous).

  • Perianth: Consists of a single whorl of typically 4 (sometimes up to 8) sepal-like lobes, fused below into a tubular or bell-shaped hypanthium. The sepals are often petaloid (colored).
  • Androecium: Stamens are equal in number to the sepal lobes (typically 4) or twice as many (typically 8), inserted on the hypanthium rim or throat. Filaments are free.
  • Gynoecium: A single, superior ovary (though appearing inferior due to the surrounding hypanthium) composed of one carpel containing a single basal ovule. The style is single and elongate, with a simple stigma.

Fruits and Seeds

The fruit is an achene or nutlet enclosed by the persistent, fleshy, and often brightly colored hypanthium, resulting in a drupe-like or berry-like structure. This fleshy part is derived from the floral tube, not the ovary wall. The single seed has little or no endosperm.

Chemical Characteristics

The family is known for producing various secondary metabolites, including flavonoids, carotenoids (especially in fruits like sea buckthorn), and alkaloids. The nitrogen-fixing symbiosis is a key physiological characteristic.

Field Identification

Identifying members of the Elaeagnaceae family in the field often relies on their distinctive surface coverings and habit:

Primary Identification Features

  • Scales/Hairs: Look for the characteristic silvery, gray, or brownish covering of tiny, shield-shaped (peltate) or star-shaped (stellate) scales or hairs on leaves (especially undersides), young stems, and buds. This often gives the plants a metallic sheen.
  • Habit: Shrubs or small trees, frequently thorny.
  • Leaves: Simple, entire leaves arranged alternately or oppositely.
  • Apetalous Flowers: Flowers lack true petals; the perianth consists only of sepals (usually 4) fused into a tube below.
  • Fruit Type: Berry-like or drupe-like fruits where the fleshy part is the persistent hypanthium surrounding the true achene fruit.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Nitrogen-Fixing Nodules: While not easily seen, knowledge that these plants fix nitrogen can be a clue if found in poor soils or pioneer habitats.
  • Flower Arrangement: Small clusters of flowers in leaf axils.
  • Dioecy: Some genera (Hippophae, Shepherdia) have separate male and female plants.

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Year-round: The distinctive scales/hairs on leaves (if evergreen) and stems are present year-round. Thorns are also a permanent feature.
  • Spring/Summer: Look for small, often fragrant, apetalous flowers in leaf axils.
  • Late Summer/Fall: Identify by the characteristic fleshy fruits (often red, orange, or silvery).

Common Confusion Points

The unique scales make Elaeagnaceae fairly distinct, but confusion might arise with:

  • Willows (Salix spp.): Some willows have silvery hairs on leaves but have catkins and different flower/fruit structures.
  • Olive (Olea europaea): True olives have opposite leaves often with silvery undersides (due to different types of hairs), but belong to the Oleaceae family with different flower and fruit structures (petals present, true drupe).
  • Other silvery-leaved shrubs: Various unrelated plants can have silvery foliage due to hairs (e.g., some Artemisia), but lack the combination of scales, simple entire leaves, apetalous flowers, and the specific fruit type of Elaeagnaceae.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Shrub or small tree habit
  • Silvery/brown scales or stellate hairs
  • Simple, entire leaves (alt/opp)
  • Often thorny
  • Apetalous flowers (usually 4 sepals)
  • Axillary flower clusters
  • Berry/drupe-like fruit (fleshy hypanthium)

Key Distinctions:

  • Scales vs. simple hairs
  • Absence of petals
  • Fruit structure (hypanthium vs. true drupe)
  • Nitrogen-fixing capability
  • Compare leaf arrangement (alt/opp)

Notable Examples

The Elaeagnaceae family includes several well-known species:

Elaeagnus angustifolia

Russian Olive

A thorny shrub or small tree native to western and central Asia. Widely planted for ornamental purposes and windbreaks but has become invasive in parts of North America. Recognizable by its silvery leaves, fragrant yellow flowers, and silvery olive-like fruits.

Hippophae rhamnoides

Sea Buckthorn

A thorny, dioecious shrub native to Europe and Asia, often found in coastal areas. Known for its narrow silvery leaves and clusters of bright orange, highly nutritious berries produced on female plants. Cultivated for fruit, soil stabilization, and traditional medicine.

Shepherdia argentea

Silver Buffaloberry

A thorny, dioecious shrub native to central and western North America. Characterized by opposite, silvery leaves and clusters of bright red, tart berries on female plants. Important wildlife food source and traditionally used by Native Americans.

Phylogeny and Classification

Elaeagnaceae belongs to the order Rosales, a large and diverse order within the rosid clade of eudicots. Molecular studies place Elaeagnaceae within a clade known as the "nitrogen-fixing clade" of Rosales, which also includes families like Rosaceae (partially), Rhamnaceae, and Ulmaceae, though nitrogen fixation is not universal across all these related families.

The family's closest relatives are likely the Rhamnaceae (Buckthorn family) and possibly Barbeyaceae. The distinctive morphology (scales, apetalous flowers, fruit type) and the consistent presence of actinorhizal nitrogen fixation clearly define Elaeagnaceae as a monophyletic group.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Clade: Angiosperms (Flowering plants)
  • Clade: Eudicots
  • Clade: Rosids
  • Order: Rosales
  • Family: Elaeagnaceae

Related Families

Within the order Rosales, Elaeagnaceae is related to several other major families:

  • Rhamnaceae (Buckthorn family): Shrubs and trees, often thorny, with simple leaves and small flowers (usually with petals). Fruits are typically drupes. Shares nitrogen fixation in some genera.
  • Rosaceae (Rose family): A huge and diverse family including roses, apples, cherries, etc. Typically have 5 petals, numerous stamens, and varied fruit types. Some subfamilies engage in nitrogen fixation.
  • Ulmaceae (Elm family): Trees and shrubs with simple, often asymmetrical leaves and wind-pollinated, reduced flowers. Fruits are typically samaras or nutlets. Part of the broader nitrogen-fixing clade.
  • Moraceae (Mulberry family): Includes figs and mulberries, often with milky latex and multiple fruits.
  • Cannabaceae (Hemp family): Includes hops and cannabis, often herbs or vines with lobed/compound leaves.

Evolutionary Significance

Elaeagnaceae is significant for several evolutionary reasons:

  • Nitrogen Fixation: Represents a key lineage within the Rosales that consistently utilizes actinorhizal symbiosis, playing a crucial role in nutrient cycling in ecosystems.
  • Adaptations to Stress: The scales/hairs are a notable adaptation to drought or saline conditions.
  • Convergent Evolution: The drupe-like fruit, formed from the hypanthium, is an example of convergent evolution towards fleshy fruits attractive to dispersers.
  • Biogeography: Its distribution pattern provides insights into the historical connections between continents in the Northern Hemisphere.
