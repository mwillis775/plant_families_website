Elatinaceae

Waterwort Family

The Elatinaceae, or Waterwort family, is a small family of flowering plants in the order Malpighiales. It consists of annual or perennial herbs or subshrubs, typically found in aquatic or damp terrestrial habitats worldwide, particularly in temperate and tropical regions. The family includes two genera, Elatine (waterworts) and Bergia.

Overview

The Elatinaceae family comprises approximately 2 genera and 35-50 species. These are generally small, often inconspicuous plants adapted to wet environments, such as the edges of ponds, streams, lakes, and seasonally flooded areas. Elatine species are typically aquatic, often forming mats, while Bergia species are more often found in damp terrestrial or semi-aquatic habitats and can be somewhat woodier at the base.

The family has a cosmopolitan distribution but is most diverse in temperate and tropical zones. Despite their small size, they can sometimes form extensive populations in suitable habitats. They are characterized by simple, opposite or whorled leaves with small stipules, tiny flowers borne in the leaf axils, and capsular fruits that split along the septa.

Economically, the family is of minor importance, although some species are occasionally encountered in the aquarium trade or studied for their ecological roles in wetland ecosystems. Their small size and specific habitat requirements make them interesting subjects for ecological and evolutionary studies related to aquatic plant adaptations.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Elatinaceae Dumort.
  • Common Name: Waterwort Family
  • Number of Genera: 2 (Elatine, Bergia)
  • Number of Species: Approximately 35-50
  • Distribution: Cosmopolitan, especially temperate and tropical wetlands
  • Evolutionary Group: Eudicots - Rosids - Malpighiales
  • Key Feature: Small aquatic or wetland herbs/subshrubs; opposite/whorled leaves with stipules; tiny axillary flowers (2-5 parts); septicidal capsule.

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Members of Elatinaceae are annual or perennial herbs or subshrubs. Elatine species are typically small, creeping or mat-forming aquatic plants, often rooting at the nodes. Bergia species are more erect or sprawling, sometimes slightly woody at the base, inhabiting damp soil or shallow water.

Leaves

Leaves are simple, usually small, and arranged oppositely or in whorls along the stem. Leaf margins are typically entire (untoothed) or sometimes finely toothed (serrulate). Small, paired, membranous or scale-like stipules are present at the base of the leaves.

Inflorescence

Flowers are very small and inconspicuous, borne solitary or in small clusters (cymes or fascicles) directly in the leaf axils.

Flowers

Flowers are actinomorphic (radially symmetrical), bisexual, and typically hypogynous (parts attached below the ovary).

  • Calyx: Consists of 2 to 5 sepals, which may be distinct (free) or slightly fused at the base. They are persistent in fruit.
  • Corolla: Consists of 2 to 5 distinct petals, usually similar in number to the sepals.
  • Androecium: Stamens are distinct, usually equal in number to the petals or twice as many. They are inserted at the base of the ovary or on a small receptacle.
  • Gynoecium: A superior ovary composed of 2 to 5 fused carpels, forming an equal number of locules. Placentation is axile, with numerous small ovules in each locule. Styles are distinct, equal in number to the carpels, with simple capitate (head-like) stigmas.

Fruits and Seeds

The fruit is a thin-walled capsule that is characteristically septicidal, meaning it splits open along the partitions (septa) between the locules. The capsule contains numerous very small seeds. Seeds are often slightly curved or straight, with a reticulated (net-like) or ribbed surface pattern, and lack endosperm or have very little.

Chemical Characteristics

Specific chemical profiles are not widely highlighted for the family. Some species may contain tannins or simple phenolic compounds, but they are not known for unique or potent secondary metabolites.

Field Identification

Identifying members of the Elatinaceae family requires close examination due to their small size, often in wet or submerged conditions:

Primary Identification Features

  • Habitat: Aquatic or damp terrestrial environments (pond margins, mudflats, shallow water).
  • Habit: Small, often creeping or mat-forming herbs (Elatine) or slightly larger, erect/sprawling herbs/subshrubs (Bergia).
  • Leaves: Small, simple, opposite or whorled leaves.
  • Stipules: Presence of small, paired, often membranous stipules at the leaf bases (may require magnification).
  • Flowers: Tiny, inconspicuous flowers borne directly in the leaf axils, with parts typically in 2s, 3s, 4s, or 5s.
  • Fruit: Small, globose or elongated capsule that splits along the septa (septicidal dehiscence).

Secondary Identification Features

  • Flower Structure: Number of sepals, petals, stamens, and carpels (often consistent within a species).
  • Seed Surface: Sculpturing patterns on the tiny seeds (visible under magnification) are important for species identification.
  • Glands: Some species may have glandular hairs.

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Growing Season: Identification is easiest during the active growing season (spring/summer/fall depending on climate) when flowers and fruits are present.
  • Submerged Forms: Aquatic Elatine species can look different when fully submerged versus emergent, sometimes having longer internodes or altered leaf shapes.
  • Dry Conditions: Some species survive dry periods as seeds or dormant fragments. Look for them when habitats rehydrate.

Common Confusion Points

Small aquatic or wetland herbs can be easily confused:

  • Callitrichaceae (Water-starwort family - now in Plantaginaceae): Aquatic herbs with opposite leaves, but lack stipules and have highly reduced, unisexual flowers.
  • Lythraceae (Loosestrife family): Some small wetland species (e.g., Rotala, Ammannia) have opposite leaves and axillary flowers, but typically have a distinct hypanthium (floral tube) and different fruit dehiscence (often irregularly splitting or circumscissile).
  • Caryophyllaceae (Pink family): Some small species (e.g., Sagina - Pearlworts) can resemble Elatinaceae but usually have notched petals (if present) and free-central placentation in a capsule opening by teeth.
  • Crassulaceae (Stonecrop family): Some small succulent species might grow in damp places, but typically have fleshy leaves, often lack stipules, and have different floral structures (often parts in 5s with distinct carpels).

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Small aquatic/wetland herbs
  • Opposite or whorled simple leaves
  • Small paired stipules present
  • Tiny axillary flowers (2-5 parts)
  • Superior ovary
  • Septicidal capsule
  • Numerous tiny seeds

Key Distinctions:

  • Presence of stipules (vs. Callitriche)
  • Absence of hypanthium (vs. Lythraceae)
  • Septicidal capsule (vs. Caryophyllaceae)
  • Non-succulent leaves (usually vs. Crassulaceae)
  • Axile placentation

Notable Examples

While often overlooked, some species are widespread or representative of the two genera:

Elatine triandra

Three-stamen Waterwort

A widespread, small aquatic annual found in shallow water and mudflats across North America, Europe, and Asia. Characterized by its tiny size, opposite leaves, and flowers typically having 3 sepals, 3 petals, and 3 stamens.

Bergia texana

Texas Bergia

An annual herb found in moist, often sandy or disturbed soils in the central and southern United States and Mexico. More robust than most Elatine, with erect or decumbent stems, opposite leaves, and small whitish flowers typically with 5 parts.

Elatine minima

Small Waterwort

A very small, mat-forming aquatic annual found in sandy or peaty pond margins and shallow waters in eastern North America and parts of Europe. Distinguished by its minute size and flowers typically having 2 sepals, 2 petals, and 2 stamens.

Phylogeny and Classification

Elatinaceae is placed within the large and diverse order Malpighiales, part of the rosid clade of eudicots. Molecular phylogenetics confirms the monophyly of the family and places it within a large clade containing families such as Malpighiaceae, but its exact sister relationship is still debated, sometimes suggesting a close relationship with Malpighiaceae.

The order Malpighiales itself is exceptionally diverse, containing around 40 families and over 16,000 species, including well-known groups like violets (Violaceae), willows (Salicaceae), spurges (Euphorbiaceae), passion flowers (Passifloraceae), and mangroves (Rhizophoraceae). The placement of the small, herbaceous, aquatic/wetland family Elatinaceae within this largely tropical woody order highlights the broad range of morphological and ecological diversification within Malpighiales.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Clade: Angiosperms (Flowering plants)
  • Clade: Eudicots
  • Clade: Rosids
  • Order: Malpighiales
  • Family: Elatinaceae

Related Families

Within the vast order Malpighiales, Elatinaceae is related to numerous families, including:

  • Malpighiaceae: Mostly tropical trees, shrubs, and lianas, often with opposite leaves, stipules, distinctive stalked glands on the calyx, and winged fruits (samaras).
  • Violaceae (Violet family): Herbs, shrubs, or trees with often characteristic zygomorphic (bilaterally symmetrical) flowers (like violets) or actinomorphic flowers; fruit usually a capsule.
  • Euphorbiaceae (Spurge family): Extremely diverse family (herbs, shrubs, trees, succulents), often with milky latex, unisexual flowers (sometimes highly reduced in cyathia), and typically 3-lobed schizocarpic fruits.
  • Salicaceae (Willow family): Trees and shrubs (willows, poplars) typically with catkins, simple alternate leaves, and capsular fruits with comose (hairy) seeds. Includes former Flacourtiaceae members.
  • Passifloraceae (Passion flower family): Mostly vines with often elaborate, complex flowers and berry or capsular fruits. Includes passionflowers and adenias.
  • Rhizophoraceae (Mangrove family): Trees and shrubs, famously including mangroves adapted to saline coastal habitats, often with opposite leaves, stipules, and viviparous seedlings.

Evolutionary Significance

Elatinaceae, while small, is significant for understanding plant adaptations to aquatic environments within the context of the largely terrestrial Malpighiales order.

  • Aquatic Adaptation: Represents an independent evolution of aquatic or semi-aquatic habits within a major angiosperm order.
  • Morphological Simplicity: The small size and relatively simple floral structure may reflect adaptations to their specific niche or potentially paedomorphosis (retention of juvenile features).
  • Biogeography: Its cosmopolitan distribution suggests effective dispersal mechanisms (likely via water birds carrying seeds) or an ancient origin.
  • Phylogenetic Placement: Helps anchor phylogenetic reconstructions within the massive and complex Malpighiales order.
