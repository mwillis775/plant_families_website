Francoaceae

Francoa Family / Melianthus Family (broad sense)

The Francoaceae is a family of flowering plants in the order Geraniales, primarily found in the Southern Hemisphere. As circumscribed broadly under the APG system, it is quite diverse, including herbs, shrubs, and trees formerly placed in families like Melianthaceae, Greyiaceae, and Vivianiaceae. Characteristic features vary but often include stipulate leaves (simple or compound) and flowers with nectar discs.

Overview

The Francoaceae family, in its broad sense (sensu APG IV), unites several lineages previously treated as separate families. It now includes around 8-10 genera and 30-40 species, distributed mainly across South America (especially Chile), southern Africa, and Australasia (New Zealand, Tasmania). This broad circumscription reflects molecular phylogenetic evidence showing these groups form a single evolutionary lineage sister to the Geraniaceae.

The family encompasses considerable morphological diversity. It includes perennial herbs with basal rosettes and flowers on tall scapes (Francoa, Tetilla), small cushion-forming herbs or subshrubs (Viviania), distinctive shrubs or small trees with large pinnately compound leaves and often unusual, nectar-rich flowers (Melianthus, Bersama - formerly Melianthaceae), and shrubs or small trees with simple leaves and showy red flowers (Greyia - formerly Greyiaceae).

Several species are cultivated as ornamentals, such as Francoa sonchifolia (Bridal Wreath) for its elegant flower spikes, Melianthus major (Honey Bush) for its striking foliage and unusual flowers, and Greyia sutherlandii (Natal Bottlebrush) for its bright red flower clusters. Some Melianthus species are known for producing abundant nectar, attracting birds, while others (like Bersama) have uses in traditional medicine or provide timber.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Francoaceae A.Juss.
  • Common Name: Francoa Family / Melianthus Family (broad sense)
  • Number of Genera: Approximately 8-10 (e.g., Francoa, Tetilla, Melianthus, Bersama, Greyia, Viviania)
  • Number of Species: Approximately 30-40
  • Distribution: Primarily Southern Hemisphere (S. America, S. Africa, Australasia).
  • Evolutionary Group: Eudicots - Rosids - Geraniales
  • Key Feature: Diverse herbs, shrubs, trees; leaves simple or compound, often stipulate; flowers often 4-5 merous, sometimes zygomorphic; nectar disc common; fruit usually a capsule. Includes former Melianthaceae, Greyiaceae, Vivianiaceae.

Key Characteristics (Broad Sense)

Growth Form and Habit

Highly variable: perennial herbs (often with basal rosettes), subshrubs, shrubs, or small to medium-sized trees.

Leaves

Leaves are arranged alternately or oppositely, simple or pinnately compound (often large in Melianthus, Bersama). Leaf margins can be entire, lobed, or serrated. Stipules are usually present, sometimes large and leaf-like or intrapetiolar.

Inflorescence

Flowers are borne in various types of inflorescences, including terminal or axillary racemes, panicles, or sometimes solitary or in small clusters. Scapose inflorescences (flowers on a leafless stalk from a basal rosette) occur in Francoa and Tetilla.

Flowers

Flowers are typically bisexual (sometimes unisexual in Bersama), and can be actinomorphic (radially symmetrical, e.g., Francoa, Viviania) or distinctly zygomorphic (bilaterally symmetrical, e.g., Melianthus, Greyia). They are usually 4- or 5-merous.

  • Calyx: Consists of 4 or 5 sepals, distinct or fused at the base.
  • Corolla: Consists of 4 or 5 distinct petals (rarely absent), sometimes clawed, often unequal in zygomorphic flowers.
  • Nectar Disc: Often present, located inside or outside the stamens, sometimes ring-like or developed into distinct glands, occasionally unilateral (one-sided) in zygomorphic flowers (e.g., Melianthus).
  • Androecium: Stamens variable, typically 4-10 (sometimes more), often equal in number or twice the number of petals. Filaments can be distinct or fused at the base. Staminodes may be present.
  • Gynoecium: A superior ovary composed of typically 2-5 fused carpels, forming an equal number of locules. Placentation is usually axile. Styles are free or fused.

Fruits and Seeds

The fruit is most commonly a septicidal or loculicidal capsule. In some genera, it can be a schizocarp splitting into segments, or rarely berry-like. Seeds are variable, sometimes winged or hairy, usually containing endosperm.

Chemical Characteristics

Various compounds are found, including tannins, saponins, and sometimes cyanogenic glycosides or bufadienolides (in Melianthus).

Field Identification

Identifying members of the broadly defined Francoaceae requires recognizing the key features of its major constituent groups, combined with geographic location:

Primary Identification Features

  • Distribution: Primarily Southern Hemisphere (S. America, S. Africa, Australasia).
  • Habit Diversity: Includes herbs with basal rosettes, shrubs, and trees.
  • Leaves: Simple or pinnately compound, alternate or opposite, usually stipulate.
  • Flowers: Actinomorphic or zygomorphic, 4-5 merous, often with a prominent nectar disc.
  • Fruit: Typically a capsule.
  • Key Genera Signatures:
    • Francoa/Tetilla: Herbs with basal rosettes, flowers on scapes (S. America).
    • Melianthus/Bersama: Shrubs/trees with large pinnate leaves, often zygomorphic flowers with abundant nectar (S. Africa).
    • Greyia: Shrubs/small trees with simple, rounded leaves, showy red zygomorphic flowers (S. Africa).
    • Viviania: Small herbs/subshrubs with simple opposite leaves, actinomorphic flowers (S. America).

Secondary Identification Features

  • Stipules: Often present and sometimes conspicuous.
  • Nectar Disc: Often visible within the flower.
  • Flower Symmetry: Note whether flowers are regular or irregular.

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Flowering Season: Varies greatly by genus and location. Showy flowers of Melianthus, Greyia, and Francoa are key identifiers when present.
  • Fruiting Season: Capsular fruits develop after flowering.
  • Year-round: Leaf type (simple vs. compound), arrangement (alternate vs. opposite), presence/type of stipules, and overall habit are useful year-round clues, especially when combined with geographic range.

Common Confusion Points

Given the family's diversity, confusion can arise with various groups:

  • Geraniaceae (Geranium family): Sister family, mostly herbs, typically with lobed or compound leaves, actinomorphic or slightly zygomorphic flowers, distinctive schizocarpic fruit with elastic awns ('stork's bill').
  • Rosaceae (Rose family): Some shrubs/trees have similar leaves, but Rosaceae typically have numerous stamens and often a hypanthium.
  • Sapindaceae (Soapberry family): Many trees/shrubs with pinnately compound leaves, but flowers are often unisexual, smaller, zygomorphic with a different structure, and fruits are diverse (capsules, samaras, drupes, schizocarps).
  • Bignoniaceae (Trumpet Creeper family): Some shrubs/trees (though many vines) with opposite, compound leaves, but flowers are strongly zygomorphic, tubular, with fewer stamens (typically 4), and fruit is usually a long capsule with winged seeds.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For (General):

  • Herbs, shrubs, trees (often S. Hemisphere)
  • Leaves simple or pinnate, often stipulate
  • Flowers actinomorphic or zygomorphic
  • 4-5 sepals, 4-5 petals
  • Nectar disc often present
  • Stamens usually 4-10
  • Fruit usually a capsule

Key Group Signatures:

  • Basal rosette + scape (Francoa)
  • Large pinnate leaves + zygomorphic flowers (Melianthus)
  • Simple leaves + showy red flowers (Greyia)
  • Small herbs + opposite leaves (Viviania)
  • Compare fruit type (capsule vs. Geranium schizocarp)

Notable Examples

Examples representing the diversity within the broadly defined Francoaceae:

Francoa sonchifolia

Bridal Wreath

A perennial herb native to Chile, forming a basal rosette of lobed leaves. Produces tall, slender scapes bearing elegant racemes of small, pink or white, 4-petaled flowers. Cultivated as an ornamental.

Melianthus major

Honey Bush / Giant Honey Flower

A large shrub native to South Africa, known for its striking, large, pinnately compound, blue-green leaves with conspicuous stipules. Bears racemes of unusual, brownish-red, zygomorphic flowers that produce copious black nectar, attracting birds.

Greyia sutherlandii

Natal Bottlebrush / Beacon Bush

A shrub or small tree native to South Africa. Features simple, rounded, somewhat succulent leaves and produces spectacular dense racemes of bright scarlet-red, zygomorphic flowers with prominent stamens, attracting birds.

Phylogeny and Classification

Francoaceae belongs to the order Geraniales, within the rosid clade of eudicots. The current broad circumscription of Francoaceae is a result of molecular phylogenetic studies demonstrating that several small families (Francoaceae sensu stricto, Greyiaceae, Melianthaceae, Vivianiaceae, Ledocarpaceae, Rhynchothecaceae) form a single, well-supported monophyletic group.

This combined Francoaceae family is the sister group to the Geraniaceae (Geranium family). Together, Francoaceae and Geraniaceae constitute the entire order Geraniales. This placement highlights a close evolutionary relationship between the diverse Francoaceae and the more uniform Geraniaceae, despite their considerable differences in morphology and habit.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Clade: Angiosperms (Flowering plants)
  • Clade: Eudicots
  • Clade: Rosids
  • Order: Geraniales
  • Family: Francoaceae (sensu lato)

Related Families

Within the order Geraniales, Francoaceae's only relative is its sister family:

  • Geraniaceae (Geranium or Cranesbill family): Mostly herbs, some shrubs; often with palmately lobed or compound leaves; flowers typically actinomorphic or slightly zygomorphic, 5-merous; fruit a distinctive schizocarp with elastic awns.

The order Geraniales itself is part of the rosid clade, related to other major orders like:

  • Myrtales (e.g., Myrtaceae - Myrtle family, Onagraceae - Evening Primrose family, Lythraceae - Loosestrife family)
  • Crossosomatales (e.g., Staphyleaceae - Bladdernut family, Crossosomataceae)
  • And the large nitrogen-fixing clade including Fabales, Rosales, Fagales, Cucurbitales.

Evolutionary Significance

The broadly defined Francoaceae is significant for:

  • Resolving Phylogeny: Its current circumscription demonstrates how molecular data can unite morphologically disparate groups into a natural evolutionary lineage, resolving previous taxonomic uncertainty.
  • Sister Group Relationship: Its position as sister to Geraniaceae provides context for understanding the evolution of key traits within the Geraniales order.
  • Biogeography: The predominantly Southern Hemisphere distribution of the family's constituent lineages offers insights into Gondwanan biogeography and subsequent diversification.
  • Morphological Diversity: Encompasses a wide range of growth forms, leaf types, and floral symmetries, showcasing adaptive radiation within the lineage.
