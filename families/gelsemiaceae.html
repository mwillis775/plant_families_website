Gelsemiaceae

Jessamine Family

The Gelsemiaceae is a small family of flowering plants in the order Gentianales (Lamiid clade of Asterids), comprising lianas (woody vines) or shrubs. The family includes two or three genera found in tropical America, Southeast Asia, and Madagascar. Members are often characterized by opposite leaves with stipules, funnel-shaped flowers, and the presence of potent alkaloids.

Overview

The Gelsemiaceae family is small, containing only about 11-13 species in two main genera: Gelsemium (3 species of lianas) and Mostuea (around 8-10 species of shrubs or small lianas). A third genus, Pteleocarpa, is sometimes included here but occasionally placed in its own family or Boraginales. Gelsemium occurs in Southeast Asia and North/Central America, while Mostuea is found in tropical South America and Africa/Madagascar.

These plants are typically woody vines (lianas) or shrubs with opposite, simple leaves that possess stipules (often forming a line or sheath between the petioles). The flowers are generally 5-merous (parts in fives), actinomorphic (radially symmetrical), and feature fused petals (sympetalous corolla), often forming a funnel or trumpet shape. Heterostyly (different style and stamen lengths promoting cross-pollination) is common, especially in Gelsemium.

The family is well known for containing potent indole alkaloids, such as gelsemine in Gelsemium species. These alkaloids make the plants highly poisonous if ingested, but some have historical uses in traditional medicine or as sources for pharmacological research. Gelsemium sempervirens (Carolina or Yellow Jessamine) is also widely cultivated as an ornamental vine for its fragrant yellow flowers, despite its toxicity.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Gelsemiaceae Struwe & V.A.Albert
  • Common Name: Jessamine Family
  • Number of Genera: 2-3 (Gelsemium, Mostuea, sometimes Pteleocarpa)
  • Number of Species: Approximately 11-13
  • Distribution: Tropical America, SE Asia, Madagascar.
  • Evolutionary Group: Eudicots - Asterids - Lamiids - Gentianales
  • Key Feature: Lianas or shrubs; opposite leaves with interpetiolar stipules/line; sympetalous 5-merous flowers (often yellow/white); fruit a capsule or berry; often poisonous alkaloids.

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Members of Gelsemiaceae are typically woody vines (lianas) that climb by twining stems (Gelsemium) or shrubs/subshrubs (Mostuea).

Leaves

Leaves are simple, arranged oppositely (rarely whorled), with entire (untoothed) margins. Stipules are present, often small and connecting the bases of the opposite petioles (interpetiolar stipules) or forming a distinct line across the node.

Inflorescence

Flowers are borne solitary in leaf axils or, more commonly, in axillary or terminal cymes (branched clusters).

Flowers

Flowers are bisexual, actinomorphic (radially symmetrical), and typically 5-merous (rarely 4-merous). Heterostyly (distyly or tristyly) is common.

  • Calyx: Consists of 5 (or 4) distinct or basally fused sepals.
  • Corolla: Consists of 5 (or 4) petals fused together (sympetalous) to form a funnel-shaped, trumpet-shaped (salverform), or bell-shaped (campanulate) tube with spreading lobes. Corolla color is often yellow or white.
  • Androecium: Stamens are 5 (or 4), alternating with the corolla lobes and attached to the corolla tube (epipetalous). Filaments are distinct; anthers dehisce via longitudinal slits.
  • Gynoecium: A superior ovary composed of 2 fused carpels, forming a 2-locular ovary with axile placentation. Each locule contains several to numerous ovules. The style is single but typically branches into 2 or 4 stigmas.

Fruits and Seeds

The fruit is typically a septicidal capsule (splitting along the septa) that is often flattened (e.g., Gelsemium). In Mostuea, the fruit can be a berry. Seeds are often flattened and may possess wings or hairs, aiding in dispersal. Seeds contain endosperm.

Chemical Characteristics

The family is characterized by the presence of potent indole alkaloids, such as gelsemine, sempervirine, and related compounds, particularly in Gelsemium. These alkaloids are responsible for the plants' toxicity.

Field Identification

Identifying Gelsemiaceae involves recognizing their growth habit, leaf arrangement, stipules, and flower structure:

Primary Identification Features

  • Habit: Lianas (woody vines) or shrubs.
  • Leaves: Opposite, simple, entire leaves.
  • Stipules: Presence of interpetiolar stipules or a distinct line connecting the petiole bases across the node.
  • Flowers: 5-merous (usually), actinomorphic, with fused petals forming a funnel or trumpet shape (sympetalous corolla), often yellow or white. Heterostyly often present.
  • Fruit: Septicidal capsule (often flattened) or a berry.
  • Toxicity: Plants are known to be highly poisonous.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Flower Scent: Flowers of Gelsemium sempervirens are notably fragrant.
  • Habitat: Forests, thickets, riverbanks in tropical or warm-temperate regions.
  • Seed Morphology: Seeds often winged or hairy (requires mature fruit).

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Flowering Season: Varies by species and location (e.g., late winter/spring for G. sempervirens). Flowers are key for identification.
  • Fruiting Season: Capsules or berries develop after flowering.
  • Year-round: The combination of liana/shrub habit, opposite simple leaves, and interpetiolar stipules/line is useful year-round.

Common Confusion Points

Gelsemiaceae, especially the vining Gelsemium, can be confused with other vines or shrubs with opposite leaves:

  • Loganiaceae: Closely related family, also with opposite leaves and interpetiolar stipules/line, and often sympetalous flowers. Distinctions can be subtle and may involve ovary/fruit details (Loganiaceae often has septicidal capsules but structure can vary) or specific alkaloid chemistry.
  • Apocynaceae (Dogbane family, includes Asclepiadaceae): Large family, many vines/shrubs with opposite leaves, but typically possess milky latex (usually absent in Gelsemiaceae) and often have more complex floral structures (e.g., corona, pollinia in former Asclepiads) and fruit types (often follicles).
  • Rubiaceae (Coffee family): Also has opposite leaves and interpetiolar stipules, but typically has an inferior ovary (vs. superior in Gelsemiaceae) and different flower/fruit details.
  • Bignoniaceae (Trumpet Creeper family): Many vines with opposite leaves, but leaves are usually compound (vs. simple in Gelsemiaceae), and flowers are typically zygomorphic with fewer stamens (2 or 4).
  • Lonicera (Honeysuckles, Caprifoliaceae): Vines or shrubs with opposite leaves, but flowers are often zygomorphic, sympetalous with a different shape, and lack interpetiolar stipules (though leaf pairs may be fused).

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Liana (vine) or shrub
  • Opposite, simple, entire leaves
  • Interpetiolar stipules or line
  • Flowers 5-merous, sympetalous (fused petals)
  • Corolla funnel/trumpet-shaped (often yellow/white)
  • Stamens 5, attached to corolla
  • Superior ovary, 2 carpels
  • Fruit a capsule (often flat) or berry
  • Often poisonous

Key Distinctions:

  • Opposite leaves + stipules/line (vs. Bignoniaceae)
  • Superior ovary (vs. Rubiaceae)
  • Absence of milky latex (usually vs. Apocynaceae)
  • Simple leaves (vs. many Bignoniaceae)
  • Flower shape/structure (vs. Lonicera)
  • Compare carefully with Loganiaceae

Notable Examples

The family includes the well-known Jessamines and less common Mostuea species:

Gelsemium sempervirens

Carolina Jessamine / Yellow Jessamine / Evening Trumpetflower

A popular ornamental woody vine native to the southeastern and south-central United States and Central America. Known for its abundant, fragrant, bright yellow, funnel-shaped flowers in late winter/early spring. All parts are highly poisonous.

Gelsemium elegans

Graceful Jessamine / Heartbreak Grass

A climbing vine native to Southeast Asia and China. Similar to G. sempervirens with yellow, funnel-shaped flowers, but considered even more toxic due to high alkaloid content. Used historically as a poison and in some traditional medicines (with extreme caution).

Mostuea batesii

(No common name)

A representative species of the genus Mostuea, found in tropical Africa. Typically a shrub or small liana with smaller, often white or pale yellow flowers compared to Gelsemium. The fruit in Mostuea is often described as a berry rather than a dry capsule.

Phylogeny and Classification

Gelsemiaceae is classified within the order Gentianales, a major order belonging to the Lamiid clade of Asterids. This order is characterized by features like opposite leaves, sympetalous corollas, and often the presence of alkaloids and iridoids. It includes several large and well-known families.

The family Gelsemiaceae was segregated from the Loganiaceae based largely on molecular phylogenetic data, which showed that Loganiaceae in its traditional, broader sense was polyphyletic. Gelsemiaceae forms a distinct lineage within Gentianales, often placed near Loganiaceae sensu stricto and the large Apocynaceae family. Its exact sister relationship can sometimes vary depending on the dataset, but its position within the Gentianales is well-established.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Clade: Angiosperms (Flowering plants)
  • Clade: Eudicots
  • Clade: Asterids
  • Clade: Lamiids
  • Order: Gentianales
  • Family: Gelsemiaceae

Related Families

Within the order Gentianales, Gelsemiaceae is related to:

  • Loganiaceae: Family of trees, shrubs, lianas with opposite leaves and stipules; flowers often 4-5 merous; fruit usually a capsule or berry. Contains Strychnos (source of strychnine). Gelsemiaceae was formerly included here.
  • Apocynaceae (Dogbane family, includes Asclepiadaceae): Large family, often with milky latex, opposite leaves; flowers 5-merous, often complex; diverse fruits (follicles, drupes, berries). Includes milkweeds, oleander, periwinkle.
  • Rubiaceae (Coffee or Madder family): Very large family, characterized by opposite leaves with interpetiolar stipules, usually an inferior ovary, and sympetalous flowers. Includes coffee, quinine, gardenia.
  • Gentianaceae (Gentian family): Mostly herbs with opposite leaves; flowers often showy, 4-5 merous, sympetalous; superior ovary; fruit usually a capsule.

Evolutionary Significance

Gelsemiaceae is significant for:

  • Refining Gentianales Phylogeny: Its recognition as a distinct family helped clarify the evolutionary relationships within the Gentianales, particularly regarding the Loganiaceae complex.
  • Alkaloid Chemistry: Represents a distinct lineage within Gentianales known for producing potent indole alkaloids, contributing to the understanding of chemical evolution in plants.
  • Heterostyly: Provides examples of heterostyly, an important reproductive strategy for promoting outcrossing, within the Asterids.
  • Biogeography: The disjunct distribution of Gelsemium (SE Asia and Americas) suggests ancient origins and long-distance dispersal or vicariance events.
