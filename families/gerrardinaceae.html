Gerrardinaceae

Gerrardina Family

The Gerrardinaceae is a small family of flowering plants in the order Huerteales (Malvid clade of Rosids), containing only the single genus Gerrardina. It includes two species of shrubs or small trees native to eastern and southern Africa. They are characterized by alternate, serrated leaves with stipules and small, apetalous flowers.

Overview

The Gerrardinaceae family comprises just two species in the genus Gerrardina: G. foliosa (found widely in eastern and southern Africa) and G. eylesiana (restricted to a smaller area in south-central Africa). These plants are shrubs or small trees typically found in montane forests, forest margins, or rocky outcrops.

Historically, the placement of Gerrardina was uncertain; it was sometimes placed within Flacourtiaceae or even considered related to Rosaceae. However, molecular phylogenetic studies revealed its unique position, leading to the establishment of the family Gerrardinaceae within the small order Huerteales, part of the Malvid clade (one of the major groups within Rosids).

The plants have simple, alternate leaves with toothed margins and small stipules. Their flowers are small, lack petals, and are borne in axillary clusters. The fruit is a small, dry or slightly fleshy berry-like structure. The family has no significant economic importance but is of interest for understanding the phylogeny and diversification of the Malvid lineage.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Gerrardinaceae Alvarado & B.W. van Ee
  • Common Name: Gerrardina Family
  • Number of Genera: 1 (Gerrardina)
  • Number of Species: 2
  • Distribution: Eastern and Southern Africa.
  • Evolutionary Group: Eudicots - Rosids - Malvids - Huerteales
  • Key Feature: Shrubs/small trees; alternate, serrated leaves with stipules; small apetalous flowers in axillary clusters; fruit a small berry or capsule-like.

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Members of Gerrardinaceae are shrubs or small trees, often scrambling or somewhat sparsely branched.

Leaves

Leaves are simple, arranged alternately and spirally along the stems. They have serrated (toothed) margins and pinnate venation. Small stipules are present at the base of the petiole but often fall off early (caducous).

Inflorescence

Flowers are borne in small axillary clusters, typically cymes or fascicles.

Flowers

Flowers are small, actinomorphic (radially symmetrical), and possibly bisexual or functionally unisexual (reports vary). They are typically 4- or 5-merous.

  • Calyx: Consists of 4 or 5 sepals, fused at the base into a short tube or cup, persistent.
  • Corolla: Petals are absent (apetalous).
  • Androecium: Stamens are 4 or 5, alternating with the sepals, inserted on the rim of the calyx tube or a nectar disc. Filaments are distinct; anthers dehisce via longitudinal slits.
  • Nectar Disc: An annular (ring-like) nectar disc is typically present inside the stamens.
  • Gynoecium: A superior ovary composed of 2-3 (or possibly 4) fused carpels, forming a single locule with parietal placentation (ovules attached to the ovary wall) or sometimes appearing basal. Ovules are few (1-6). The style is single and short, with a slightly lobed or capitate stigma.

Fruits and Seeds

The fruit is a small, dry or slightly fleshy, indehiscent structure, often described as berry-like or a capsule containing usually only one seed. The persistent calyx often surrounds the base of the fruit. Seeds contain endosperm.

Chemical Characteristics

Limited information is available, but tannins are likely present.

Field Identification

Identifying Gerrardina species involves recognizing their specific geographic range, habit, and combination of leaf and floral features:

Primary Identification Features

  • Distribution: Montane forests or rocky areas in Eastern and Southern Africa.
  • Habit: Shrubs or small trees.
  • Leaves: Alternate, simple, serrated leaves.
  • Stipules: Small stipules present (may need to look closely at young nodes).
  • Flowers: Small, apetalous (no petals), 4-5 merous flowers borne in small axillary clusters.
  • Fruit: Small, berry-like or capsule-like fruit, often with persistent calyx at the base.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Flower Color: Flowers are typically greenish, yellowish, or whitish (lacking colorful petals).
  • Stamen Number: Usually 4 or 5 stamens.
  • Habitat: Often associated with forest margins, stream banks, or rocky slopes at higher elevations.

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Flowering/Fruiting Season: Varies with location and altitude, often during warmer, wetter periods. Flowers and fruits are small and may require close inspection.
  • Year-round: The combination of shrub/tree habit, alternate serrated leaves, and presence of stipules (or stipule scars) is useful year-round within its specific range.

Common Confusion Points

Distinguishing Gerrardina from other shrubs/trees with similar leaves in Africa:

  • Celastraceae (Staff Vine family): Often have simple, alternate or opposite, serrated leaves, but flowers typically possess petals and a prominent nectar disc, and fruits are often capsules with brightly colored arillate seeds.
  • Rhamnaceae (Buckthorn family): Shrubs/trees often with simple, alternate, serrated leaves and stipules, but flowers usually have petals (often small and hooding the stamens) and the fruit is typically a drupe.
  • Salicaceae (Willow family - broad sense, including former Flacourtiaceae): A diverse family, some members have alternate, serrated leaves and stipules. Flowers can be apetalous but often unisexual and arranged differently (e.g., catkins in Salix). Fruit is usually a capsule or berry, but floral details differ.
  • Rosaceae: Some genera have similar leaves, but typically have 5 petals and numerous stamens, and often a hypanthium.

The combination of alternate serrated leaves WITH stipules AND apetalous flowers with 4-5 stamens and a superior ovary is key for Gerrardinaceae.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Shrub/small tree (E/S Africa)
  • Alternate, simple, serrated leaves
  • Small stipules present (may fall early)
  • Small flowers in axillary clusters
  • Apetalous (no petals)
  • 4-5 sepals, 4-5 stamens
  • Superior ovary
  • Fruit small, berry/capsule-like

Key Distinctions:

  • Apetalous flowers (vs. Celastraceae, Rhamnaceae)
  • Alternate leaves (vs. many opposite-leaved families)
  • Stipules present (vs. some families)
  • Stamen number (4-5 vs. numerous in Rosaceae)
  • Specific African distribution

Notable Examples

The family contains only two recognized species in the genus Gerrardina:

Gerrardina foliosa

(No common name)

The more widespread species, occurring from Tanzania south to South Africa. A shrub or small tree found in montane forests and scrub, characterized by its alternate, serrated leaves and small, apetalous flowers.

Gerrardina eylesiana

(No common name)

Found in a more restricted area of south-central Africa (e.g., Zimbabwe, Malawi). Very similar to G. foliosa, differing in subtle details of leaf shape, hairiness, or floral measurements.

Phylogeny and Classification

Gerrardinaceae is classified within the small order Huerteales, which belongs to the Malvid clade (one of the two major clades within Rosids). The placement of Gerrardina was problematic for a long time until molecular data revealed its affinities with other families now grouped in Huerteales.

The order Huerteales is relatively small and includes only three other families besides Gerrardinaceae: Tapisciaceae (two genera in Asia and South America), Dipentodontaceae (two genera in Asia), and Petenaeaceae (one monotypic genus in Central America). Huerteales represents one of the lineages within the diverse Malvid clade, which also includes major orders like Malvales, Brassicales, and Sapindales.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Clade: Angiosperms (Flowering plants)
  • Clade: Eudicots
  • Clade: Rosids
  • Clade: Malvids
  • Order: Huerteales
  • Family: Gerrardinaceae

Related Families

Within the order Huerteales, Gerrardinaceae is related to:

  • Tapisciaceae: Contains two genera (Tapiscia, Huertea) of trees found in Asia and South America, often with compound leaves and small flowers.
  • Dipentodontaceae: Contains two genera (Dipentodon, Perrottetia) of trees and shrubs found primarily in Asia, with small flowers and often capsular or berry-like fruits.
  • Petenaeaceae: Monotypic family containing Petenaea cordata, a shrub or small tree from Central America with distinctive reddish flowers/inflorescences.

The order Huerteales itself is part of the Malvid clade, related to other major orders such as:

  • Malvales (e.g., Malvaceae - Mallow family, Dipterocarpaceae)
  • Brassicales (e.g., Brassicaceae - Mustard family, Caricaceae - Papaya family)
  • Sapindales (e.g., Sapindaceae - Soapberry family, Rutaceae - Citrus family, Anacardiaceae - Cashew family)

Evolutionary Significance

Gerrardinaceae is significant for:

  • Resolving Malvid Phylogeny: Its placement helped clarify the composition and relationships within the Huerteales order and the broader Malvid clade.
  • Biogeography: Its African distribution, alongside related families primarily in Asia and the Americas, contributes to understanding the complex historical biogeography of the Malvids.
  • Morphological Simplicity: The relatively simple, apetalous flowers may represent either an ancestral condition or a derived reduction within its lineage.
  • Endemism: Represents a small, geographically restricted lineage within a major angiosperm clade.
