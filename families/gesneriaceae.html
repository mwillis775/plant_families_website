Gesneriaceae

Gesneriad Family / African Violet Family

The Gesneriaceae is a large family of flowering plants in the order Lamiales (Lamiid clade of Asterids), comprising mostly herbs and shrubs, many of which are epiphytic (growing on other plants) or lithophytic (growing on rocks). The family is renowned for its numerous ornamental species, including African Violets, Gloxinias, and Lipstick Plants, known for their often showy, zygomorphic flowers.

Overview

The Gesneriaceae family is substantial, containing around 150 genera and approximately 3500-3800 species. It has a primarily tropical and subtropical distribution worldwide, with centers of diversity in South America, Southeast Asia, and Africa/Madagascar. Members inhabit a wide range of environments, but are particularly common in moist, shaded habitats like rainforest understories, cloud forests, and rocky slopes, often growing as epiphytes or on rocks.

Gesneriads exhibit diverse growth forms, including annual and perennial herbs (often with rhizomes, tubers, or stolons), subshrubs, shrubs, and rarely small trees or lianas. A common characteristic is the presence of opposite leaves that are often soft, hairy, and sometimes unequal in size within a pair (anisophylly). The flowers are typically bisexual, strongly zygomorphic (bilaterally symmetrical), and often brightly colored and showy, adapted for pollination by insects, birds, or bats. The corolla is sympetalous (fused petals), frequently forming tubes or two-lipped structures.

This family is exceptionally important horticulturally, providing numerous popular houseplants and greenhouse ornamentals appreciated for their attractive foliage and long blooming periods. Examples include African Violets (Streptocarpus sect. Saintpaulia), Gloxinias (Sinningia speciosa), Cape Primroses (other Streptocarpus species), Flame Violets (Episcia), Lipstick Plants (Aeschynanthus), and Goldfish Plants (Columnea, Nematanthus).

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Members of Gesneriaceae are predominantly perennial herbs or subshrubs, sometimes annual herbs, shrubs, lianas, or rarely small trees. Many species possess rhizomes or tubers for storage. Epiphytic or lithophytic habits are common.

Leaves

Leaves are usually simple (rarely compound), typically arranged oppositely (less often alternate or whorled). They are often soft-textured and hairy (pubescent). A notable feature in many species is anisophylly, where the two leaves of an opposite pair are unequal in size, sometimes dramatically so. Stipules are absent (exstipulate).

Inflorescence

Flowers are borne solitary in leaf axils or, more commonly, in axillary or terminal cymes (often appearing paired in the axils). Sometimes flowers are arranged in racemes or panicles.

Flowers

Flowers are typically bisexual and usually strongly zygomorphic (bilaterally symmetrical), although nearly actinomorphic flowers occur in some basal lineages. They are typically 5-merous.

  • Calyx: Consists of 5 sepals, distinct or fused at the base.
  • Corolla: Consists of 5 petals fused together (sympetalous) into various shapes, commonly tubular, funnel-shaped, bell-shaped, or distinctly two-lipped (bilabiate). Colors are often bright and varied (red, orange, yellow, pink, purple, blue, white).
  • Androecium: Stamens are typically reduced in number compared to petals, usually 4 (often didynamous: two long and two short) or sometimes only 2 fertile stamens. A fifth stamen is often present as a sterile staminode. Stamens are epipetalous (attached to the corolla tube), and the anthers are often coherent (sticking together) or connate (fused) in pairs.
  • Nectar Disc: An annular (ring-like) or one-sided nectar disc is usually present at the base of the ovary.
  • Gynoecium: The ovary is typically superior (sometimes half-inferior or fully inferior), composed of 2 fused carpels, usually forming a single locule with parietal placentation (ovules on the ovary wall), or sometimes 2 locules with axile placentation. The style is single with a stigma that is often capitate or bilobed.

Fruits and Seeds

The fruit is variable, most commonly a dry capsule that dehisces loculicidally or septicidally (sometimes irregularly). In many groups (especially Neotropical epiphytes), the fruit is a fleshy berry. Seeds are numerous and typically very small, often with little or no endosperm.

Chemical Characteristics

The family is not generally known for potent alkaloids or toxins (unlike some related Lamiales families), though various iridoids, flavonoids, and phenolic compounds are present.

Field Identification

Identifying Gesneriaceae often relies on recognizing their characteristic flowers, leaves, and habit:

Primary Identification Features

  • Habit: Often herbs or soft-wooded shrubs, frequently epiphytic or growing on rocks/cliffs in moist tropical/subtropical habitats.
  • Leaves: Usually opposite, simple, often hairy or velvety, sometimes unequal in size (anisophyllous), lacking stipules.
  • Flowers: Typically showy and zygomorphic (bilaterally symmetrical), often tubular or two-lipped, with fused petals (sympetalous).
  • Stamens: Usually 4 or 2 fertile stamens attached to the corolla tube, often with coherent anthers.
  • Ovary/Fruit: Superior or inferior ovary; fruit either a dry capsule (often elongated) or a fleshy berry.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Flower Color/Shape: Highly variable and often diagnostic for genera/species (e.g., spurred flowers, specific lip shapes).
  • Nectar Disc: Often visible at the base of the ovary.
  • Placentation: Parietal placentation common in the single-loculed ovary.
  • Habitat Specificity: Many species have specific requirements (e.g., limestone rocks, tree branches).

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Flowering Season: Many species flower over long periods, especially in cultivation or stable tropical climates. Flowers are the most diagnostic feature.
  • Fruiting Season: Capsules or berries follow flowering. The type of fruit can be important for identification.
  • Year-round: Vegetative features like opposite, often hairy leaves (check for anisophylly) and growth habit (epiphytic, terrestrial rosette, etc.) are useful clues.

Common Confusion Points

Gesneriaceae can be confused with several other families in the Lamiales order or with unrelated families having zygomorphic flowers:

  • Lamiaceae (Mint family): Also often have opposite leaves and zygomorphic, 2-lipped flowers. However, Lamiaceae typically have square stems, aromatic leaves, a gynobasic style (arising from base of ovary lobes), and fruit consisting of 4 nutlets.
  • Acanthaceae (Acanthus family): Often have opposite leaves and showy zygomorphic flowers, but frequently possess conspicuous bracts, characteristic pollen morphology, and explosively dehiscent capsules often with specialized seed stalks (retinacula).
  • Scrophulariaceae/Plantaginaceae/Orobanchaceae (Figwort/Plantain/Broomrape families - complex group): Many members have opposite leaves and zygomorphic flowers, but details of stamen number/arrangement, ovary structure, and fruit type differ. Some Orobanchaceae are parasitic.
  • Bignoniaceae (Trumpet Creeper family): Often have opposite leaves (usually compound) and large, showy, zygomorphic flowers, but typically have only 2 or 4 stamens and fruit is usually a long capsule with winged seeds.
  • Orchidaceae (Orchid family): Epiphytic monocots with highly specialized zygomorphic flowers, but floral structure (labellum, column, pollinia) is completely different, and leaves are typically alternate with parallel veins.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Herbs/shrubs (often epiphytic/lithophytic)
  • Opposite, simple, often hairy leaves
  • Anisophylly common (unequal leaf pairs)
  • No stipules
  • Showy zygomorphic flowers
  • Corolla sympetalous (fused petals), often tubular/2-lipped
  • Stamens 4 or 2, epipetalous
  • Fruit a capsule or berry

Key Distinctions:

  • No square stem/nutlets (vs. Lamiaceae)
  • No retinacula in capsule (vs. Acanthaceae)
  • Simple leaves (usually vs. Bignoniaceae)
  • Dicot features (vs. Orchidaceae)
  • Compare ovary/placentation/fruit details with other Lamiales

Notable Examples

The family is rich in popular horticultural plants:

Streptocarpus sect. Saintpaulia

African Violet

Native to mountains of Tanzania and Kenya. Extremely popular houseplants grown for their fuzzy rosette leaves and clusters of violet, pink, or white flowers. Formerly genus Saintpaulia, now included within Streptocarpus.

Sinningia speciosa

Gloxinia

Native to Brazil, grown from tubers. Cultivated varieties (Florist's Gloxinia) are popular potted plants known for their large, velvety leaves and large, bell-shaped, brightly colored flowers (purple, red, pink, white).

Aeschynanthus radicans

Lipstick Plant

A trailing epiphytic plant native to Southeast Asia. Cultivated in hanging baskets for its glossy leaves and clusters of bright red tubular flowers that emerge from darker, tubular calyces, resembling lipstick emerging from its tube.

Phylogeny and Classification

Gesneriaceae is a well-established family within the large order Lamiales, part of the Lamiid clade of Asterids. This order contains many familiar families characterized by generally opposite leaves, often zygomorphic flowers, and sympetalous corollas.

Within Lamiales, Gesneriaceae belongs to a core group that excludes the basal families like Oleaceae and Plocospermataceae. Its exact sister relationship has been debated, but it is often placed near families like Plantaginaceae, Scrophulariaceae, Calceolariaceae, and Lamiaceae itself, though relationships among these core Lamiales families can be complex and subject to ongoing research. Gesneriaceae is distinguished from many of these relatives by its frequent epiphytic habit, parietal placentation (common but not universal), and specific fruit types (capsules or berries).

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Clade: Angiosperms (Flowering plants)
  • Clade: Eudicots
  • Clade: Asterids
  • Clade: Lamiids

Related Families

Within the order Lamiales, Gesneriaceae is related to numerous families, including:

  • Lamiaceae (Mint family): Often aromatic herbs/shrubs with square stems, opposite leaves, zygomorphic flowers, gynobasic style, fruit of 4 nutlets.
  • Plantaginaceae (Plantain family - broad sense): Very diverse family including Plantago, Veronica, Antirrhinum (Snapdragons); highly variable morphology.
  • Scrophulariaceae (Figwort family - narrower sense): Herbs/shrubs often with zygomorphic flowers, 4-5 stamens, capsule fruit.
  • Bignoniaceae (Trumpet Creeper family): Often vines or trees with opposite, usually compound leaves; showy zygomorphic flowers; fruit usually a long capsule with winged seeds.
  • Acanthaceae (Acanthus family): Often herbs/shrubs with opposite leaves; zygomorphic flowers often with prominent bracts; specialized capsule dehiscence.
  • Verbenaceae (Vervain family): Herbs, shrubs, trees with often opposite leaves; flowers often slightly zygomorphic; fruit typically a drupe or schizocarp.
  • Oleaceae (Olive family): Trees/shrubs with opposite leaves; flowers usually actinomorphic, 4-merous, with 2 stamens; diverse fruits. Represents a more basal lineage within Lamiales.

Evolutionary Significance

Gesneriaceae is significant for studying:

  • Adaptive Radiation: Shows remarkable diversification in growth forms (terrestrial, epiphytic, lithophytic), pollination syndromes (insect, bird, bat), and floral morphology, particularly in tropical mountain regions.
  • Evolution of Zygomorphy: Provides numerous examples of bilaterally symmetrical flowers, a key feature associated with specialized pollination in Lamiales.
  • Anisophylly: The frequent occurrence of unequal leaf pairs offers insights into developmental biology and adaptation to light conditions.
  • Horticultural Diversity: Represents a major source of ornamental houseplants, showcasing the results of natural diversity and artificial selection.
  • Biogeography: Its complex global distribution patterns reflect ancient connections, dispersal events, and regional radiations.
