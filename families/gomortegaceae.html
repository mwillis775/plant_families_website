Gomortegaceae

Queule Family

Gomortegaceae is a monotypic family of flowering plants, meaning it contains only a single genus and a single species, Gomortega keule. This rare, aromatic evergreen tree is endemic to a very restricted area in coastal central Chile. It belongs to the order Laurales, a group of relatively early-diverging angiosperms (Magnoliids). Due to habitat loss and over-exploitation, Gomortega keule is considered endangered.

Overview

The Gomortegaceae family is unique in containing just one species, Gomortega keule, commonly known as Queule or Hualhual. This evergreen tree is a relict species, representing an ancient lineage within the Laurales order. Its natural habitat is confined to the coastal Maulino forest and adjacent areas in central Chile, primarily between latitudes 35 degrees S and 38 degrees S.

Gomortega keule is recognized by its aromatic leaves and produces edible, fleshy yellow fruits. Historically, the wood was valued, and the fruits were used to make a type of alcoholic beverage (chicha de keule). However, extensive deforestation for agriculture and pine/eucalyptus plantations has severely fragmented its populations, leading to its endangered status.

As a phylogenetically isolated member of the Magnoliids, Gomortegaceae is of significant scientific interest for understanding the evolution of flowering plants. Conservation efforts are crucial to prevent the extinction of this unique family.

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Gomortega keule is an evergreen tree that can reach heights of up to 15-25 meters, although often found smaller. It has a relatively dense crown and grayish-brown bark.

Leaves

Leaves are simple, arranged oppositely on the stem, and lack stipules. They are leathery (coriaceous) in texture, typically elliptical or oblong in shape, with entire (smooth) margins. A key feature is that the leaves are aromatic when crushed, releasing a spicy scent due to essential oils.

Flowers

The flowers are relatively small (about 1 cm diameter), greenish-yellow, and borne in short, few-flowered racemes in the leaf axils. They are typically bisexual (perfect) and actinomorphic (radially symmetrical).

  • Perianth: Consists of usually 6-7 undifferentiated tepals (not clearly sepals and petals), which are fleshy and spirally arranged or in indistinct whorls.
  • Androecium: Stamens number between 7 and 13, are spirally arranged, and often some are sterile (staminodes). The anthers open via valves, typical of many Laurales.
  • Gynoecium: The ovary is inferior, composed of 2 or 3 fused carpels, containing a single locule (chamber) usually with one ovule (sometimes 2-3). There is a single simple style.

Wood Anatomy

The wood contains vessel elements, characteristic of angiosperms.

Fruits and Seeds

The fruit is a fleshy drupe, somewhat resembling a small plum. It is typically ovoid or globose, about 3-5 cm long, turning yellow when ripe. The flesh is edible but often described as bland or slightly sweet. It usually contains a single large seed (occasionally 2 or 3).

Chemical Characteristics

The presence of essential oils in tissues, particularly the leaves, is characteristic, contributing to the aromatic scent. This is common among families in the Laurales order.

Field Identification

Identifying Gomortega keule relies on its restricted geographic range (central Chile) combined with its distinctive characteristics as an aromatic evergreen tree with opposite leaves and unique flower/fruit features.

Primary Identification Features

  • Habit: Evergreen tree.
  • Leaves: Opposite, simple, entire, leathery, aromatic when crushed.
  • Flowers: Small, greenish-yellow, in axillary racemes; perianth of tepals; stamens spiral; inferior ovary.
  • Fruit: Fleshy, yellow drupe, about 3-5 cm long.
  • Distribution: Endemic to coastal central Chile.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Grayish-brown bark.
  • Lack of stipules at the base of leaves.
  • Habitat within remnants of the Maulino coastal forest.

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Year-round: The evergreen, opposite, aromatic leaves are always present.
  • Flowering: Occurs mainly in late spring to summer (November to January in the Southern Hemisphere).
  • Fruiting: Fruits mature in autumn and winter (March to June), turning yellow and becoming conspicuous.

Common Confusion Points

  • Lauraceae (e.g., Persea lingue): Some Chilean Lauraceae are evergreen trees with aromatic leaves, but they typically have alternate leaves and superior ovaries.
  • Myrtaceae (e.g., Luma apiculata): Myrtaceae often have opposite leaves and can be aromatic, but their flowers usually have numerous conspicuous stamens and distinct petals, and the fruit is typically a berry, not a large drupe.
  • Check for the combination of opposite aromatic leaves, inferior ovary in the flower, and the characteristic yellow drupe.

Field Guide Quick Reference

  • Opposite, simple, leathery, aromatic leaves
  • Small greenish-yellow flowers
  • Inferior ovary
  • Fleshy yellow drupe (3-5 cm)

Notable Examples

The family contains only one species.

Gomortega keule

Queule, Hualhual, Keule

The sole species in the family, this aromatic evergreen tree is endemic to the coastal cordillera of central Chile. It grows in moist ravines and forest remnants. Recognizable by its opposite, leathery, aromatic leaves and large, yellow, edible drupes. Its populations are fragmented and declining due to habitat destruction, making it an endangered species of high conservation priority.

Phylogeny and Classification

Gomortegaceae belongs to the order Laurales, which is part of the Magnoliids clade. Magnoliids represent one of the early diverging lineages of flowering plants (angiosperms), distinct from monocots and eudicots. The Laurales order includes other well-known families like Lauraceae (laurel family).

Within Laurales, Gomortegaceae occupies a somewhat isolated position. Molecular studies often place it as sister to a clade containing Atherospermataceae and Siparunaceae, or sometimes as sister to all other Laurales except Calycanthaceae. This isolated placement underscores its status as a relict lineage, preserving ancient traits and providing insights into the early evolution of the Laurales order and Magnoliids in general.

Related Families (within Laurales)

Gomortegaceae is related to other families within the Laurales order, including:

  • Lauraceae (Laurel Family): Large family including laurel, avocado, cinnamon; typically alternate aromatic leaves, superior ovary.
  • Monimiaceae: Diverse family, often with opposite leaves, variable flower structures.
  • Atherospermataceae: Southern Hemisphere trees/shrubs, often with opposite leaves, winged seeds in some.
  • Siparunaceae: Tropical trees/shrubs, often with opposite leaves, distinctive floral structures.
  • Calycanthaceae: Includes spicebush and wintersweet; often considered sister to the rest of Laurales.

Evolutionary Significance

Gomortegaceae holds significant evolutionary importance:

  • Relictual Lineage: As a monotypic family endemic to a small region, it represents a surviving branch of an ancient lineage within the Laurales.
  • Magnoliid Insights: Studying its characteristics helps illuminate the early evolution of flowering plants, particularly the Magnoliid clade.
  • Phylogenetic Placement: Its isolated position within Laurales helps resolve relationships within this complex order.
  • Conservation Imperative: Its rarity and endangered status highlight the importance of conserving unique evolutionary history.
