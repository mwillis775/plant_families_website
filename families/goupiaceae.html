Goupiaceae

Goupia Family

Goupiaceae is a small family of flowering plants containing a single genus, Goupia, with two species of large trees native to the Neotropics (northern South America and Central America). Known for their valuable timber and distinctive stipules, their precise evolutionary relationships within the Rosid clade remain somewhat uncertain, though they are often placed within the order Malpighiales.

Overview Characteristics Identification Examples Phylogeny

Overview

The Goupiaceae family includes only the genus Goupia, comprising two species: Goupia glabra (found widely in the Amazon and Guianas) and Goupia guatemalensis (found from Central America to Colombia). These are large, fast-growing canopy trees of tropical rainforests, often reaching heights of 30-50 meters.

They are recognized in the field by their alternate, simple leaves coupled with prominent, often reddish, pointed stipules that enclose the terminal bud and leave characteristic ring-like scars on the twigs after falling. The trees produce small, greenish-yellow flowers in dense clusters and small, berry-like fruits.

Goupia glabra, known by many local names including Kabukalli, Goupi, or Copiúba, produces a durable, dense, and somewhat siliceous hardwood that is commercially important for heavy construction, flooring, and decking, although it can be difficult to work. The phylogenetic placement of Goupiaceae has been debated, historically linked to Celastraceae, but molecular data suggests a position within the Rosids, often associated with Malpighiales or occasionally placed in its own order, Goupiales.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Goupiaceae
  • Common Name: Goupia Family
  • Number of Genera: 1 (Goupia)
  • Number of Species: 2
  • Distribution: Tropical northern South America and Central America.
  • Evolutionary Group: Angiosperms - Eudicots - Rosids - Malpighiales (placement somewhat uncertain)

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Large, deciduous or semi-deciduous trees, often reaching canopy or emergent status in tropical rainforests. They can develop buttresses at the base.

Leaves

Leaves are simple, alternate, and spirally arranged along the stem. The leaf blade is typically ovate to elliptical with an entire margin and pinnate venation. A highly distinctive feature is the presence of prominent, lanceolate (spear-shaped), often reddish stipules that tightly enclose the terminal bud and fall off as the leaf expands, leaving conspicuous annular (ring-like) scars around the twig at each node.

Flowers

Flowers are small (less than 1 cm), greenish-yellow, actinomorphic (radially symmetrical), and arranged in dense, umbel-like cymes that arise from leaf axils or near the ends of branches.

  • Calyx: Consists of 5 small sepals, fused at the base and persistent in fruit.
  • Corolla: Consists of 5 free petals, often with a noticeable reflexed (bent back) posture. The petals frequently have a small appendage or a tuft of hairs near the tip on the inner (adaxial) surface.
  • Androecium: Stamens are 5, alternating with the petals. The filaments are slender, and the anthers open via longitudinal slits.
  • Gynoecium: The ovary is superior, composed of 5 fused carpels forming a 5-locular ovary with several ovules per locule on axile placentas. There are 5 short styles which may be free or fused at the base.

Fruits and Seeds

The fruit is a small (about 5-7 mm diameter), globose, berry-like drupe. It ripens to a black or dark purple color and contains several small seeds embedded in pulp.

Wood Anatomy

The wood is known for being hard, heavy, durable, and often contains silica, making it somewhat difficult to saw but resistant to decay and marine borers.

Chemical Characteristics

Limited specific information is widely documented, but tannins are likely present, contributing to wood durability.

Field Identification

Identifying Goupia trees in their Neotropical forest habitat relies strongly on the distinctive vegetative features, especially the stipules.

Primary Identification Features

  • Habit: Large forest trees.
  • Leaves: Alternate, simple, entire.
  • Stipules: Conspicuous, pointed, often reddish stipules enclosing the terminal bud, leaving annular scars on the twigs after falling (key diagnostic feature).
  • Flowers: Small, 5-parted, greenish-yellow, in dense umbel-like clusters. Petals often with apical appendage/hairs.
  • Ovary: Superior.
  • Fruit: Small, black/purple, berry-like drupe.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Neotropical distribution (Amazon, Guianas, Central America).
  • Often reddish young leaves and stipules.
  • Hard, heavy wood (if examining timber).

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Stipules and Scars: The prominent stipules on buds and the resulting annular scars on twigs are visible year-round and are the most reliable identification feature.
  • Flowering/Fruiting: Occurs seasonally, varying by location within the tropics, often linked to dry or wet seasons.

Common Confusion Points

  • Other Trees with Stipules: Several tropical tree families have stipules (e.g., some Euphorbiaceae, Chrysobalanaceae, Rubiaceae), but the combination of alternate simple leaves with large, pointed stipules leaving complete ring scars is highly characteristic of Goupia.
  • Check flower details (5-parted, appendaged petals, superior ovary) and fruit type (small drupe) to confirm.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Large Neotropical trees
  • Alternate, simple, entire leaves
  • Prominent pointed stipules leaving ring scars
  • Small 5-parted flowers in clusters
  • Superior ovary
  • Small berry-like drupes

Key Variations:

  • (Between the two species, differences are subtle, often relating to leaf size/shape and flower details)
  • Stipule color (often reddish but variable)

Notable Examples

The family contains only two recognized species.

Goupia glabra

Kabukalli, Goupi, Copiúba, Stinkwood

A large canopy tree widespread in the rainforests of northern South America, including the Amazon basin and the Guianas. Highly valued for its hard, heavy, durable, reddish-brown timber used in construction. The wood sometimes has an unpleasant odor when freshly cut, hence the name "Stinkwood" in some regions.

Goupia guatemalensis

Pilon, Lacachon

Found from Guatemala and Belize south to Colombia and Ecuador, often in wet lowland forests. Very similar morphologically to G. glabra, and sometimes considered a subspecies or variety of it. Differences are subtle, often related to leaf and flower dimensions. Its wood is also used locally.

Phylogeny and Classification

Goupiaceae's placement within the broader classification of flowering plants has been historically uncertain. It was sometimes included in or near Celastraceae. Modern molecular phylogenetics firmly places it within the Rosids clade of eudicots.

Its position within the Rosids remains debated. Many recent studies place Goupiaceae within the large and diverse order Malpighiales, based on analyses of DNA sequence data. However, its exact relationships within Malpighiales are not always consistently resolved, and it lacks some common morphological features of other families in the order. Due to this uncertainty and its distinct characteristics, some classification systems propose placing it in its own separate order, Goupiales, potentially sister to Malpighiales or elsewhere within the Rosid phylogeny. For practical purposes, inclusion within Malpighiales is a common current treatment.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Clade: Tracheophytes (Vascular Plants)
  • Clade: Angiosperms (Flowering Plants)
  • Clade: Eudicots
  • Clade: Rosids
  • Order: Malpighiales (or Goupiales - placement uncertain)
  • Family: Goupiaceae

Related Families (within Malpighiales, if placed there)

If included in Malpighiales, Goupiaceae would be distantly related to numerous diverse families within this large order, such as:

  • Euphorbiaceae (Spurge Family): Highly diverse, often with latex, complex flower structures (cyathia in Euphorbia).
  • Malpighiaceae (Barbados Cherry Family): Often vines or shrubs with opposite leaves, distinctive winged fruits (samaras), and specialized floral glands.
  • Violaceae (Violet Family): Mostly herbs with zygomorphic flowers, familiar violets and pansies.
  • Passifloraceae (Passion Flower Family): Mostly vines with tendrils, complex and distinctive flowers.
  • Salicaceae (Willow Family): Includes willows and poplars, typically trees/shrubs with catkins.
  • Note: The relationship is not considered particularly close to any specific family within Malpighiales.

Evolutionary Significance

Goupiaceae represents an intriguing lineage within the Rosids:

  • Phylogenetic Enigma: Its uncertain placement highlights the challenges in resolving deep relationships within the vast Rosid clade and the limitations of relying solely on morphology or specific gene sequences.
  • Distinct Morphology: The unique combination of features, especially the prominent stipules leaving annular scars and the appendaged petals, sets it apart morphologically.
  • Rosid Diversification: Understanding its precise position is important for reconstructing the evolutionary history and diversification patterns within the Rosids.
