Guamatelaceae

Guamatela Family

Guamatelaceae is a monotypic family of flowering plants, containing only the single species Guamatela tuerckheimii. This rare scrambling shrub or liana is endemic to the cloud forests of Guatemala and Honduras in Central America. It belongs to the small order Crossosomatales within the Rosid clade and possesses a unique combination of features, including opposite trifoliate leaves with prominent stipules.

Overview

The Guamatelaceae family consists of a single species, Guamatela tuerckheimii, a woody plant that grows as a scrambling shrub or liana in moist, high-altitude cloud forest environments in Guatemala and Honduras. Its rarity and restricted distribution make it of conservation interest.

This plant is notable for its combination of opposite, compound leaves (trifoliate) with serrated edges and large, persistent stipules located between the leaf petioles (interpetiolar stipules). The flowers are relatively small, with white or pinkish petals, and importantly, the gynoecium is apocarpous, meaning it consists of several separate carpels (typically three) rather than fused carpels common in many related groups.

Guamatelaceae belongs to the order Crossosomatales, a small order within the Rosids that includes several other small, often geographically restricted families. Guamatelaceae itself appears to be an isolated lineage within this order, potentially representing an early diverging branch.

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Scrambling or climbing evergreen shrubs or lianas (woody vines).

Leaves

Leaves are arranged oppositely on the stem and are trifoliate (compound with three leaflets). The leaflets have serrated margins. A very distinctive feature is the presence of conspicuous, persistent, often leaf-like interpetiolar stipules (paired stipules located on the stem between the petioles of the opposite leaves).

Flowers

Flowers are relatively small, actinomorphic (radially symmetrical), bisexual, and borne either solitary or in few-flowered axillary cymes. A short hypanthium (floral cup) is present.

  • Sepals: 5 sepals, fused at the base, persistent in fruit.
  • Petals: 5 free petals, typically white or pinkish, longer than the sepals.
  • Androecium: Stamens number 10, usually arranged in two whorls of 5.
  • Gynoecium: The gynoecium is apocarpous, consisting of 3 (sometimes 4) separate, superior carpels situated within the hypanthium. Each carpel contains several ovules.

Fruits and Seeds

The fruit is an aggregate of dry, dehiscent fruits called follicles, with typically 3 follicles developing per flower (one from each carpel). Each follicle opens along one seam to release several small seeds.

Field Identification

Identifying Guamatela tuerckheimii relies on its specific geographic location (Central American cloud forests) and its unique combination of vegetative and floral characteristics.

Primary Identification Features

  • Habit: Scrambling shrub or liana.
  • Leaves: Opposite, trifoliate with serrated leaflets.
  • Stipules: Conspicuous, persistent, interpetiolar stipules.
  • Flowers: Small, 5 white/pink petals, 10 stamens.
  • Gynoecium: Apocarpous (3-4 separate carpels).
  • Fruit: Aggregate of follicles (usually 3).
  • Distribution: Endemic to cloud forests of Guatemala and Honduras.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Flowers borne in axillary cymes or solitary.
  • Persistent sepals on the fruit base.

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Year-round: The distinctive opposite, trifoliate leaves and prominent interpetiolar stipules are the best features for identification at any time.
  • Flowering/Fruiting: Occur seasonally within its cloud forest habitat; specific timing may vary.

Common Confusion Points

  • Other Vines/Shrubs with Opposite Compound Leaves: Some Bignoniaceae or Verbenaceae might have opposite compound leaves, but they lack the prominent interpetiolar stipules and typically have very different flower structures (zygomorphic, fused petals) and fruit types (capsules, drupes).
  • Rosaceae: Some Rosaceae have stipules and follicles or achenes (from apocarpous ovaries), but they usually have alternate leaves and often numerous stamens.
  • The combination of opposite trifoliate leaves, interpetiolar stipules, and apocarpous gynoecium is highly diagnostic for Guamatelaceae.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Scrambling shrub/liana (Central America)
  • Opposite, trifoliate leaves (serrated)
  • Prominent interpetiolar stipules
  • Small white/pink flowers, 5 petals, 10 stamens
  • 3(-4) separate carpels
  • Fruit = aggregate of follicles

Notable Examples

The family contains only one species.

Guamatela tuerckheimii

No widely accepted common name

The sole member of the Guamatelaceae family, this rare plant is found in high-altitude (1200-2500m) cloud forests in Guatemala and Honduras. It grows as a scrambling shrub or liana, recognized by its opposite trifoliate leaves with serrated edges and large interpetiolar stipules. Its isolated phylogenetic position and restricted range make it scientifically interesting and potentially vulnerable.

Phylogeny and Classification

Guamatelaceae belongs to the small order Crossosomatales, situated within the large Rosids clade of eudicot flowering plants. Crossosomatales is an order composed of several small families, many of which have restricted distributions and unique morphological features.

Within Crossosomatales, Guamatelaceae represents a distinct and isolated lineage. Molecular phylogenetic studies consistently place it as sister to the clade containing all other families in the order (Crossosomataceae, Stachyuraceae, Staphyleaceae, Aphloiaceae, Geissolomataceae, Strasburgeriaceae). This basal position suggests it diverged early in the evolutionary history of the order.

Related Families (within Crossosomatales)

Guamatelaceae is related to the other families within the order Crossosomatales, including:

  • Crossosomataceae: Small shrubs from arid regions of SW USA and Mexico.
  • Stachyuraceae: Single genus Stachyurus; shrubs/small trees from East Asia with flowers in drooping racemes.
  • Staphyleaceae (Bladdernut Family): Trees/shrubs with opposite, usually compound leaves and often inflated capsule fruits.
  • Aphloiaceae: Single species Aphloia theiformis; tree/shrub from Africa and Mascarene Islands.
  • Geissolomataceae: Single species Geissoloma marginatum; ericoid shrub endemic to South Africa.
  • Strasburgeriaceae: Single genus Strasburgeria (plus sometimes Ixerba); trees endemic to New Caledonia (and NZ if Ixerba included).

Evolutionary Significance

Guamatelaceae, despite being monotypic, holds evolutionary importance:

  • Basal Lineage in Crossosomatales: Its position as sister to the rest of the order provides crucial information about the ancestral traits and early diversification of Crossosomatales.
  • Rosid Relationships: Understanding Crossosomatales, including Guamatelaceae, helps refine our knowledge of the broader relationships within the vast Rosid clade.
  • Unique Trait Combination: It possesses an unusual mix of characteristics (opposite trifoliate leaves, interpetiolar stipules, apocarpy) not found together in closely related families.
  • Relictual Status: Its rarity and restricted distribution suggest it may be a relictual lineage, a survivor from an earlier period of diversification.
