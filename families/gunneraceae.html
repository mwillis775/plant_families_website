Gunneraceae

Giant Rhubarb Family

Gunneraceae is a unique family of flowering plants containing the single genus Gunnera. The family is renowned for its often gigantic perennial herbs bearing some of the largest leaves in the plant kingdom. Found primarily in moist environments across the Southern Hemisphere and tropical mountains, Gunnera species are also notable for their specialized symbiosis with nitrogen-fixing cyanobacteria (Nostoc). Gunneraceae belongs to the order Gunnerales.

Overview

The Gunneraceae family, encompassing only the genus Gunnera, stands out for the impressive scale of many of its species. Often referred to as "giant rhubarb" due to their massive leaves (though unrelated to true rhubarb), these perennial herbs can form imposing clumps in suitable habitats. Species range from giants with leaves spanning over 2 meters to smaller, mat-forming types.

A key biological feature of Gunneraceae is the intracellular symbiosis with nitrogen-fixing cyanobacteria (Nostoc species), housed within specialized glands on the stem near the leaf bases. This is the only known example of such an intimate cyanobacterial symbiosis within flowering plants, allowing Gunnera to thrive in nitrogen-poor soils.

Distributed across diverse regions like South America, New Zealand, Australia, Southeast Asia, Africa, Madagascar, and Hawaii, Gunnera typically prefers cool, moist, high-humidity environments such as streambanks, bogs, and cloud forests. Several large species are popular horticultural subjects, valued for their dramatic architectural foliage in large gardens and temperate landscapes.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Gunneraceae
  • Common Name: Giant Rhubarb family
  • Number of Genera: 1 (Gunnera)
  • Number of Species: Approximately 40-50 (some estimates up to 70)
  • Distribution: Disjunct: South America, Central America, Mexico, New Zealand, Australia (Tasmania), Pacific Islands (Hawaii, Juan Fernández), Southeast Asia, Africa, Madagascar.
  • Evolutionary Group: Core Eudicots - Order Gunnerales

Key Characteristics (of Gunnera)

Growth Form and Habit

Perennial herbs, ranging from gigantic species with massive rhizomes to small, creeping, mat-forming species. Stems possess unique mucilage-secreting glands near petiole bases that house symbiotic nitrogen-fixing cyanobacteria (Nostoc).

Leaves

Leaves are typically basal, simple, and often enormous (up to 2-3+ meters across in large species), borne on thick, long petioles. Leaf blades are usually palmately lobed or veined, sometimes nearly circular, often with a rough texture or prickles on veins and petioles. Stipules are present, often large and scale-like, protecting the apical buds.

Inflorescence

Inflorescences are large to massive, erect, typically densely branched spikes or panicles that arise directly from the rhizome or leaf axils. They bear numerous, densely packed, tiny flowers.

Flowers

Flowers are very small, inconspicuous, unisexual or bisexual (plants can be monoecious, dioecious, or have mixed flower types). They are adapted for wind pollination.

  • Perianth: Typically reduced, often with 2 scale-like sepals and 2 (or 0) small petals.
  • Androecium: Stamens usually 1 or 2, with short filaments.
  • Gynoecium: Ovary is inferior, composed of 2 fused carpels, containing a single ovule. There are typically 2 distinct styles, often reddish.

Fruits and Seeds

The fruit is a small, one-seeded drupe (fleshy) or achene/nutlet (dry), often brightly colored (red, orange, or yellowish) when mature. Fruits are typically clustered densely on the inflorescence axes.

Chemical Characteristics

Plants contain tannins and ellagic acid. The defining chemical and biological feature is the symbiosis with Nostoc cyanobacteria, which provides fixed nitrogen to the host plant. The glands produce mucilage which facilitates the cyanobacterial colonization.

Field Identification

Identifying members of the Gunneraceae family means identifying the genus Gunnera. Its features are often distinctive, especially in the larger species.

Primary Identification Features

  • Habit: Perennial herbs, often of enormous size (look for giant clumps).
  • Leaves: Very large (often >1m), basal, long-petiolate, palmately lobed or veined leaves.
  • Inflorescence: If present, look for a large, erect, highly branched structure arising from the base, covered in tiny flowers or small fruits.
  • Habitat: Typically found in very moist, cool environments like streambanks, seepages, or cloud forests.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Leaf/Petiole Surface: Often rough or prickly.
  • Symbiotic Glands: Small gland-like structures or associated mucilage may be visible at the very base of the petioles where they join the rhizome/stem (though often inconspicuous).
  • Fruit: Look for dense clusters of small, often brightly colored (red/orange) fleshy drupes on the large infructescence stalks.
  • Stipules: Large, scale-like stipules may be visible protecting the central growing point.

Common Confusion Points

  • Rhubarb (Rheum spp., Polygonaceae): Also has large leaves but possesses distinct, sheathing stipules (ochreae), flowers with 6 tepals and a superior ovary, and winged fruits (achenes). Lacks symbiotic glands.
  • Butterburs (Petasites spp., Asteraceae): Some species have large basal leaves, but they belong to the sunflower family and produce characteristic composite flower heads (capitula), often appearing before the leaves.
  • Sister Family (Myrothamnaceae): Completely different; Myrothamnus is a small, woody resurrection shrub from arid Africa/Madagascar. No risk of confusion in the field.

Field Guide Quick Reference (Gunnera)

Look For:

  • Herbaceous (often giant)
  • Massive, lobed basal leaves
  • Long, thick petioles (often rough)
  • Large, erect, branched inflorescence
  • Moist habitat
  • Small, colorful drupes (in fruit)

Key Variations:

  • Gigantic species (e.g., G. manicata)
  • Smaller, mat-forming species (e.g., G. magellanica)
  • Leaf size and prickliness varies
  • Inflorescence shape varies (spike vs. panicle)

Notable Examples (Gunnera species)

The single genus Gunnera shows considerable variation in size and form:

Gunnera manicata / G. tinctoria

Giant Rhubarb, Chilean Rhubarb

Native to South America (Brazil, Colombia, Chile). These are the archetypal giant species, cultivated worldwide for their immense, deeply lobed leaves reaching over 2m wide on stout, prickly petioles up to 2.5m long. They produce massive, cone-like inflorescences up to 1m tall. Require consistently moist soil and cool conditions.

Gunnera magellanica

Devil's Strawberry

Native to southern South America (Patagonia, Tierra del Fuego, Falkland Islands). A much smaller, creeping, mat-forming species. Its kidney-shaped leaves are only a few centimeters across, forming dense ground cover. Produces small inflorescences with bright red fruits resembling tiny strawberries, hence the common name. Tolerates colder conditions than the giant species.

Gunnera perpensa

River Pumpkin

Native to Africa (south of Ethiopia to South Africa) and Madagascar. A medium-sized species compared to the giants, with rounded or kidney-shaped leaves typically 15-30 cm across on long petioles. Forms clumps via rhizomes in marshy areas and along streams. Has various uses in traditional African medicine.

Phylogeny and Classification

Gunneraceae is one of two families within the order Gunnerales, a key lineage positioned near the base of the core eudicots. Molecular data strongly supports Gunneraceae (containing only Gunnera) as the sister group to Myrothamnaceae (containing only Myrothamnus). Together, these two families constitute the entire Gunnerales order.

The order Gunnerales itself holds the crucial phylogenetic position as sister to the Pentapetalae, the enormous clade comprising all other core eudicots (Superrosids, Superasterids, etc.). This placement makes Gunneraceae, as part of Gunnerales, vital for understanding the early evolutionary history and ancestral characteristics of the core eudicot lineage.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Clade: Angiosperms (Flowering plants)
  • Clade: Eudicots
  • Clade: Core Eudicots
  • Order: Gunnerales
  • Family: Gunneraceae

Related Groups

  • Sister Family: Myrothamnaceae (Resurrection Plant family) - Together they form the order Gunnerales.
  • Sister Clade to Order: Pentapetalae (Includes Superrosids, Superasterids, Dilleniales, Caryophyllales, etc.) - Gunnerales branched off before the diversification of this massive group.

Evolutionary Significance

The Gunneraceae family is evolutionarily significant primarily due to:

  • Basal Core Eudicot Placement (within Gunnerales): Helps reconstruct ancestral traits for core eudicots.
  • Unique Cyanobacterial Symbiosis: Represents a singular evolutionary event among angiosperms for intimate intracellular nitrogen-fixing symbiosis with Nostoc.
  • Gigantism: The evolution of massive herbaceous forms in Gunnera is remarkable and likely linked to their specific ecological niches and symbiotic relationship.
  • Biogeographic Patterns: The disjunct Southern Hemisphere distribution points to ancient origins and vicariance or long-distance dispersal events.
