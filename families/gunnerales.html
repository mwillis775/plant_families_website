Gunnerales

An Order Containing Giant Herbs and Resurrection Shrubs

Gunnerales is a small but remarkable order of flowering plants, occupying a key phylogenetic position near the base of the core eudicots. It comprises just two families: Gunneraceae, with the single genus Gunnera known for its gigantic herbaceous species often called 'giant rhubarb'; and Myrothamnaceae, with the single genus Myrothamnus, consisting of small woody shrubs famous for their ability to survive extreme desiccation ('resurrection plants'). Despite their vastly different appearances and ecologies, these two families are sister groups.

Overview

The order Gunnerales presents a fascinating study in contrasts. It includes only two genera, Gunnera (Gunneraceae) and Myrothamnus (Myrothamnaceae), which differ dramatically in size, habit, and habitat. Gunnera species are often massive perennial herbs with enormous leaves, thriving in moist, cool environments primarily in the Southern Hemisphere and tropical mountains. They are notable for hosting nitrogen-fixing cyanobacteria (Nostoc) within specialized stem glands, a unique symbiosis among angiosperms.

In stark contrast, Myrothamnus species are small, twiggy shrubs adapted to seasonally arid regions of southern Africa and Madagascar. They are classic examples of 'resurrection plants' (poikilohydric), capable of drying out completely during drought, appearing dead, but rapidly rehydrating and resuming photosynthesis when water becomes available. Both families feature highly reduced, wind-pollinated flowers.

The phylogenetic placement of Gunnerales as the sister group to the vast Pentapetalae clade (all other core eudicots) makes it exceptionally important for understanding the early evolution of this major angiosperm lineage. Studying Gunnerales provides insights into the ancestral traits and evolutionary pathways of core eudicots.

  • Scientific Name: Gunnerales (Order)
  • Families Included: Gunneraceae, Myrothamnaceae

Key Characteristics

Gunnerales exhibits extreme morphological divergence between its two families:

Growth Form and Habit

Gunneraceae (Gunnera): Mostly large to gigantic perennial herbs with thick rhizomes; some species are smaller, mat-forming. Stems possess unique glands housing symbiotic cyanobacteria (Nostoc).
Myrothamnaceae (Myrothamnus): Small, woody, dioecious shrubs with remarkable desiccation tolerance (resurrection capability).

Leaves

Gunneraceae (Gunnera): Leaves are basal, simple, very large (up to 2-3 meters across in some species), long-petiolate, typically palmately lobed or veined, often with rough surfaces or prickles. Stipules are often large, protecting buds.
Myrothamnaceae (Myrothamnus): Leaves are small (< 1.5 cm), opposite and decussate (arranged in pairs at right angles), fan-shaped (flabellate) with serrated tips, aromatic/resinous. They fold up and turn reddish-black when dry, rapidly rehydrating to green when wet.

Inflorescence

Gunneraceae (Gunnera): Inflorescences are typically massive, erect, branched spikes or panicles arising from the rhizome, bearing numerous tiny flowers.
Myrothamnaceae (Myrothamnus): Inflorescences are small, dense, terminal spikes or catkin-like structures.

Flowers

Flowers in both families are small, highly reduced, unisexual or bisexual, and adapted for wind pollination (anemophily).

  • Gunneraceae (Gunnera): Flowers often bisexual or unisexual (plants monoecious or dioecious). Perianth typically consists of 2 scale-like sepals and often 2 petals (or petals absent). Stamens usually 1 or 2. Ovary is inferior, composed of 2 fused carpels, with 2 styles.
  • Myrothamnaceae (Myrothamnus): Flowers unisexual (plants dioecious). Perianth is absent. Male flowers have 3-4 (up to 8) stamens with short filaments and fused anthers opening by longitudinal slits. Female flowers have a superior ovary of 2 (or 3-4) fused carpels, each with a separate style bearing a plumose (feathery) stigma.

Fruits and Seeds

Gunneraceae (Gunnera): Fruit is a small, one-seeded drupe (fleshy) or nutlet (dry), often brightly colored (red or orange).
Myrothamnaceae (Myrothamnus): Fruit is a small, dry, septicidal capsule containing numerous tiny seeds.

Chemical Characteristics

Both families contain tannins and ellagic acid. Gunnera is unique among angiosperms for its endosymbiosis with nitrogen-fixing cyanobacteria (Nostoc) housed in specialized glands. Myrothamnus produces specific compounds related to its extreme desiccation tolerance, including arbutin derivatives and trehalose, which protect cellular structures during drying.

Field Identification

Identifying Gunnerales in the field primarily involves distinguishing between its two highly distinct genera, Gunnera and Myrothamnus.

Primary Identification Features

  • Gunnera: Look for very large, herbaceous plants (often gigantic) with massive, lobed basal leaves resembling rhubarb but often much larger and rougher/prickly. Check for the huge, upright, branched inflorescence (if present). Found in moist, cool habitats.
  • Myrothamnus: Look for small, twiggy, woody shrubs in dry, rocky environments (southern Africa/Madagascar). Leaves are small, opposite, fan-shaped, and serrated at the tip. If dry, the plant will appear desiccated and reddish-black; if wet, the leaves will be green and unfolded.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Gunnera: Examine the base of petioles or stems for small gland-like structures (housing cyanobacteria). Note the tiny flowers densely packed on the large inflorescence stalks, followed by small, often red/orange fruits.
  • Myrothamnus: Crush a leaf to detect a resinous aroma. Observe the opposite, decussate leaf arrangement and the fan-like venation. Flowers are inconspicuous in small terminal spikes.

Common Confusion Points

  • Gunnera: May be confused with true rhubarb (Rheum species, Polygonaceae) due to large leaves, but Rheum has prominent sheathing stipules (ochreae), different flower structure (typically 6 tepals, superior ovary), and lacks symbiotic glands. Large-leaved Petasites (Asteraceae) have composite flower heads.
  • Myrothamnus: Its resurrection capability is shared with some ferns, mosses, and a few other angiosperms (e.g., some Velloziaceae), but the combination of woody shrub habit, opposite fan-shaped leaves, and specific floral structure is unique to Myrothamnus.

Notable Examples

The order Gunnerales includes only two genera, each representing its family:

Gunnera manicata / Gunnera tinctoria

Giant Rhubarb, Chilean Rhubarb

These South American species are among the largest in the genus, famed for their enormous leaves that can reach over 2 meters wide on thick, prickly petioles. They produce towering, cone-like inflorescences covered in tiny reddish flowers, followed by small fruits. Widely cultivated as architectural plants in large, moist gardens in temperate climates. G. tinctoria has traditional uses for tanning and dyeing.

Myrothamnus flabellifolius

Resurrection Plant

This small shrub is native to southern and eastern Africa, growing in rocky outcrops. During dry periods, it appears as a collection of dead, brittle, reddish-black twigs. Upon rainfall, the leaves rapidly rehydrate, unfold, and turn green, resuming photosynthesis within hours. It has aromatic leaves and is used in traditional medicine. The second species, M. moschatus, is found in Madagascar.

Phylogeny and Classification

Gunnerales holds a pivotal position in the phylogeny of flowering plants. Molecular phylogenetic studies consistently place Gunnerales as the sister group to the massive Pentapetalae clade, which encompasses the vast majority of core eudicot diversity (including Superrosids and Superasterids). This means Gunnerales diverged early from the lineage leading to almost all other core eudicots.

The order consists of only two families, Gunneraceae and Myrothamnaceae, each containing a single genus (Gunnera and Myrothamnus, respectively). Despite their extreme differences in morphology and ecology, molecular data strongly supports their close relationship as sister groups within the order Gunnerales. This relationship highlights the potential for rapid or ancient morphological divergence following lineage splitting.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Clade: Angiosperms (Flowering plants)
  • Clade: Eudicots
  • Clade: Core Eudicots
  • Order: Gunnerales

Sister Group Relationship

Gunnerales is the sister order to the Pentapetalae. The Pentapetalae clade includes virtually all other core eudicots, broadly divided into:

  • Superrosids (including Rosids: Fabales, Rosales, Malpighiales, Brassicales, Malvales, etc.)
  • Superasterids (including Asterids: Gentianales, Lamiales, Solanales, Asterales, Apiales, etc.)
  • Smaller orders like Santalales, Berberidopsidales, Caryophyllales, Dilleniales.

Gunnerales branched off before the diversification of this enormous group.

Evolutionary Significance

The Gunnerales order is highly significant for several reasons:

  • Basal Core Eudicot Position: Its status as sister to Pentapetalae makes it crucial for inferring ancestral traits of core eudicots and understanding the evolutionary steps leading to the diversification of Pentapetalae.
  • Morphological Extremes: The stark contrast between the giant herbs (Gunnera) and tiny resurrection shrubs (Myrothamnus) within a single small order demonstrates the potential for dramatic evolutionary divergence.
  • Unique Symbiosis: The nitrogen-fixing cyanobacterial symbiosis in Gunnera is unique among angiosperms and offers insights into the evolution of plant-microbe interactions.
  • Extreme Adaptation: The poikilohydry (resurrection capability) of Myrothamnus represents an extreme adaptation to arid environments, providing a model system for studying desiccation tolerance.
