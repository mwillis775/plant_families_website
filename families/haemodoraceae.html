Haemodoraceae

Bloodwort Family / Kangaroo Paw Family

Haemodoraceae is a family of monocotyledonous herbs, often characterized by reddish pigments in their roots or rhizomes (giving the name "bloodwort"). They typically possess basal, iris-like leaves arranged in a fan. Flowers are often covered in dense, sometimes colorful hairs (especially in Kangaroo Paws), usually have 6 tepals and 3 or 6 stamens, and are borne in various types of inflorescences on stalks (scapes). The ovary is frequently inferior. The family has a predominantly Southern Hemisphere distribution.

Overview

The Haemodoraceae family comprises about 13-14 genera and around 100-115 species of perennial herbs. The family exhibits a striking disjunct distribution, primarily found in temperate and subtropical regions of the Southern Hemisphere, particularly southwestern Australia (a major center of diversity, including the iconic Kangaroo Paws) and southern Africa. Other significant groups occur in South and Central America, the southeastern USA, and New Guinea.

These plants typically grow from rhizomes, corms, or bulbs, often in seasonally dry or fire-prone habitats. A distinctive feature of many species is the presence of red pigments (phenylphenalenones) in the roots and rhizomes. The flowers are notable for often being covered in dense, woolly hairs, which can be brightly colored and contribute significantly to the floral display, as seen in the Kangaroo Paws (Anigozanthos).

Economically, the family is best known for the Kangaroo Paws (Anigozanthos and Macropidia), which are popular ornamental plants cultivated worldwide for their unique, bird-pollinated flowers. Some species have been used traditionally for dyes or medicines by indigenous peoples. Phylogenetically, Haemodoraceae belongs to the order Commelinales, alongside families like Commelinaceae and Pontederiaceae.

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Perennial herbs, usually growing from underground storage organs like rhizomes, corms, or bulbs. Roots and rhizomes often contain conspicuous red, orange, or yellow pigments (phenylphenalenones). Plants are often tufted.

Leaves

Leaves are mostly basal, arranged alternately in two ranks (distichous), and typically equitant (overlapping at the base and flattened in the same plane, like an iris fan). They are usually linear or sword-shaped (ensiform) with entire margins and parallel venation. Stipules are absent.

Inflorescence

Inflorescences are typically terminal, borne on a leafless stalk (scape). The structure is variable, including panicles, thyrses, racemes, or cymes. A distinctive feature in many genera is the presence of dense, often brightly colored, woolly or glandular hairs covering the inflorescence branches, bracts, and/or the outside of the flowers.

Flowers

Flowers are bisexual and can be radially symmetrical (actinomorphic) or bilaterally symmetrical (zygomorphic - notably in Anigozanthos). They are typically 3-merous (parts in threes).

  • Perianth: Consists of 6 tepals arranged in two whorls (3+3), which are often petal-like (petaloid). Tepals can be free or fused basally to form a perianth tube of varying length. They are frequently covered externally with dense hairs, which may be colored. Tepal color varies widely (red, yellow, orange, green, blackish, purple, white).
  • Androecium: Stamens number usually 3 (opposite the inner tepals) or 6 (in two whorls), attached to the inside of the perianth tube or tepal bases. Filaments are distinct; anthers open via longitudinal slits.
  • Gynoecium: Ovary is usually inferior (sometimes superior or semi-inferior), composed of 3 fused carpels forming a 3-locular ovary (rarely 1-locular). Placentation is typically axile, with few to numerous ovules per locule. There is a single, slender style topped by a small, often capitate or slightly 3-lobed stigma.

Fruits and Seeds

The fruit is most commonly a dry, loculicidal capsule (splitting along the locule midlines), often enclosed or crowned by the persistent perianth remnants. Less commonly, it may be an achene (indehiscent, one-seeded) or a schizocarp (splitting into segments). Seeds are often small, sometimes flattened, winged, or hairy. Endosperm is present.

Chemical Characteristics

The most distinctive chemical feature is the presence of arylphenalenone pigments (often called phenylphenalenones), responsible for the red, orange, and yellow colors in the roots and sometimes flowers. These compounds are rare elsewhere in the plant kingdom. Flavonoids are also present.

Field Identification

Identifying Haemodoraceae involves recognizing the combination of monocot habit, iris-like leaves, often hairy/woolly flowers with 6 tepals, usually inferior ovary, and characteristic pigments or distribution.

Primary Identification Features

  • Habit: Perennial herb, often tufted, from rhizome/corm/bulb.
  • Leaves: Basal, distichous, equitant (iris-like), linear or sword-shaped.
  • Roots/Rhizomes: Often contain red/orange/yellow pigments.
  • Inflorescence/Flowers: Often covered in dense, woolly or glandular hairs (sometimes colored). Flowers with 6 tepals, often fused into a tube.
  • Stamens: Usually 3 or 6.
  • Ovary: Usually inferior.
  • Fruit: Typically a capsule.
  • Distribution: Primarily Southern Hemisphere (esp. Australia, S. Africa) or Americas.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Flower Symmetry: Actinomorphic or zygomorphic (e.g., kangaroo paw shape).
  • Flower Color: Diverse, often bright or dark.
  • Hairs: Presence, type, and color of hairs on flowers/inflorescence.

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Flowering Season: Varies by region (e.g., spring/summer in Australia/South Africa). Inflorescences are often the most conspicuous feature.
  • Vegetative Season: The distinctive equitant leaves are present year-round for perennial species. Red pigments in roots may be visible if disturbed.

Common Confusion Points

Plants with iris-like leaves or showy monocot flowers can cause confusion:

  • Iridaceae (Iris family): Also have equitant leaves and often showy flowers with 6 tepals and an inferior ovary. However, Iridaceae flowers are typically not densely hairy/woolly, always have only 3 stamens, and lack the characteristic red phenylphenalenone pigments.
  • Bromeliaceae (Pineapple family): Belongs to a different order (Poales). Usually form rosettes (not typically equitant fans), flowers often borne in dense spikes with colorful bracts (different structure), superior or inferior ovary, different fruit types.
  • Orchidaceae (Orchid family): Flowers are highly zygomorphic with a distinctive labellum and column (fused style/stamens), inferior ovary, and produce dust-like seeds. Leaves are highly variable but rarely truly equitant.
  • Asphodelaceae (e.g., Bulbine): Some have linear leaves and yellow flowers, but leaves are not usually equitant, flowers are typically star-shaped, not woolly, ovary is superior, and pigments differ.

The combination of equitant leaves, often densely hairy/woolly flowers with 6 tepals, usually 3 or 6 stamens, usually inferior ovary, and often reddish root pigments helps distinguish Haemodoraceae.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Perennial herb
  • Basal, iris-like (equitant) leaves
  • Reddish pigments in roots/rhizome (often)
  • Flowers often hairy/woolly
  • Inflorescence on a scape
  • 6 tepals (often fused into tube)
  • 3 or 6 stamens
  • Ovary usually inferior
  • Fruit a capsule

Key Variations:

  • Flower symmetry (radial vs. bilateral)
  • Flower color and hairiness
  • Stamen number (3 vs. 6)
  • Ovary position (inf. vs. sup./semi-inf.)
  • Geographic location

Notable Examples

Includes the iconic Kangaroo Paws of Australia and Bloodroots.

Anigozanthos spp.

Kangaroo Paws

A genus endemic to southwestern Australia, famous for its unique zygomorphic flowers resembling kangaroo paws. Flowers are tubular, covered in dense, colorful velvety hairs (red, green, yellow, pink, orange), and borne in racemes or panicles on tall scapes. Pollinated primarily by birds. Widely cultivated as ornamentals.

Haemodorum spp.

Bloodroot

A genus found in Australia and New Guinea. Named for the conspicuous red sap in their corms or rhizomes. Leaves are linear, often terete. Flowers are typically dark-colored (blackish, dark red, purple, or green), actinomorphic, with 6 tepals, 3 stamens, and an inferior ovary, borne in panicles or racemes. Not typically hairy like Kangaroo Paws.

Lachnanthes caroliniana

Redroot / Carolina Redroot

A species native to eastern North America (from Nova Scotia south to Florida and Cuba), often found in wet, sandy pinelands and bogs. Has iris-like leaves and rhizomes with bright red sap. Bears flat-topped clusters (cymes) of woolly, yellowish flowers with 6 tepals, 3 stamens, and a semi-inferior to superior ovary.

Phylogeny and Classification

Haemodoraceae is placed in the order Commelinales, which belongs to the large commelinid clade of monocots. Commelinids also include orders like Poales (grasses, sedges), Zingiberales (gingers, bananas), and Arecales (palms). Commelinales itself is characterized by features like vessel elements primarily in roots and specific cell wall constituents (UV-fluorescent ferulic acid).

Within Commelinales, Haemodoraceae forms a distinct lineage. Its exact sister relationship is still debated in some phylogenetic analyses, but it is consistently grouped with other core families of the order, including Commelinaceae (Spiderwort family), Pontederiaceae (Pickerelweed family), Philydraceae, and Hanguanaceae. The order shows considerable diversity in floral morphology and habitat preferences, ranging from terrestrial herbs to aquatic plants.

Related Families

Haemodoraceae belongs to the order Commelinales. Other families in this order include:

  • Commelinaceae (Spiderwort family): Often succulent herbs; leaves with closed sheaths; flowers typically ephemeral, often blue/purple, 3 petals, usually 6 stamens (sometimes modified).
  • Pontederiaceae (Pickerelweed/Water Hyacinth family): Aquatic herbs (floating or emergent); often showy blue/purple zygomorphic flowers; fruit a capsule or nutlet.
  • Philydraceae: Herbs of wet places (Australia/Asia); often hairy; flowers zygomorphic, yellow or white, with unique structure (2 petaloid tepals, 1 stamen).
  • Hanguanaceae: Robust herbs (SE Asia/Australia); large leaves; small unisexual flowers in dense panicles.

Evolutionary Significance

Haemodoraceae holds several points of evolutionary interest:

  • Biogeography: Its striking disjunct distribution across the Southern Hemisphere continents and the Americas points to ancient Gondwanan origins and subsequent vicariance or long-distance dispersal.
  • Chemical Evolution: The production of unique phenylphenalenone pigments is a distinctive biochemical trait with potential ecological roles (coloration, defense).
  • Pollination Biology: Exhibits diverse pollination syndromes, including specialized bird pollination (ornithophily) in the highly modified Kangaroo Paws (Anigozanthos).
  • Adaptation to Environment: Includes adaptations to fire-prone ecosystems (e.g., resprouting from rhizomes/corms in Australian species) and nutrient-poor soils.
  • Commelinales Diversity: Represents a distinct morphological and ecological radiation within the Commelinales order.
