Haloragaceae

Water Milfoil Family

Haloragaceae is a family of flowering plants comprising mostly perennial herbs adapted to aquatic or damp terrestrial environments, along with some terrestrial shrubs. Found nearly worldwide but most diverse in Australia, the family includes well-known aquatic plants like water milfoils (Myriophyllum), often characterized by finely dissected submerged leaves and reduced flowers adapted for wind or self-pollination. Haloragaceae belongs to the order Saxifragales.

Overview

The Haloragaceae family encompasses about 8-9 genera and roughly 145 species, distributed globally but with a strong concentration of diversity in Australia and the Southern Hemisphere. The largest genus, Myriophyllum (water milfoils), consists of aquatic herbs famous for their feathery, submerged leaves, though they often produce simpler emergent leaves and flowers on aerial spikes. Other genera like Haloragis and Gonocarpus are primarily terrestrial herbs or shrubs found in damp habitats, mostly in Australasia.

A common theme in the family is adaptation to wet environments, ranging from fully submerged aquatic life to growth in marshes, bogs, or damp soils. This is often reflected in leaf heterophylly (different leaf forms on the same plant, e.g., dissected submerged leaves vs. entire emergent leaves). The flowers are typically small and significantly reduced, lacking showy petals and adapted for wind pollination (anemophily) or self-pollination, especially common in aquatic species where flowers emerge just above the water surface.

While some species are used locally (e.g., traditional medicine), the family is perhaps best known for the ecological role of Myriophyllum species as aquatic vegetation, providing habitat for invertebrates and fish, but also for the invasive nature of some species like Eurasian watermilfoil (M. spicatum) in North American waterways.

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Mostly perennial herbs, rooted in substrate (aquatic species) or terrestrial in damp places. Some genera (Haloragis, Gonocarpus) include subshrubs or shrubs. Aquatic species often have rhizomes and produce emergent flowering stems.

Leaves

Leaves are highly variable in arrangement (whorled, opposite, or alternate) and form. Aquatic species, particularly Myriophyllum, often exhibit heterophylly: submerged leaves are typically pinnately dissected into fine, thread-like segments (appearing feathery), while emergent or aerial leaves are simpler, linear to ovate, and entire, toothed, or lobed. Terrestrial species usually have simpler leaves. Stipules are generally absent or small and scale-like.

Flowers

Flowers are typically small, inconspicuous, actinomorphic (radially symmetrical), and often unisexual (plants monoecious or dioecious) or sometimes bisexual. They are usually borne solitary in the axils of upper leaves or bracts, or arranged in terminal or axillary spikes, racemes, or panicles, which are often emergent in aquatic species. Flowers are adapted for wind pollination (anemophily) or self-pollination.

  • Perianth: Highly reduced. Sepals typically number 2-4 (sometimes absent), small and tooth-like. Petals number 2-4 (sometimes absent), often boat-shaped or hooded, usually falling off very early (caducous). Petals are typically reddish, yellowish, or greenish, not showy.
  • Androecium: Stamens usually number 2-8, often twice the number of sepals or petals. Filaments are short; anthers are relatively large, opening by longitudinal slits.
  • Gynoecium: The ovary is inferior, composed of (2-)4 fused carpels forming a (2-)4-locular ovary. Each locule contains a single pendulous ovule. Styles number (2-)4, are free and short, often bearing feathery or brush-like stigmas adapted for catching airborne pollen.

Fruits and Seeds

The fruit is typically small, dry, and indehiscent, functioning as a nutlet or breaking apart into 1-seeded segments (a schizocarp). The fruit surface is often ribbed, tuberculate, or winged. Each segment or nutlet contains a single seed.

Pollination

Predominantly wind-pollinated (anemophilous), reflected in the reduced perianth, exposed stamens with large anthers, and often feathery stigmas. Some species may be self-pollinating or occasionally insect-pollinated.

Chemical Characteristics

Plants often contain tannins. Some species are known to produce cyanogenic glycosides. They lack betalains and glucosinolates.

Field Identification

Identifying Haloragaceae often involves recognizing the habitat (aquatic or damp terrestrial), leaf arrangement and form (especially the dissected submerged leaves of Myriophyllum), and the characteristics of the small, reduced flowers and fruits.

Primary Identification Features

  • Habit: Mostly perennial herbs (aquatic or damp terrestrial) or shrubs.
  • Leaves: Variable: whorled, opposite, or alternate. Often finely dissected/feathery (submerged forms) or simpler (emergent/terrestrial forms) - i.e., heterophylly common.
  • Flowers: Small, reduced perianth (sepals 2-4, petals 2-4 or absent, falling early). Usually unisexual. Often in emergent spikes/racemes.
  • Stamens: 2-8.
  • Ovary: Inferior, (2-)4-locular.
  • Stigmas: Often feathery.
  • Fruit: Small nutlet or schizocarp splitting into 1-seeded segments.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Stipules usually absent.
  • Cosmopolitan distribution, diversity center in Australia.

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Submerged Vegetation: The feathery submerged leaves of aquatic species like Myriophyllum are often visible year-round where water persists.
  • Flowering/Fruiting: Emergent flower spikes or terrestrial flowers/fruits appear seasonally, typically in warmer months. The small size requires close inspection.

Common Confusion Points

  • Aquatic Forms (Myriophyllum): Can be confused with other submerged plants with dissected leaves:
    • Ceratophyllum (Ceratophyllaceae): Leaves dichotomously branched (Y-forks), lacks true roots.
    • Cabomba (Cabombaceae): Often has opposite dissected leaves plus entire floating leaves, flowers very different (showy petals).
    • Ranunculus sect. Batrachium (Ranunculaceae): Often alternate leaves, flowers with distinct yellow/white petals, superior ovaries, achene fruits.
    • Utricularia (Utriculariaceae): Possess small bladder traps (carnivorous), flowers zygomorphic.
  • Terrestrial Forms (Haloragis, Gonocarpus): Can be confused with various small-flowered herbs or subshrubs. Check for the combination of leaf arrangement, reduced perianth, inferior ovary with (usually) 4 locules/styles, and the characteristic nutlet or schizocarp fruit.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Aquatic/damp habitat herbs/shrubs
  • Leaves often whorled, finely dissected (submerged)
  • Heterophylly common
  • Small flowers, reduced perianth
  • Inferior ovary ((2-)4 parts)
  • Fruit a small nutlet or schizocarp

Key Variations:

  • Leaf arrangement (whorled, opp, alt)
  • Leaf dissection (highly dissected to simple)
  • Habit (aquatic herb to terrestrial shrub)
  • Flower sexuality (unisexual vs bisexual)
  • Fruit surface (smooth, ribbed, winged)

Notable Examples

The family includes ecologically significant aquatic plants and diverse terrestrial species.

Myriophyllum spicatum

Eurasian Watermilfoil

A submerged aquatic perennial native to Europe, Asia, and North Africa. It has whorls of finely dissected, feather-like leaves. It produces emergent spikes of small, wind-pollinated flowers. It has become a major invasive aquatic weed in North America, forming dense mats that crowd out native vegetation.

Haloragis erecta

Shrubby Raspwort, Toatoa

A perennial herb or subshrub native to New Zealand. Unlike Myriophyllum, it is terrestrial, growing in open or damp places. It has opposite, simple, toothed leaves and produces panicles of small, reddish, wind-pollinated flowers followed by small, ribbed nutlet fruits.

Gonocarpus teucrioides

Germander Raspwort

A common perennial herb or subshrub found in forests and woodlands of eastern Australia. It has opposite, toothed leaves and produces slender spikes or racemes of tiny reddish or greenish flowers. The fruit is a small, ribbed nutlet.

Proserpinaca palustris

Marsh Mermaid Weed

An aquatic or semi-aquatic perennial herb native to eastern North America and parts of Central/South America. It exhibits strong heterophylly: submerged leaves are finely pinnately dissected, while emergent leaves are simple, lanceolate, and sharply toothed. Flowers are tiny, borne solitary in leaf axils.

Phylogeny and Classification

Haloragaceae belongs to the diverse order Saxifragales, positioned within the core eudicots and considered sister to the Rosids clade. The order Saxifragales includes a wide range of plant forms, from large trees to tiny succulents.

The exact placement of Haloragaceae within Saxifragales has been subject to some variation in phylogenetic studies. It is clearly distinct from the core Saxifragaceae/Grossulariaceae clade and the Crassulaceae clade. Some analyses group Haloragaceae with the small families Penthoraceae (containing only Penthorum) and Tetracarpaeaceae (containing only Tetracarpaea, endemic to Tasmania). Regardless of its precise sister group, Haloragaceae represents one of the distinct lineages within this large and ancient order.

Related Families (within Saxifragales)

Haloragaceae shares the order Saxifragales with many other families. Potential close relatives include Penthoraceae and Tetracarpaeaceae. Other major families in the order include:

  • Penthoraceae: Single genus Penthorum (Ditch Stonecrop); herbs of E Asia and E North America.
  • Tetracarpaeaceae: Single species Tetracarpaea tasmannica; shrub endemic to Tasmania.
  • Saxifragaceae (Saxifrage Family): Mostly herbs, often with basal rosettes, superior ovaries, capsule fruits.
  • Crassulaceae (Stonecrop Family): Succulent herbs and shrubs, often with CAM photosynthesis.
  • Hamamelidaceae (Witch-hazel Family): Mostly trees and shrubs, often with strap-like petals.
  • Paeoniaceae (Peony Family): Herbs or shrubs with large flowers.
  • Grossulariaceae (Gooseberry Family): Contains only Ribes; shrubs with palmately lobed leaves and berries.
  • Iteaceae: Shrubs and trees including Itea and Pterostemon.

Evolutionary Significance

Haloragaceae is evolutionarily significant for several aspects:

  • Adaptation to Aquatic Life: The family, particularly Myriophyllum, provides excellent examples of morphological adaptations to aquatic environments, including dissected leaves and emergent, wind-pollinated flowers (convergent with other unrelated aquatic plant groups).
  • Floral Reduction: Shows trends towards reduction in floral parts (especially petals) associated with wind or self-pollination strategies.
  • Biogeography: Its cosmopolitan yet patchy distribution, with diversity centers in the Southern Hemisphere, offers clues about historical dispersal and vicariance events.
  • Saxifragales Diversity: Represents one of the distinct ecological and morphological forms within the broad Saxifragales order, helping to illustrate the evolutionary breadth of this lineage.
