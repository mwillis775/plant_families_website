Hernandiaceae

Hernandia Family

Hernandiaceae is a family of flowering plants comprising trees, shrubs, and large woody vines (lianas) found throughout the tropics worldwide (pantropical). The family includes about 5 genera and 55-60 species, characterized by alternate simple or compound leaves, small flowers often in cymose inflorescences, and distinctive fruits that are either enclosed in a fleshy cupule or are dry and winged. Hernandiaceae belongs to the order Laurales within the Magnoliid clade.

Overview

The Hernandiaceae family is distributed across tropical regions of Asia, Africa, Australia, the Pacific islands, and the Americas. It is typically divided into two subfamilies based largely on fruit type: Hernandioideae (genera Hernandia, Hazomalania) have fleshy fruits often enclosed in an inflated cup-like structure, while Gyrocarpoideae (genera Gyrocarpus, Illigera, Sparattanthelium) have dry, winged fruits adapted for wind dispersal.

Members of the family are woody plants, ranging from large rainforest trees and coastal trees (Hernandia, Gyrocarpus) to shrubs and prominent lianas (Illigera, Sparattanthelium) climbing high into the canopy. Leaves can be simple (often heart-shaped or peltate in Hernandia) or palmately compound. The flowers are generally small and arranged in branched clusters (cymes).

Economically, some species provide lightweight timber (e.g., Gyrocarpus) or have local uses in traditional medicine. Phylogenetically, Hernandiaceae is placed within the Laurales, an ancient order of Magnoliids, and is considered closely related to the laurel family (Lauraceae).

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Hernandiaceae
  • Common Name: Hernandia Family
  • Number of Genera: Approximately 5
  • Number of Species: Approximately 55-60
  • Distribution: Pantropical (tropical regions worldwide); lowland rainforests, coastal forests, sometimes drier forests.
  • Evolutionary Group: Angiosperms - Magnoliids - Laurales

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Evergreen trees, shrubs, or large woody vines (lianas). Lianas climb using twining petioles or tendrils.

Leaves

Leaves are alternate and spirally arranged. They can be simple (often entire, sometimes peltate or heart-shaped, as in Hernandia, Gyrocarpus) or palmately compound with 3-7 leaflets (as in Illigera, Sparattanthelium). Stipules are absent. Leaves or bark are often aromatic when crushed.

Flowers

Flowers are typically small, actinomorphic (radially symmetrical), and usually bisexual or unisexual (plants then monoecious or dioecious). They are arranged in axillary or terminal cymose inflorescences, which are often subtended by bracts (sometimes large and enclosing flower clusters in Hernandia).

  • Perianth: Consists of undifferentiated tepals, typically (3-)4-8(-10) arranged in (1-)2 whorls. Tepals are often petaloid (white, greenish, or yellowish) and free.
  • Androecium: Stamens usually number 3-5(-7), typically arranged in a single whorl opposite the outer tepals. Filaments often have nectar glands (staminodes) at their base. Anthers typically open via valves (similar to Lauraceae).
  • Gynoecium: The ovary is inferior, composed of a single carpel (monocarpellary) containing a single pendulous ovule. The style is simple, with a stigma that is often capitate (head-like) or slightly lobed.

Fruits and Seeds

Fruit morphology is highly distinctive and differs between the two subfamilies:

  • Hernandioideae (Hernandia, Hazomalania): The fruit is a drupe (fleshy with a hard stone) which is characteristically enclosed, partially or wholly, within an inflated, fleshy or papery, often lantern-like cupule derived from fused bracteoles.
  • Gyrocarpoideae (Gyrocarpus, Illigera, Sparattanthelium): The fruit is dry and indehiscent, adapted for wind dispersal (a samara). It bears two prominent wings, which develop from the persistent tepals or the ovary wall.

In both types, the fruit contains a single seed.

Wood Anatomy

Wood often lightweight; anatomical features vary across the genera.

Chemical Characteristics

Plants often contain various alkaloids (e.g., aporphine alkaloids) and essential oils, contributing to aromatic properties.

Field Identification

Identifying Hernandiaceae involves recognizing the woody habit (tree, shrub, or liana), alternate simple or palmately compound leaves, small flowers with tepals, inferior monocarpellary ovary, and especially the highly distinctive fruit types (enclosed drupe or winged samara).

Primary Identification Features

  • Habit: Trees, shrubs, or lianas (pantropical).
  • Leaves: Alternate, simple (often peltate/cordate) or palmately compound, stipules absent, often aromatic.
  • Flowers: Small, actinomorphic, with tepals (usually 6-8 in 2 whorls); stamens few (3-5); ovary inferior, 1-carpellate.
  • Fruit: Highly distinctive - either a drupe enclosed in a fleshy/papery cupule (Hernandia) OR dry with 2 large wings (Gyrocarpus, Illigera).

Secondary Identification Features

  • Flowers often in cymose inflorescences.
  • Stamens often associated with basal glands (staminodes).
  • Anthers often opening by valves.

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Flowering/Fruiting: Flowers are small, but the distinctive fruits are key identification aids when present, often persisting for some time. Seasonality varies with tropical location.
  • Vegetative: Look for the combination of woody habit, alternate leaves (check for peltate/cordate simple leaves or palmately compound leaves), and absence of stipules. Crushed leaves may be aromatic.

Common Confusion Points

  • Winged Fruits (Gyrocarpus, Illigera): May resemble winged fruits of unrelated families like Combretaceae (often opposite leaves) or Sapindaceae (maples have opposite leaves, superior ovaries). Check flower structure and leaf arrangement.
  • Enclosed Fruits (Hernandia): The fruit enclosed in a cupule is quite unique.
  • Lauraceae: Also in Laurales, often aromatic trees/shrubs with alternate simple leaves, but usually have superior ovaries and typical drupes (not enclosed).
  • Lianas (Illigera, Sparattanthelium): Compound-leaved lianas exist in other families (Fabaceae, Bignoniaceae), but flower structure (tepals, few stamens, inferior ovary) and the winged fruits distinguish Hernandiaceae lianas.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Trees, shrubs, lianas (tropical)
  • Alternate leaves (simple or palmately compound)
  • No stipules, often aromatic
  • Small flowers with tepals
  • Inferior, 1-carpellate ovary
  • Fruit enclosed in cupule OR 2-winged

Key Variations:

  • Habit (tree vs liana)
  • Leaf type (simple vs compound)
  • Fruit type (enclosed drupe vs winged samara)
  • Flower sexuality (bisexual vs unisexual)

Notable Examples

Examples showcase the diversity in habit and fruit type within the family.

Hernandia nymphaeifolia

Sea Hearse, Lantern Tree

A medium-sized evergreen tree found on tropical coastlines bordering the Indian and Pacific Oceans. It has large, simple, often peltate or heart-shaped leaves. Its distinctive fruit is a black drupe enclosed within a fleshy, white or reddish, inflated cupule, aiding dispersal by sea currents.

Gyrocarpus americanus

Propeller Tree, Helicopter Tree, Whirly-whirly Tree

A deciduous tree with a wide pantropical distribution, often found in drier forests or coastal areas. It has simple, often lobed leaves. It is named for its highly distinctive fruit, a small nut bearing two long, thin wings that cause it to spin like a propeller or helicopter rotor as it falls, aiding wind dispersal.

Illigera celebica

No widely accepted common name

A species of large woody vine (liana) found in Southeast Asia (e.g., Sulawesi). Like other Illigera species, it typically has palmately compound leaves (3-5 leaflets) and produces dry, winged fruits similar to Gyrocarpus, adapted for wind dispersal from the forest canopy.

Phylogeny and Classification

Hernandiaceae belongs to the order Laurales, an ancient lineage within the Magnoliids clade of flowering plants. Magnoliids represent one of the early diverging groups of angiosperms, distinct from monocots and eudicots.

Within Laurales, Hernandiaceae is consistently shown by molecular studies to be closely related to Lauraceae (laurel family). These two families, along with Monimiaceae and potentially Siparunaceae, often form a core group within the order. The relationship highlights shared ancestry despite significant differences in ovary position (inferior in Hernandiaceae, superior in Lauraceae) and fruit morphology.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Clade: Tracheophytes (Vascular Plants)
  • Clade: Angiosperms (Flowering Plants)
  • Clade: Magnoliids
  • Order: Laurales
  • Family: Hernandiaceae

Related Families (within Laurales)

Hernandiaceae shares the order Laurales with several other families characterized by often aromatic tissues and simple flowers with tepals:

  • Lauraceae (Laurel Family): Large family of mostly trees/shrubs (e.g., laurel, avocado, cinnamon, sassafras); typically alternate simple leaves, superior ovary, drupe fruit. Likely the closest relative.
  • Monimiaceae: Diverse family of trees/shrubs, mostly Southern Hemisphere; variable leaves and flowers, often with concave receptacles.
  • Siparunaceae: Trees/shrubs from Neotropics and West Africa; opposite simple leaves, unisexual flowers often enclosed in receptacles.
  • Gomortegaceae: Monotypic family (Gomortega keule) endemic to Chile; tree with opposite leaves, inferior ovary, drupe fruit.
  • Atherospermataceae: Trees/shrubs from Southern Hemisphere; opposite leaves, distinctive flowers/fruits.
  • Calycanthaceae: Shrubs from North America and E Asia; opposite leaves, flowers with numerous spirally arranged tepals.

Evolutionary Significance

Hernandiaceae offers insights into the evolution of early angiosperms:

  • Ancient Lineage and Biogeography: Its pantropical distribution suggests an ancient origin, likely dating back to Gondwanan times, making it important for studying historical biogeography.
  • Fruit Dispersal Syndromes: The striking divergence in fruit morphology between the two subfamilies (fleshy, water/animal-dispersed vs. dry, wind-dispersed) within a single family provides a clear example of adaptation to different dispersal strategies.
  • Laurales Relationships: Its phylogenetic position helps clarify relationships within the Laurales order, particularly its close link to the large and economically important Lauraceae family.
  • Magnoliid Diversity: Contributes to understanding the morphological and ecological diversity present among the early diverging Magnoliid lineages.
