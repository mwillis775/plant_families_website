Hookeriaceae

Hookeria Moss Family

Hookeriaceae is a significant family of primarily pleurocarpous mosses, largely distributed in tropical and subtropical regions, often extending into moist temperate areas. They typically form delicate, loose mats or wefts in very humid, shaded habitats, frequently growing on wet soil, rocks, logs, or as epiphytes or epiphylls. Key characteristics include often complanate (flattened) shoots, frequently asymmetric leaves that are commonly bordered, large, lax, smooth leaf cells, and a costa that is typically short and double, or absent (ecostate).

Overview

The Hookeriaceae family, containing around 15-20 genera and 300-400 species (with taxonomic boundaries often debated, especially concerning Daltoniaceae), is the type family for the order Hookeriales. These mosses are characteristic inhabitants of deeply shaded, constantly humid environments like tropical rainforest floors, cloud forests, stream banks, and waterfall spray zones. Their preference for low light often results in a glossy or even iridescent appearance when moist.

Plants are typically pleurocarpous, forming creeping or sometimes pendent mats. Many genera exhibit complanate foliage, where the leaves are flattened into two rows, giving the shoots a flattened appearance. The leaves themselves often have a distinct border of narrow cells, and the main laminal cells are notably large, thin-walled, and smooth, contributing to the delicate texture. A critical feature distinguishing Hookeriaceae from the closely related Daltoniaceae is the costa: in Hookeriaceae, it is usually short and double, or completely absent, whereas the core genus Daltonia typically has a single costa.

Sporophytes arise laterally and often feature inclined or pendulous capsules on setae of varying lengths, with well-developed double peristomes being common. The family showcases significant diversity within the Hookeriales lineage.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Hookeriaceae
  • Common Name: Hookeria Moss Family
  • Number of Genera: Approximately 15-20
  • Number of Species: Approximately 300-400
  • Distribution: Pantropical and subtropical, extending into moist temperate regions (esp. Southern Hemisphere).
  • Habitat: Humid, shaded forests; on wet soil, rocks, logs, bark (epiphytic), leaves (epiphyllous).
  • Evolutionary Group: Bryophytes - Mosses (Class: Bryopsida, Order: Hookeriales)

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Plants are pleurocarpous, typically forming loose, delicate, often glossy or iridescent mats or wefts. Stems creeping or sometimes pendent. Shoots are frequently complanate (flattened in one plane).

Stems

Stems creeping or pendent, usually slender, often irregularly or pinnately branched. Central strand variable.

Leaves

Leaves are often complanate and frequently asymmetric. Shape is variable, including ovate, oblong, lingulate, or lanceolate. Apex is usually acute, obtuse, or apiculate. A distinct leaf border of narrow, elongated cells is common. Crucially, the costa is typically short and double, or entirely absent (ecostate). Leaf cells are characteristically large, lax, smooth, thin-walled (parenchymatous), and usually hexagonal or rhomboidal. Alar cells are generally not differentiated.

Asexual Reproduction

Asexual reproduction via specialized gemmae, fragile leaves, or deciduous branchlets occurs in some species.

Sporophytes (Reproductive Structures)

Sporophytes arise laterally. The capsule is elevated on a seta of variable length (short to long), which can be smooth or papillose. The capsule is typically inclined to pendulous (though sometimes erect), symmetric or slightly asymmetric, and ovoid to cylindric. The peristome is double (diplolepidous), often well-developed and sometimes elaborate (Hookerioid type), though occasionally reduced. The operculum (lid) is usually rostrate (beaked). The calyptra (capsule covering) is often mitrate (cap-like) and frequently fringed, hairy, or roughened.

Habitat

Typically found in very humid, deeply shaded conditions, often near water sources like streams, waterfalls, or seepage areas. Grows on wet soil, humus, rocks, decaying logs, tree bases, twigs, and commonly as epiphylls on living leaves.

Field Identification

Identifying Hookeriaceae involves recognizing the delicate, often complanate pleurocarpous habit in very wet, shaded habitats, combined with leaf features like borders, large cells, and the absence of a strong single costa.

Primary Identification Features

  • Habitat (Key Feature): Very humid, deeply shaded environments (rainforests, stream banks, spray zones), often epiphytic or epiphyllous.
  • Growth Habit: Delicate, pleurocarpous mats/wefts, often glossy or iridescent, frequently complanate (flattened).
  • Costa (Key Feature): Costa short and double, or absent (ecostate).
  • Leaf Cells (Key Feature): Cells appear large, lax, and smooth (requires hand lens, best with microscope).
  • Leaf Border (Common Feature): Presence of a distinct border of narrow cells along the leaf margin.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Leaves: Often asymmetric.
  • Sporophytes (if present): Capsules often inclined to pendulous on setae. Calyptra often fringed/hairy.

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Year-round: The delicate gametophytes are visible year-round in suitable humid habitats. The large cells may give a translucent appearance.
  • Sporophyte Timing: Sporophyte production varies; capsules may be found at different times depending on species and location.

Common Confusion Points

Hookeriaceae are most likely confused with closely related families in Hookeriales or other complanate mosses:

  • Daltoniaceae: Very closely related. Daltonia typically has a single costa, whereas Hookeriaceae usually have a short/double or absent costa. Both share large cells and often bordered leaves.
  • Pilotrichaceae: Also Hookeriales, often more robust, sometimes aquatic/rheophytic.
  • Leucomiaceae: Also Hookeriales, very pale whitish-green, delicate, always ecostate.
  • Neckeraceae: Often larger, complanate, but leaf cells usually smaller and firmer, peristome different, habitat sometimes drier.
  • Plagiotheciaceae: Often complanate, but leaf cells typically narrower (linear), costa short/double or absent, habitat often different (e.g., temperate forests).

Look for the combination of wet shade habitat, often complanate growth, bordered leaves (often), large lax cells, and costa short/double or absent.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Habitat: Wet shade, epiphytic/epiphyllous
  • Delicate pleurocarpous mats (often glossy)
  • Shoots often complanate (flattened)
  • Leaves often with distinct border
  • Leaf cells large, lax, smooth
  • Costa short & double, or absent (Key!)
  • Capsule often inclined/pendulous

Key Variations:

  • Degree of complanation
  • Leaf shape (ovate, lingulate, etc.)
  • Presence/absence of border
  • Seta length and texture

Notable Examples

This family includes several genera characteristic of humid tropical and temperate forests:

Hookeria lucens

Shining Hookeria / Lucid Moss

A distinctive species found in very wet, shaded habitats in temperate regions (e.g., Europe, Pacific Northwest). Forms large, pale green, translucent mats. Shoots strongly complanate. Leaves large, ovate, asymmetric, ecostate (no costa), unbordered, with very large hexagonal cells.

Callicostella spp.

(Callicostella Mosses)

A large pantropical genus. Plants often form dense mats on wet rocks, soil, or logs near streams. Leaves typically ovate, often bordered, with large cells and usually a short double costa. Capsules often inclined on papillose setae.

Cyclodictyon spp.

(Cyclodictyon Mosses)

Another large pantropical genus, often found in very wet, shaded places, sometimes submerged. Shoots typically complanate. Leaves strongly bordered with very large, clear cells. Costa short and double. Capsules inclined to pendulous.

Lepidopilum spp.

(Lepidopilum Mosses)

A diverse genus primarily found in the Neotropics, often epiphytic or epiphyllous. Leaves variable, often bordered, costa usually short and double or absent. Sporophytes often have distinctive features like long setae or specialized calyptrae.

Phylogeny and Classification

Hookeriaceae is the type family for the order Hookeriales within the class Bryopsida. This order forms a distinct lineage of mostly pleurocarpous mosses, separate from the larger Hypnales order. Hookeriales are characterized by their predominantly tropical/subtropical distribution, preference for humid habitats, and often by large, lax leaf cells, bordered leaves, and elaborate double peristomes.

Within Hookeriales, Hookeriaceae is very closely related to Daltoniaceae. The distinction between the two families has traditionally relied heavily on costa characters (short/double/absent in Hookeriaceae vs. often single in Daltoniaceae), which molecular studies suggest may not always reflect true evolutionary divisions. Some classifications merge Daltoniaceae into Hookeriaceae, while others maintain them as separate sister families. The order also includes other distinct families like Pilotrichaceae and Leucomiaceae.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Division: Bryophyta (Mosses)
  • Class: Bryopsida
  • Subclass: Bryidae
  • Order: Hookeriales
  • Family: Hookeriaceae (closely related to/sometimes including Daltoniaceae)

Related Families/Groups

Hookeriaceae is most closely related to other families within the order Hookeriales:

  • Daltoniaceae: Sister group or potentially part of Hookeriaceae sensu lato. Differs primarily in costa characters.
  • Pilotrichaceae: Often more robust plants, sometimes aquatic.
  • Leucomiaceae: Very pale, delicate, ecostate plants.
  • Saulomataceae: Small family within the order.

The order Hookeriales is distinct from other major pleurocarpous orders like Hypnales.

Evolutionary Significance

Hookeriaceae is significant for:

  • Defining Hookeriales: As the type family, it exemplifies many characteristics of this distinct moss order.
  • Adaptation to Wet Shade: Shows extensive adaptation to low-light, high-humidity environments, including epiphyllism.
  • Large Cell Morphology: The prevalence of large, parenchymatous leaf cells represents a major morphological theme within the order.
  • Peristome Diversity: Contributes to the remarkable diversity of peristome structures found within Hookeriales.
  • Tropical Diversity Hotspot: Represents a major component of bryophyte diversity in tropical rainforests.
