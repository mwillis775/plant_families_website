Hypericaceae

St. John's Wort Family

Hypericaceae, commonly known as the St. John's wort family, is a widespread family of flowering plants in the order Malpighiales. It comprises herbs, shrubs, and trees found nearly worldwide, particularly diverse in temperate regions. The family is well-known for the genus Hypericum, which includes species used in traditional medicine and horticulture, often recognized by their yellow flowers with numerous stamens and leaves with translucent dots.

Overview

The Hypericaceae family includes about 9 genera and over 500 species, with the vast majority belonging to the genus Hypericum (St. John's worts). Members are distributed globally, absent only from arctic regions and extreme deserts. They occupy diverse habitats, from grasslands and woodlands to wetlands and alpine zones. The family exhibits a range of growth forms, including annual and perennial herbs, shrubs, and even small trees.

Many species, particularly within Hypericum, are characterized by the presence of glands (appearing as translucent or dark dots or streaks) on their leaves, petals, and other parts. These glands often contain secondary metabolites, such as hypericin (a red pigment) and hyperforin, which contribute to the medicinal properties attributed to plants like common St. John's wort (Hypericum perforatum). This species is widely known for its use as an herbal remedy, particularly for depression, though its use requires caution due to potential drug interactions.

Beyond medicinal uses, several Hypericaceae species are cultivated as ornamentals for their attractive, often bright yellow flowers with prominent stamens. Some species can also be weedy in certain regions. The family was historically often included within the Clusiaceae (Guttiferae), but molecular data supports its recognition as a distinct, closely related family within the large and diverse order Malpighiales.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Hypericaceae Juss.
  • Common Name: St. John's Wort Family
  • Number of Genera: Approximately 9
  • Number of Species: Approximately 560
  • Distribution: Nearly worldwide, especially temperate regions
  • Evolutionary Group: Eudicots - Rosids - Malpighiales

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Plants are typically annual or perennial herbs, subshrubs, shrubs, or small trees. Stems are often angled or winged.

Leaves

Leaves are usually simple, entire (smooth margins), sessile or shortly petiolate, and typically arranged oppositely or sometimes whorled. A key characteristic is the frequent presence of translucent or dark glandular dots or streaks on the leaf surface, visible when held up to the light.

Inflorescence

Flowers are borne solitarily or, more commonly, in terminal or axillary cymose inflorescences, which may be paniculate or corymbose.

Flowers

Flowers are typically bisexual, actinomorphic (radially symmetrical), and often showy, usually yellow (sometimes white, pink, or orange). Key features include:

  • Sepals: Usually 4 or 5, distinct or slightly fused at the base, often persistent in fruit, sometimes glandular.
  • Petals: Usually 4 or 5, distinct, often yellow, sometimes asymmetrical, frequently with dark glands along the margins or surface.
  • Androecium: Stamens are numerous (often >12), typically fused at their bases into 3 or 5 bundles (fascicles), or sometimes appearing distinct.
  • Gynoecium: Ovary is superior, composed of 3-5 fused carpels forming a single locule with parietal placentation, or 3-5 locules with axile placentation. Styles are usually distinct or fused only at the base, numbering 3-5.

Fruits and Seeds

The fruit is most commonly a dehiscent capsule (septicidal or loculicidal), but can sometimes be berry-like (e.g., some Hypericum sections) or drupaceous (e.g., Vismia). Seeds are typically small and numerous.

Chemical Characteristics

The family is known for producing a variety of secondary metabolites, particularly phloroglucinol derivatives (like hyperforin) and naphthodianthrones (like hypericin, which causes the red staining when tissues are crushed). These compounds contribute to the family's medicinal properties and ecological interactions (e.g., defense against herbivores, photosensitization).

Field Identification

Identifying members of Hypericaceae often relies on a combination of leaf and flower characteristics:

Primary Identification Features

  • Leaves: Simple, usually opposite, with entire margins. Look closely for characteristic translucent or dark glandular dots/streaks (hold leaf to light).
  • Flowers: Typically yellow, radially symmetrical, with 4 or 5 sepals and petals.
  • Stamens: Numerous stamens, often visibly grouped into 3 or 5 bundles.
  • Ovary/Styles: Superior ovary with usually 3 or 5 distinct styles.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Habit: Herbs, shrubs, or small trees.
  • Flower Color: While usually yellow, note any variations (white, pinkish).
  • Fruit Type: Check if fruit is a dry capsule or fleshy berry/drupe.
  • Glands: Note presence of dark glands on petal margins or sepals.

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Summer: Peak flowering season for most temperate species. Flowers and leaves are key for identification.
  • Late Summer/Fall: Fruits develop, which can help distinguish between species or sections (capsule vs. berry).
  • Vegetative State: Opposite leaves with translucent dots are highly suggestive of Hypericum even without flowers.

Common Confusion Points

  • Rosaceae (Rose family - some yellow-flowered genera): Genera like Potentilla can have yellow, 5-petaled flowers, but usually have compound or lobed leaves and different stamen/pistil arrangements (often numerous distinct pistils).
  • Linaceae (Flax family - e.g., Linum): Some yellow-flowered species exist, but they typically have alternate leaves and fewer stamens (usually 5 or 10).
  • Fabaceae (Pea family - some yellow-flowered genera): Usually have distinctly irregular (papilionaceous) flowers and compound leaves.
  • Clusiaceae (Guttiferae): Very closely related; some classifications merge Hypericaceae into Clusiaceae. Clusiaceae sensu stricto often includes plants with milky or colored latex and different fruit types.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Leaves usually opposite, simple, entire
  • Glandular dots on leaves (translucent/dark)
  • Flowers usually yellow, 4-5 parts
  • Numerous stamens, often in bundles
  • Superior ovary, usually 3-5 styles

Key Variations:

  • Habit (herb, shrub, tree)
  • Presence/density of glands
  • Number of stamen bundles (3 or 5)
  • Fruit type (capsule, berry, drupe)

Notable Examples

The genus Hypericum dominates the family, but other genera exist, particularly in the tropics.

Hypericum perforatum

Common St. John's Wort

A perennial herb native to Eurasia, now widespread globally, often as a weed. Famous for its medicinal uses. Characterized by opposite leaves with numerous translucent dots ("perforations") and bright yellow flowers with 5 petals, numerous stamens in 3 bundles, and black dots along petal margins.

Hypericum calycinum

Rose of Sharon / Aaron's Beard

A low-growing, evergreen shrub often used as groundcover. Native to Southeast Europe and Southwest Asia. Features large (up to 3 inches wide) bright yellow flowers with very prominent bushy stamens in 5 bundles and large, persistent sepals.

Hypericum prolificum

Shrubby St. John's Wort

A deciduous shrub native to eastern North America. Forms dense mounds with numerous bright yellow flowers throughout the summer. Leaves typically lack conspicuous dots. Fruit is a persistent capsule.

Vismia guianensis

Wax Tree / Pichi

Represents another genus in the family, found in tropical America. Typically shrubs or trees often exuding yellowish or orange latex (unlike most Hypericum). Flowers are often yellowish or greenish, and fruits are typically berries.

Phylogeny and Classification

Hypericaceae belongs to the large and diverse order Malpighiales, within the Rosid clade of eudicots. This order contains over 40 families, including well-known groups like Euphorbiaceae (spurges), Violaceae (violets), Salicaceae (willows), and Passifloraceae (passionflowers).

Within Malpighiales, Hypericaceae is part of the "clusioid clade," a group of closely related families that also includes Clusiaceae, Calophyllaceae, Podostemaceae, and Bonnetiaceae. The relationship between Hypericaceae and Clusiaceae has been particularly close, with historical classifications often merging them. However, current molecular evidence generally supports keeping them as distinct families, with Hypericaceae characterized by features like the absence of latex canals (usually present in Clusiaceae s.s.) and specific chemical profiles.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Clade: Angiosperms (Flowering plants)
  • Clade: Eudicots
  • Clade: Rosids
  • Order: Malpighiales
  • Family: Hypericaceae

Related Families (within Malpighiales)

Hypericaceae's closest relatives are within the clusioid clade of Malpighiales:

  • Clusiaceae (Mangosteen family): Tropical trees and shrubs, often with opposite leaves and colored latex. Includes genera like Clusia and Garcinia.
  • Calophyllaceae: Tropical trees, often with latex. Includes Calophyllum (beautyleaf).
  • Podostemaceae (Riverweed family): Highly specialized aquatic herbs living attached to rocks in fast water (rheophytes), showing extreme morphological modification. Surprisingly close relatives despite the vastly different appearance.
  • Bonnetiaceae: Small family of trees and shrubs in tropical South America and Malesia.
  • Other Malpighiales families (more distantly related): Euphorbiaceae, Violaceae, Salicaceae, Passifloraceae, Rhizophoraceae, Erythroxylaceae, etc.

Evolutionary Significance

Hypericaceae is significant for several evolutionary reasons:

  • Chemical Diversity: The production of unique compounds like hypericin and hyperforin highlights the evolution of complex secondary metabolite pathways.
  • Glandular Structures: The evolution and diversification of secretory structures (glandular dots, streaks, canals) within the clusioid clade is an area of interest.
  • Medicinal Importance: The widespread use of Hypericum provides a case study in ethnobotany and the evolution of plant-derived medicines.
  • Phylogenetic Relationships: Its position within the large Malpighiales order and close relationship to families with vastly different morphologies (like Podostemaceae) illustrate the diversity of evolutionary trajectories within major angiosperm lineages.
