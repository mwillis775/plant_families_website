Hypodematiaceae

Glandular Fern Family

Hypodematiaceae is a small family of ferns in the order Polypodiales, containing 2 genera (Hypodematium and Leucostegia) with approximately 22 species. These ferns are characterized by their glandular hairs on the fronds, reniform (kidney-shaped) indusia, and short-creeping to erect rhizomes covered with scales. The family is primarily distributed in Asia, with the greatest diversity in China, though some species extend to Africa, Europe, and the Pacific islands. Most species grow on limestone substrates in rocky habitats, often in crevices or on cliff faces. Previously classified within Dryopteridaceae or Athyriaceae, molecular studies have confirmed Hypodematiaceae as a distinct family within the eupolypod I clade of ferns.

Overview

The Hypodematiaceae family is a small but distinctive group of ferns that has only recently been recognized as a separate family based on molecular phylogenetic studies. It contains two genera: Hypodematium (with approximately 20 species) and Leucostegia (with 2 species).

The family has a complex taxonomic history, with its members previously classified within Dryopteridaceae or Athyriaceae based on morphological characteristics. However, molecular studies have revealed that Hypodematiaceae represents a distinct evolutionary lineage within the eupolypod I clade of ferns, warranting recognition as a separate family.

Hypodematiaceae is primarily distributed in Asia, with the greatest diversity in China, particularly in limestone areas. Some species extend to Africa, Europe, and the Pacific islands. Most members of the family are adapted to limestone substrates and grow in rocky habitats, often in crevices or on cliff faces.

The genus Hypodematium has undergone significant taxonomic revision in recent years, with several new species described from China and other parts of Asia. The genus Leucostegia, previously included in Davalliaceae, has been transferred to Hypodematiaceae based on molecular evidence.

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Members of Hypodematiaceae are terrestrial or lithophytic ferns (growing on rocks), with a preference for limestone substrates. They typically have short-creeping to erect rhizomes that are covered with scales. The plants often grow in rocky habitats, particularly in crevices or on cliff faces.

Rhizomes

The rhizomes are short-creeping to erect and are densely covered with scales. In Hypodematium, the scales are typically lanceolate to ovate, brown to dark brown, and often have ciliate or fimbriate margins (fringed with hair-like projections). In Leucostegia, the rhizomes are more distinctly creeping and the scales are narrower.

Fronds

The fronds of Hypodematiaceae show variation between the two genera:

  • Hypodematium: Fronds are typically bipinnate-pinnatifid to tripinnate, triangular to pentagonal in outline, and covered with glandular hairs that give the plants a characteristic sticky feel and often a distinctive odor. The stipes (stems) are grooved on the upper surface and densely scaly at the base.
  • Leucostegia: Fronds are more finely divided (tripinnate to quadripinnate), triangular to ovate in outline, and glabrous (without hairs) or sparsely hairy. The stipes are not grooved and have fewer scales.

Reproductive Structures

The sori (clusters of spore-producing structures) in Hypodematiaceae are round and positioned near the tips of the veins on the underside of the fronds. They are covered with reniform (kidney-shaped) indusia that are attached at the notch.

In Hypodematium, the indusia are often glandular-hairy, while in Leucostegia, they are glabrous. The spores are bilateral and monolete (with a single linear scar), and the chromosome number is typically n = 41 in Hypodematium and n = 42 in Leucostegia.

Distinguishing Features

Hypodematiaceae can be distinguished from other fern families by a combination of:

  • Glandular hairs on the fronds (in Hypodematium)
  • Reniform (kidney-shaped) indusia attached at the notch
  • Short-creeping to erect rhizomes covered with scales
  • Preference for limestone substrates
  • Round sori positioned near the tips of the veins

Identification

Field Identification Tips

When trying to identify members of the Hypodematiaceae family in the field, look for:

  • Glandular hairs on the fronds (in Hypodematium), giving them a sticky feel and often a distinctive odor
  • Reniform (kidney-shaped) indusia covering round sori on the underside of the fronds
  • Short-creeping to erect rhizomes covered with scales
  • Growth on limestone substrates, often in crevices or on cliff faces
  • Bipinnate-pinnatifid to tripinnate fronds (in Hypodematium) or more finely divided fronds (in Leucostegia)

Similar Families

Hypodematiaceae may be confused with several other fern families:

  • Dryopteridaceae: Some members have similar frond architecture and reniform indusia, but they typically lack the glandular hairs characteristic of Hypodematium and have different rhizome structure.
  • Athyriaceae: Can have similar frond architecture but typically has elongated sori with hooked or J-shaped indusia rather than round sori with reniform indusia.
  • Davalliaceae: Leucostegia was previously classified in this family and shares some morphological similarities, but differs in rhizome structure, indusia shape, and molecular characteristics.
  • Tectariaceae: Some members have similar frond architecture but typically lack the glandular hairs and preference for limestone substrates.

Distinguishing Features

The most reliable features for distinguishing Hypodematiaceae from similar ferns include:

  • Glandular hairs: The presence of glandular hairs on the fronds of Hypodematium is a key diagnostic feature
  • Indusium shape and attachment: The reniform (kidney-shaped) indusia attached at the notch are characteristic
  • Habitat preference: The strong association with limestone substrates is distinctive
  • Rhizome structure: The short-creeping to erect rhizomes covered with scales differ from many similar ferns

For definitive identification, examination of the glandular hairs (in Hypodematium) and indusia under magnification is often necessary, as these features are the most diagnostic for the family.

Notable Examples

The family Hypodematiaceae contains two genera with approximately 22 species. Here are some notable representatives:

Hypodematium crenatum

Glandular Rock Fern

The most widespread species in the genus, found across Asia from Japan to India and extending to parts of Africa and Europe. It has bipinnate-pinnatifid fronds that are triangular to pentagonal in outline and densely covered with glandular hairs. The fronds typically reach 20-40 cm in length and emerge from a short-creeping to erect rhizome covered with brown scales.

This species is highly variable across its range and has been divided into several subspecies or varieties by different authors. It typically grows on limestone rocks in crevices or on cliff faces and is adapted to seasonally dry conditions.

Hypodematium sinense

Chinese Glandular Fern

Endemic to China, this species is characterized by its tripinnate fronds that are pentagonal in outline and densely covered with glandular and non-glandular hairs. The fronds typically reach 30-50 cm in length and emerge from an erect rhizome covered with dark brown scales.

It grows primarily on limestone rocks in central and southern China, often in crevices or on cliff faces in montane regions. This species has been the subject of recent taxonomic studies, with several new species described from populations previously included within H. sinense.

Leucostegia immersa

Immersed Cup Fern

Widely distributed across tropical and subtropical Asia, from India to China, Japan, and Southeast Asia. It has tripinnate to quadripinnate fronds that are triangular to ovate in outline and glabrous (without hairs) or sparsely hairy. The fronds typically reach 30-60 cm in length and emerge from a long-creeping rhizome covered with narrow, pale brown scales.

Unlike Hypodematium species, L. immersa is not strictly associated with limestone and can grow on various rock types or as an epiphyte on tree trunks. It was previously classified in Davalliaceae but has been transferred to Hypodematiaceae based on molecular evidence.

Phylogenetic Note

Hypodematiaceae has undergone significant taxonomic revisions based on molecular phylogenetic studies. Previously, Hypodematium was classified within Dryopteridaceae or Athyriaceae based on morphological characteristics such as the reniform indusia and frond architecture. Leucostegia was previously placed in Davalliaceae due to its creeping rhizome and finely divided fronds.

Molecular analyses based on both plastid and nuclear DNA have revealed that these genera form a distinct evolutionary lineage within the eupolypods I clade of ferns. This phylogenetic placement, combined with distinctive morphological features such as the glandular hairs in Hypodematium and the preference for limestone substrates, supports the recognition of Hypodematiaceae as a separate family.

The exact position of Hypodematiaceae within the eupolypods I clade has been somewhat controversial, with different studies placing it in different positions. Some analyses have suggested a close relationship with Didymochlaenaceae, while others have placed it closer to Dryopteridaceae or as sister to a clade containing Peranemaceae and Tectariaceae. Despite these uncertainties, the monophyly of Hypodematiaceae (including both Hypodematium and Leucostegia) is well-supported by molecular data.

The chromosome numbers of Hypodematium (n = 41) and Leucostegia (n = 42) differ from those of most Dryopteridaceae and Davalliaceae, providing additional evidence for their distinct evolutionary history.
