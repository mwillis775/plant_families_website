Irvingiaceae

African Mango Family

Irvingiaceae is a small family of flowering trees belonging to the order Malpighiales, native to tropical Africa and Southeast Asia. The family is best known for the genus Irvingia, particularly Irvingia gabonensis (African mango or Dika nut), whose seeds and fruit pulp are economically important food sources in Africa. These trees are characterized by their large stipules and large, drupaceous fruits.

Overview

The Irvingiaceae family comprises 3 genera (Irvingia, Desbordesia, Klainedoxa) and about 10-12 species of medium to large trees. They inhabit tropical rainforests in Africa and Southeast Asia. The family's most prominent member, Irvingia gabonensis, is highly valued in West and Central Africa. Its fruit pulp is eaten fresh, and the seeds ("Dika nuts") are rich in fat and protein. These seeds are ground into a paste ("ogbono") used as a thickening agent for traditional soups and stews, contributing significantly to local economies and nutrition.

Members of Irvingiaceae are typically large forest trees, often with buttressed trunks. They are distinguished by their simple, alternate leaves accompanied by large, conspicuous stipules that enclose the terminal bud and fall off early, often leaving a characteristic scar around the twig. The flowers, though relatively small, are borne in panicles and possess a noticeable nectar disc. The fruit is a large drupe, resembling a mango in some species.

Phylogenetically, Irvingiaceae is placed within the large order Malpighiales, part of the Rosid clade of eudicots. This placement differs significantly from the previous families discussed, like Icacinaceae (Lamiids) or Hypoxidaceae (Monocots).

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Irvingiaceae Exell & Mendonça
  • Common Name: African Mango Family, Dika Nut Family
  • Number of Genera: 3 (primarily Irvingia)
  • Number of Species: Approximately 10-12
  • Distribution: Tropical Africa and Southeast Asia
  • Evolutionary Group: Eudicots - Rosids - Malpighiales

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Medium to large, evergreen or briefly deciduous trees, often with buttressed trunks. They are forest dwellers.

Leaves

Leaves are simple, alternate, petiolate, and usually entire. A key feature is the presence of large, often pointed, deciduous stipules that sheath the terminal bud and leave an annular (ring-like) scar on the twig upon falling.

Inflorescence

Flowers are borne in axillary or terminal panicles (branched inflorescences).

Flowers

Flowers are bisexual, actinomorphic (radially symmetrical), and typically 5-merous (parts in fives). Key features include:

  • Calyx: Consists of 5 small sepals, fused at the base.
  • Corolla: Consists of 5 free petals, often greenish-white or yellowish.
  • Androecium: Usually 10 stamens in two whorls of 5 (sometimes only 5 stamens), inserted below the nectar disc.
  • Nectar Disc: A prominent, fleshy intrastaminal nectar disc is present.
  • Gynoecium: Ovary is superior, composed of 2-5 fused carpels, forming 2-5 locules (though sometimes reduced to 1 functional locule). Each locule contains a single pendulous ovule. A single style is present.

Fruits and Seeds

The fruit is a large drupe, often resembling a mango, with fleshy or fibrous pulp and a hard, woody stone (endocarp) containing 1 seed (rarely more). The seeds are often rich in oils and fats.

Chemical Characteristics

The seeds are notably rich in fatty acids (like lauric and myristic acids), contributing to their use as food thickeners and potential sources of vegetable oil. The family may also produce various tannins and other polyphenolic compounds.

Field Identification

Identifying Irvingiaceae trees relies on a combination of vegetative and fruit characteristics:

Primary Identification Features

  • Habit: Medium to large forest trees, often buttressed.
  • Stipules: Presence of large, deciduous stipules sheathing the terminal bud, leaving an annular scar on the twig.
  • Leaves: Simple, alternate, entire.
  • Fruit: Large drupe (mango-like), often with fibrous flesh.
  • Flowers: 5-merous, with a prominent nectar disc, borne in panicles.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Habitat: Tropical rainforests (Africa, SE Asia).
  • Bark/Trunk: Note presence of buttresses.
  • Flower details: 10 stamens, superior ovary (2-5 carpels).
  • Seed: Single large seed within a hard stone, often oily.

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Vegetative State: The combination of tree habit, alternate simple leaves, and especially the large terminal stipules (or their annular scars) is highly diagnostic year-round.
  • Flowering Season: Panicles of small, whitish/yellowish flowers appear, timing varies geographically.
  • Fruiting Season: Large, conspicuous drupes mature, aiding identification and indicating species like Irvingia gabonensis.

Common Confusion Points

Irvingiaceae trees might be confused with members of other tropical tree families:

  • Anacardiaceae (Cashew family, e.g., Mangifera - Mango): Mango trees also have large drupes, but typically have resin ducts (often visible as dark streaks in bark/petioles) and lack the large terminal stipules and annular scars of Irvingiaceae.
  • Clusiaceae/Calophyllaceae: Some trees in these related families (Malpighiales) might have simple, opposite or alternate leaves, but often possess latex and have different flower/fruit structures (e.g., berries or capsules, different stamen arrangements).
  • Sapotaceae: Trees often with latex and simple alternate leaves, but typically have flowers with staminodes and berry fruits. Lack large terminal stipules.
  • Moraceae (Fig family): Many trees in this family also have prominent stipules leaving annular scars (e.g., Ficus), but they typically possess latex and have highly specialized inflorescences (syconia in Ficus) and different fruit types (syncarps, achenes).

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Large tropical trees, often buttressed
  • Large deciduous stipules leaving annular scars
  • Leaves simple, alternate, entire
  • Flowers 5-merous with nectar disc
  • Fruit a large drupe

Key Variations:

  • Fruit pulp (fleshy vs. fibrous)
  • Seed use (edible vs. not)
  • Specific flower color nuances

Notable Examples

The genus Irvingia is the most well-known and economically important.

Irvingia gabonensis

African Mango / Dika Nut / Ogbono

A large tree native to West and Central Africa. Highly valued for its edible fruit pulp and especially its seeds (Dika nuts), which are ground to thicken soups and stews. Fruit resembles a small mango.

Irvingia wombolu

Bitter Dika / Bush Mango

Closely related to I. gabonensis and also found in West/Central Africa. Its fruit pulp is typically bitter and not eaten, but the seeds are also used as a soup thickener (sometimes preferred over I. gabonensis for certain dishes).

Irvingia malayana

(Pauh Kijang)

A species found in Southeast Asia (e.g., Malaysia, Thailand, Indonesia). A large forest tree producing similar large drupes, though perhaps less utilized economically than its African relatives. Provides timber.

Klainedoxa gabonensis

(Eveuss)

Represents another genus sometimes included in Irvingiaceae. Large African tree with very hard wood and large, distinctively segmented woody fruits (drupes splitting into sections). Stipules are also prominent.

Phylogeny and Classification

Irvingiaceae is placed within the large order Malpighiales, a major group within the Rosid clade of eudicots. This order is exceptionally diverse, containing around 40 families and over 16,000 species, exhibiting a vast range of growth forms and ecological strategies.

Within Malpighiales, Irvingiaceae belongs to a specific clade that includes families like Putranjivaceae, Lophopyxidaceae, Erythroxylaceae (coca family), and Rhizophoraceae (mangrove family). Its placement within the Rosids clearly separates it from the Asterid families discussed previously (like Icacinaceae, Hydroleaceae, Hydrostachyaceae) and the Monocot family Hypoxidaceae.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Clade: Angiosperms (Flowering plants)
  • Clade: Eudicots
  • Clade: Rosids
  • Order: Malpighiales
  • Family: Irvingiaceae

Related Families (within Malpighiales)

Irvingiaceae is related to other families within the diverse Malpighiales order. Some potentially closely related families include:

  • Putranjivaceae: Trees/shrubs, often with mustard oils, simple alternate leaves, small unisexual flowers, drupaceous fruits.
  • Lophopyxidaceae: Monotypic family (Lophopyxis maingayi), a large liana from SE Asia/Malesia.
  • Erythroxylaceae: Includes Erythroxylum (coca plant), shrubs/trees with often prominent stipules, flowers with nectar appendages, drupaceous fruits.
  • Rhizophoraceae: Includes mangrove trees (e.g., Rhizophora) and some inland relatives, often with opposite leaves and distinctive flower/fruit features.
  • Other major Malpighiales families (more distantly related): Euphorbiaceae, Salicaceae, Violaceae, Passifloraceae, Hypericaceae, Clusiaceae, Phyllanthaceae.

Evolutionary Significance

Irvingiaceae, though small, holds evolutionary interest:

  • Economic Botany: Provides a significant example of a non-major crop food source (Dika nuts) vital to regional diets and economies in Africa.
  • Morphological Features: The prominent stipules and large drupes are characteristic features within its clade in Malpighiales.
  • Biogeography: Its disjunct distribution between tropical Africa and Southeast Asia points to historical connections or long-distance dispersal events.
  • Phylogenetic Placement: Its position within the vast Malpighiales order contributes to understanding the diversification patterns within this major Rosid lineage.
