Krameriaceae

Ratany Family

Krameriaceae is a small, monogeneric family of dicotyledonous flowering plants in the order Zygophyllales, containing only the genus Krameria. These are hemiparasitic herbs or shrubs native to arid and semi-arid regions of the Americas. They are characterized by often silky-hairy leaves, distinctive zygomorphic flowers with oil-secreting petals (elaiophores), and spiny or bristly fruits.

Overview

The Krameriaceae family consists of a single genus, Krameria, with approximately 17-18 species. Commonly known as rhatany or ratany, these plants are perennial herbs or low shrubs adapted to dry environments, ranging from the southwestern United States south through Mexico and Central America to Chile and Argentina. A key biological feature is their hemiparasitic nature; they possess chlorophyll and photosynthesize but also attach to the roots of host plants via haustoria to obtain water and nutrients.

Krameriaceae are often recognizable by their grayish or silvery appearance due to dense coverings of appressed hairs on stems and leaves. Their flowers are quite distinctive: bilaterally symmetrical (zygomorphic), often brightly colored (red, purple, pink), with petal-like sepals and highly modified petals. Two lower petals are transformed into specialized oil-secreting glands called elaiophores. These glands attract specific female bees (often of the genus Centris) that collect the oil, along with pollen, to provision their nests, thus serving as pollinators. The fruit is also characteristic, being a small, indehiscent, one-seeded pod covered in spines or barbed bristles, facilitating dispersal by animals (epizoochory).

Some species, particularly Andean Krameria lappacea (syn. K. triandra), are sources of rhatany root, which is rich in tannins and has astringent properties used in traditional medicine and formerly in commercial products like toothpaste and gargles. Phylogenetically, Krameriaceae is placed in the small order Zygophyllales, sister to the family Zygophyllaceae (caltrop family).

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Krameriaceae Dumort.
  • Common Name: Ratany Family, Rhatany Family
  • Number of Genera: 1 (Krameria)
  • Number of Species: Approximately 17-18
  • Distribution: Arid/Semi-arid regions of the Americas (SW USA to South America)
  • Evolutionary Group: Eudicots - Rosids - Fabids - Zygophyllales

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Perennial herbs or low shrubs, often much-branched. Hemiparasitic on the roots of other plants via haustoria. Stems and leaves frequently covered in dense, appressed, silvery or grayish hairs.

Leaves

Leaves are simple, usually alternate (rarely opposite), sessile or shortly petiolate, linear to lanceolate or ovate, with entire margins. They are often covered in silky or appressed hairs. Stipules are usually absent.

Inflorescence

Flowers are borne solitarily in leaf axils or in terminal or axillary racemes.

Flowers

Flowers are bisexual, strongly zygomorphic (bilaterally symmetrical), and often showy (red, purple, pink, or combinations). Key features include:

  • Calyx: 4 or 5 sepals, free, often unequal in size, the inner surface often colored and petal-like. The lowermost sepal may be larger or spurred.
  • Corolla: 4 or 5 petals, typically smaller than the sepals. The upper 3 petals are often smaller, narrower, sometimes fused or reduced. The lower 2 petals are modified into thick, fleshy, glandular elaiophores (oil-secreting glands).
  • Androecium: Usually 4 stamens (sometimes 5), often unequal in length, typically clustered towards the upper side of the flower. Filaments are thick; anthers open by pores or short slits.
  • Nectar Disc: Absent. Pollinator reward is oil from elaiophores and pollen.
  • Gynoecium: Ovary is superior, composed of 2 fused carpels, but usually only 1 locule develops, containing 2 ovules (one typically aborts). A single, often curved style with a small stigma is present.

Fruits and Seeds

The fruit is an indehiscent, 1-seeded, nut-like or drupe-like achene, characteristically covered in stout spines or barbed bristles, aiding in dispersal by attaching to animals.

Chemical Characteristics

The family contains tannins (especially in roots, e.g., rhataniatannic acid) and unique compounds like rhatanin. They lack the glucosinolates found in Koeberliniaceae and other Brassicales.

Field Identification

Identifying Krameriaceae (Krameria) involves recognizing its hemiparasitic habit, hairy appearance, unique flower structure, and spiny fruit:

Primary Identification Features

  • Habit: Hemiparasitic herbs or shrubs, often with dense silvery/grayish hairs.
  • Leaves: Simple, alternate, entire, often linear/lanceolate and hairy.
  • Flowers: Distinctly zygomorphic (bilaterally symmetrical), often red/purple/pink.
  • Petals: Lower 2 petals modified into thick, glandular elaiophores (oil glands).
  • Stamens: Usually 4, often clustered.
  • Ovary Position: Ovary is superior.
  • Fruit: Indehiscent, 1-seeded pod covered in spines or barbed bristles.
  • Habitat: Arid/semi-arid regions of the Americas.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Sepals: Often petal-like and colored.
  • Hair Type: Appressed, silky hairs common.
  • Parasitic Connection: Haustorial connections to host roots (not easily observed).

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Flowering Season: Varies with species and rainfall, often spring through fall in warmer regions. The unique flowers are key.
  • Fruiting Season: The spiny/bristly fruits are highly diagnostic and persist for some time.
  • Vegetative State: The combination of low shrubby/herbaceous habit, often silvery-hairy alternate leaves, and arid habitat can suggest the family.

Common Confusion Points

Krameria might be confused with other arid-land plants, especially those with similar flower colors or hairy leaves:

  • Fabaceae (Pea family): Many Fabaceae occur in arid regions and have zygomorphic flowers (papilionaceous). However, they typically have compound leaves, 10 stamens (often fused), a superior ovary developing into a legume (pod), and lack elaiophores and spiny fruits (though some pods are bristly).
  • Some Asteraceae (Sunflower family): Some desert composites might have hairy leaves, but their flowers are arranged in heads (capitula) composed of multiple florets, and the fruit is an achene often with a pappus.
  • Zygophyllaceae (Caltrop family): Sister family. Often has opposite, pinnately compound leaves (though sometimes simple), flowers usually actinomorphic with 5 petals and 10 stamens, and fruit typically a capsule or schizocarp (sometimes spiny, e.g., Tribulus).
  • Malvaceae (Mallow family - e.g., Sphaeralcea): Some desert mallows have hairy leaves and reddish/pinkish flowers, but flowers are actinomorphic with numerous stamens fused into a tube around the style, and fruit is typically a schizocarp splitting into segments.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Hemiparasitic herbs/shrubs
  • Leaves simple, alternate, often hairy
  • Flowers zygomorphic (red/purple/pink)
  • Lower 2 petals are elaiophores (oil glands)
  • Usually 4 stamens
  • Superior ovary
  • Fruit indehiscent, 1-seeded, with spines/bristles

Key Variations:

  • Flower color
  • Leaf shape and hairiness
  • Spine type on fruit (simple vs. barbed)
  • Habit (herbaceous vs. shrubby)

Notable Examples (Genus Krameria)

The family contains only the genus Krameria.

Krameria erecta

Range Ratany / Littleleaf Ratany / Purple Heather

A common species in deserts of the southwestern US and Mexico. A low, intricately branched shrub with small, grayish-hairy leaves. Produces numerous small, purple-red flowers. Fruit is covered in slender, barbed bristles.

Krameria lanceolata

Trailing Ratany

Found in grasslands and open woodlands from central US south to Mexico. Typically more herbaceous with trailing or prostrate stems. Leaves are lanceolate and hairy. Flowers are reddish-purple. Fruit has barbed spines.

Krameria grayi (often syn. K. bicolor)

White Ratany

A shrub found in the Sonoran and Mojave deserts (SW USA, NW Mexico). Distinctive for its dense white-woolly hairs covering stems and leaves. Flowers are typically purplish-pink. Fruit is spiny.

Krameria lappacea (syn. K. triandra)

Rhatany

Native to the Andes mountains in South America (Peru, Bolivia, Chile). A low shrub whose roots are the primary source of medicinal rhatany, rich in tannins. Flowers are typically red. Fruit is spiny.

Phylogeny and Classification

Krameriaceae is placed in the small order Zygophyllales, which belongs to the Fabid (or Eurosids I) clade within the Rosids. This placement is strongly supported by molecular phylogenetic studies.

The order Zygophyllales contains only two families: Krameriaceae and Zygophyllaceae (caltrop or creosote bush family). Krameriaceae is sister to Zygophyllaceae. Zygophyllaceae is a larger family (~22 genera, ~285 species) of herbs, shrubs, and trees found mostly in arid regions worldwide, characterized often by opposite, pinnately compound leaves and usually actinomorphic flowers. The Zygophyllales order itself is part of the nitrogen-fixing clade within the Fabids, which also includes orders like Fabales (legumes), Rosales (roses), and Fagales (oaks, beeches), although nitrogen fixation is not known in Zygophyllales.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Clade: Angiosperms (Flowering plants)
  • Clade: Eudicots
  • Clade: Rosids
  • Clade: Fabids (Eurosids I)
  • Order: Zygophyllales
  • Family: Krameriaceae

Related Families (within Zygophyllales)

Krameriaceae's only close relative at the family level is its sister family within the order Zygophyllales:

  • Zygophyllaceae: Caltrop or Creosote Bush family; herbs, shrubs, trees, often with opposite compound leaves, usually actinomorphic flowers with 5 petals and 10 stamens, fruit often a capsule or schizocarp (e.g., Tribulus, Larrea, Guaiacum).

The Zygophyllales order is part of the larger Fabid clade, related to orders like Fabales, Rosales, and Fagales.

Evolutionary Significance

Krameriaceae, though small, is significant for several evolutionary aspects:

  • Hemiparasitism: Represents an independent evolution of root hemiparasitism within the Rosids.
  • Pollination Biology: The evolution of specialized oil-secreting petals (elaiophores) and co-evolution with oil-collecting bees (like Centris) is a fascinating example of specialized pollination systems.
  • Phylogenetic Position: Its placement with Zygophyllaceae in the distinct order Zygophyllales within the Fabids helps define the major lineages within the Rosids.
  • Fruit Dispersal: The spiny/bristly fruit adapted for epizoochory (dispersal via attachment to animals) is a key adaptation for survival and spread in its environment.
  • Medicinal Chemistry: The production of high levels of tannins in some species highlights the evolution of chemical defenses and compounds with ethnobotanical importance.
