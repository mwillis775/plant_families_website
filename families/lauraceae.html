Lauraceae

Lauraceae, the laurel family, is a significant family of flowering plants belonging to the Magnoliid clade (Order Laurales), representing an early diverging lineage of angiosperms. It comprises about 50 genera and around 2,500-3,000 species, consisting primarily of evergreen trees and shrubs with aromatic tissues. Key characteristics include simple, usually alternate leaves; small, often greenish or yellowish flowers typically with parts in threes (6 tepals, stamens in whorls of 3); stamens often opening by valves; a superior ovary usually of a single carpel; and fruit typically a single-seeded drupe or berry often seated in a cup-like structure (cupule).

Overview

The Laurel family is predominantly found in tropical and subtropical regions worldwide, with major centers of diversity in Southeast Asia and Central/South America, though some genera extend into temperate zones. Members are often important components of forest ecosystems. The family is well-known for the aromatic essential oils present in the bark, wood, leaves, and fruits of many species, leading to significant economic importance.

Notable products include spices like true cinnamon (from the bark of Cinnamomum verum), cassia (bark of C. cassia), and bay leaves (from Laurus nobilis). Avocado (Persea americana) is a globally important fruit crop. Camphor is distilled from Cinnamomum camphora. Various species provide valuable timber or are used in traditional medicine. Many Lauraceae are also cultivated as ornamentals.

Their phylogenetic position among the Magnoliids places them separate from the main groups of monocots and eudicots, representing an earlier branch in angiosperm evolution. In Oklahoma (current location as of March 27, 2025), native representatives include the deciduous Sassafras tree (Sassafras albidum), easily recognized by its variably shaped leaves (entire, mitten-shaped, 3-lobed) and aromatic twigs, and Spicebush (Lindera benzoin), an aromatic shrub common in moist woodlands.

Key Characteristics (Magnoliid Features)

Growth Form and Habit

Mostly evergreen trees and shrubs, rarely deciduous (Sassafras, Lindera). An exception is the genus Cassytha, which consists of parasitic, thread-like vines superficially resembling dodder (Cuscuta, Convolvulaceae) but are greenish/yellowish and slightly photosynthetic. Tissues (bark, leaves, twigs) are typically aromatic due to specialized oil cells.

Leaves

Leaves are usually simple and arranged alternately and spirally along the stem (less commonly opposite or whorled). Margins are typically entire (smooth), though famously variable in Sassafras albidum which can have entire oval leaves, mitten-shaped leaves (one lobe), and three-lobed leaves on the same tree. Leaves are often leathery in texture and lack stipules (exstipulate). Venation is usually pinnate.

Characteristic leaf variation in Sassafras albidum: entire, mitten-shaped, and three-lobed leaves.

Inflorescence

Inflorescences are usually borne in the axils of leaves and are typically cymose, paniculate, racemose, or pseudo-umbellate clusters. They are often relatively inconspicuous and may be enclosed by bracts when young.

Flowers

Flowers are generally small, actinomorphic (radially symmetric), often greenish, yellowish, or whitish, and typically 3-merous in arrangement (parts in multiples of three). They can be bisexual or unisexual (plants then monoecious or dioecious).

  • Perianth: Typically undifferentiated into calyx and corolla, consisting of usually 6 tepals arranged in two whorls of 3. Tepals are often similar in size and appearance, free or slightly fused at the base, and usually fall off after flowering (though sometimes persist or enlarge around the fruit base).
  • Androecium: Stamens are typically arranged in 3 or 4 whorls of 3 (total usually 9 or 12, sometimes fewer). The innermost whorl often consists of sterile stamens (staminodes) which may be gland-like and function as nectaries. A key characteristic of many Lauraceae is that the anthers dehisce via 2 or 4 flaps (valves) that curl upward from the base or side to release pollen, rather than splitting longitudinally. Filaments often possess paired glandular appendages near the base, especially on the inner whorls.
  • Gynoecium: Ovary is superior (rarely inferior), typically composed of a single carpel (monocarpous) containing a single locule and a single pendulous ovule. A single style terminates in a simple or slightly lobed stigma.

Generalized Lauraceae flower showing 6 tepals and stamens (often 9+ fertile) typically opening by valves (flaps).

Fruits and Seeds

The characteristic fruit is a single-seeded drupe (a fleshy fruit with a central stone containing the seed, like an avocado or cherry) or sometimes a berry (entirely fleshy). A distinctive feature in many genera is that the base of the fruit sits within or upon a persistent, often enlarged, cup-like structure called a cupule, formed from the receptacle and/or the base of the perianth (e.g., Sassafras, Lindera, Persea). The seed is large, lacks endosperm (or has very little), and contains a large, well-developed embryo.

Typical Lauraceae fruit: a single-seeded drupe often seated in a persistent cupule.

Chemical Characteristics

The family is defined by the presence of scattered spherical ethereal oil cells in parenchyma tissues, containing a wide variety of aromatic volatile compounds (terpenoids like camphor, linalool; phenylpropanoids like cinnamaldehyde, eugenol, safrole). Benzylisoquinoline alkaloids (related to those in Magnoliales and Ranunculales) are also commonly produced. These compounds contribute to the characteristic scents, flavors, medicinal properties, and toxicity of various species.

Identification Basics

Identifying Lauraceae usually relies on recognizing the woody habit, aromatic tissues, simple alternate leaves, and specific floral or fruit characteristics.

Primary Identification Features

  • Woody Habit: Mostly evergreen (or deciduous) trees or shrubs (except parasitic Cassytha).
  • Aromatic Tissues: Leaves, twigs, or bark typically release a distinct fragrance when crushed (bay, cinnamon, sassafras-like, etc.).
  • Alternate, Simple Leaves: Leaves usually alternate, simple, mostly entire margins (variable in Sassafras), exstipulate.
  • Small, 3-merous Flowers: Flowers typically small, greenish/yellowish, with 6 tepals in two whorls.
  • Stamens in Whorls of 3: Usually 9 or 12 stamens (some may be staminodes), often with valvate anther dehiscence (opening by flaps - need lens).
  • Superior Ovary (1 Carpel): Ovary positioned above other parts, typically formed from a single carpel with one ovule.
  • Drupe or Berry Fruit +/- Cupule: Fruit fleshy, single-seeded, often seated in a cup-like base.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Valvate Anther Dehiscence: Observing flaps opening on anthers (requires good lens).
  • Cupule Presence/Color: The cup at the base of the fruit (Sassafras has red cupules).
  • Leaf Shape Variation: Recognizing the unique lobing of Sassafras.
  • Specific Aroma: Identifying characteristic scents.
  • Parasitic Vine Habit: Unique to Cassytha.

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Year-round: Aromatic nature of twigs/bark is always present. Evergreen species retain leaves. Bark patterns can be useful.
  • Spring/Summer: Flowering occurs (often inconspicuous flowers). Leaves fully developed.
  • Summer/Fall: Fruits mature. Drupes with cupules are distinctive. Deciduous species (Sassafras, Lindera) show fall color.
  • (Oklahoma Context: Sassafras is recognizable year-round by its aromatic, often greenish, furrowed twigs and variable leaves/fall color. Lindera is a shrub with aromatic twigs/leaves, small yellow flowers in early spring before leaves, and red drupes in fall).

Common Confusion Points

  • Magnoliaceae (Magnolia Family): Also Magnoliids, woody, alternate simple aromatic leaves, superior ovary. Key differences: Flowers large, showy, with numerous separate petals/sepals spirally arranged; numerous distinct pistils; fruit an aggregate of follicles/samaras; stipule scar encircles twig.
  • Annonaceae (Pawpaw Family): Also Magnoliids, woody, alternate simple aromatic leaves. Key differences: Flowers often larger, 3-merous perianth whorls often thicker/fleshy; stamens numerous; pistils usually multiple and distinct; fruit usually a large fleshy berry or aggregate berry.
  • Fagaceae (Oak Family): Trees with alternate simple leaves (often lobed). Key differences: Non-aromatic (usually); flowers tiny, unisexual, often in catkins; fruit a nut (acorn).
  • Hamamelidaceae (Witch Hazel Family): Shrubs/trees, alternate simple leaves. Key differences: Non-aromatic (usually); flowers often have strap-like petals, different structure; fruit a woody capsule.
  • Cassytha vs. Cuscuta (Dodder, Convolvulaceae): Both parasitic vines. Key differences: Cassytha (Lauraceae) is greenish/yellowish (slightly photosynthetic), woody at base, flowers 3-merous, fruit a drupe enclosed by fleshy receptacle. Cuscuta is non-green (yellow/orange), herbaceous, flowers 4-5 merous, fruit a capsule.

Key combination for Lauraceae: Aromatic woody plant + Alternate simple leaves + Small 6-tepaled flower + Stamens often opening by valves + Single superior carpel + Drupe/berry +/- cupule.

Field Guide Quick Reference (Lauraceae)

Look For:

  • Woody tree or shrub (mostly evergreen)
  • Aromatic leaves, twigs, bark
  • Leaves alternate, simple, usually entire (exstipulate)
  • Flowers small, 3-merous (6 tepals)
  • Stamens usually 9+, often opening by valves
  • Ovary superior, 1 carpel (usually)
  • Fruit a drupe or berry, often with cupule
  • (Cassytha is parasitic vine)

Key Variations:

  • Leaf shape (Sassafras lobes)
  • Evergreen vs. Deciduous
  • Specific aroma
  • Cupule presence/color
  • Stamen valve number (2 vs 4)
  • Flower sexuality

Notable Examples

The Laurel family provides valuable spices, fruits, timber, and aromatic compounds.

Laurus nobilis

Bay Laurel / Bay Leaf / Sweet Bay

An evergreen shrub or small tree native to the Mediterranean region. Leaves are leathery, aromatic, and widely used as a culinary herb (bay leaves). Produces small yellowish flowers followed by small, blackish drupes. Source of the laurel wreaths of classical antiquity.

Persea americana

Avocado

A tree native to south-central Mexico, now cultivated pantropically for its large, oily fruit. Leaves are alternate and evergreen. Flowers small, greenish-yellow. The fruit is botanically a large, single-seeded berry (though often called a drupe). Numerous cultivars exist.

Cinnamomum spp.

Cinnamon & Cassia

Genus of aromatic evergreen trees/shrubs native to Asia. Bark is the source of cinnamon spice. C. verum (Ceylon cinnamon) provides 'true' cinnamon. C. cassia and others provide 'cassia', often sold as cinnamon. Leaves often opposite or subopposite, sometimes tri-veined from base. Flowers small; fruit a drupe.

Sassafras albidum

Sassafras

A deciduous tree native to eastern North America, including Oklahoma. Distinctive for having three leaf shapes on the same tree (entire oval, mitten-shaped, three-lobed). All parts are highly aromatic (root beer scent). Small yellow flowers appear before leaves in spring (dioecious). Fruit is a dark blue drupe on a bright red, club-shaped cupule/pedicel.

Lindera benzoin

Spicebush

A deciduous shrub native to eastern North America, common in moist woods in eastern Oklahoma. Highly aromatic leaves and twigs (lemony-spicy scent). Produces clusters of small, yellow flowers along the stems very early in spring before leaves emerge (dioecious). Female plants bear small, bright red drupes in late summer/fall. Host plant for spicebush swallowtail butterfly.

Umbellularia californica

California Bay Laurel / Oregon Myrtle

An evergreen tree native to coastal forests of California and Oregon. Leaves are highly aromatic, similar to Bay Laurel (Laurus) but much more pungent. Produces small yellowish flowers in umbel-like clusters followed by purplish, olive-like drupes. Wood is valued for woodworking.

Phylogeny and Classification

Lauraceae is the largest family in the order Laurales, a key order within the Magnoliid clade. Magnoliids represent one of the major early diverging lineages of flowering plants (Angiosperms), distinct from both Monocots and Eudicots. Other orders in the Magnoliid clade include Magnoliales, Canellales, and Piperales. Within Laurales, Lauraceae is part of the core group, related to families like Monimiaceae, Gomortegaceae, Hernandiaceae, and Calycanthaceae.

The family's position among early angiosperm lineages is supported by features sometimes considered 'primitive', such as flowers with undifferentiated tepals, parts often in threes, stamens sometimes numerous and in multiple whorls, and often a simple (monocarpous) pistil. However, it also exhibits specialized features like valvate anther dehiscence and complex secondary chemistry (essential oils, alkaloids). The evolution of the fleshy, single-seeded drupe or berry, often associated with a cupule, has been a successful dispersal strategy. The parasitic habit of Cassytha represents a unique derivation within this predominantly woody, autotrophic family.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Clade: Angiosperms (Flowering plants)
  • Clade: Magnoliids
  • Order: Laurales
  • Family: Lauraceae

Related Families (Magnoliids / Laurales)

Within the order Laurales, Lauraceae is related to:

  • Monimiaceae: Trees/shrubs, often opposite leaves, flowers often perigynous with variable parts.
  • Calycanthaceae (Sweetshrubs): Shrubs, opposite leaves, distinctive flowers with numerous tepals/stamens, fruit an aggregate of achenes enclosed in receptacle.
  • Gomortegaceae, Siparunaceae, Atherospermataceae, Hernandiaceae.

The Laurales order is part of the Magnoliid clade, related to other orders like:

  • Magnoliales (e.g., Magnoliaceae, Annonaceae): Trees/shrubs, often large flowers with numerous spirally arranged parts.
  • Piperales (e.g., Piperaceae - Pepper family, Aristolochiaceae - Birthwort family): Often herbaceous or climbing, small flowers often lacking perianth.
  • Canellales: Small order of aromatic trees.

Evolutionary Significance

Lauraceae provides crucial insights into the diversity and characteristics of early angiosperm lineages (Magnoliids). Its successful radiation across tropical and subtropical environments highlights the effectiveness of its combination of features: woody habit, chemical defenses (aromatic oils, alkaloids), simple flowers often adapted for generalist pollinators, and fleshy fruits dispersed by animals. The distinctive valvate anther dehiscence and the frequent presence of a cupule associated with the fruit are notable evolutionary specializations. The family's rich fossil record contributes to understanding angiosperm history and biogeography.
