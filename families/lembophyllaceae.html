Lembophyllaceae

(No widely accepted common name)

Lembophyllaceae is a family of pleurocarpous mosses, meaning they grow in spreading mats or wefts with sporophytes arising from the sides of the stems or branches. This family is predominantly found in the temperate and tropical regions of the Southern Hemisphere, particularly Australasia and southern South America, often growing on tree trunks, logs, rocks, and soil.

Overview

The Lembophyllaceae family belongs to the order Hypnales, one of the largest orders of mosses. It comprises around 10-12 genera and approximately 50-60 species. These mosses typically form glossy, often yellowish-green to golden-brown mats or drooping festoons on various substrates in moist forest environments.

Members of this family exhibit considerable variation in size and branching patterns, ranging from regularly pinnate (feather-like) to more irregularly branched forms. Their leaves are often concave and broadly ovate, contributing to the plump or turgid appearance of the shoots in many species. Sporophytes, when present, feature capsules that are typically held horizontally or are slightly inclined.

Ecologically, Lembophyllaceae contribute to forest biodiversity and microhabitats, particularly in the moist forests of the Southern Hemisphere. They play roles in water retention and provide substrate for invertebrates. The family is primarily of scientific interest, with little direct economic importance.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Lembophyllaceae
  • Common Name: None widely used
  • Number of Genera: Approximately 10-12
  • Number of Species: Approximately 50-60
  • Distribution: Primarily Southern Hemisphere (Australasia, southern South America, southern Africa), some extending to tropical mountains.
  • Evolutionary Group: Bryophytes - Mosses - Hypnales

Key Characteristics (Moss Morphology)

Gametophyte: Growth Form and Habit

Plants are pleurocarpous, forming loose to dense mats, wefts, or pendent festoons. They range from medium-sized to robust. Stems are creeping, ascending, or pendent, often showing regular to irregular pinnate branching (feather-like) or sometimes dendroid (tree-like) branching patterns. Shoots often appear turgid or julaceous (smoothly cylindrical) due to concave, overlapping leaves.

Gametophyte: Stems

Stems possess a central strand (a core of specialized conductive cells) or lack one. Paraphyllia (small, leaf-like or filamentous outgrowths on the stem) are usually absent, but pseudoparaphyllia (small, modified leaves at branch bases) may be present.

Gametophyte: Leaves

Stem and branch leaves can be similar (homomallous) or differentiated (heteromallous). Leaves are typically crowded, erect-spreading to imbricate (closely overlapping), often strongly concave, broadly ovate to oblong-ovate, and usually symmetrical. Leaf apices range from acute to broadly rounded or obtuse. Margins are typically entire or finely serrulate near the apex. The costa (nerve) is variable: absent, short and double, or occasionally single but usually weak and not reaching the leaf apex. Leaf cells (laminal cells) are typically smooth, rhomboidal to linear in the upper part, sometimes becoming shorter and broader towards the base; alar cells (at the basal corners) may be differentiated (inflated, colored) or indistinct.

Sporophyte: Seta, Capsule, and Peristome

Sexual condition is typically dioicous (male and female reproductive structures on separate plants). The seta (stalk supporting the capsule) is elongated, smooth, and usually reddish or brownish. The capsule is typically exserted (held beyond the leaves), ovoid to cylindrical, horizontal to inclined (rarely erect), and symmetrical or slightly curved. The operculum (lid) is usually conical to rostrate (beaked). The peristome (tooth-like structure surrounding the capsule mouth, regulating spore release) is typically hypnoid (double), with well-developed outer teeth (exostome) and inner segments (endostome) arising from a high basal membrane, often with cilia (hair-like structures) present between the segments.

Field Identification (Mosses)

Identifying Lembophyllaceae often requires attention to growth habit, branching pattern, leaf shape, and sometimes microscopic features:

Primary Identification Features (Field/Hand Lens)

  • Pleurocarpous growth habit: Forming mats, wefts, or pendent strands, not upright tufts like acrocarps.
  • Branching pattern: Look for regularly pinnate (feather-like) or sometimes dendroid (tree-like) branching.
  • Turgid/Julaceous shoots: Many species have plump, smooth-looking shoots due to concave, overlapping leaves.
  • Leaf shape and arrangement: Broadly ovate, concave leaves that are typically imbricate (closely overlapping).
  • Habitat and Location: Predominantly found on trees, logs, and rocks in moist forests of the Southern Hemisphere.

Secondary Identification Features (Microscope often needed)

  • Costa/Nerve: Check if the leaf nerve is absent, short and double, or single and weak (rarely strong).
  • Leaf cell shape: Upper cells typically rhomboidal to linear, smooth.
  • Alar cells: Note if cells at the basal corners of the leaves are differentiated (e.g., inflated, colored).
  • Capsule orientation: Capsules usually horizontal or inclined (not erect).
  • Peristome type: Double, hypnoid peristome (requires mature, open capsules).

Common Confusion Points (Other Moss Families)

  • Neckeraceae: Some Neckeraceae have flattened (complanate) shoots and often stronger costae. Many grow pendent.
  • Hypnaceae: A large family with diverse habits; many have more slender, less turgid shoots and often possess a short, double costa.
  • Brachytheciaceae: Often have stronger single costae reaching mid-leaf or beyond, and leaves may be plicate (folded lengthwise).
  • Meteoriaceae: Often form long, pendent strands, but leaf shapes and cell structures differ.

Field Guide Quick Reference (Mosses)

Look For:

  • Pleurocarpous mats/wefts/pendent strands
  • Turgid, often glossy shoots
  • Regularly pinnate or dendroid branching
  • Broadly ovate, concave leaves
  • Weak/absent/short-double costa
  • Horizontal/inclined capsules (if present)
  • Southern Hemisphere forest habitats

Key Variations:

  • Branching pattern (pinnate, irregular, dendroid)
  • Leaf apex shape (acute to rounded)
  • Costa presence and length
  • Alar cell differentiation
  • Plant size (medium to robust)

Notable Genera

Some representative genera within the Lembophyllaceae family include:

Lembophyllum

(Genus)

The type genus for the family. Plants often form dense, glossy, yellowish-green to golden mats. Shoots are turgid and julaceous with strongly concave, broadly ovate leaves that typically lack a costa. Common in Australasia.

Camptochaete

(Genus)

Includes species with regularly pinnate to somewhat dendroid branching patterns, often forming robust mats on logs and tree bases. Leaves are concave, often glossy, with short double or absent costae. Found in Australasia and surrounding islands.

Weymouthia

(Genus)

Characterized by pendent growth, forming long, stringy festoons hanging from tree branches in moist forests. Shoots are slender but turgid, with concave leaves. Distributed in Australasia and southern South America.

Fallaciella

(Genus)

Often forms intricate mats with somewhat flattened (subcomplanate) foliage. Leaves are typically broadly ovate with a short double costa or none. Found in Australasia.

Acrocladium

(Genus)

A genus (sometimes placed in its own family Acrocladiaceae, but often included here) with robust, often yellowish, pinnately branched plants forming dense mats. Leaves are strongly concave with rounded apices. Widespread in the Southern Hemisphere.

Phylogeny and Classification

Lembophyllaceae is placed within the large order Hypnales in the class Bryopsida (true mosses). The Hypnales represent one of the most recent and diverse radiations of mosses. Lembophyllaceae is part of the core Hypnales lineage, characterized by pleurocarpous growth and typically double, hypnoid peristomes.

Molecular phylogenetic studies have confirmed the placement of Lembophyllaceae within Hypnales and helped refine its relationships with other families. It appears closely related to families like Neckeraceae, Brachytheciaceae, and Meteoriaceae, although the exact relationships among these large pleurocarpous families are complex and subject to ongoing research. The circumscription of the family itself has varied, with some genera occasionally moved in or out based on new data.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Division: Bryophyta (Mosses)
  • Class: Bryopsida
  • Order: Hypnales
  • Family: Lembophyllaceae

Related Families (Order Hypnales)

Lembophyllaceae is related to other families within the diverse Hypnales order, including:

  • Neckeraceae: Often complanate (flattened) or pendent mosses, some with transverse undulations on leaves.
  • Brachytheciaceae: Diverse family, often with stronger single costae and sometimes plicate leaves.
  • Hypnaceae: Typically slender mosses, often with falcate-secund (curved to one side) leaves and short double costae.
  • Meteoriaceae: Primarily tropical/subtropical pendent epiphytes.
  • Thuidiaceae: Often regularly pinnately or bi-pinnately branched (fern-like) with paraphyllia usually present on stems.

Evolutionary Significance

The Lembophyllaceae family represents a distinct lineage within the successful pleurocarpous moss radiation (Hypnales):

  • Southern Hemisphere Radiation: Exemplifies diversification primarily in temperate Gondwanan regions.
  • Morphological Diversity: Shows variation in branching (pinnate, dendroid) and habit (mats, pendent) within the pleurocarpous form.
  • Costa Reduction: Exhibits a trend towards weak or absent costae in many genera, contrasting with related families like Brachytheciaceae.
  • Ecological Specialization: Primarily epiphytic or epilithic in moist forest environments.
