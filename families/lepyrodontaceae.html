Lepyrodontaceae

Scale-tooth Moss Family (Informal)

Lepyrodontaceae is a monotypic family of robust, often glossy pleurocarpous mosses belonging to the order Hypnales. It contains the single genus Lepyrodon. The family is characterized by its often julaceous branches, concave leaves lacking a costa but with strongly differentiated alar cells, and a primarily Southern Hemisphere distribution.

Overview Characteristics Identification Examples Phylogeny

Overview

The Lepyrodontaceae family, established by Brotherus, is currently understood to be monotypic, containing only the genus Lepyrodon. These are generally robust, somewhat stiff pleurocarpous mosses, often forming dense, glossy mats or tufts in shades of yellow-green to golden-brown. The name Lepyrodon means "scale tooth," likely referring to the structure of the peristome teeth.

Like the related Leptodontaceae, the branches often appear smoothly cylindrical (julaceous) when dry due to closely appressed, concave leaves. However, Lepyrodontaceae typically differs in having smooth (not papillose) leaf cells and, most notably, strongly differentiated alar cells – distinct groups of cells at the basal corners of the leaves, which are often enlarged, inflated, or colored.

Lepyrodontaceae are primarily found as epiphytes on tree trunks and branches, sometimes on rocks, in humid forests, often at higher elevations. Their distribution is predominantly in temperate regions of the Southern Hemisphere (southern South America, South Africa, Australasia, Subantarctic islands), with some occurrences in tropical mountains. Phylogenetically, the family is placed within the order Hypnales, though its precise relationships have sometimes been debated.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Lepyrodontaceae Broth.
  • Common Name: Scale-tooth Moss Family (Informal)
  • Number of Genera: 1 (Lepyrodon Hampe)
  • Number of Species: Several (e.g., ~5-10)
  • Distribution: Primarily Southern Hemisphere temperate regions, some tropical mountains.
  • Evolutionary Group: Bryophytes - Mosses (Class Bryatae - Order Hypnales)

Key Characteristics (Moss Features)

Gametophyte (Leafy Plant)

The dominant stage. Plants robust, somewhat stiff, often glossy yellow-green to golden-brown, pleurocarpous, forming dense mats or tufts.

  • Stems: Creeping primary stems give rise to erect or ascending, often densely crowded secondary stems/branches. Branching typically irregular or subpinnate. Branches often appear smoothly cylindrical (julaceous) when dry.
  • Leaves (Phyllids): Crowded, closely appressed when dry, erect-spreading when moist. Leaves typically ovate, strongly concave, usually abruptly narrowed to an acuminate (tapering) point. Margins generally plane or slightly recurved, entire or serrulate near the apex.
  • Costa (Midrib): Characteristically absent, or very short and double and often indistinct at the extreme base.
  • Leaf Cells: Typically long and narrow (linear), smooth, often thick-walled. Alar cells at the basal corners are characteristically strongly differentiated, forming distinct, often large groups of inflated, hyaline (clear), or colored (yellowish/brownish) cells.

Reproductive Structures (Gametophyte)

Antheridia and archegonia are borne in lateral buds on the stems or branches.

Sporophyte (Spore-Producing Structure)

Arises laterally from the gametophyte stem, often produced infrequently. Consists of:

  • Seta: Usually relatively short to moderate in length, smooth, often reddish or yellowish.
  • Capsule (Sporangium): Typically erect and symmetric, ovoid to shortly cylindrical. Operculum usually conical or rostrate.

Peristome

Typically double but often reduced, similar to Leptodontaceae and Leucodontaceae. The 16 outer exostome teeth are usually present, lanceolate, sometimes fragile or perforated. The inner endostome is often rudimentary, consisting of a low basal membrane and poorly developed or absent segments and cilia.

Field Identification

Identifying Lepyrodontaceae involves recognizing its habit, leaf shape, and especially the alar cells:

Primary Identification Features

  • Julaceous Branches (when dry): Branches often appear smooth and cylindrical due to tightly appressed concave leaves.
  • Glossy Appearance: Plants are often distinctly shiny, yellow-green to golden-brown.
  • Concave Leaves, Abruptly Pointed: Leaves are spoon-shaped but typically narrow quickly to a pointed tip.
  • Costa Absent / Very Short Double: Lack of a distinct midrib is key (use hand lens).
  • Distinct Alar Cells: The groups of differentiated cells at the leaf base corners are often visible with a good hand lens as distinct patches, sometimes appearing inflated or colored. This is a crucial feature.
  • Habitat and Location: Typically epiphytic in humid forests in Southern Hemisphere temperate regions.

Common Confusion Points

  • Leptodontaceae (Leptodon): Similar julaceous habit, ecostate leaves, habitat, and reduced peristome. Distinguished by having papillose leaf cells (dull appearance) and usually less strongly differentiated alar cells.
  • Leucodontaceae (Leucodon): Similar julaceous habit, ecostate leaves, smooth cells, and epiphytic habitat. Distinguished by having generally undifferentiated or less conspicuously differentiated alar cells.
  • Entodontaceae: Glossy, ecostate, with erect capsules, but usually strongly complanate (not julaceous) and with different alar cell structure.
  • Sematophyllaceae: Many genera have strongly differentiated alar cells, but typically differ in leaf shape (often falcate-secund), costa presence (sometimes short/double), or capsule orientation (usually inclined).

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Robust, often glossy moss mats/tufts
  • Branches often julaceous when dry
  • Leaves concave, abruptly pointed
  • Costa absent or very short/double
  • Strongly differentiated alar cells (key!)
  • Smooth leaf cells
  • Epiphytic in S. Hemisphere forests

Key Variations:

  • Glossiness and color
  • Length of leaf acumen
  • Size/color of alar cell groups
  • (Variations exist among Lepyrodon species)

Notable Examples

The family contains the single genus Lepyrodon:

Lepyrodon lagurus

(No widely used common name)

A characteristic species found in southern South America and Australasia. It forms dense, often golden, glossy mats with julaceous branches, concave ecostate leaves ending in a point, and distinct groups of inflated alar cells. Typically epiphytic.

Lepyrodon tomentosus

(No widely used common name)

Another species from the Southern Hemisphere (e.g., South Africa, Australasia), often distinguished by the presence of dense rhizoids (tomentum) on the stems. Shares the generic features of julaceous branches, ecostate leaves, and differentiated alar cells.

Phylogeny and Classification

Lepyrodontaceae is classified within the Class Bryatae (true mosses). Like Leptodontaceae, its ordinal placement was historically debated, often associated with Leucodontales due to the similar habit and reduced peristome. However, molecular data generally support its inclusion within the order Hypnales.

Within Hypnales, Lepyrodontaceae represents a distinct lineage primarily characterized by its combination of ecostate leaves, smooth linear cells, strongly differentiated alar cells, julaceous branches, and reduced peristome. Its relationships might lie with families like Lembophyllaceae or the Ptychomniales complex, reflecting the intricate evolutionary patterns within Southern Hemisphere pleurocarpous mosses.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Division: Bryophyta (Mosses)
  • Class: Bryatae (or Bryopsida)
  • Order: Hypnales
  • Family: Lepyrodontaceae

Related Families (within Order Hypnales or historically associated)

Lepyrodontaceae shares features or phylogenetic links with several other families:

  • Leptodontaceae: Similar habit, ecostate leaves, reduced peristome, but differs in having papillose cells and usually less developed alar cells.
  • Leucodontaceae: Similar habit, ecostate leaves, smooth cells, but differs in having undifferentiated alar cells and often different peristome details.
  • Lembophyllaceae: A diverse Southern Hemisphere family, some members robust and epiphytic, but generally differ in leaf and cell structure.
  • Ptychomniaceae / Braithwaiteaceae: Robust Southern Hemisphere pleurocarps, but distinguished by strongly plicate leaves with a single costa.
  • Sematophyllaceae: Many members have differentiated alar cells but differ in other gametophyte and sporophyte features.

Evolutionary Significance

Lepyrodontaceae is significant as a monotypic family representing a distinct evolutionary line within the Hypnales, primarily diversified in the temperate Southern Hemisphere. Its characteristic features, particularly the strongly differentiated alar cells combined with ecostate leaves and a julaceous habit, highlight morphological specialization likely related to its epiphytic lifestyle. Its distribution pattern contributes to understanding Gondwanan biogeography and the evolution of pleurocarpous mosses.
