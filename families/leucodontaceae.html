Leucodontaceae

Leucodon Moss Family

Leucodontaceae is a family of pleurocarpous mosses characterized by a distinct growth habit involving creeping primary stems that give rise to erect, ascending, or arching secondary stems. These secondary stems are often densely covered in leaves, giving them a characteristic braided, cord-like, or 'squirrel-tail' appearance (especially in the genus Leucodon). They typically grow as epiphytes on tree bark or sometimes on rocks (epilithic), often in relatively exposed or periodically dry habitats.

Overview

The Leucodontaceae family is placed in its own order, Leucodontales, reflecting its distinct characteristics among pleurocarpous mosses. It is a relatively small family, comprising about 5-7 genera and around 20-30 species. The most prominent genus is Leucodon. These mosses are primarily distributed in temperate regions of the Northern Hemisphere, with some representation in the Southern Hemisphere and mountainous tropical areas.

Key features distinguishing the family include the differentiation into creeping primary stems and erect/arching secondary stems, the typically dense, overlapping (imbricate) leaves that lack a costa or have only a very short, weak double one, and smooth leaf cells. Sporophytes are often rare in many species; when present, they typically feature short setae, erect symmetrical capsules, and often reduced or absent peristomes.

Ecologically, Leucodontaceae are significant components of epiphytic communities on deciduous and coniferous trees, and sometimes on rock faces. Their ability to tolerate desiccation makes them well-suited to bark habitats which experience fluctuating moisture levels.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Leucodontaceae
  • Common Name: Leucodon Moss Family
  • Number of Genera: Approximately 5-7
  • Number of Species: Approximately 20-30
  • Distribution: Primarily temperate Northern Hemisphere, also Southern Hemisphere and tropical mountains.
  • Evolutionary Group: Bryophytes - Mosses - Leucodontales

Key Characteristics (Moss Morphology)

Gametophyte: Growth Form and Habit

Plants are pleurocarpous but exhibit differentiation between primary and secondary stems. Primary stems are slender, creeping, and often stoloniform (runner-like) with reduced leaves. Secondary stems arise from the primary stems, are typically erect, ascending, or gracefully arched, often unbranched or sparsely branched, and densely covered with leaves, giving a julaceous (smoothly cylindrical), braided, or cord-like appearance. Plants form mats or tufts, often yellowish-green to brownish-green, sometimes glossy.

Gametophyte: Stems

Secondary stems typically lack a central strand. Paraphyllia are absent. Pseudoparaphyllia may be present but are often indistinct.

Gametophyte: Leaves

Leaves on secondary stems are typically crowded, imbricate (closely overlapping) especially when dry, erect-spreading to spreading when moist. They are usually broadly ovate to ovate-lanceolate, strongly concave, often with longitudinal folds (plicae). Leaf apices are acute to acuminate. Margins are typically plane or recurved below, entire or weakly serrulate near the apex. A key feature is that the costa (nerve) is absent, or very short and double, and weak. Leaf cells are typically smooth, thick-walled, and rhombic to linear; alar cells may be differentiated (quadrate or rectangular) or indistinct.

Sporophyte: Seta, Capsule, and Peristome

Sporophytes are often infrequent. Sexual condition is typically dioicous. The seta is usually short, sometimes very short, holding the capsule close to the perichaetial leaves (modified leaves surrounding the female reproductive structures). The capsule is typically erect and symmetrical, ovoid to oblong-cylindrical. The operculum is conical to rostrate. The peristome is variable but often reduced or absent (gymnostomous). When present, it may be single (appearing like only an exostome) or double, but the endostome (inner layer) is often poorly developed.

Field Identification (Mosses)

Identifying Leucodontaceae relies heavily on recognizing their distinctive growth habit and leaf features:

Primary Identification Features (Field/Hand Lens)

  • Growth habit: Look for mats with erect or arching secondary stems arising from less conspicuous creeping primary stems.
  • 'Braided' or 'Squirrel-tail' appearance: Secondary stems are often densely leafy, appearing like cords or tails.
  • Habitat: Primarily found on tree bark, occasionally on rock, often in exposed sites.
  • Lack of costa: Leaves appear nerveless under a hand lens (or costa is extremely short/faint).
  • Leaf shape/arrangement: Broadly ovate, concave, overlapping leaves.

Secondary Identification Features (Microscope often needed)

  • Costa confirmation: Verify absence or very short/double nature of the costa.
  • Cell structure: Smooth, often thick-walled rhombic to linear cells.
  • Stem cross-section: Absence of a central strand in secondary stems.
  • Sporophyte features: Short seta, erect capsule, reduced/absent peristome (if sporophytes are found).

Common Confusion Points (Other Mosses)

  • Anomodon (Anomodontaceae): Also common on bark/rock, forms mats with ascending stems, but typically has a distinct single costa ending below the apex and often papillose cells.
  • Neckeraceae (some genera): Some form mats or festoons on bark/rock, but often have flattened (complanate) foliage or possess a costa.
  • Hedwigia (Hedwigiaceae): Grows on rock, forms hoary (whitish-tipped) cushions, lacks a costa, but leaves have strongly papillose cells and capsules are immersed on very short setae.
  • Cryphaeaceae: Also epiphytic with erect/pendent secondary stems, but often have costate leaves and immersed or shortly exserted capsules, often with different peristome structures.
  • Pylaisiadelphaceae (some genera): Can be epiphytic but usually have more slender stems, less dense foliage, often possess a costa, and different sporophyte features.

Field Guide Quick Reference (Mosses)

Look For:

  • Pleurocarpous habit with erect/arching secondary stems
  • Dense, braided/cord-like secondary stems
  • Habitat on tree bark or rock
  • Leaves lacking a costa (or very short/faint)
  • Broadly ovate, concave leaves
  • Short seta, erect capsule (if present)

Key Variations:

  • Secondary stem posture (erect vs. arching)
  • Leaf apex (acute vs. acuminate)
  • Presence/absence of sporophytes
  • Plant color (green to yellowish/brownish)

Notable Genera

The family is small, with Leucodon being the most common genus:

Leucodon

Squirrel-tail Moss (Genus)

The type genus and most representative. Forms mats with characteristic arching, densely foliate secondary stems resembling squirrel tails. Leaves broadly ovate-acuminate, concave, plicate, ecostate. Common epiphyte in temperate Northern Hemisphere. L. sciuroides is a well-known species.

Antitrichia

Pendulous Wing Moss (Genus)

Often placed here or in its own family (Antitrichiaceae). Robust, forming large mats or pendent strands on trees or rocks. Secondary stems pinnately branched (unlike typical Leucodon). Leaves ovate-lanceolate with a costa ending mid-leaf or below the apex (unlike typical Leucodontaceae!). Shows taxonomic complexity.

Pterogonium

Slender Wing Moss (Genus)

Sometimes included in Leucodontaceae. Forms dense, often golden-brown mats on rocks or tree bases, typically in Mediterranean climates or western North America. Stems pinnately branched, somewhat flattened. Leaves ovate, ecostate, with recurved margins.

Forsstroemia

(Genus)

Sometimes placed in Leucodontaceae or Cryphaeaceae. Epiphytic mosses with creeping primary stems and erect/pendent secondary stems. Leaves broadly ovate, concave, often ecostate or with a short costa. Capsules often immersed or shortly exserted.

Phylogeny and Classification

Leucodontaceae is currently placed in its own distinct order, Leucodontales, within the Bryopsida class. Historically, its placement has been debated, sometimes linked to Hypnales or Orthotrichales due to similarities in epiphytic habit or peristome features (when present). However, molecular data generally supports its recognition as a separate lineage among the pleurocarpous mosses.

The order Leucodontales is relatively small and includes families like Cryphaeaceae and Fontinalaceae (water mosses), though relationships are still actively researched. Key evolutionary trends within the Leucodontaceae lineage include adaptation to epiphytic and epilithic life, the strong differentiation between primary and secondary stems, the reduction or loss of the leaf costa, and the frequent reduction of the sporophyte, particularly the peristome.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Division: Bryophyta (Mosses)
  • Class: Bryopsida
  • Order: Leucodontales
  • Family: Leucodontaceae

Related Families (Order Leucodontales & Others)

Leucodontaceae's relationships are primarily within the Leucodontales order, but it shares habitats or some features with families in other orders:

  • Cryphaeaceae: Also in Leucodontales. Often epiphytic with differentiated primary/secondary stems, but typically have costate leaves and immersed/shortly exserted capsules.
  • Fontinalaceae: Aquatic mosses (Water Mosses), placed in Leucodontales. Very different habitat and often tristichous (3-ranked) leaves.
  • Neckeraceae (Hypnales): Often epiphytic/epilithic, some have arching stems, but usually have flattened foliage and often possess a costa.
  • Anomodontaceae (Hypnales): Similar habitats, but usually have a single costa and often papillose cells.
  • Hedwigiaceae (Hedwigiales): Epilithic, ecostate mosses forming hoary cushions, but acrocarpous with very different leaf cells and immersed capsules.

Evolutionary Significance

The Leucodontaceae family represents:

  • Specialization for Epiphytism/Epilithism: Shows strong adaptation to life on bark and rock surfaces, including desiccation tolerance.
  • Costa Reduction/Loss: A notable trend in this lineage, contrasting with many other pleurocarpous groups where the costa is retained.
  • Sporophyte Reduction: The frequent rarity of sporophytes and the reduction of seta length and peristome complexity in many species suggest alternative reproductive strategies (e.g., fragmentation) may be important.
  • Distinct Growth Architecture: The differentiation into creeping primary and erect/arching secondary stems is a key feature of their life strategy.
