Lindsaeaceae

Lindsaea Fern Family

Lindsaeaceae is a family of mostly small to medium-sized terrestrial ferns (also some epiphytes and climbers) belonging to the order Polypodiales. The family includes several genera, with Lindsaea being the largest. They typically grow from creeping rhizomes bearing narrow scales and often have distinctively shaped pinnules (e.g., asymmetric, wedge-shaped). A key feature is their marginal or submarginal sori protected by indusia that open towards the frond edge. Lindsaeaceae has a pantropical distribution.

Overview

Lindsaeaceae represents another early diverging lineage within the vast Polypodiales order. These ferns are found primarily in tropical and subtropical regions worldwide, inhabiting a range of moist forest environments, often on soil banks, rocks, or as low epiphytes on tree trunks. While some species can be large or even climbing (Odontosoria), many are relatively small and delicate.

They typically grow from short- to long-creeping rhizomes covered in characteristic narrow scales, which often bear a terminal bristle (seta). The fronds are quite variable in dissection, from simple to 4-pinnate, but many species, especially in the large genus Lindsaea, exhibit distinctive asymmetric, wedge-shaped, or fan-shaped pinnules. The stipes (leaf stalks) are often dark, slender, and wiry.

Reproductively, Lindsaeaceae are homosporous. Their sori are located along the frond margin (marginal or submarginal) and are typically linear, either continuous along the edge or interrupted into shorter segments. A key diagnostic feature is the indusium, which is attached at the base (and sometimes sides) and opens outwards, towards the frond margin (extrorse).

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Lindsaeaceae
  • Common Name: Lindsaea Fern Family
  • Number of Genera: Approximately 6-7 (e.g., Lindsaea, Odontosoria, Sphenomeris)
  • Number of Species: Approximately 200-250
  • Distribution: Pantropical, extending into some temperate regions.
  • Key Features: Mostly terrestrial, creeping rhizomes with narrow scales (often setiferous), often asymmetric/cuneate pinnules, marginal/submarginal sori with indusia opening outwards (extrorse), homosporous.
  • Evolutionary Group: Polypodiopsida (Leptosporangiate Ferns) - Polypodiales

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Mostly small to medium-sized terrestrial ferns, but includes some epiphytes and climbers (e.g., Odontosoria). Rhizomes are typically slender, short- to long-creeping (rarely erect), and bear scales.

Fronds (Leaves)

Frond morphology is diverse within the family.

  • Structure: Variable, from simple to 1-pinnate to 4-pinnate.
  • Pinnule Shape: Often characteristic, especially in Lindsaea, where pinnules are frequently asymmetric, wedge-shaped (cuneate), or fan-shaped (flabellate), attached by a narrow base. In Sphenomeris, segments are finely divided and wedge-shaped.
  • Texture: Typically herbaceous.
  • Vernation: Young fronds show circinate vernation.
  • Petioles (Stipes): Often dark, glossy, and wiry (stramineous to black). Usually glabrous above the base.

Fronds are generally monomorphic.

Indument

Rhizomes are covered in narrow scales, which are non-clathrate and often possess a single, elongated, stiff apical cell (seta). Stipes and fronds are typically glabrous (lacking scales and hairs) beyond the very base.

Sori and Indusial Characters

Lindsaeaceae are homosporous. Sori are characteristically positioned along the frond margin.

  • Sorus Position: Sori are marginal or submarginal, borne on a vascular commissure connecting the tips of usually free veins.
  • Sorus Shape: Often linear and continuous along the margin, or interrupted into shorter linear or lunate (moon-shaped) segments.
  • Indusium: Sori are protected by an indusium that opens towards the margin (extrorse). The indusium is typically attached along its base and sometimes partially along the sides, formed from modified laminar tissue, and conforms to the shape of the sorus (linear or lunate).
  • Receptacle: Sporangia are attached to the vascular commissure, which acts as the receptacle.

Spores

Spores are variable, either trilete (tetrahedral) or monolete (bilateral), depending on the genus and species. Surface ornamentation also varies.

Field Identification

Identifying Lindsaeaceae often involves looking for the combination of creeping rhizomes with narrow scales, characteristic pinnule shapes (especially in Lindsaea), and marginal sori with indusia opening outwards.

Primary Identification Features

  • Habit: Mostly terrestrial ferns with creeping rhizomes.
  • Indument: Narrow scales (often with apical seta) on rhizome; fronds usually glabrous.
  • Pinnule Shape: Often asymmetric, wedge-shaped, or fan-shaped (especially Lindsaea).
  • Marginal/Submarginal Sori: Sori located at or very near the edge, often linear (continuous or interrupted).
  • Extrorse Indusium: Indusium present and opening towards the frond margin.

Common Confusion Points

  • Pteridaceae (especially Adiantum, Pteris): Many Pteridaceae also have marginal or submarginal sporangia. However, Adiantum (maidenhairs) typically has sori covered by a reflexed flap of the leaf margin (false indusium). Other Pteridaceae often have continuous marginal sori lacking a true inner indusium (sporangia protected only by the margin) or different indument/habit.
  • Hymenophyllaceae (Filmy Ferns): Have marginal sori and indusia, but are distinguished by their very thin, often translucent fronds (usually one cell thick) and different indusial shapes (tubular, bivalvate).
  • Saccolomataceae: Has marginal sori but with distinct pouch-shaped indusia and different scales/habit.
  • Dennstaedtiaceae: Some have marginal sori, but indusia are often cup-shaped or formed differently, and indument is typically hairy.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Creeping rhizome with narrow scales
  • Fronds usually glabrous
  • Pinnules often asymmetric/wedge-shaped
  • Marginal/submarginal sori (often linear)
  • Indusium opening outwards
  • Pantropical moist forests

Distinguish From:

  • Pteridaceae (false indusia, scales/hairs)
  • Filmy Ferns (texture, indusia)
  • Saccolomataceae (pouch indusia, scales)
  • Dennstaedtiaceae (often hairy, sori)

Notable Examples

The family includes several genera, with Lindsaea being the most diverse.

Lindsaea

(Lindsaea ferns)

The largest genus, characterized by its often 1-pinnate or 2-pinnate fronds with distinctively asymmetric, wedge-shaped, or fan-shaped pinnules. Sori are typically marginal and follow the upper/outer edge of the pinnules. Found throughout the tropics.

Odontosoria

(Climbing Lindsaea ferns)

This genus includes species with long, scrambling or climbing fronds that are highly divided (3-4 pinnate). The ultimate segments are often small and wedge-shaped. Found in the Neotropics and some Pacific islands.

Sphenomeris

(Wedgelet Fern)

Characterized by finely dissected fronds (3-4 pinnate) with ultimate segments that are narrow and wedge-shaped (cuneate). Sori are submarginal at the segment tips. Sphenomeris chinensis is widespread in the paleotropics.

Phylogeny and Classification

Lindsaeaceae is placed within the order Polypodiales. Along with Saccolomataceae, it represents one of the earliest diverging lineages within the order, branching off before the main radiation of eupolypod ferns. Its exact relationship to Saccolomataceae and the rest of the Polypodiales (specifically the eupolypods I clade) is a subject of ongoing research, but it is consistently recovered near the base of the order.

The family comprises several distinct genera that form a monophyletic group, supported by both molecular data and shared morphological traits like the marginal sori with extrorse indusia and characteristic rhizome scales.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Clade: Tracheophytes (Vascular plants)
  • Class: Polypodiopsida (Leptosporangiate ferns)
  • Order: Polypodiales
  • Family: Lindsaeaceae

Related Families (within Polypodiales)

As an early diverging lineage in Polypodiales, it is related to all other families in the order. Comparisons are often made with:

  • Saccolomataceae: Another early diverging family, possibly sister to Lindsaeaceae, but with different soral structure (pouch-like indusia) and scales.
  • Dennstaedtiaceae: A large, diverse family also diverging relatively early, but with different soral types and often hairy indument.
  • Pteridaceae: A large family within eupolypods I, frequently having marginal sori but typically lacking a true inner indusium opening outwards.
  • (Related to all other families within Polypodiales).

Evolutionary Significance

Lindsaeaceae is significant for:

  • Basal Polypodiales Lineage: Crucial for understanding the early evolution and diversification of the largest fern order.
  • Morphological Diversity: Exhibits considerable variation in frond dissection and habit (terrestrial, epiphytic, climbing) within the family.
  • Soral Characters: The marginal sorus with an extrorse indusium is a key character state found in several unrelated fern groups, highlighting convergent evolution or retention of ancestral traits.
