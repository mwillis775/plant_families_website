Makinoaceae

No widely accepted common name

Makinoaceae is a monotypic family of simple thalloid liverworts (order Metzgeriales or Makinoales), containing only Makinoa crispata. It is endemic to East Asia and characterized by its large, dichotomously branched thallus and unique reproductive structures where archegonia are borne underneath specialized ascending dorsal branches.

Overview Characteristics Identification Examples Phylogeny

Overview

The Makinoaceae family comprises a single species, Makinoa crispata, making it monotypic. This distinctive liverwort belongs to the simple thalloid grade and is endemic to East Asia (Japan, Korea, China, Taiwan, Philippines). It typically grows in moist, shaded environments, often found on wet rocks, soil banks, or humus near streams and waterfalls in forested areas.

Makinoa possesses a relatively large, robust, prostrate thallus that branches dichotomously and lacks complex internal differentiation like air chambers or pores. Its most remarkable feature lies in its reproductive structures. Unlike most simple thalloids where archegonia are superficial or protected by simple scales or flaps, in Makinoa, the archegonia are located on the ventral (underside) surface of specialized, ascending, flap-like or horn-like branches that arise from the dorsal surface of the main thallus. This unique arrangement for archegonial protection sets the family apart.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Makinoaceae Nakai
  • Common Name: (None widely used)
  • Number of Genera: 1 (Makinoa)
  • Number of Species: 1 (Makinoa crispata)
  • Distribution: East Asia (Japan, Korea, China, Taiwan, Philippines). Found in moist, shaded habitats, often on rocks or soil near water.
  • Evolutionary Group: Bryophytes - Liverworts (Marchantiophyta) - Jungermanniopsida - Metzgeriales (or Makinoales)

Key Characteristics

Note: Makinoaceae are simple thalloid liverworts with unique reproductive morphology.

Gametophyte (Thallus) Structure

The dominant phase is the simple thallus:

  • Form: Large, prostrate, relatively thick and somewhat fleshy thallus, branching dichotomously. Margins are often undulate or crisped (hence 'crispata').
  • Texture & Colour: Typically deep green to somewhat brownish-green, opaque.
  • Internal Structure: Simple and undifferentiated parenchyma tissue. Lacks air chambers and pores. May have a poorly defined central strand.
  • Rhizoids: Numerous, arising from the ventral surface, typically hyaline or brownish.

Reproductive Structures

Species are dioicous (separate male and female plants).

  • Antheridia: Male organs are sunken in pits within distinct, often elongated or crescent-shaped receptacles located on the dorsal surface of male thalli.
  • Archegonia: Female organs have a unique arrangement. They are borne in clusters on the ventral surface (underside) of specialized, erect or ascending, flap-like or somewhat horn-shaped branches/innovations that arise from the dorsal surface of the female thallus, typically near the apex. These specialized archegonial branches protect the developing archegonia.

Sporophyte

The sporophyte develops protected beneath the specialized archegonial branch:

  • Foot: Embedded in the tissue of the archegonial branch.
  • Seta: Distinct, elongating considerably at maturity, pale and relatively thick, pushing the capsule out from beneath the protective branch.
  • Capsule: Globose to ovoid, dark brown to black when mature. Capsule wall is multistratose.
  • Dehiscence: Capsule typically splits open longitudinally into four valves.
  • Protection: Developing sporophyte is protected by the overarching archegonial branch and the calyptra.

Spores and Elaters

Contained within the capsule.

  • Spores: Relatively large, spherical, with reticulate or complex surface ornamentation.
  • Elaters: Present, long, typically with 2 spiral thickenings, aiding spore dispersal.

Field Identification

Identifying Makinoaceae (Makinoa crispata) relies on recognizing its large simple thallus, East Asian distribution, moist shaded habitat, and especially the unique ascending archegonial branches.

Primary Identification Features

  • Large Simple Thallus: Robust, dichotomously branched thallus lacking pores/chambers, often with crisped margins.
  • Ascending Archegonial Branches: The most diagnostic feature (on female plants) is the presence of specialized, erect or ascending, flap-like or horn-like branches arising from the dorsal surface. Archegonia and sporophytes develop underneath these structures.
  • Habitat & Distribution: Found in moist, shaded places (wet rocks, stream banks) exclusively in East Asia.
  • Sporophyte Position: Mature sporophytes emerge on long setae from beneath the specialized dorsal branches.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Dichotomous Branching: Thallus typically forks repeatedly.
  • Crisped Margins: Thallus edges often undulate.
  • Antheridial Receptacles: Distinct dorsal receptacles on male plants.

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Year-round: Vegetative thalli can be found year-round in suitable moist habitats.
  • Reproductive Periods: Look for the distinctive archegonial branches on female plants or antheridial receptacles on male plants. Sporophytes may mature seasonally.

Common Confusion Points

Makinoa might be confused with:

  • Other Large Simple Thalloids (e.g., Pellia, Noteroclada, Aneura): These lack the unique, ascending dorsal branches bearing archegonia ventrally. Their archegonia are typically superficial or protected by simpler involucres (flaps, scales, tubes) directly associated with the main thallus surface. Distribution also differs (Pellia is widespread N Hemisphere, Noteroclada S Hemisphere).
  • Complex Thalloids (Marchantiales): Easily distinguished by the presence of air pores and chambers, and different reproductive structures (e.g., stalked archegoniophores in Marchantia, ventral involucres in Targionia).
  • Hornworts (Anthocerotophyta): Have different cell structure and distinctive horn-like sporophytes.

Key differentiator: The presence of the specialized, ascending dorsal branches with archegonia/sporophytes developing underneath is unique to Makinoa among simple thalloids.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Large, simple, dichotomous thallus
  • Crisped margins (common)
  • Ascending dorsal branches (female plants)
  • Archegonia/sporophytes under these branches
  • Moist, shaded habitat (East Asia)

Key Distinctions:

  • Archegonia position unique (under dorsal branches)
  • Simple thallus (unlike Marchantiales)
  • Sporophyte is capsule on seta (unlike hornworts)
  • Distribution restricted to East Asia

Notable Examples

The family is monotypic, containing only one species.

Makinoa crispata

(Makinoa)

The sole representative of the family, endemic to East Asia. It displays all the characteristic features: a large, simple, crisped thallus and the unique reproductive strategy with archegonia borne underneath specialized ascending dorsal branches. Found in cool, moist, shaded habitats.

Phylogeny and Classification

Makinoaceae represents a distinct and somewhat isolated lineage within the simple thalloid liverworts. Traditionally placed in the order Metzgeriales, its unique reproductive morphology has led some classifications to place it in its own monotypic order, Makinoales. Molecular studies generally support its placement within the broader Metzgeriales complex but confirm its status as a distinct, early-diverging lineage within that group, not closely allied with families like Pelliaceae or Aneuraceae.

The evolution of the specialized archegonial branches is a major autapomorphy (unique derived character) of the family. This structure represents a novel way to protect the developing female gametes and sporophyte compared to the involucres or perianths found in other liverwort groups. Its phylogenetic isolation suggests a long independent evolutionary history.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Division: Marchantiophyta (Liverworts)
  • Class: Jungermanniopsida
  • Order: Metzgeriales (or Makinoales)
  • Family: Makinoaceae

Related Families

Within the Metzgeriales complex, Makinoaceae is phylogenetically distinct. Its relationships to other families are not always clear, but it is generally considered distant from:

  • Pelliaceae: (Pellia) Differs in reproductive structures (apical involucre) and overall morphology.
  • Aneuraceae: (Aneura, Riccardia) Typically more branched, often smaller, different reproductive structures.
  • Metzgeriaceae: (Metzgeria) Distinct thallus with unistratose wings and a midrib.
  • Fossombroniaceae / Allisoniaceae / Petalophyllaceae: These families (sometimes placed in Fossombroniales) represent different morphological lines (pseudo-leafy or different thallus/reproductive structures).

It is fundamentally different from complex thalloids (Marchantiales) and leafy liverworts (Jungermanniales).

Evolutionary Significance

Makinoaceae is significant for:

  • Unique Reproductive Morphology: Demonstrates an unusual and highly specialized method of archegonial protection among liverworts.
  • Phylogenetic Isolation: Represents a distinct, relatively ancient lineage within the simple thalloid liverworts, contributing to understanding the deep branching patterns in liverwort evolution.
  • Biogeography: Its restricted East Asian distribution provides insights into regional plant diversification and endemism.
