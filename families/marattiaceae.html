Marattiaceae

Giant Fern Family / Marattia Family

Marattiaceae is the only extant family in the ancient order Marattiales, representing a lineage of eusporangiate ferns distinct from the common leptosporangiate ferns. These are typically large terrestrial ferns, often possessing massive, fleshy rhizomes and enormous, complexly divided fronds (leaves). A key characteristic is the presence of large, thick-walled sporangia that are usually fused into structures called synangia on the underside of fertile fronds. The family is found throughout tropical regions worldwide.

Overview

Marattiaceae represents an ancient lineage of ferns, the Marattiales, which were much more diverse during the Carboniferous and Permian periods. Today, this family stands apart from the vast majority of modern ferns (leptosporangiates) due to its eusporangiate development â€“ sporangia arise from multiple cells and have thick walls, producing a large number of spores.

These ferns are often called "giant ferns" due to the impressive size many species attain. They possess substantial, fleshy underground stems (rhizomes), which in some species can become short, trunk-like structures. Their fronds are among the largest in the plant kingdom, sometimes reaching several meters in length. Unique features include the swollen bases of the petioles (frond stalks) flanked by persistent, fleshy stipule-like structures called auricles, and the aggregation of sporangia into synangia (fused structures) or tight clusters on the fertile fronds. They inhabit humid, shaded forest floors in tropical regions across the globe.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Marattiaceae
  • Common Name: Giant Fern Family, Marattia Family
  • Number of Genera: Approximately 6
  • Number of Species: Approximately 150-200
  • Distribution: Pantropical
  • Evolutionary Group: Ferns (Division Pteridophyta / Monilophyta, Class Polypodiopsida, Subclass Marattiidae, Order Marattiales, Type: Eusporangiate)

Key Characteristics

Rhizome and Habit

Plants are terrestrial ferns with massive, fleshy rhizomes (underground stems) that are typically radial or dorsiventral in symmetry. Rhizomes can be creeping or erect, sometimes forming short, stout trunks covered in persistent petiole bases and auricles. Roots are thick and fleshy.

Fronds (Leaves)

Fronds are typically very large, sometimes exceeding 5 meters in length. They are usually pinnately compound, ranging from 1-pinnate to 5-pinnate. Young fronds exhibit circinate vernation (coiling into a "fiddlehead"). The petiole (frond stalk) is thick and fleshy, characteristically swollen at the base, and flanked by a pair of large, persistent, fleshy stipule-like outgrowths called auricles or stipules. Venation is usually free (veins dichotomously branching but not interconnecting), except in Christensenia where it is reticulate (net-like).

Reproductive Structures (Sporangia and Synangia)

Reproduction is via spores produced in eusporangia. These are large sporangia developing from multiple initial cells, having thick walls (more than one cell layer thick), and producing hundreds to thousands of spores each. They lack the specialized ring of cells for dehiscence (annulus) found in leptosporangiate ferns.

The sporangia are borne on the abaxial (lower) surface of fertile pinnae (leaflets). In most genera (e.g., Marattia, Danaea, Christensenia, Eupodium, Ptisana), the sporangia are fused laterally into distinct structures called synangia, which can be elongated along veins or circular. In Angiopteris, the sporangia are arranged in dense clusters (sori) but remain separate or only slightly coherent at the base, not fully fused into synangia. Sporangia dehisce via apical pores or slits.

Spores are typically monolete (having a single scar line) and numerous. The gametophyte generation develops from the spore and is a relatively large, surface-dwelling, photosynthetic thallus, often long-lived and associated with endophytic fungi.

Field Identification

Identifying Marattiaceae relies on recognizing their status as large, terrestrial ferns with several unique features, particularly at the base of the fronds and on the fertile leaflets.

Primary Identification Features

  • Large Fern Habit: Typically robust, large terrestrial ferns.
  • Massive Fleshy Rhizome: Underground stem is substantial and fleshy.
  • Swollen Petiole Base with Auricles: The base of the frond stalk is noticeably swollen and flanked by a pair of persistent, fleshy stipule-like structures (auricles). This is highly diagnostic.
  • Large Compound Fronds: Leaves are usually large and pinnately divided multiple times.
  • Synangia or Dense Sporangial Clusters: Look on the underside of fertile fronds for eusporangia fused into elongated or circular synangia (most genera) or tightly clustered but separate sporangia (Angiopteris). Sporangia lack an annulus.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Circinate Vernation: Developing fronds uncoil as fiddleheads.
  • Free Venation (usually): Veins typically branch but do not form a network (except Christensenia).
  • Tropical Habitat: Found in humid forests in tropical regions worldwide.

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Year-Round Presence: In tropical climates, mature fronds are typically present year-round.
  • Fiddleheads: Look for large, coiled fiddleheads during periods of active growth.
  • Fertile Fronds: Identification is greatly aided by finding fertile fronds bearing the characteristic synangia or sporangial clusters on the underside.

Common Confusion Points

  • Tree Ferns (e.g., Cyatheaceae, Dicksoniaceae): Can be large but are leptosporangiate, have sori with indusia (protective flaps) or exindusiate sori containing small sporangia with an annulus, and typically have scaly rhizomes and petioles (lacking fleshy auricles).
  • Osmundaceae (Royal Fern Family): Also large ferns, some considered intermediate between eusporangiate and leptosporangiate, but have distinct fertile portions of the frond or separate fertile fronds, and sporangia are not fused into synangia. Petiole bases lack fleshy auricles.
  • Other Large Tropical Ferns: Various large leptosporangiate ferns exist; check for the presence of an annulus on the sporangia (requires magnification) and the absence of fleshy auricles and synangia.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Very large terrestrial fern
  • Massive, fleshy rhizome
  • Swollen petiole base + fleshy auricles
  • Large, pinnately compound fronds
  • Synangia (fused sporangia) or dense clusters of large sporangia on underside
  • Lack of annulus on sporangia
  • Tropical forest habitat

Key Variations:

  • Frond dissection (1-pinnate to 5-pinnate)
  • Synangia shape (elongate vs. circular)
  • Sporangia fused (most genera) vs. clustered (Angiopteris)
  • Venation free vs. reticulate (Christensenia)

Notable Examples

The family includes several distinct genera, differing mainly in synangium structure and venation.

Angiopteris Hoffm.

Giant Fern, Mule's Foot Fern (Genus; species include A. evecta)

Characterized by enormous fronds and sporangia arranged in dense clusters (sori) near the leaflet margin, but not fused into true synangia. Possesses the typical swollen petiole base with large auricles. Widely distributed in the Paleotropics.

Marattia Sw.

Marattia Fern (Genus; species include M. laevis, M. douglasii - King Fern)

The type genus, featuring large fronds and distinct, boat-shaped or elliptical synangia containing two rows of sporangia that open by slits. Found in tropical regions worldwide.

Danaea Sm.

Danaea Fern (Genus; species include D. nodosa, D. elliptica)

Distinctive genus with typically 1-pinnate fronds (simpler than others). Fertile fronds often look different from sterile ones. Has very long, linear synangia covering much of the underside of fertile pinnae. Restricted to the Neotropics.

Christensenia Maxon

(Genus; species C. aesculifolia)

Unique within the family for its palmately compound fronds (resembling a horse chestnut leaf) and reticulate venation (veins forming a network). Has circular synangia scattered on the frond underside. Found in Southeast Asia and Malesia.

Phylogeny and Classification

Marattiaceae is the sole surviving family of the order Marattiales, an ancient lineage of ferns that diverged early in fern evolution. They belong to the subclass Marattiidae, which is sister to the Polypodiidae (containing the vast majority of modern ferns, the leptosporangiates). Marattiales represents one of the main lineages of eusporangiate ferns.

Their fossil record extends back to the Carboniferous period, indicating they are a very old group. Their unique combination of features (eusporangia, synangia, auricles) clearly separates them from other living fern groups. Relationships among the extant genera within Marattiaceae have been clarified by molecular studies.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Division: Pteridophyta / Monilophyta (Ferns and Allies)
  • Class: Polypodiopsida
  • Subclass: Marattiidae
  • Order: Marattiales
  • Family: Marattiaceae

Related Groups

Marattiales is an isolated order. Its sister group is the subclass Polypodiidae (leptosporangiate ferns).

  • Polypodiidae: The largest subclass of ferns, characterized by leptosporangiate development (sporangia from a single cell, thin walls, annulus present). Includes familiar families like Polypodiaceae, Pteridaceae, Dryopteridaceae, Aspleniaceae, Cyatheaceae etc.
  • Other Eusporangiate Lineages: Other groups of ferns also exhibiting eusporangiate development exist but are more distantly related to Marattiales:
    • Ophioglossales: Adder's-tongues and Grape ferns (Subclass Ophioglossidae).
    • Psilotales: Whisk ferns (Subclass Ophioglossidae or sister to it).
  • Equisetidae: Horsetails represent another distinct subclass within Polypodiopsida.

Note: Marattiaceae is the only extant family within the order Marattiales.

Evolutionary Significance

Marattiaceae holds significant evolutionary importance:

  • Ancient Lineage: Represents a lineage of ferns with deep evolutionary roots, providing insights into early fern diversification and morphology.
  • Eusporangiate Development: Exhibits the ancestral type of sporangium development found in early land plants, contrasting with the derived leptosporangium of most modern ferns.
  • Gigantism: Shows the potential for large size in ferns, with some of the largest fronds known.
  • Unique Structures: Features like synangia and petiole auricles are unique evolutionary innovations within this group.
  • Phylogenetic Benchmark: Serves as a key reference point for understanding the relationships between major fern groups and other vascular plants.
