Mazaceae

(Mazus Family - informal)

Mazaceae is a small family of flowering plants in the order Lamiales (Asterids), comprising primarily small, annual or perennial herbs. The family includes about 3-4 genera and 30-40 species, mostly native to Asia, Australia, and New Zealand, often found in moist, open, or disturbed habitats. They are characterized by their often low-growing habit and distinctly bilabiate (two-lipped) flowers.

Overview

Mazaceae is a relatively small family established based on molecular phylogenetic data, separating these genera from the large, historically heterogeneous Scrophulariaceae family or sometimes from Phrymaceae. The core genus is Mazus, which contains most of the species. These plants are typically small herbs, often growing as annuals or perennials that form basal rosettes or spread via creeping stems (stolons). They commonly occur in grasslands, along streams, in moist open areas, disturbed ground, and sometimes in alpine environments across their range in Asia and Australasia.

The flowers, though small, are quite distinctive, being strongly zygomorphic and two-lipped, often resembling miniature snapdragons or monkeyflowers. The lower lip frequently has a prominent raised area (palate) near the throat. Some species, like Mazus reptans, are occasionally cultivated as groundcover plants in temperate gardens.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Mazaceae
  • Common Name: Mazus Family (informal)
  • Number of Genera: Approximately 3-4
  • Number of Species: Approximately 30-40
  • Distribution: Asia, Australia, New Zealand.
  • Habit: Small annual or perennial herbs, often creeping or forming rosettes.
  • Evolutionary Group: Eudicots - Asterids (Lamiids) - Lamiales

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Small, annual or perennial herbs. Habit is often low-growing, either forming basal rosettes of leaves or spreading via creeping stems (stolons) that root at the nodes. Stems are typically herbaceous.

Leaves

Leaves are simple, usually arranged oppositely near the base of the plant and sometimes becoming alternate higher up on flowering stems, or they may be entirely in a basal rosette. Leaf margins are typically toothed (dentate or serrate) or sometimes lobed. Stipules are absent.

Inflorescence

Flowers are borne in terminal or axillary racemes, or sometimes occur solitarily in the axils of leaves.

Flowers

Flowers are distinctly zygomorphic (bilaterally symmetrical), bisexual, and 5-merous.

  • Calyx: 5 sepals, fused at the base into a tube, with 5 distinct lobes. The calyx is often persistent and sometimes enlarges slightly as the fruit develops.
  • Corolla: 5 petals fused into a tube (sympetalous) with a distinctly bilabiate (two-lipped) limb. The upper lip is typically 2-lobed and erect or reflexed. The lower lip is larger, spreading, 3-lobed, and usually features two prominent longitudinal ridges or swellings in the throat known as a palate, which may partially close the opening. Nectar guides (spots or lines) may be present on the lower lip. Corolla colors are commonly blue, purple, lilac, white, or yellow.
  • Androecium: 4 fertile stamens, arranged in two pairs of unequal length (didynamous). They are attached to the corolla tube (epipetalous). The anthers of each pair often touch or stick together (connivent) beneath the upper lip. A fifth (posterior) staminode is typically absent.
  • Gynoecium: The ovary is superior, composed of 2 fused carpels forming a 2-locular ovary. Placentation is axile, with numerous ovules attached to the central axis in each locule. A single slender style arises from the ovary, terminating in a sensitive, 2-lobed stigma that closes when touched (a feature common in related Lamiales families).

Fruits and Seeds

The fruit is typically a loculicidal capsule, splitting open along the partitions into the locules to release the seeds. The capsule is often contained within the persistent calyx. Seeds are numerous and small, frequently with a reticulated or patterned seed coat.

Field Identification

Identifying Mazaceae involves recognizing the combination of small herbaceous habit, leaf arrangement, and the characteristic two-lipped flower structure:

Primary Identification Features

  • Small Herbaceous Habit: Often low-growing, creeping, or forming basal rosettes.
  • Zygomorphic, Bilabiate Flowers: Flowers clearly two-lipped, resembling small snapdragons.
  • Palate on Lower Lip: Look for the two ridges or swellings near the throat of the lower corolla lip.
  • 4 Didynamous Stamens: Two pairs of stamens of different lengths inside the corolla tube.
  • Opposite/Alternate or Basal Leaves: Leaves often opposite below, alternate above, or all basal; margins usually toothed.
  • Superior Ovary: Ovary positioned above other floral parts.
  • Sensitive Stigma: The 2-lobed stigma closes when touched (may require magnification and gentle probing).
  • Asian/Australasian Distribution: Primarily found in this geographic region.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Capsule Fruit: Often enclosed in the persistent calyx.
  • Habitat: Typically moist, open, or disturbed areas.

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Flowering Season: Varies by species and climate, often spring through summer or during monsoon periods. Flowers are essential for identification.
  • Fruiting Season: Capsules develop after flowering.
  • Vegetative Season: The habit (rosette or creeping) and leaf arrangement/shape can be suggestive.

Common Confusion Points

Mazaceae flowers resemble those of several other families within Lamiales:

  • Phrymaceae (Lopseed Family): Very closely related; includes Mimulus (Monkeyflowers) which have similar bilabiate flowers with palates and 4 stamens. Distinctions can be subtle, sometimes involving capsule dehiscence details, calyx shape, or molecular data. Mazus was sometimes placed in Phrymaceae.
  • Plantaginaceae (Plantain Family - some genera): Includes genera like Linaria (Toadflax) and Antirrhinum (Snapdragon) with strongly bilabiate flowers, often with a palate, and 4 stamens. These often differ in capsule type, seed morphology, or vegetative features.
  • Scrophulariaceae (Figwort Family - sensu stricto): Now a smaller family, but some members have zygomorphic, bilabiate flowers and 4 stamens. Differ in details of floral structure, placentation, or capsule type.
  • Lamiaceae (Mint Family): Also have bilabiate flowers and often 4 didynamous stamens, but typically possess square stems, consistently opposite leaves, aromatic oils, and fruit consisting of 4 nutlets (not a capsule).
  • Linderniaceae (False Pimpernel Family): Small herbs with bilabiate flowers, often 4 or 2 stamens. Differ in details of stamen structure (e.g., appendages), stigma shape (often sensitive flat lobes vs. closing lobes), and capsule shape.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Small herb (rosette or creeping)
  • Opposite/alternate or basal leaves, often toothed
  • Zygomorphic, bilabiate flowers
  • Lower lip usually with a palate
  • 4 didynamous stamens
  • Superior ovary (2 carpels)
  • Sensitive 2-lobed stigma
  • Capsule fruit
  • Asia/Australasia distribution

Key Variations:

  • (Relatively small family - variations between species)
  • Flower color (blue, purple, white, yellow)
  • Habit (rosette vs. creeping)
  • Leaf shape and toothing
  • Palate prominence

Notable Examples

The main genus representing the family is Mazus:

Mazus reptans

Creeping Mazus

A low-growing perennial herb native to the Himalayan region. It spreads via creeping stems (stolons) to form dense mats. Produces small, purplish-blue flowers with a white and yellow palate. Commonly cultivated as a groundcover or rock garden plant in temperate climates.

Mazus pumilus

Japanese Mazus

An annual species, possibly native to Asia and Australasia, but now a widespread weed in many parts of the world, often found in lawns and disturbed moist ground. It typically forms a basal rosette of leaves and sends up short racemes of small blue-violet flowers.

Lancea spp.

(Lancea)

A small genus of perennial herbs found in the Himalayas and western China, often in alpine meadows. They typically form rosettes of leaves and bear relatively large (for the family) purplish flowers in short racemes. Sometimes included within Mazaceae or placed in Phrymaceae.

Phylogeny and Classification

Mazaceae belongs to the large order Lamiales, within the Asterid clade (specifically Lamiids or Euasterids I). This order is characterized by prevalent zygomorphic flowers, often fused petals, and superior ovaries.

The recognition of Mazaceae as a distinct family is relatively recent, based on molecular phylogenetic analyses. Genera like Mazus and Lancea were traditionally placed within Scrophulariaceae (a historically very large and heterogeneous family) or sometimes within Phrymaceae. Molecular data showed that these genera form a distinct clade more closely related to Phrymaceae, Paulowniaceae, and Orobanchaceae than to Scrophulariaceae sensu stricto. Establishing Mazaceae helps create more natural, monophyletic family groupings within the complex Lamiales order.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Clade: Angiosperms (Flowering plants)
  • Clade: Eudicots
  • Clade: Asterids
  • Clade: Lamiids (Euasterids I)
  • Order: Lamiales
  • Family: Mazaceae

Related Families

Within the order Lamiales, Mazaceae is closely related to:

  • Phrymaceae (Lopseed family): Includes lopseeds (Phryma) and monkeyflowers (Mimulus sensu lato); herbs often with opposite leaves, zygomorphic bilabiate flowers, 4 stamens, superior ovary, capsule fruit. Very closely related.
  • Paulowniaceae (Empress Tree family): Small family, mostly the tree genus Paulownia.
  • Orobanchaceae (Broomrape family): Large family including many parasitic plants (hemi- and holoparasites), flowers often zygomorphic/bilabiate.
  • Lamiaceae (Mint family): Herbs/shrubs, square stems, opposite leaves, aromatic, zygomorphic flowers, fruit of 4 nutlets.
  • Plantaginaceae (Plantain family): Very diverse family including snapdragons, veronicas, plantains.
  • Scrophulariaceae (Figwort family): Now smaller family, zygomorphic flowers.
  • Verbenaceae (Verbena family): Herbs/shrubs, often opposite leaves, zygomorphic flowers.
  • Acanthaceae, Bignoniaceae, Gesneriaceae, Pedaliaceae, Martyniaceae: Other major families within Lamiales.

Evolutionary Significance

Mazaceae, though small, is significant for:

  • Phylogenetic resolution in Lamiales: Its recognition helps clarify relationships within a complex part of the Lamiales order, contributing to a more natural classification system based on evolutionary history.
  • Biogeography: Its distribution primarily in Asia and Australasia provides data points for understanding the historical biogeography of Lamiales.
  • Floral evolution: Exhibits typical Lamiales features like zygomorphic, bilabiate flowers, didynamous stamens, and a sensitive stigma, showcasing adaptations for specialized insect pollination.
  • Habit diversity: Includes both rosette-forming and creeping/stoloniferous habits, showing adaptation to different ground-level niches.
