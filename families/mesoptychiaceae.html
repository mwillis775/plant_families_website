Mesoptychiaceae

Mesoptychia Family

Mesoptychiaceae is a family of leafy liverworts, primarily distributed in the Northern Hemisphere, that was recently segregated from the large family Lophoziaceae. It includes genera like Mesoptychia (formerly part of Lophozia). These plants typically have succubous, bilobed leaves, lack underleaves, and are often characterized by brownish or purplish pigmentation and the presence of both a perianth and a basal perigynium.

Overview

The Mesoptychiaceae family represents a relatively recent taxonomic refinement within the Jungermanniales, carved out from the diverse Lophoziaceae based largely on molecular phylogenetic evidence and subtle but consistent morphological features, particularly in the reproductive structures. The family mainly comprises the genus Mesoptychia, which includes species formerly classified under Lophozia subgenus Leiocolea, along with the genus Protolophozia.

These liverworts are predominantly found in temperate, boreal, arctic, and alpine regions of the Northern Hemisphere. They typically grow as small to medium-sized plants forming mats or turfs on soil, humus, or rock crevices. While vegetatively similar to many Lophoziaceae (succubous, usually bilobed leaves; absent or tiny underleaves; often pigmented cell walls; frequent gemmae production), a key distinguishing feature is the consistent development of a perigynium – a low, fleshy ring or collar of stem tissue – at the base of the well-developed, plicate perianth. This structure slightly elevates the archegonia and developing sporophyte. Many species also show a preference for base-rich or calcareous substrates, although this is not universal.

Recognizing Mesoptychiaceae highlights the ongoing progress in understanding liverwort evolution and the importance of integrating molecular data with detailed morphological study, especially of reproductive structures, to define natural groups.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Mesoptychiaceae
  • Common Name: Mesoptychia Family
  • Number of Genera: 2 (Mesoptychia, Protolophozia)
  • Number of Species: Approximately 15-20
  • Distribution: Primarily Northern Hemisphere (temperate, boreal, arctic, alpine)
  • Evolutionary Group: Liverworts (Marchantiophyta) - Jungermanniopsida - Jungermanniineae
  • Key Feature: Perianth subtended by a low perigynium

Key Characteristics

Gametophyte Form and Habit

Plants are small to medium-sized, typically forming prostrate to ascending mats or turfs. Color is often characteristic, frequently brownish, reddish-brown, purplish-green, or sometimes pure green. Stems are creeping to ascending, with variable branching, often lateral or terminal.

Leaves and Underleaves

Leaves are arranged succubously. They are typically bilobed, often shallowly to moderately so, with lobes usually rounded or obtuse, and the sinus between them often rounded or crescent-shaped. Underleaves are generally absent or rudimentary (small, lanceolate or subulate scales), inconspicuous.

Rhizoids

Rhizoids are usually numerous, colorless or brownish, scattered along the ventral side of the stem.

Cellular Structure

Leaf cells are typically isodiametric (polygonal). Cell walls are often somewhat thickened, and trigones are usually distinct, sometimes large and bulging. Cell walls themselves are frequently pigmented brownish or purplish. Oil bodies are present, variable among species.

Asexual Reproduction

Asexual reproduction via gemmae is present in some species, similar to Lophoziaceae. Gemmae are typically angular and may be pigmented, produced on leaf margins or apices.

Reproductive Structures

Plants are mostly dioicous, some species monoicous.

  • Antheridia are borne in the axils of saccate bracts, forming intercalary spikes.
  • Archegonia are terminal on main shoots or leading branches, surrounded by bracts often similar to leaves but larger.
  • The key distinguishing feature lies in the structures surrounding the archegonia/sporophyte: A well-developed perianth is present, typically cylindrical to fusiform, strongly plicate towards the contracted mouth. Crucially, this perianth arises from, or is subtended by, a distinct perigynium – a low, fleshy ring or short tube of stem tissue developed at the base. This perigynium slightly elevates the archegonial cluster and the base of the perianth.
  • Marsupia are absent.

Sporophytes and Spores

The sporophyte develops protected by both the perigynium and the perianth. The seta elongates to push the capsule out. The capsule is typically ovoid, dark, with a wall composed of multiple layers of cells. Dehiscence occurs via four valves.

Field Identification

Identifying Mesoptychiaceae often requires careful examination, especially of fertile material, as vegetative plants strongly resemble Lophoziaceae. Habitat can be a clue.

Primary Identification Features

  • Leaves: Succubous, typically bilobed with rounded/obtuse lobes and rounded sinus.
  • Underleaves: Usually absent or rudimentary.
  • Color: Often brownish, reddish, or purplish tinged.
  • Reproductive Structures: Key feature (requires fertile plants & magnification): Presence of both a plicate perianth AND a low, fleshy perigynium at its base.
  • Habitat: Diverse, but frequently found in base-rich or calcareous sites (though not exclusively), often on soil or rock crevices in temperate to arctic/alpine zones.
  • Gemmae: May be present, angular, often pigmented.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Cells (Microscopic): Often with pigmented walls and distinct trigones.
  • Habit: Mat or turf-forming.

Seasonal Identification Tips

Mesoptychiaceae gametophytes are perennial:

  • Year-Round: Vegetative features like leaf shape and color are visible.
  • Fertile Material: Look for plants with terminal perianths, especially during favorable growing seasons, to observe the critical perigynium at the base.
  • Gemmae: Can be present at various times, aiding identification when reproductive structures are absent.

Common Confusion Points

Distinguishing Mesoptychiaceae, especially vegetatively, can be challenging:

  • Lophoziaceae: Very similar vegetatively (succubous bilobed leaves, no underleaves, often pigmented, often with gemmae). Lophoziaceae lack the distinct perigynium at the base of the perianth. Habitat preferences may differ, with Lophoziaceae often more common in acidic sites.
  • Jungermanniaceae: Typically have entire or retuse leaves, not distinctly bilobed. Lack a perigynium.
  • Scapaniaceae: Distinguished by complicate-bilobed leaves.
  • Cephaloziellaceae: Much smaller plants with typically smaller cells.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Succubous, bilobed leaves (often rounded lobes/sinus)
  • Underleaves absent/rudimentary
  • Often brownish/purplish
  • Gemmae often present, angular
  • Habitat often base-rich/calcareous
  • Perianth + Perigynium present (fertile plants)

Key Distinctions:

  • Presence of perigynium below perianth (vs. absent in Lophoziaceae)
  • Leaves simply bilobed (vs. complicate in Scapaniaceae)
  • Leaves distinctly bilobed (vs. entire/retuse in Jungermanniaceae)
  • Underleaves absent/tiny (vs. large in Geocalycaceae)

Notable Examples

The family includes species formerly placed in Lophozia subg. Leiocolea:

Mesoptychia heterocolpos

(Formerly Leiocolea heterocolpos)

A characteristic species often found in base-rich habitats, such as calcareous rock crevices or soil. Plants are typically brownish or purplish-green, with bilobed leaves having rounded lobes. Paroicous (antheridia below archegonia). Produces brown, angular gemmae.

Mesoptychia badensis

(Formerly Leiocolea badensis)

Another species favouring calcareous substrates, often on damp soil or rocks. Typically smaller and greener than M. heterocolpos, but also shows bilobed leaves with rounded lobes and the characteristic perianth/perigynium combination. Dioicous.

Mesoptychia bantriensis

(Formerly Leiocolea bantriensis)

Often found in wet, base-rich flushes or near springs. Can be larger and greener than other species. Features succubous, bilobed leaves and the family's typical reproductive structures. Dioicous.

Phylogeny and Classification

Mesoptychiaceae is classified within the liverwort division Marchantiophyta, class Jungermanniopsida, order Jungermanniales, and suborder Jungermanniineae. Its recognition as a distinct family is relatively recent, based primarily on molecular phylogenetic analyses that showed species formerly placed in Lophozia subgenus Leiocolea (along with Protolophozia) formed a separate clade from the core Lophoziaceae.

This molecular separation is supported by the consistent morphological feature of possessing both a well-developed perianth and a basal perigynium, a combination absent in Lophoziaceae sensu stricto. Mesoptychiaceae is thus considered a sister group or closely related lineage to Lophoziaceae within the Jungermanniineae, highlighting the ongoing refinement of liverwort classification through the integration of molecular and morphological data.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Clade: Embryophytes (Land Plants)
  • Division: Marchantiophyta (Liverworts)
  • Class: Jungermanniopsida
  • Order: Jungermanniales
  • Suborder: Jungermanniineae
  • Family: Mesoptychiaceae

Related Families

Within the suborder Jungermanniineae, Mesoptychiaceae is most closely related to:

  • Lophoziaceae: Its sister family or very closely related lineage, distinguished primarily by the absence of a perigynium in Lophoziaceae s.s.
  • Jungermanniaceae: Another major family in the suborder, typically differing in leaf shape (often entire/retuse) and reproductive structures.
  • Scapaniaceae: Distinguished by complicate-bilobed leaves.
  • Gymnomitriaceae: Adapted to extreme environments, with appressed leaves and often lacking a perianth.

Evolutionary Significance

Mesoptychiaceae is significant for:

  • Refining Phylogeny: Its segregation from Lophoziaceae exemplifies how molecular data clarifies relationships within large, morphologically diverse groups.
  • Reproductive Morphology: Highlights variation in the development of protective structures around the archegonia (co-occurrence of perianth and perigynium).
  • Ecological Niche: Includes many species characteristic of base-rich or calcareous habitats, contrasting with the often acidophilic nature of core Lophoziaceae.
  • Biogeography: Primarily Northern Hemisphere distribution contributes to understanding diversification patterns in that region.
