Meteoriaceae

Meteor Moss Family

Meteoriaceae is a large family of pleurocarpous mosses most renowned for their characteristic pendent (hanging) growth habit. They typically form long, graceful strands or festoons dangling from tree branches, logs, or sometimes cliffs, especially prevalent in humid tropical and subtropical forests worldwide. Their appearance is often slender, stringy, or feathery.

Overview

The Meteoriaceae family belongs to the large and diverse order Hypnales. It is a moderately large family, encompassing around 15-20 genera and approximately 200-250 species. These mosses are predominantly found in tropical and subtropical regions across the globe, extending into moist temperate zones, where they are conspicuous epiphytes in rainforests and cloud forests.

Their defining feature is the pendent habit, with long secondary stems hanging down from creeping primary stems attached to the substrate. Branching is often irregular or pinnate. Leaves are typically small, numerous, and often uniform along the stems and branches. Leaf cells are frequently papillose (covered in small bumps), contributing to a somewhat dull appearance in many species, though some are glossy. Sporophytes are often produced infrequently; when present, they typically arise from the sides of the pendent stems on short to moderate setae, with capsules that are often erect and ovoid.

Ecologically, Meteoriaceae are key components of epiphytic communities in humid forests, contributing significantly to biomass and habitat structure in the canopy and understory. They play roles in water interception and nutrient cycling within these ecosystems.

  • Number of Genera: Approximately 15-20

  • Evolutionary Group: Bryophytes - Mosses - Hypnales

Key Characteristics (Moss Morphology)

Gametophyte: Growth Form and Habit

Plants are pleurocarpous, with creeping primary stems giving rise to long, typically pendent secondary stems that form hanging strands, festoons, or sometimes dense wefts. Secondary stems are often filiform (thread-like) and flaccid, usually irregularly or pinnately branched. Color ranges from pale green or yellowish-green to golden-brown or dark green.

Gametophyte: Stems

Secondary stems often lack a distinct central strand or have a weak one. Paraphyllia are usually absent. Pseudoparaphyllia are typically present at branch bases.

Gametophyte: Leaves

Leaves are typically crowded and uniform along stems and branches (homomallous), often small, erect-spreading to loosely appressed, usually concave, sometimes plicate (longitudinally folded). Leaf shape is variable, commonly ovate-lanceolate, lanceolate, or triangular-lanceolate. Leaf apices are usually acute to acuminate. Margins are typically plane, entire or serrulate towards the apex. The costa is variable: often single and short to moderate (ending mid-leaf or below), sometimes very short and double, or occasionally absent. Leaf cells are typically short-rhomboidal to linear, and frequently papillose (with one or multiple papillae per cell), although some genera have smooth cells.

Sporophyte: Seta, Capsule, and Peristome

Sporophytes are often infrequent in many species. Sexual condition is typically dioicous. The seta is usually short to moderately long, often rough (papillose or hispid), sometimes smooth. The capsule is typically immersed among perichaetial leaves or shortly exserted, usually erect and symmetrical, ovoid to oblong-cylindrical. The operculum is conical to rostrate. The peristome is double and typically hypnoid (well-developed exostome and endostome), though sometimes reduced in certain genera.

Field Identification (Mosses)

Identifying Meteoriaceae primarily relies on recognizing their characteristic growth form and habitat:

Primary Identification Features (Field/Hand Lens)

  • Pendent growth habit: The most obvious feature – look for long, hanging strands or festoons.
  • Habitat: Typically epiphytic on tree branches/trunks or logs in humid forests (tropical, subtropical, moist temperate).
  • Filiform/Stringy appearance: Stems are often slender and thread-like.
  • Leaf arrangement/shape: Small, numerous, often uniform leaves along the stems.
  • Papillose cells (often): Leaves may appear dull under a hand lens (requires confirmation with microscope).

Secondary Identification Features (Microscope often needed)

  • Costa length/presence: Check if costa is single and short/moderate, short/double, or absent.
  • Cell papillosity: Confirm presence, number, and location of papillae on leaf cells.
  • Cell shape: Note if cells are short-rhomboidal, linear, etc.
  • Seta roughness: Examine seta surface (smooth, papillose, hispid).
  • Capsule position: Immersed, emergent, or shortly exserted.

Common Confusion Points (Other Moss Families)

  • Neckeraceae (some genera): Some Neckeraceae are pendent (e.g., Neckera), but often have flattened (complanate) foliage and may have different costa or cell features.
  • Lembophyllaceae (e.g., Weymouthia): Includes pendent species, but often have more turgid shoots and typically ecostate leaves with smooth cells.
  • Pterobryaceae: Another primarily tropical epiphytic family, some pendent, but often more robust or with different branching patterns and leaf structures.
  • Hookeriaceae (some genera): Some tropical Hookeriaceae can be pendent, but often have larger, flattened leaves, sometimes bordered, and different cell structures.
  • Phyllogoniaceae: Pendent epiphytes with leaves arranged in two ranks (distichous).

Field Guide Quick Reference (Mosses)

Look For:

  • Long, pendent (hanging) growth form
  • Epiphytic habitat (on trees/logs)
  • Humid forest environments
  • Slender, stringy stems
  • Small, numerous leaves
  • Often dull appearance (papillose cells)

Key Variations:

  • Branching pattern (irregular vs. pinnate)
  • Leaf shape (ovate vs. lanceolate)
  • Costa (present vs. absent/short)
  • Cell papillosity (present vs. absent)
  • Seta length and roughness

Notable Genera

Some common and representative genera in the Meteoriaceae family include:

Meteorium

(Genus)

The type genus. Forms long, pendent strands, often yellowish-green. Leaves typically ovate-lanceolate, concave, often with papillose cells. Costa usually single and ending mid-leaf or below. Common in tropical and subtropical regions.

Papillaria

(Genus)

Characterized by densely papillose leaf cells (as the name suggests), giving the plants a very dull, often dark or blackish appearance when dry. Forms long pendent festoons. Costa single, short to moderate. Widespread in tropical/subtropical areas.

Aerobryopsis

(Genus)

Often forms robust, pendent strands, sometimes somewhat flattened. Leaves can be glossy or dull, often ovate-lanceolate with acuminate tips. Costa variable, single or short/double. Cells may be smooth or papillose. Found in tropical/subtropical Asia, Australasia.

Barbella

(Genus)

Typically very slender, filiform pendent mosses forming delicate strands. Leaves small, often lanceolate with long-acuminate tips. Costa usually absent or very short. Cells often linear and smooth or weakly papillose. Widespread in warm regions.

Floribundaria

(Genus)

Forms delicate, often pinnately branched pendent strands. Leaves typically ovate-lanceolate, often with papillose cells. Costa single, ending below apex. Sporophytes often have smooth setae.

Phylogeny and Classification

Meteoriaceae is placed within the large pleurocarpous order Hypnales, class Bryopsida. Its exact position within Hypnales can be complex, as this order contains numerous families with overlapping characteristics, particularly among epiphytic lineages. Molecular studies generally place Meteoriaceae within the core Hypnales, potentially related to families like Brachytheciaceae, Lembophyllaceae, and Neckeraceae.

The evolution of the pendent habit is a key feature of the family, likely representing a successful adaptation for epiphytic life in high-humidity environments, maximizing exposure to moisture and light while minimizing substrate competition. Features like papillose cells (common but not universal) may relate to water retention or surface properties. The variation in costa length and sporophyte features across genera suggests considerable diversification within this epiphytic niche.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Division: Bryophyta (Mosses)
  • Class: Bryopsida
  • Order: Hypnales
  • Family: Meteoriaceae

Related Families (Order Hypnales)

Meteoriaceae shares the order Hypnales with many other pleurocarpous families, with potential close relationships to:

  • Brachytheciaceae: A large, diverse family, mostly terrestrial or epilithic, but some epiphytic; typically costate.
  • Lembophyllaceae: Primarily Southern Hemisphere, often epiphytic/epilithic, includes pendent genera (Weymouthia) but typically ecostate with smooth cells.
  • Neckeraceae: Often epiphytic/epilithic, includes pendent forms, but frequently with flattened foliage and sometimes undulate leaves.
  • Pterobryaceae: Another largely tropical epiphytic family, sometimes pendent, but often more robust with different branching or leaf structures.
  • Phyllogoniaceae: Pendent epiphytes easily distinguished by their distichous (2-ranked) leaves.

Evolutionary Significance

The Meteoriaceae family exemplifies:

  • Adaptation to Pendent Epiphytism: Represents a major radiation of mosses specialized for hanging growth in humid forest canopies.
  • Morphological Convergence: The pendent habit has evolved in other unrelated moss lineages (e.g., some Neckeraceae, Pterobryaceae), showcasing convergent evolution in similar environments.
  • Diversity within Hypnales: Highlights the vast ecological and morphological diversity within one of the largest moss orders.
  • Role in Tropical Ecosystems: Significant contributors to epiphytic biomass and habitat structure in tropical and subtropical forests.
