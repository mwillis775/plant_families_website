Microtheciellaceae

Micro Capsule Moss Family (Informal)

Microtheciellaceae is a monotypic family containing the single, extremely small moss genus Microtheciella with its sole species M. kerrii. This unique family is placed in its own order, Microtheciellales. It is characterized by its tiny, ephemeral, acrocarpous habit, ecostate leaves with large lax cells, and distinctive immersed, cleistocarpous capsules (lacking a lid). It is known only from Southeast Asia.

Overview

The Microtheciellaceae family, established by Miller & Harrington, is remarkable for being monotypic at all levels: it contains only the genus Microtheciella, which includes only the species Microtheciella kerrii. This is one of the smallest known mosses, typically growing as scattered individuals or tiny tufts and often ephemeral (short-lived).

It exhibits an acrocarpous growth form, where the sporophytes terminate the main stem or short branches. Key features include the lack of a leaf midrib (costa), large lax leaf cells similar to those in families like Funariaceae, and a highly unusual sporophyte. The capsule is tiny, spherical, lacks a lid (operculum) and peristome, and remains immersed among the upper leaves on a virtually non-existent stalk (seta). This type of capsule, which releases spores only upon decay, is termed cleistocarpous.

Microtheciella kerrii is extremely rare and has a very restricted known distribution, found only on soil in seasonally dry, open habitats in parts of Southeast Asia (Thailand and Hainan, China). Its unique combination of morphological features and molecular data place it in its own distinct order, Microtheciellales, highlighting its isolated evolutionary position among mosses.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Microtheciellaceae H.A. Mill. & A.J. Harr.
  • Common Name: Micro Capsule Moss Family (Informal)
  • Number of Genera: 1 (Microtheciella Dixon)
  • Number of Species: 1 (Microtheciella kerrii Dixon)
  • Distribution: Southeast Asia (Thailand, China - Hainan). Very rare.
  • Evolutionary Group: Bryophytes - Mosses (Class Bryatae - Order Microtheciellales)

Key Characteristics (Moss Features)

Gametophyte (Leafy Plant)

The dominant stage. Plants extremely small (often only 1-2 mm high), ephemeral, growing scattered or in small tufts, typically pale green. Acrocarpous (sporophytes terminal).

  • Stems: Very short, simple or sparsely branched.
  • Leaves (Phyllids): Small, crowded, especially near the stem apex, often contorted when dry. Shape ovate to lanceolate, concave. Apex acute or acuminate. Margins usually plane, entire or nearly so.
  • Costa (Midrib): Characteristically absent.
  • Leaf Cells: Large, lax, thin-walled, smooth, typically hexagonal to rhomboidal. Similar in appearance to cells of Funariaceae or Gigaspermaceae.

Reproductive Structures (Gametophyte)

Antheridia and archegonia are borne terminally on stems or short branches, often mixed together (paroicous or synoicous).

Sporophyte (Spore-Producing Structure)

Arises terminally, highly reduced and distinctive. Consists of:

  • Seta: Extremely short, almost absent, keeping the capsule immersed among the upper leaves (perichaetial leaves).
  • Capsule (Sporangium): Tiny, spherical (globose), cleistocarpous (lacking a distinct line of dehiscence, operculum, and annulus). Spores are released only when the thin capsule wall decays.
  • Calyptra: Very small, covering the young capsule, mitrate (cap-like) or cucullate (hood-like).

Peristome

Completely absent, consistent with the cleistocarpous nature of the capsule.

Field Identification

Identifying Microtheciellaceae requires finding these minute plants and observing key features, likely needing magnification:

Primary Identification Features

  • Extremely Small Size: Plants are minute (1-2 mm), easily overlooked.
  • Ephemeral Nature: Often present only seasonally.
  • Habitat: Terrestrial on soil in open, seasonally dry areas.
  • Location: Restricted to specific localities in Southeast Asia (Thailand, Hainan).
  • Acrocarpous Habit: Sporophytes (if present) are terminal.
  • Costa Absent: Leaves lack a midrib.
  • Immersed, Globose, Cleistocarpous Capsule: The most distinctive feature. Look for tiny, spherical capsules nestled among the upper leaves, lacking a lid or peristome, borne on almost no stalk.
  • Large, Lax Leaf Cells: Gives the leaves a somewhat translucent appearance (microscope needed).

Common Confusion Points

  • Other Tiny Ephemeral Mosses (e.g., Pottiaceae, Ephemeraceae): Many small acrocarpous mosses grow on soil, but they typically have costate leaves and operculate capsules (often with peristomes), even if small or immersed.
  • Gigaspermaceae: Also have large cells and somewhat reduced sporophytes, but are generally larger plants with different capsule structure (though sometimes immersed).
  • Funariaceae (e.g., Physcomitrium): Some have immersed or shortly exserted capsules and lax cells, but capsules are typically operculate and leaves are costate.
  • Protonemal Stages: Could be confused with the persistent protonema of other mosses if only gametophytes are present, but Microtheciella forms distinct leafy stems.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Extremely small (1-2 mm), ephemeral moss
  • Acrocarpous habit
  • Leaves ecostate (costa absent)
  • Large, lax leaf cells
  • Capsule tiny, globose, cleistocarpous, immersed (key!)
  • Habitat: Soil in open, seasonally dry areas
  • Location: SE Asia (rare)

Key Variations:

  • Presence/absence of sporophytes
  • (Family is monotypic)

Notable Examples

The family is monotypic, containing only one genus and species:

Microtheciella kerrii

(No widely used common name)

The sole species in the family Microtheciellaceae. This extremely rare and tiny moss is unique for its combination of acrocarpous habit, ecostate leaves with large lax cells, and especially its immersed, spherical, cleistocarpous capsule. It is known only from limited locations in Thailand and Hainan, China.

Phylogeny and Classification

Microtheciellaceae is classified within the Class Bryatae (true mosses). Its phylogenetic position has been enigmatic due to its highly specialized and reduced morphology. While superficial similarities exist with families like Gigaspermaceae (large cells, reduced sporophyte) or Funariaceae (lax cells), molecular data have shown it to be highly distinct from these groups.

Based on its unique combination of morphological features and distinct molecular signature, Microtheciellaceae is now placed in its own monotypic order, Microtheciellales. This highlights its isolated evolutionary position within the moss phylogeny, representing a separate, ancient lineage with significant morphological modifications, particularly in its reproductive structures (cleistocarpy).

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Division: Bryophyta (Mosses)
  • Class: Bryatae (or Bryopsida)
  • Order: Microtheciellales
  • Family: Microtheciellaceae

Related Families

Microtheciellaceae is placed in its own order and is not considered closely related to other extant moss families based on current molecular data. Families sometimes superficially compared due to certain features include:

  • Gigaspermaceae: Share large cells and reduced sporophytes but differ significantly in capsule structure and overall morphology; not closely related.
  • Funariaceae: Share large, lax cells but differ greatly in habit, costa presence, and capsule structure (operculate); not closely related.
  • Ephemeraceae: Also includes tiny, ephemeral mosses, but they possess costate leaves and different capsule types; not closely related.

Evolutionary Significance

Microtheciellaceae is highly significant from an evolutionary perspective. Its unique morphology (especially the cleistocarpous capsule) and isolated phylogenetic position suggest it represents a relictual lineage, potentially preserving features or evolutionary pathways not seen in other major moss groups. Its extreme reduction and ephemeral life strategy are likely adaptations to its specific habitat. Studying this family is crucial for understanding the full scope of morphological and evolutionary diversity within mosses.
