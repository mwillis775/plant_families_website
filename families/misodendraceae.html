Misodendraceae

Nothofagus Mistletoes / Feather Mistletoes

Misodendraceae is a unique family of flowering plants containing the single genus Misodendrum. These plants are aerial hemiparasites, exclusively parasitizing trees of the genus Nothofagus (Southern Beeches). Found only in temperate South America (Chile and Argentina), they belong to the order Santalales and are characterized by reduced flowers and wind-dispersed fruits bearing distinctive feathery bristles.

Overview

The Misodendraceae family comprises about 8-10 species within the sole genus Misodendrum. These specialized plants are obligate aerial hemiparasites, meaning they grow attached to the branches of host trees and derive water and nutrients from them, while still performing photosynthesis with their own (often reduced) leaves. Their defining characteristic is their strict host specificity to Southern Beeches (Nothofagus).

They are found in the temperate forests and shrublands of the southern Andes. Morphologically, they exhibit significant reductions, particularly in their floral structures. Plants are dioecious, with separate male and female individuals. Their fruits are uniquely adapted for wind dispersal via long, feathery bristles derived from the styles/stigmas.

The family's placement within the Santalales order groups it with other parasitic and hemiparasitic lineages, such as mistletoes (Santalaceae, Loranthaceae) and root parasites. Studying Misodendraceae provides insights into the evolution of parasitism and host specificity in plants.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Misodendraceae J.Agardh
  • Common Name: Nothofagus Mistletoe, Feather Mistletoe
  • Number of Genera: 1 (Misodendrum)
  • Number of Species: Approximately 8-10
  • Distribution: Temperate South America (Chile, Argentina)
  • Evolutionary Group: Eudicots - Core Eudicots - Santalales

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Aerial hemiparasitic shrubs, attaching to branches of Nothofagus hosts via haustoria. Stems are often brittle and much-branched.

Leaves

Leaves are alternate, simple, and highly variable, ranging from linear or lanceolate to scale-like and much reduced. They lack stipules (exstipulate).

Inflorescence

Flowers are arranged in racemes or spikes, often catkin-like, especially the male inflorescences.

Flowers

Flowers are unisexual (plants dioecious), highly reduced, and inconspicuous. Key features differ between sexes:

  • Male Flowers: Possess a small, cup-like, typically 2-3 lobed perianth (calyx). Stamens are 2 or 3, opposite the perianth lobes, with short filaments and anthers that dehisce by pores or short slits. A pistillode (sterile pistil) is absent.
  • Female Flowers: Perianth is absent or represented by a minute rim. Ovary is inferior, unilocular (one chamber), containing typically 3 ovules pendulous from a free-central placenta. Style is short, bearing 3 elongated, often feathery stigmas/style branches that become the fruit bristles. Staminodes are absent.

Fruits and Seeds

The fruit is a small, dry, single-seeded achene. It is crowned by three persistent, elongated, plumose (feathery) bristles derived from the styles/stigmas. These bristles act as a pappus-like structure, facilitating wind dispersal (anemochory).

Chemical Characteristics

Specific chemical profiles are less documented compared to larger families. As hemiparasites, their chemistry may be influenced by the host. Like other Santalales, they likely possess specific secondary metabolites related to parasitism and defense.

Field Identification

Identifying Misodendraceae in the field is primarily based on recognizing their parasitic growth habit exclusively on Nothofagus trees within their specific geographic range (southern Andes).

Primary Identification Features

  • Habit: Aerial parasitic shrubs growing on Nothofagus branches.
  • Host Specificity: Found only on Nothofagus species.
  • Distribution: Temperate forests of southern Chile and Argentina.
  • Leaves: Alternate, often scale-like or reduced, sometimes linear.
  • Flowers: Highly reduced, unisexual (plants dioecious), in spike-like or catkin-like inflorescences.
  • Fruit: Small achene bearing 3 long, feathery bristles for wind dispersal.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Stems: Often brittle, forming dense clusters on host branches.
  • Dioecious Nature: Male and female flowers occur on separate plants.
  • Feathery Bristles: The distinctive fruit structure is diagnostic when present.

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Year-round: The parasitic growth form on Nothofagus is visible year-round.
  • Flowering Season: Typically spring or early summer, though flowers are inconspicuous.
  • Fruiting Season: Fruits with feathery bristles are most evident in summer and autumn, aiding identification.

Common Confusion Points

  • Other Mistletoes (e.g., Santalaceae like Viscum or Loranthaceae): While other mistletoes occur in South America, Misodendrum is distinguished by its exclusive association with Nothofagus, dioecious nature, highly reduced flowers (especially female), and the unique wind-dispersed fruit with three feathery bristles. Other mistletoes often have more developed flowers and fleshy, bird-dispersed berries.
  • Witches' Brooms: Dense clusters of Misodendrum might superficially resemble witches' brooms caused by fungi or other agents, but close inspection reveals the distinct stems, leaves (if present), and reproductive structures of the mistletoe.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Parasitic shrub on Nothofagus
  • Temperate South American Andes
  • Alternate, often reduced leaves
  • Dioecious plants
  • Reduced unisexual flowers
  • Inferior ovary (female flowers)
  • Achene fruit with 3 feathery bristles

Key Variations:

  • Leaf shape (scale-like to linear)
  • Plant size and density
  • Bristle length

Notable Examples

The family contains only the genus Misodendrum. Species are distinguished by leaf morphology, bristle length, and host preferences within Nothofagus:

Misodendrum punctulatum

(Dotted Mistletoe)

One of the most common species, parasitizing various deciduous and evergreen Nothofagus. Characterized by relatively well-developed, linear to lanceolate leaves and long feathery bristles on the fruit.

Misodendrum linearifolium

(Linear-leaved Mistletoe)

As the name suggests, this species typically has distinctly linear leaves. It parasitizes several Nothofagus species.

Misodendrum brachystachyum

(Short-spiked Mistletoe)

This species often has more scale-like, reduced leaves compared to M. punctulatum, and relatively shorter inflorescences. It parasitizes evergreen Nothofagus species.

Phylogeny and Classification

Misodendraceae belongs to the order Santalales, a group known for containing many parasitic and hemiparasitic lineages. Molecular studies place it within the core Santalales clade.

Its exact phylogenetic position relative to other Santalales families like Santalaceae (sandalwoods and typical mistletoes) and Loranthaceae (showy mistletoes) has been debated, but it represents a distinct, early-diverging lineage within the order, characterized by its unique adaptations (wind-dispersed fruit, extreme host specificity).

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Clade: Angiosperms (Flowering plants)
  • Clade: Eudicots
  • Clade: Core Eudicots
  • Order: Santalales
  • Family: Misodendraceae

Related Families (Order Santalales)

Misodendraceae is related to other families within the Santalales order, many of which also exhibit parasitic habits:

  • Santalaceae (Sandalwood family): A large family including hemiparasitic trees (like sandalwood), shrubs, and mistletoes (e.g., Viscum, Phoradendron, Arceuthobium). Fruits are often drupes or berries, typically bird-dispersed.
  • Loranthaceae (Showy Mistletoe family): Primarily tropical aerial hemiparasites, often with large, colorful flowers adapted for bird pollination. Fruits are typically berries dispersed by birds.
  • Opiliaceae: Root hemiparasitic trees, shrubs, and lianas found in the tropics.
  • Balanophoraceae: Holoparasitic (lacking chlorophyll) root parasites with highly modified, fungus-like appearances, found in the tropics and subtropics.
  • Schoepfiaceae: Hemiparasitic trees and shrubs.

Evolutionary Significance

Misodendraceae is significant for several evolutionary reasons:

  • Evolution of Parasitism: Provides a case study within Santalales, showing a distinct strategy compared to bird-dispersed mistletoes.
  • Host Specificity: Its extreme specialization on Nothofagus is a remarkable example of co-evolution.
  • Wind Dispersal Adaptation: The evolution of feathery bristles for anemochory is unusual among mistletoes, which are predominantly zoochorous (animal-dispersed).
  • Biogeography: Its restricted distribution in temperate South America, mirroring its host's range, reflects Gondwanan biogeographic patterns.
  • Floral Reduction: Represents an extreme case of floral reduction within the Santalales.
