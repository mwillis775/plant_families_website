Molluginaceae

Carpetweed Family

Molluginaceae, commonly known as the Carpetweed family, is a family of flowering plants primarily comprising annual or perennial herbs, occasionally subshrubs. Found widely in tropical and subtropical regions around the world, often in open or disturbed habitats, they belong to the order Caryophyllales. The family is characterized by often whorled leaves, small flowers with tepals, and fruit typically a capsule. Unlike the core Caryophyllales, they possess anthocyanin pigments instead of betalains.

Overview

The Molluginaceae family includes about 9 genera and around 80-90 species. Its circumscription has been refined over time, with several genera previously included now placed in separate families (e.g., Limeaceae, Lophiocarpaceae, Gisekiaceae) based on molecular data. Key remaining genera include Mollugo and Glinus.

These plants are typically low-growing herbs, often found as weeds or colonizers of open, sandy, or disturbed ground in warmer climates worldwide. The common name "Carpetweed" refers to the often prostrate, mat-forming habit of species like Mollugo verticillata.

Within the Caryophyllales order, Molluginaceae is notable for possessing anthocyanins (like most flowering plants) rather than the betalain pigments characteristic of the "core Caryophyllales" group (which includes families like Cactaceae, Amaranthaceae, Aizoaceae). Some species, particularly in the genus Mollugo, exhibit C4 photosynthesis, an adaptation often found in plants growing in hot, dry, or high-light environments.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Molluginaceae Bartl.
  • Common Name: Carpetweed family
  • Number of Genera: Approximately 9
  • Number of Species: Approximately 80-90
  • Distribution: Widespread in tropical and subtropical regions worldwide.
  • Evolutionary Group: Eudicots - Caryophyllales (non-core)

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Mostly annual or perennial herbs, sometimes suffrutescent (slightly woody at base) or small shrubs. Stems can be erect, ascending, or prostrate, often forming mats.

Leaves

Leaves are simple and entire. Arrangement is variable: opposite, alternate, or commonly appearing whorled (pseudo-whorled) due to clustering at nodes. Basal rosettes may also occur. Stipules are usually present but small, often membranous (scarious) or falling early (deciduous).

Inflorescence

Flowers are borne in cymose inflorescences, which can be terminal or axillary, lax or congested. Sometimes flowers are solitary or clustered in leaf axils.

Flowers

Flowers are typically small, actinomorphic (radially symmetrical), and usually bisexual. Key features include:

  • Perianth: Consists of a single whorl of (4-)5 tepals (sepal-like), which are free or fused only at the base. They are often herbaceous with membranous margins, typically whitish, greenish, or pinkish, and persistent in fruit.
  • Androecium: Stamens few to numerous (commonly 3-10, sometimes up to 30 or more), free or basally connate. Filaments are slender. Staminodes (sterile stamens) may sometimes be present, alternating with fertile stamens.
  • Gynoecium: Ovary is superior, composed of 2-5 (rarely more) fused carpels (syncarpous). It is typically plurilocular (with as many chambers as carpels), with axile placentation (ovules attached to central axis). Less commonly, the ovary may be unilocular with basal placentation. Styles are usually free, as many as carpels.

Fruits and Seeds

The fruit is typically a loculicidal capsule (splitting open along the back of each chamber), enclosed by the persistent tepals. Less often, the fruit may be an achene or utricle. Seeds are often numerous, small, kidney-shaped (reniform) or rounded, sometimes with a small appendage (aril).

Chemical Characteristics

Possess anthocyanin pigments (responsible for red/purple colors) rather than betalains. Many species accumulate saponins. Some species exhibit C4 photosynthesis.

Field Identification

Identifying Molluginaceae often involves recognizing their herbaceous habit, characteristic leaf arrangement (often whorled), small flowers with tepals, and capsule fruits.

Primary Identification Features

  • Habit: Mostly herbs, often low-growing or mat-forming.
  • Leaves: Simple, entire, often appearing whorled at the nodes.
  • Stipules: Usually present, small, sometimes falling early.
  • Flowers: Small, actinomorphic, with (4-)5 greenish or whitish tepals (not distinct sepals and petals).
  • Ovary: Superior, typically with 2-5 carpels/styles.
  • Fruit: Usually a capsule splitting into segments, enclosed by persistent tepals.
  • Pigments: Anthocyanins present (no betalains - stems/leaves don't usually turn beet-red when bruised).

Secondary Identification Features

  • Habitat: Often found in open, sunny, dry, or disturbed areas in warm regions.
  • Stamens: Variable number (3-many).
  • Placentation: Typically axile.

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Growing Season: Most active during warmer months. Flowers and fruits are necessary for definitive identification.
  • Distinguishing Features: The combination of whorled leaves, small tepal-bearing flowers, and capsule fruit is characteristic.

Common Confusion Points

  • Caryophyllaceae (Pink Family): Often have opposite leaves (not whorled) and flowers typically with 5 distinct (often notched) petals and 5 sepals. Fruit is usually a capsule opening by teeth or valves.
  • Rubiaceae (Coffee Family): Often have opposite or whorled leaves, but possess interpetiolar stipules (stipules located between the petioles) and typically have flowers with both calyx and corolla, and inferior ovaries.
  • Aizoaceae (Ice Plant Family): Many are succulent (Molluginaceae usually not), and flowers often have numerous petal-like staminodes.
  • Euphorbiaceae (Spurge Family): Highly variable, but often have milky latex and specialized cyathium inflorescences (neither found in Molluginaceae).

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Herbs (often low/mat-forming)
  • Leaves often appearing whorled
  • Simple, entire leaves
  • Small stipules (may fall early)
  • Small flowers with (4-)5 tepals
  • Superior ovary (2-5 carpels)
  • Capsule fruit (usually)
  • Anthocyanin pigments

Key Variations:

  • Leaf arrangement (whorled, opposite, alternate)
  • Stamen number
  • Inflorescence structure (cymes, clusters)
  • Plant habit (prostrate to erect)

Notable Examples

Key genera in the Molluginaceae family include:

Mollugo verticillata

Carpetweed, Green Carpetweed

A very common, widespread annual weed. Characterized by its prostrate, mat-forming habit and distinct whorls of linear to oblanceolate leaves at the nodes. Small whitish flowers are borne in clusters in the leaf axils. Exhibits C4 photosynthesis.

Glinus lotoides

(Damascisa)

An annual or perennial herb found in warmer parts of the Old World and introduced elsewhere. Often has a grayish appearance due to stellate (star-shaped) hairs covering the stems and leaves. Flowers are clustered in axils, tepals larger than in Mollugo.

Hypertelis spp.

(Hypertelis)

A genus primarily found in Africa and Madagascar. Includes herbs and subshrubs, sometimes with slightly succulent leaves. Flowers are often in terminal cymes.

Mollugo spp.

(Mollugo)

The largest genus in the family, with diverse species found globally in warm regions. Habits range from prostrate annuals to more erect perennials. Many exhibit C4 photosynthesis.

Phylogeny and Classification

Molluginaceae is placed in the large order Caryophyllales. Within this order, it belongs to the broader group sometimes referred to as the "non-core" Caryophyllales, lineages that diverged before the main radiation of the core group (which is characterized by betalain pigments and other synapomorphies).

Molecular studies show that Molluginaceae, in its current stricter sense, is related to other families like Phytolaccaceae, Nyctaginaceae, and the clade containing the core Caryophyllales. Several families (Limeaceae, Lophiocarpaceae, Kewaceae, Gisekiaceae, Macarthuriaceae) containing genera previously included in or associated with Molluginaceae are now recognized as distinct lineages within Caryophyllales, highlighting the complex evolutionary history of this part of the order.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Clade: Angiosperms (Flowering plants)
  • Clade: Eudicots
  • Clade: Core Eudicots
  • Order: Caryophyllales
  • Family: Molluginaceae

Related Families (Order Caryophyllales)

Molluginaceae is broadly related to all other families within the Caryophyllales order. Some notable relatives include:

  • Core Caryophyllales Clade: Including Amaranthaceae (Amaranth family), Caryophyllaceae (Pink family), Aizoaceae (Ice Plant family), Cactaceae (Cactus family), Nyctaginaceae (Four O'Clock family), Portulacaceae (Purslane family) - Molluginaceae diverged before this large radiation and lacks their characteristic betalain pigments.
  • Other Non-Core Families: Including Phytolaccaceae (Pokeweed family), Polygonaceae (Buckwheat family - sometimes placed outside Caryophyllales s.s.), and smaller families recently segregated from Molluginaceae/Phytolaccaceae complexes like Limeaceae, Lophiocarpaceae, Kewaceae.

Evolutionary Significance

Molluginaceae plays a role in understanding the evolution of Caryophyllales:

  • Pigment Evolution: As an anthocyanin-containing family outside the betalain-producing core clade, it informs studies on the switch between these pigment types within the order.
  • Evolution of Photosynthesis: The presence of C4 photosynthesis in some members (like Mollugo) provides examples for studying the repeated evolution of this pathway in response to environmental conditions.
  • Taxonomic Refinement: The history of Molluginaceae's changing circumscription illustrates how molecular data helps resolve relationships within complex groups and establish more natural (monophyletic) family classifications.
  • Adaptation to Disturbed Habitats: Many species are successful colonizers, offering models for studying weed ecology and adaptation to open environments.
