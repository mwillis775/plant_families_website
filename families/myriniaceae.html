Myriniaceae

Myrinia Moss Family (Informal)

Myriniaceae is a small family of very delicate, slender pleurocarpous mosses, closely related to (and sometimes included within) Fabroniaceae, belonging to the order Hypnales. The primary genus is Myrinia. These mosses are characterized by their tiny size, ecostate leaves with smooth rhomboidal cells, and typically epiphytic habit, often near water in temperate Northern Hemisphere regions.

Overview

The Myriniaceae family, established by Schimper, is a small group of diminutive pleurocarpous mosses centered around the genus Myrinia. Its status as a distinct family is sometimes debated, as it shares many characteristics with Fabroniaceae and is occasionally included within it or within the broader Leskeaceae complex. These are among the smaller pleurocarpous mosses, forming thin, intricate, often silky or somewhat glossy mats.

Plants are very slender and delicate, with thread-like creeping stems that branch irregularly. The leaves are tiny, typically ovate, concave, and lack a midrib (costa) or have only a very faint, short double one. The leaf cells are smooth and generally rhomboidal to fusiform (spindle-shaped).

Myriniaceae typically grow as epiphytes on the bark of deciduous trees and shrubs, particularly species like willows and poplars growing near water in riparian zones or floodplains. They are primarily found in temperate and boreal regions of the Northern Hemisphere. Phylogenetically, they belong to the order Hypnales, closely allied with Fabroniaceae.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Myriniaceae Schimp.
  • Common Name: Myrinia Moss Family (Informal)
  • Number of Genera: Few (Primarily Myrinia Schimp.), taxonomic status debated.
  • Number of Species: Few (e.g., Myrinia pulvinata).
  • Distribution: Primarily Northern Hemisphere temperate and boreal regions.
  • Evolutionary Group: Bryophytes - Mosses (Class Bryatae - Order Hypnales)

Key Characteristics (Moss Features)

Gametophyte (Leafy Plant)

The dominant stage. Plants very small, slender, delicate, often silky or somewhat glossy green to yellowish-green, pleurocarpous, forming thin, intricate mats.

  • Stems: Creeping, filiform (thread-like), densely and irregularly branched. Branches short, often ascending.
  • Leaves (Phyllids): Very small (often < 1 mm), crowded, typically appressed when dry, erect-spreading when moist. Leaves ovate to ovate-lanceolate, usually concave. Apex acute to acuminate. Margins usually plane, entire or finely serrulate.
  • Costa (Midrib): Characteristically absent, or very short and double and often indistinct.
  • Leaf Cells: Typically rhomboidal to fusiform (spindle-shaped) or shortly linear, smooth, often relatively thin-walled. Alar cells usually not or only weakly differentiated, sometimes a few quadrate cells at the basal corners.

Reproductive Structures (Gametophyte)

Antheridia and archegonia are borne in lateral buds on the stems or branches.

Sporophyte (Spore-Producing Structure)

Arises laterally from the gametophyte stem, often frequent. Consists of:

  • Seta: Usually relatively short to moderate in length, smooth, often yellowish or pale.
  • Capsule (Sporangium): Small, typically erect and symmetric, ovoid to shortly cylindrical. Operculum usually conical or shortly rostrate.

Peristome

Typically double but often highly reduced or variable, sometimes appearing single or rudimentary. Exostome teeth may be pale, papillose. Endostome often consists of a low basal membrane and rudimentary or absent segments and cilia. Peristome structure is similar to that found in Fabroniaceae and important for classification.

Field Identification

Identifying Myriniaceae requires recognizing its very small size and specific habitat:

Primary Identification Features

  • Tiny Size and Delicate Habit: Look for very small, slender mosses forming thin, intricate, often silky mats.
  • Habitat: Bark Near Water: Typically epiphytic on deciduous trees (especially willow, poplar) or shrubs in floodplains, riparian zones, or lake margins.
  • Costa Absent / Very Short Double: Leaves lack a distinct midrib (requires hand lens).
  • Smooth, Rhomboidal/Fusiform Cells: Contribute to the somewhat silky texture (microscope needed for confirmation).
  • Northern Hemisphere Temperate/Boreal Distribution: Found in these specific regions.

Common Confusion Points

  • Fabroniaceae: Very similar in size, habit, habitat, ecostate leaves, and reduced peristome. Often considered the most closely related family or potentially encompassing Myriniaceae. Distinction often relies on subtle peristome or cell shape details.
  • Amblystegiaceae (e.g., small Amblystegium species): Can grow in similar wet habitats but usually have a distinct single costa, even if short, and different cell shapes.
  • Leskeaceae (e.g., small Leskea species): Often grow on bark but typically have papillose cells (dull appearance) and usually a single costa.
  • Small Pylaisiadelphaceae: Some genera are small epiphytes but often differ in cell shape or peristome structure.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Very small, delicate, slender moss
  • Thin, intricate mats, often silky
  • Pleurocarpous habit
  • Habitat: Bark near water (willows, poplars)
  • Costa absent or very short/double (key!)
  • Smooth rhomboidal/fusiform cells
  • Northern Hemisphere

Key Variations:

  • Glossiness
  • Branch density
  • Frequency of sporophytes
  • (Few species, mainly M. pulvinata)

Notable Examples

The family is primarily represented by the genus Myrinia:

Myrinia pulvinata

(No widely used common name)

The main species representing the family. It forms very delicate, thin, silky mats on the bark of trees like willows and poplars in periodically flooded areas across temperate and boreal Northern Hemisphere regions. Its tiny size and ecostate leaves are characteristic.

Phylogeny and Classification

Myriniaceae is classified within the Class Bryatae (true mosses) and is placed in the large pleurocarpous order Hypnales. Its taxonomic status as a distinct family is sometimes questioned, as it is morphologically and phylogenetically very close to the family Fabroniaceae.

Molecular studies often place Myrinia within or sister to the Fabroniaceae clade. Both families share characteristics like small size, ecostate leaves, smooth rhomboidal cells, and reduced peristomes, suggesting a close evolutionary relationship. They represent a lineage of highly reduced pleurocarpous mosses specialized for epiphytic life, often in specific microhabitats like riparian zones. Further research continues to refine the boundaries and relationships within this complex.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Division: Bryophyta (Mosses)
  • Class: Bryatae (or Bryopsida)
  • Order: Hypnales
  • Family: Myriniaceae (closely related to/sometimes included in Fabroniaceae)

Related Families (within Order Hypnales)

Myriniaceae is most closely related to:

  • Fabroniaceae: Very similar morphologically and phylogenetically; Myriniaceae is sometimes considered a synonym or subfamily.
  • Leskeaceae: Shares small size and sometimes habitat but differs in papillose cells and usually having a costa.
  • Amblystegiaceae: Some small species grow in wet habitats but usually have a costa and different cell structure.
  • Pylaisiadelphaceae: Includes small epiphytes but generally differs in cell shape and peristome structure.

Evolutionary Significance

Myriniaceae, along with the closely related Fabroniaceae, represents an evolutionary trend towards miniaturization and simplification within the pleurocarpous mosses. Features like the delicate habit, lack of a costa, and reduced peristome are likely adaptations to their specific epiphytic niche, possibly related to rapid life cycles or specific water relations on bark surfaces, especially in periodically inundated environments. Studying this group helps understand the processes of morphological reduction and ecological specialization in bryophyte evolution.
