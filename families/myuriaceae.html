Myuriaceae

Cat-tail Moss Family (Informal)

Myuriaceae is a family of robust, often glossy pleurocarpous mosses belonging to the order Hypnales, including genera like Myurium and Oedicladium. They are characterized by their often tumid, julaceous branches (resembling cat-tails), concave leaves lacking a costa but possessing strongly differentiated alar cells. The family has a wide distribution, particularly in tropical, subtropical, and oceanic temperate regions.

Overview

The Myuriaceae family, established by Fleischer, consists of medium-sized to robust pleurocarpous mosses, primarily including the genera Myurium and Oedicladium. These mosses often form dense, glossy mats or tufts, frequently appearing yellowish-green, golden, or reddish-brown. A characteristic feature is the appearance of the branches, which are often swollen-looking (tumid) and smoothly cylindrical (julaceous) due to the densely overlapping, concave leaves.

Key diagnostic features include the absence of a leaf midrib (costa) or its presence only as a very short double trace, combined with strongly differentiated groups of cells at the basal corners of the leaves (alar cells), which are often large and inflated. The main leaf cells are typically long, narrow, and smooth.

Myuriaceae are commonly found as epiphytes on tree trunks and branches, or growing on rocks (epilithic), particularly in humid forests and coastal or oceanic environments. They have a broad distribution across tropical, subtropical, and temperate regions worldwide, with notable diversity in Asia, Australasia, and the Pacific islands. The family is placed within the large pleurocarpous order Hypnales.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Myuriaceae M. Fleisch.
  • Common Name: Cat-tail Moss Family (Informal)
  • Number of Genera: Few (e.g., Myurium, Oedicladium).
  • Number of Species: Several species across the genera.
  • Distribution: Widespread (Tropical, Subtropical, Temperate - esp. oceanic/coastal).
  • Evolutionary Group: Bryophytes - Mosses (Class Bryatae - Order Hypnales)

Key Characteristics (Moss Features)

Gametophyte (Leafy Plant)

The dominant stage. Plants medium to robust, often glossy, yellowish-green to golden or reddish-brown, pleurocarpous, forming dense mats or tufts.

  • Stems: Creeping primary stems give rise to ascending or pendent secondary stems/branches. Branching typically irregular or subpinnate. Branches often tumid (swollen-looking) and smoothly cylindrical (julaceous) due to densely imbricate leaves.
  • Leaves (Phyllids): Densely crowded and overlapping (imbricate), typically erect-appressed when dry, spreading slightly when moist. Leaves broadly ovate to nearly circular, strongly concave (cochleariform). Apex usually abruptly narrowed to a short point (apiculate or cuspidate). Margins generally plane, entire or finely serrulate near apex.
  • Costa (Midrib): Characteristically absent, or very short and double and often indistinct.
  • Leaf Cells: Typically long and narrow (linear), smooth, often with thick walls (prosenchymatous). Alar cells at the basal corners characteristically strongly differentiated, forming distinct, often large groups of inflated, thin-walled, hyaline (clear) or colored cells.

Reproductive Structures (Gametophyte)

Antheridia and archegonia are borne in lateral buds on the stems or branches.

Sporophyte (Spore-Producing Structure)

Arises laterally from the gametophyte stem, often infrequent. Consists of:

  • Seta: Usually relatively long, smooth, often reddish.
  • Capsule (Sporangium): Typically erect to slightly inclined, symmetric or nearly so, ovoid to cylindrical. Operculum usually conical to rostrate.

Peristome

Typically double and well-developed (hypnoid type), though the endostome cilia may sometimes be reduced or absent. Consists of 16 outer exostome teeth and an inner endostome with segments.

Field Identification

Identifying Myuriaceae involves recognizing its glossy, often julaceous appearance and key leaf features:

Primary Identification Features

  • Glossy Appearance: Plants are typically shiny, often golden or reddish-brown.
  • Tumid, Julaceous Branches: Branches often look swollen and smoothly cylindrical due to tightly packed, concave leaves.
  • Broadly Ovate, Concave Leaves: Leaves are shell-like or spoon-shaped, densely overlapping.
  • Costa Absent / Very Short Double: Lack of a distinct midrib is crucial (use hand lens).
  • Distinct Alar Cells: The groups of differentiated cells at the leaf base corners are often conspicuous (may need hand lens) as large, clear, or colored patches.
  • Habitat: Typically epiphytic or epilithic in humid forests or coastal areas.

Common Confusion Points

  • Lepyrodontaceae (Lepyrodon): Very similar habit (julaceous, glossy, ecostate, distinct alar cells). Distinguished by leaves usually being more abruptly narrowed to a longer point and often subtle differences in alar cell shape or peristome structure. Distribution primarily Southern Hemisphere.
  • Leucodontaceae / Leptodontaceae: Also have julaceous branches and often ecostate leaves. Distinguished by lacking strongly differentiated alar cells (Leucodon has smooth cells, Leptodon has papillose cells).
  • Sematophyllaceae: Many genera have distinct alar cells and are often glossy. Differ in typically having non-julaceous branches, often falcate leaves, and frequently inclined capsules.
  • Pylaisiadelphaceae / Stereophyllaceae: Some genera are glossy pleurocarps, sometimes julaceous or complanate, but differ in alar cell structure, leaf shape, or peristome details.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Robust, glossy moss mats/tufts
  • Branches often tumid & julaceous
  • Leaves broadly ovate, very concave, imbricate
  • Costa absent or very short/double
  • Strongly differentiated alar cells (key!)
  • Smooth, linear leaf cells
  • Epiphytic/epilithic in humid regions

Key Variations:

  • Color (green, golden, reddish)
  • Degree of branch tumidity
  • Size/shape of alar cell groups
  • (Variations exist among species/genera)

Notable Examples

The family includes genera like Myurium and Oedicladium:

Myurium hebridarum

Mouse-tail Moss

A species found in oceanic regions of Europe (like the Hebrides, hence the name), Macaronesia, and elsewhere. It forms dense, glossy, yellowish mats with characteristic smooth, julaceous branches resembling mouse tails. Grows on rocks and soil, often near the coast.

Oedicladium rufescens

(No widely used common name)

A common species in tropical and subtropical Asia, Australasia, and the Pacific. Often robust, forming reddish or golden-brown glossy mats with tumid, julaceous branches on trees and rocks in humid forests.

Phylogeny and Classification

Myuriaceae is classified within the Class Bryatae (true mosses) and is placed in the large pleurocarpous order Hypnales. Its phylogenetic position within Hypnales suggests relationships with other families characterized by strong alar cell differentiation and often glossy appearance, such as Sematophyllaceae or Pylaisiadelphaceae, although its exact placement can vary between studies.

The family represents a distinct lineage of pleurocarpous mosses adapted primarily to humid, often oceanic or tropical/subtropical environments. The combination of julaceous branches, ecostate leaves, and prominent alar cells defines the group morphologically.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Division: Bryophyta (Mosses)
  • Class: Bryatae (or Bryopsida)
  • Order: Hypnales
  • Family: Myuriaceae

Related Families (within Order Hypnales)

Myuriaceae shares ancestry and some morphological similarities with other families in the Hypnales:

  • Sematophyllaceae: A large family, many members have distinct alar cells but typically differ in habit (often non-julaceous, leaves may be falcate).
  • Pylaisiadelphaceae: Includes glossy pleurocarps, some epiphytic, but generally differ in leaf shape and alar cell structure.
  • Lepyrodontaceae: Also julaceous, ecostate, with distinct alar cells, but leaves usually more pointed and distribution mainly Southern Hemisphere.
  • Hypnaceae / Plagiotheciaceae: Large families within Hypnales, generally differing in costa presence, alar cell structure, and habit.

Evolutionary Significance

Myuriaceae showcases a successful morphological form within the pleurocarpous mosses, characterized by the combination of julaceous branches, ecostate leaves, and strong alar cell development. This suite of features likely represents adaptations to epiphytic or epilithic life in humid environments, possibly related to water retention or structural support. The family's wide distribution across different continents provides insights into the biogeography and diversification of the Hypnales order.
