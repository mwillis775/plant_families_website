Neckeraceae

Neckera Moss Family

Neckeraceae is a large and diverse family of primarily pleurocarpous mosses, often characterized by their distinctive flattened (complanate) shoots, where the leaves are arranged to lie mostly in one plane. Many species exhibit transversely undulate (wavy) leaves. They typically grow as epiphytes on trees or epiliths on rocks, forming mats, tufts, or pendent festoons, and are found worldwide, especially in temperate and tropical regions.

Overview

The Neckeraceae family belongs to the order Hypnales (though sometimes placed in Leucodontales or its own order Neckerales). It is a moderately large family with around 15-20 genera and approximately 200 species. Key genera include Neckera, Homalia, Thamnobryum, and Porotrichum. These mosses are cosmopolitan but show greatest diversity in tropical mountains and temperate forests.

The complanate arrangement of shoots and leaves is a hallmark of many Neckeraceae, giving them a flattened, frond-like appearance. Undulate leaves are also common in several genera, adding to their distinctive look. While primarily pleurocarpous, the growth habit varies from creeping mats to erect, tree-like (dendroid) forms or long, hanging festoons. Sporophytes are notable for often having short setae, resulting in capsules that are immersed among the perichaetial leaves, emergent, or only shortly exserted. The peristome is double but frequently reduced.

Ecologically, Neckeraceae are important epiphytes and epiliths, contributing to biodiversity and ecosystem processes in forest environments. Their flattened structure may be an adaptation for light capture or water shedding/retention in their specific microhabitats.

  • Number of Species: Approximately 200
  • Distribution: Cosmopolitan, most diverse in tropical and temperate regions.
  • Evolutionary Group: Bryophytes - Mosses - Hypnales (or Neckerales/Leucodontales)

Key Characteristics (Moss Morphology)

Gametophyte: Growth Form and Habit

Plants are pleurocarpous, exhibiting diverse habits: forming mats, tufts, dendroid (tree-like) structures, or pendent festoons. A key feature of many genera is the complanate arrangement of stems and leaves, appearing flattened in one plane. Plants range from small to large and robust, often glossy or silky.

Gametophyte: Stems

Primary stems are typically creeping, giving rise to secondary stems that may be simple, pinnately branched, or dendroid. A central strand is variable, present or absent depending on the genus or stem type.

Gametophyte: Leaves

Leaves are often differentiated between main stems and branches, and frequently asymmetric to facilitate the complanate arrangement. Lateral leaves are typically larger and spreading, while dorsal and ventral leaves may be smaller and appressed. Leaf shape is variable (e.g., ovate, oblong, lingulate). A distinctive feature in many species is the presence of transverse undulations (waves) across the leaf surface. The costa is typically single and short to moderate in length (ending mid-leaf or below), but can be short and double or absent in some genera. Leaf cells are usually smooth, typically short (isodiametric, rhombic) to moderately elongated (linear), often thick-walled.

Sporophyte: Seta, Capsule, and Peristome

Sexual condition is usually dioicous or autoicous. The seta is typically short (sometimes longer in dendroid forms like Thamnobryum), often smooth. This results in the capsule being immersed among perichaetial leaves, emergent, or only shortly exserted above them. Capsules are usually erect and symmetrical, ovoid to oblong-cylindrical. The operculum is typically conical to rostrate. The peristome is double but often reduced compared to the typical hypnoid type; the endostome (inner teeth) is particularly variable, often with narrow segments, rudimentary cilia, or a low basal membrane.

Field Identification (Mosses)

Identifying Neckeraceae often involves recognizing their growth form, leaf arrangement, and habitat:

Primary Identification Features (Field/Hand Lens)

  • Complanate shoots: Look for stems and leaves flattened into one plane, like a pressed frond.
  • Undulate leaves: Many species have distinctly wavy leaves (visible with hand lens).
  • Pleurocarpous habit: Growing in mats, tufts, dendroid forms, or hanging strands (not upright tufts with terminal sporophytes).
  • Habitat: Typically epiphytic on tree bark or epilithic on rocks, often in shaded, humid locations.
  • Immersed/Emergent capsules: If sporophytes are present, look for capsules nestled among the upper leaves on short setae.

Secondary Identification Features (Microscope often needed)

  • Leaf asymmetry: Confirm that lateral leaves differ in shape/size from dorsal/ventral leaves.
  • Costa details: Check length and structure (single, short/double, absent).
  • Cell shape: Typically short, smooth cells (isodiametric, rhombic, short-linear).
  • Peristome structure: Examine the often reduced double peristome in mature capsules.

Common Confusion Points (Other Mosses)

  • Plagiotheciaceae: Also pleurocarpous and often complanate, but usually grow in flatter mats on soil/logs, leaves typically symmetric and non-undulate, costa short/double or absent, capsules usually long-exserted and inclined.
  • Hookeriaceae: Some are complanate, but often have larger cells, bordered leaves, or double costae. Primarily tropical/subtropical.
  • Meteoriaceae: Often pendent and epiphytic, but typically have radially arranged (not complanate) leaves and more filiform stems.
  • Lembophyllaceae: Some are epiphytic/epilithic, but usually have radially arranged, often ecostate leaves and turgid shoots.
  • Homomallium (Hypnaceae): Can be complanate but typically smaller, with different leaf shape and cell structure.

Field Guide Quick Reference (Mosses)

Look For:

  • Flattened (complanate) shoots
  • Wavy (undulate) leaves (often)
  • Pleurocarpous habit (mats, tufts, dendroid, pendent)
  • Epiphytic or epilithic habitat
  • Immersed or shortly exserted capsules
  • Short, single costa (usually)

Key Variations:

  • Growth habit (mat vs. dendroid vs. pendent)
  • Presence/absence of leaf undulations
  • Leaf shape
  • Glossiness
  • Costa length

Notable Genera

Key genera within the Neckeraceae family include:

Neckera

(Genus)

The type genus. Typically forms mats or pendent festoons with strongly complanate shoots and transversely undulate leaves. Costa is usually short and single or absent. Capsules immersed or emergent. N. pennata and N. complanata are well-known species.

Homalia

(Genus)

Forms glossy, green mats, often on tree bases or rocks near water. Shoots are strongly complanate but leaves are typically not undulate, often oblong-lingulate and asymmetric. Costa single, ending below apex. Capsules shortly exserted. H. trichomanoides is common.

Thamnobryum

Tree Moss (Genus)

Distinctive for its dendroid (tree-like) growth habit, with erect secondary stems arising from creeping primary stems, resembling miniature trees. Often found on rocks or soil near streams. Leaves on 'branches' are often ovate; costa strong. Capsules exserted on longer setae than typical Neckeraceae. (Placement sometimes debated - Thamnobryaceae).

Porotrichum (syn. Porothamnium)

(Genus)

Similar to Thamnobryum with a dendroid or frondose habit, common in tropical and subtropical regions. Often grows pendent or spreading from trunks or rocks. Distinguished by details of branching and leaf structure. Costa typically strong.

Phylogeny and Classification

Neckeraceae is traditionally placed within the order Hypnales, although its exact phylogenetic position and circumscription have been debated, with some classifications placing it or related families in orders like Leucodontales or a separate Neckerales. Molecular studies generally support its inclusion within the broader Hypnalean lineage, related to other epiphytic/epilithic pleurocarpous families.

The evolution of the complanate shoot system is a major feature, likely an adaptation for optimizing light capture or water relations in shaded, humid environments. The frequent reduction of the sporophyte (short seta, reduced peristome) might be linked to dispersal strategies in these habitats, possibly favoring vegetative propagation or short-range spore dispersal. The diversity of growth forms (mats, dendroid, pendent) within the family highlights adaptive radiation within the epiphytic/epilithic niche.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Division: Bryophyta (Mosses)
  • Class: Bryopsida
  • Order: Hypnales (or Neckerales / Leucodontales)
  • Family: Neckeraceae

Related Families (Hypnales / Leucodontales)

Neckeraceae shares evolutionary links with other pleurocarpous families adapted to similar habitats:

  • Lembophyllaceae: Often epiphytic/epilithic, includes pendent forms, but typically have radially arranged leaves, often ecostate.
  • Meteoriaceae: Primarily pendent epiphytes, but usually with radially arranged leaves on filiform stems.
  • Leucodontaceae: Epiphytic/epilithic, often with arching secondary stems, but leaves typically ecostate and radially arranged.
  • Fontinalaceae: Aquatic mosses, sometimes placed near Neckeraceae phylogenetically, but ecologically very different.
  • Cryphaeaceae: Often epiphytic with short setae and immersed/emergent capsules, but typically with radially arranged leaves.

Evolutionary Significance

The Neckeraceae family is significant for illustrating:

  • Evolution of Complanate Growth: A key adaptation in pleurocarpous mosses, likely related to light capture or water management on vertical surfaces.
  • Diversity in Epiphytic/Epilithic Niches: Shows radiation into various growth forms (mats, dendroid, pendent) within bark and rock habitats.
  • Sporophyte Reduction: A common trend in some pleurocarpous lineages, possibly linked to habitat stability or alternative reproductive modes.
  • Phylogenetic Complexity: Represents a challenging group within pleurocarp classification, highlighting ongoing research into moss evolution.
