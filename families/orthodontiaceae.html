Orthodontiaceae

Orthodontium Moss Family

Orthodontiaceae is a small family of typically slender, small acrocarpous mosses that form loose tufts or cushions. They are often found on decaying wood, acidic humus, peat, or sandstone rocks, particularly in temperate regions. The family is characterized by its long, narrow leaves (often linear-lanceolate to setaceous) and erect capsules borne on long setae, featuring a distinctive double peristome.

Overview

The Orthodontiaceae family belongs to the large order Bryales. It is a small family, primarily comprising the genus Orthodontium, with perhaps one other small genus sometimes included (Hymenodon, often placed elsewhere). There are approximately 10-15 species recognized within Orthodontium. While the family has centers of diversity in the temperate Southern Hemisphere (Australasia, southern Africa, South America), some species, notably Orthodontium lineare, have become widespread and even invasive in the Northern Hemisphere.

These mosses are typically inconspicuous due to their small size and slender habit. Key features include the very narrow, often flexuose (wavy when dry) leaves composed of linear cells, and the erect, symmetrical capsules elevated on long, thin setae. The double peristome is characteristic, with pale exostome teeth and narrow, linear endostome segments that often initially cohere into a cone.

Ecologically, Orthodontiaceae species are often pioneers or inhabitants of specific acidic substrates like rotting logs, peaty banks, or sandstone crevices. The invasive success of Orthodontium lineare in Europe and North America, particularly on coniferous stumps, highlights the adaptability of some members of this family.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Orthodontiaceae
  • Common Name: Orthodontium Moss Family
  • Number of Genera: 1-2 (Mainly Orthodontium)
  • Number of Species: Approximately 10-15
  • Distribution: Widespread, especially temperate Southern Hemisphere; some species cosmopolitan or invasive in Northern Hemisphere.
  • Evolutionary Group: Bryophytes - Mosses - Bryales

Key Characteristics (Moss Morphology)

Gametophyte: Growth Form and Habit

Plants are acrocarpous, small and slender, typically forming loose, soft, green to yellowish-green tufts or cushions. Stems are erect, usually simple or sparsely branched.

Gametophyte: Stems

Stems are thin. A central strand is usually present but may be weak.

Gametophyte: Leaves

Leaves are typically crowded, erect-spreading to spreading, often becoming flexuose or crisped when dry. They are characteristically long and narrow (linear-lanceolate to linear-setaceous or subulate - tapering to a fine point). Leaf apices are acute to acuminate. Margins are usually plane and entire or weakly serrulate near the apex. The costa is single, relatively narrow, and typically ends below the leaf apex. Leaf cells are predominantly linear, smooth, and relatively thin-walled.

Sporophyte: Seta, Capsule, and Peristome

Sexual condition is typically autoicous or sometimes dioicous. The seta is long, slender, smooth, often yellowish or pale brown. The capsule is exserted far above the gametophyte, typically erect and symmetrical (or very slightly inclined), usually oblong-cylindric to pyriform (pear-shaped), often smooth or slightly ribbed when dry, especially below the mouth. The operculum is conical to shortly rostrate. The peristome is double and distinctive: the 16 exostome teeth are lanceolate, pale yellowish, often finely papillose; the endostome consists of a low to moderate basal membrane bearing 16 narrow, linear segments that are often as long as the teeth and frequently cohere at their tips initially, forming a cone. Cilia are rudimentary or absent.

Field Identification (Mosses)

Identifying Orthodontiaceae usually involves recognizing the combination of small size, narrow leaves, habitat, and sporophyte features:

Primary Identification Features (Field/Hand Lens)

  • Habit: Small, slender acrocarpous mosses forming loose tufts.
  • Leaves: Very long and narrow (linear-lanceolate to setaceous), often appearing wavy or flexuose when dry.
  • Habitat: Typically on decaying wood (especially conifer stumps for O. lineare), acidic humus, peat, or sandstone.
  • Sporophytes: Erect, symmetrical capsules borne on long, slender, often pale setae.
  • Acrocarpous growth: Sporophytes terminal on stems/branches.

Secondary Identification Features (Microscope often needed)

  • Leaf Cells: Confirm long, linear, smooth laminal cells.
  • Costa: Single costa ending below the apex.
  • Peristome Structure: Pale exostome teeth; narrow, linear endostome segments often forming a cone (requires mature, open capsules).

Common Confusion Points (Other Mosses)

  • Ditrichaceae (e.g., Ditrichum): Also small acrocarps with narrow, often setaceous leaves, but typically grow on soil, have different peristome structures (often longer, divided teeth), and different cell shapes.
  • Dicranaceae (e.g., Dicranella): Some small species have narrow leaves, but often have falcate-secund leaves, stronger costae, inclined/curved capsules, and different peristomes.
  • Bryaceae (small species): Some small Bryum or related genera might be confused, but usually have broader leaves, different cell shapes (often rhomboidal), and typical Bryoid peristomes with well-developed cilia.
  • Seligeriaceae: Tiny mosses growing on rock, usually calcareous, with setaceous leaves but very different capsules and peristomes.

Field Guide Quick Reference (Mosses)

Look For:

  • Small, slender acrocarpous tufts
  • Long, narrow, often flexuose leaves
  • Habitat on wood, peat, humus, sandstone
  • Erect capsules on long, pale setae
  • Single costa ending below apex

Key Variations:

  • Leaf length and degree of flexuosity
  • Substrate preference
  • Capsule shape (cylindric vs. pyriform)

Notable Genera

The family is dominated by a single main genus:

Orthodontium

Orthodontium Moss (Genus)

The primary genus, encompassing nearly all species in the family. Characterized by the features described for the family: small size, narrow flexuose leaves, long setae, erect capsules, and distinctive peristome. Includes the widespread and invasive species O. lineare, often found colonizing conifer stumps outside its native Southern Hemisphere range.

Orthodontium gracile

Slender Thread-moss

Another species within the genus, illustrating the typical slender habit and narrow leaves. Found in various temperate regions, often on sandstone or peaty soil.

Phylogeny and Classification

Orthodontiaceae is classified within the order Bryales, belonging to the class Bryopsida. It is considered part of the core Bryales lineage, which is characterized by acrocarpous growth and well-developed double peristomes. Within Bryales, Orthodontiaceae is distinguished by its combination of narrow leaves with linear cells, erect capsules, and the specific structure of its peristome (pale exostome, narrow linear endostome segments without cilia).

Molecular studies confirm its placement within Bryales, often grouping near families like Bryaceae and Mniaceae, although its exact sister relationships can vary depending on the study. The family represents an adaptation to specific acidic, often nutrient-poor substrates like decaying wood and peat, with the narrow leaves possibly relating to water retention or uptake in these environments. The invasive nature of O. lineare suggests effective spore dispersal and colonization abilities.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Division: Bryophyta (Mosses)
  • Class: Bryopsida
  • Order: Bryales
  • Family: Orthodontiaceae

Related Families (Order Bryales)

Orthodontiaceae is related to other families within the Bryales order, such as:

  • Bryaceae: A large family sharing the Bryoid peristome type but typically differing in leaf shape (often broader), cell shape (often rhomboidal), and capsule orientation (often inclined/pendent).
  • Mniaceae: Characterized by much larger plants with broad, often bordered leaves and large, isodiametric cells.
  • Leptostomataceae: Mostly Southern Hemisphere family with distinctive peristome features.
  • Phyllodrepaniaceae: Distinguished by strongly asymmetric, falcate leaves.

Evolutionary Significance

The Orthodontiaceae family is interesting for:

  • Adaptation to Specific Substrates: Specialization for growth on acidic wood, peat, and rock.
  • Peristome Specialization: Exhibits a distinct variation of the Bryoid double peristome.
  • Invasive Potential: The case of Orthodontium lineare provides a model for studying moss invasions and dispersal mechanisms.
  • Biogeography: Shows interesting distribution patterns, with centers of diversity in the Southern Hemisphere but successful expansion in the North.
