Orthorrhynchiaceae

Elegant Button Moss Family (Informal)

Orthorrhynchiaceae is a monotypic family of robust, stiff, often glossy pleurocarpous mosses belonging to the order Hypnales. It contains the single genus Orthorrhynchium with its sole species O. elegans. The family is characterized by its often julaceous branches, strongly concave leaves ending in a short point (apiculus), absence of a costa, and strongly differentiated alar cells. It is found primarily in Australasia and the Pacific islands.

Overview

The Orthorrhynchiaceae family, established by S.H. Lin, is monotypic, encompassing only the genus Orthorrhynchium, which in turn contains only the species Orthorrhynchium elegans. These are distinctive, medium-sized to robust, somewhat stiff pleurocarpous mosses. They often form dense, glossy tufts or mats, typically dark green to brownish-green.

The branches are frequently smoothly cylindrical (julaceous) due to the densely overlapping, concave leaves. Key identifying features include the broadly ovate, strongly concave leaves that typically end in a short, abrupt point (apiculus), the absence of a leaf midrib (costa), and the presence of very distinct groups of differentiated cells (alar cells) at the basal corners of the leaves, which are often colored.

Orthorrhynchiaceae are typically found as epiphytes on tree trunks and branches, or sometimes on rocks, within humid forest environments. Their distribution is centered in Australasia (New Zealand, Australia) and extends through various Pacific islands (like New Caledonia, Fiji, Samoa), potentially reaching Southeast Asia. Phylogenetically, the family is placed within the order Hypnales, often considered related to families like Neckeraceae or Lembophyllaceae.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Orthorrhynchiaceae S.H. Lin
  • Common Name: Elegant Button Moss Family (Informal)
  • Number of Genera: 1 (Orthorrhynchium Reichardt)
  • Number of Species: 1 (Orthorrhynchium elegans (Hook.f. & Wilson) Reichardt)
  • Distribution: Australasia, Pacific Islands, possibly SE Asia.
  • Evolutionary Group: Bryophytes - Mosses (Class Bryatae - Order Hypnales)

Key Characteristics (Moss Features)

Gametophyte (Leafy Plant)

The dominant stage. Plants medium to robust, somewhat stiff, often glossy dark green to brownish, pleurocarpous, forming dense tufts or mats.

  • Stems: Creeping primary stems give rise to erect or ascending, densely foliate secondary stems/branches. Branching irregular or subpinnate. Branches often smoothly cylindrical (julaceous) due to densely imbricate leaves.
  • Leaves (Phyllids): Densely crowded and overlapping (imbricate), erect-spreading when moist, appressed when dry. Leaves broadly ovate to oblong-ovate, strongly concave (cochleariform). Apex characteristically ending in a short, abrupt point (apiculus). Margins usually plane or slightly incurved, entire or finely serrulate near apex.
  • Costa (Midrib): Characteristically absent, or very short and double and often indistinct.
  • Leaf Cells: Typically long and narrow (linear), smooth, usually with thick walls, often porose (with pits). Alar cells at the basal corners characteristically strongly differentiated, forming large, distinct groups of inflated, often thick-walled and colored (yellowish or brownish) cells.

Reproductive Structures (Gametophyte)

Antheridia and archegonia are borne in lateral buds on the stems or branches.

Sporophyte (Spore-Producing Structure)

Arises laterally from the gametophyte stem. Consists of:

  • Seta: Usually relatively short to moderate in length, smooth, often reddish.
  • Capsule (Sporangium): Typically erect and symmetric, ovoid to cylindrical. Operculum usually conical to rostrate.

Peristome

Typically double and well-developed (hypnoid type), often similar in structure to families like Neckeraceae or Lembophyllaceae. Consists of 16 outer exostome teeth and an inner endostome with segments and cilia.

Field Identification

Identifying Orthorrhynchiaceae involves recognizing its stiff, glossy habit and specific leaf details:

Primary Identification Features

  • Stiff, Glossy Appearance: Plants often form dense, somewhat rigid tufts or mats with a noticeable sheen.
  • Julaceous Branches: Branches frequently appear smooth and cylindrical due to tightly packed, concave leaves.
  • Concave Leaves with Apiculus: Leaves are broadly ovate and spoon-shaped, ending abruptly in a short point (apiculus).
  • Costa Absent / Very Short Double: Lack of a distinct midrib is crucial (use hand lens).
  • Distinct Alar Cells: The groups of differentiated cells at the leaf base corners are often large and colored (yellowish/brownish), sometimes visible with a hand lens.
  • Habitat and Location: Typically epiphytic in humid forests in Australasia and the Pacific.

Common Confusion Points

  • Myuriaceae: Also often glossy, julaceous, ecostate, with distinct alar cells. Differs in typically being less stiff, having leaves often cuspidate rather than apiculate, and potentially different peristome details. Distribution overlaps but Myuriaceae is more widespread globally.
  • Lepyrodontaceae: Also robust, julaceous, ecostate, with distinct alar cells. Differs in leaves usually being more gradually acuminate (not apiculate) and having a more southerly distribution focus. Peristome is typically reduced.
  • Neckeraceae: Some genera are robust epiphytes, but often strongly complanate (not julaceous) and usually possess a single costa or different cell structure.
  • Lembophyllaceae: Includes robust pleurocarps in similar regions, but typically have a single costa and different leaf shapes/alar cells.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Robust, stiff, glossy moss
  • Dense tufts/mats
  • Branches often julaceous
  • Leaves broad, concave, with an apiculus
  • Costa absent or very short/double
  • Strongly differentiated alar cells (often colored)
  • Epiphytic in Australasia/Pacific

Key Variations:

  • Color (dark green to brownish)
  • Degree of branch stiffness/julaceousness
  • Frequency of sporophytes
  • (Family is monotypic)

Notable Examples

The family is monotypic, containing only one genus and species:

Orthorrhynchium elegans

(No widely used common name)

The sole species in the family Orthorrhynchiaceae. It is a distinctive, robust, glossy moss forming dense tufts with julaceous branches. Its broadly concave, ecostate leaves ending in an apiculus and prominent colored alar cells are key features. Found in humid forests in Australasia and the Pacific.

Phylogeny and Classification

Orthorrhynchiaceae is classified within the Class Bryatae (true mosses) and belongs to the large pleurocarpous order Hypnales. Its exact phylogenetic position within the order has been investigated using molecular data, often placing it near families like Neckeraceae or Lembophyllaceae, suggesting relationships within the complex web of pleurocarpous moss evolution.

The family represents a distinct monotypic lineage characterized by its unique combination of morphological features: robust stiff habit, julaceous branches, ecostate apiculate leaves, and strongly differentiated alar cells. Its distribution pattern across Australasia and the Pacific provides insights into regional bryophyte biogeography.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Division: Bryophyta (Mosses)
  • Class: Bryatae (or Bryopsida)
  • Order: Hypnales
  • Family: Orthorrhynchiaceae

Related Families (within Order Hypnales)

Orthorrhynchiaceae is related to other families within the Hypnales, potentially including:

  • Neckeraceae: Often robust epiphytes, some with similar peristomes, but typically complanate and differing in costa/cell details.
  • Lembophyllaceae: A diverse Southern Hemisphere family, some members robust, but generally differ in costa presence and leaf shape.
  • Myuriaceae: Shares julaceous habit, ecostate leaves, and distinct alar cells, but differs in being less stiff, leaf apex shape, and distribution pattern.
  • Ptychomniaceae / Braithwaiteaceae: Robust Southern Hemisphere pleurocarps but distinguished by strongly plicate leaves with a single costa.

Evolutionary Significance

Orthorrhynchiaceae, as a monotypic family, represents a specialized evolutionary endpoint within the Hypnales. Its combination of features, particularly the strongly differentiated alar cells coupled with ecostate leaves and a robust, julaceous habit, highlights the diverse morphological strategies employed by pleurocarpous mosses in epiphytic environments. Its restricted geographic range makes it important for understanding bryophyte evolution and biogeography in the Australasian and Pacific regions.
