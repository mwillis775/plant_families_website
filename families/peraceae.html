Peraceae

(No common name widely used)

Peraceae is a family of flowering plants in the order Malpighiales, comprising about 5 genera and 135 species of trees, shrubs, and rarely herbs. This family was segregated from the large Euphorbiaceae family based primarily on molecular phylogenetic data. Members typically have simple, alternate leaves, unisexual flowers (plants often dioecious), usually lack petals, and often produce 3-parted capsular fruits. The family has a pantropical distribution.

Overview

The Peraceae family represents one of several lineages recently separated from the broadly defined Euphorbiaceae (spurge family). While sharing some superficial similarities with Euphorbiaceae sensu stricto (like unisexual flowers, often 3-parted fruits), Peraceae forms a distinct clade within the order Malpighiales based on DNA evidence. Key genera include Pera, Clutia, Trigonostemon, Chaetocarpus, and Pogonophora.

Most members are woody trees or shrubs found in tropical regions worldwide. Unlike many Euphorbiaceae sensu stricto, plants in Peraceae typically lack milky latex. Flowers are generally small and inconspicuous, unisexual, and often lack petals. The fruit is commonly a schizocarpic capsule that splits into three segments, releasing seeds that may possess a caruncle (a fleshy appendage aiding dispersal, often by ants).

The family has relatively minor direct economic importance compared to Euphorbiaceae s.s. (which includes rubber, cassava, poinsettia). Some species may be used locally for timber or traditional medicine.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Peraceae Klotzsch
  • Common Name: None widely used
  • Number of Genera: Approximately 5 (Pera, Clutia, Trigonostemon, Chaetocarpus, Pogonophora)
  • Number of Species: Approximately 135
  • Distribution: Pantropical (Americas, Africa, Madagascar, Asia, Malesia).
  • Evolutionary Group: Eudicots - Rosids - Fabids - Malpighiales

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Mostly evergreen or deciduous trees or shrubs. The genus Clutia includes perennial herbs or subshrubs.

Latex

Milky or colored latex is typically absent.

Leaves

Leaves are usually arranged alternately (spirally), rarely opposite. They are simple, with margins typically entire (rarely toothed). Stipules are usually present, though sometimes small or falling early (caducous).

Inflorescence

Flowers are typically borne in axillary clusters, such as fascicles, glomerules (dense clusters), or cymes. Sometimes flowers are solitary. The genus Pera has distinctive glomerules enclosed by large bracts (involucre), superficially resembling a single flower.

Flowers

Flowers are small, unisexual (plants usually dioecious, sometimes monoecious), and actinomorphic (radially symmetrical).

  • Calyx: Consists of usually (3-)5(-8) sepals, which are free or fused at the base, often small and persistent or deciduous.
  • Petals: Usually absent (apetalous). When present (e.g., sometimes in Trigonostemon), they are typically 5, small, and rudimentary.
  • Androecium (Male Flowers): Stamens number from (2-)5 to many (up to 30+). Filaments are free or fused (monadelphous). Anthers open via longitudinal slits. A rudimentary ovary (pistillode) is often present.
  • Gynoecium (Female Flowers): The ovary is superior, composed of usually 3 (sometimes 2 or 4) fused carpels (syncarpous), forming an equal number of locules. Placentation is axile, with typically one (rarely two) pendulous ovule per locule. Styles are usually 3, free or fused at the base, often deeply bifid or lobed. Staminodes (sterile stamens) may be present or absent.

Fruits and Seeds

The fruit is typically a schizocarpic capsule that splits septicidally into 3 (or 2 or 4) one-seeded segments (cocci), usually leaving behind a persistent central column (columella). Less commonly (e.g., in Pera), the fruit is indehiscent and drupe-like. Seeds often possess a conspicuous caruncle (a fleshy outgrowth near the hilum). Endosperm is present, usually fleshy and oily.

Chemical Characteristics

Chemical profiles are diverse but generally lack some of the specific compounds (like certain diterpenes or cyanogenic glycosides) common in Euphorbiaceae sensu stricto. Tannins and other polyphenols are likely present.

Field Identification

Identifying Peraceae requires careful attention, especially distinguishing it from related families within Malpighiales, like Euphorbiaceae and Phyllanthaceae:

Primary Identification Features

  • Habit: Mostly trees or shrubs (rarely herbs - Clutia).
  • Latex: Milky latex usually absent.
  • Leaves: Usually alternate, simple, typically entire, with stipules (may be small/caducous).
  • Flowers: Small, unisexual (plants often dioecious), actinomorphic, usually apetalous (lacking petals).
  • Ovary: Superior, usually 3-locular, with 1 ovule per locule. Styles often 3, bifid.
  • Fruit: Typically a 3-parted schizocarpic capsule (splitting into cocci, leaving a columella). Sometimes a drupe (Pera).
  • Seeds: Often with a caruncle.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Inflorescence: Axillary clusters, glomerules (sometimes involucrate in Pera), or cymes.
  • Stamens: Variable number (5 to many).
  • Distribution: Pantropical.

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Flowering/Fruiting Period: Essential for confirmation, as vegetative features overlap significantly with related families. Check for unisexual flowers and characteristic 3-parted capsules or drupes.
  • Vegetative State: Absence of latex in a plant resembling Euphorbiaceae, combined with alternate simple stipulate leaves, might suggest Peraceae, but requires floral/fruit confirmation.

Common Confusion Points

  • Euphorbiaceae sensu stricto: Often possess milky latex, may have specialized inflorescences (cyathia in Euphorbia), and sometimes different ovary/ovule numbers or fruit types. Petals also usually absent.
  • Phyllanthaceae: Also segregated from Euphorbiaceae s.l., generally lack latex, have alternate simple stipulate leaves, unisexual apetalous flowers, and often 3-parted capsular fruits. Key difference: Phyllanthaceae typically have 2 ovules per locule (vs. 1 in Peraceae).
  • Picrodendraceae: Another segregate family, often with palmately compound leaves or different fruit types (drupes).
  • Other tropical woody families: Requires detailed examination of stipules, flower sexuality, petal presence/absence, ovary/ovule number, placentation, and fruit type.

Field Guide Quick Reference (Pantropical)

Look For:

  • Tree/Shrub (mostly)
  • Latex: Usually absent
  • Leaves: Alternate, simple, stipulate
  • Flowers: Small, unisexual, usually apetalous
  • Ovary: Superior, usually 3-locular, 1 ovule/locule
  • Fruit: Usually 3-parted capsule (schizocarp)
  • Seeds: Often carunculate

Key Distinctions:

  • Usually no latex (vs. many Euphorbiaceae s.s.)
  • 1 ovule per locule (vs. 2 in Phyllanthaceae)
  • 3-parted schizocarp common
  • Flowers usually apetalous
  • Pantropical distribution

Notable Examples

Examples representing the main genera within Peraceae:

Pera spp.

(e.g., P. bumeliifolia)

A Neotropical genus of trees and shrubs. Distinctive for its flower clusters (glomerules) enclosed in 2-4 large bracts, resembling a single flower head ('pseudoanthium'). Flowers are apetalous. The fruit is often drupe-like and indehiscent, unlike the typical capsular fruit in the family.

Clutia spp.

(e.g., C. pulchella - Lightning Bush)

A genus primarily found in Africa and Madagascar, unique in the family for including many herbs and subshrubs alongside shrubs. Flowers are small, apetalous, with usually 5 stamens in male flowers. Fruit is a typical 3-parted schizocarpic capsule.

Trigonostemon spp.

(e.g., T. laevigatus)

A genus of shrubs and small trees found in tropical Asia and Malesia. Unlike most other Peraceae, species in this genus sometimes possess small petals (though often absent). Male flowers often have stamens fused into a column. Fruit is a 3-parted schizocarpic capsule.

Chaetocarpus spp.

(e.g., C. castanocarpus)

A pantropical genus of trees or shrubs. Flowers are apetalous. Fruit is a 3-parted schizocarpic capsule, often covered in bristles or hairs. Stamens can be numerous (10-30+).

Phylogeny and Classification

Peraceae belongs to the large and diverse order Malpighiales, within the fabid clade of rosids. This order contains numerous families, many of which were previously grouped within or associated with Euphorbiaceae sensu lato.

Molecular phylogenetics was essential in recognizing Peraceae as a distinct family. It forms a well-supported clade separate from Euphorbiaceae sensu stricto (which includes Euphorbia itself) and other major segregates like Phyllanthaceae and Picrodendraceae. Within Malpighiales, Peraceae appears to be most closely related to the enigmatic parasitic family Rafflesiaceae (containing the world's largest flowers, Rafflesia) and Euphorbiaceae s.s. These three families form a well-supported clade, sometimes referred to as the 'PER clade'.

Position in Plant Phylogeny (APG IV)

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Clade: Angiosperms (Flowering plants)
  • Clade: Eudicots
  • Clade: Rosids
  • Clade: Fabids
  • Order: Malpighiales
  • Family: Peraceae

Related Families (Order Malpighiales)

Peraceae is part of the large Malpighiales order. Its closest relatives include:

  • Euphorbiaceae (Spurge family): Often have latex, specialized cyathia inflorescences common in Euphorbia, typically 3-parted capsules. Sister or near-sister group.
  • Rafflesiaceae (Rafflesia family): Holoparasitic plants lacking stems, leaves, and chlorophyll, producing enormous flowers. Surprisingly, molecular data places them close to Peraceae/Euphorbiaceae.
  • Phyllanthaceae: Segregated from Euphorbiaceae s.l., lack latex, usually 2 ovules per locule (vs. 1 in Peraceae).
  • Picrodendraceae: Another segregate from Euphorbiaceae s.l., often differing in leaf or fruit characters.
  • (And many other diverse families within Malpighiales, such as Salicaceae, Violaceae, Passifloraceae, Rhizophoraceae, Clusiaceae, Hypericaceae, Linaceae, Erythroxylaceae, Malpighiaceae, etc.)

Evolutionary Significance

Peraceae is significant for understanding plant evolution:

  • Resolving Euphorbiaceae s.l.: Its recognition as a distinct family helps clarify the complex evolutionary history and classification of the massive Euphorbiaceae sensu lato complex.
  • Link to Parasitism: The close relationship between Peraceae/Euphorbiaceae and the holoparasitic Rafflesiaceae is a remarkable finding, suggesting potential evolutionary pathways or shared ancestry leading to extreme parasitic specialization.
  • Malpighiales Diversification: Contributes to understanding the patterns of diversification within the large and ecologically varied Malpighiales order.
  • Biogeography: Its pantropical distribution provides data points for studying the historical assembly and dispersal of tropical floras.
