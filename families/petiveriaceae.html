Petiveriaceae

The Petiveriaceae family belongs to the order Caryophyllales and comprises herbs, shrubs, small trees, and sometimes lianas, primarily distributed in the Neotropics. It includes about 9-13 genera and around 20 species. The family is perhaps best known for Petiveria alliacea (guinea hen weed or anamu), notable for its strong garlic-like odor. Other representative genera include Rivina and Trichostigma.

Overview

Petiveriaceae is a family of flowering plants centered in the tropical and subtropical regions of the Americas. Historically, its genera were often included within the pokeweed family, Phytolaccaceae. However, molecular phylogenetic studies, supported by morphological differences (particularly the single carpel and single ovule in the ovary), led to its recognition as a distinct family within the core Caryophyllales.

Members of this family exhibit a range of growth forms, from small annual herbs like Rivina humilis to shrubs and small trees like some species of Trichostigma, and the well-known perennial herb Petiveria alliacea. The latter is famous for the pungent garlic odor released when its tissues are crushed, due to sulfur compounds. Several species, including Petiveria alliacea and Rivina humilis, have traditional uses in folk medicine or are cultivated ornamentally (like the red-berried Rivina).

Ecologically, Petiveriaceae species occupy various habitats, from forest understories and edges to disturbed areas. Their dispersal mechanisms vary, with the bristly achenes of Petiveria adapted for animal dispersal (epizoochory) and the fleshy berries of Rivina and Trichostigma adapted for consumption by birds (endozoochory).

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Petiveriaceae
  • Common Name: (None widely established; sometimes informally called Guinea Hen Weed Family)
  • Number of Genera: Approximately 9-13
  • Number of Species: Approximately 20
  • Distribution: Primarily Neotropics (Tropical/Subtropical Americas); some species pantropically introduced.
  • Evolutionary Group: Eudicots - Core Eudicots - Caryophyllales

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Plants are typically herbs (annual or perennial), shrubs, small trees, or occasionally lianas (e.g., Trichostigma). Stems can be herbaceous or somewhat woody.

Leaves

Leaves are alternate, simple, usually with entire margins. Stipules are often present, though they can be small or fall off early. Leaves may possess translucent dots or lines. A strong garlic-like odor is characteristic of Petiveria alliacea when leaves are crushed.

Inflorescence

Flowers are arranged in terminal or axillary racemes, spikes, or panicles. Inflorescences are often slender and elongate.

Flowers

Flowers are generally small, actinomorphic (radially symmetrical), and can be bisexual or unisexual (plants sometimes dioecious). Key features include:

  • Perianth: Usually consists of 4(-5) distinct or basally fused tepals (undifferentiated sepals/petals). Tepals are often greenish, white, or pinkish/purplish, lack petals differentiation, and are typically persistent in fruit.
  • Androecium: Stamens vary from 4 to many (often 4 or 8), typically free.
  • Gynoecium: Features a superior ovary usually composed of a single carpel containing a single basal ovule. This unicarpellate condition is a key distinction from the closely related Phytolaccaceae (which usually has multiple carpels). Style(s) 1 or sometimes 4 (e.g., Petiveria).

Fruits and Seeds

The fruit type varies: it is typically a dry, one-seeded fruit (achene) or a fleshy berry.

  • Achenes (e.g., Petiveria) are often ribbed or winged and may bear hooked bristles or spines at the apex, aiding in dispersal by attaching to animals.
  • Berries (e.g., Rivina, Trichostigma) are often brightly colored (red in Rivina) and contain a single seed, dispersed by birds.

Chemical Characteristics

The family contains diverse secondary metabolites. Petiveria is known for sulfur-containing compounds (e.g., dibenzyl trisulfide) causing its odor. Saponins, tannins, alkaloids, and flavonoids are also reported in various species, contributing to their traditional medicinal uses and potential toxicity.

Field Identification

Identifying members of Petiveriaceae involves looking for a combination of features typical of the Caryophyllales order, with specific traits distinguishing the family:

Primary Identification Features

  • Habit: Herbs, shrubs, small trees, or lianas, primarily in the Neotropics.
  • Leaves: Alternate, simple, entire leaves, often with stipules.
  • Garlic Odor: Strong garlic smell when crushing leaves of Petiveria alliacea.
  • Inflorescence: Slender racemes, spikes, or panicles.
  • Flowers: Small, typically with 4(-5) persistent tepals (no distinct petals).
  • Ovary: Superior, composed of a single carpel with a single basal ovule.
  • Fruit: An achene (sometimes bristly, e.g., Petiveria) or a berry (often red, e.g., Rivina), containing one seed.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Stamens often 4 or 8.
  • Persistent tepals often enclose or subtend the fruit.

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Flowering and fruiting times vary depending on the species and local climate, but inflorescences and fruits are often present for extended periods.
  • The garlic odor of Petiveria is present year-round.
  • The bright red berries of Rivina humilis are conspicuous when mature.

Common Confusion Points

  • Phytolaccaceae (Pokeweed Family): Closely related, but typically distinguished by having ovaries with multiple carpels (usually 5-many) and fruits (usually berries) derived from these multi-carpellate ovaries, containing multiple seeds (e.g., Phytolacca americana).
  • Nyctaginaceae (Four O'Clock Family): Can have similar small flowers, but often have opposite leaves and fruits (anthocarps) enclosed by a persistent, modified base of the perianth.
  • Other Caryophyllales families: Careful examination of ovary structure (number of carpels/ovules), fruit type, stipule presence, and flower details is needed for differentiation.

Field Guide Quick Reference (Petiveriaceae)

Look For:

  • Habit: Herb, shrub, tree, liana (Neotropics)
  • Leaves: Alternate, simple, entire, stipules often present
  • Flowers: Small, 4(-5) persistent tepals
  • Ovary: Superior, 1 carpel, 1 ovule
  • Fruit: Achene (sometimes bristly) or berry, 1-seeded
  • Petiveria: Strong garlic odor
  • Rivina: Bright red berries

Key Distinctions:

  • Single carpel/ovule (vs. multi-carpellate in Phytolaccaceae)
  • Fruit type (achene or berry)
  • Garlic odor specific to Petiveria
  • Alternate leaves (vs. often opposite in Nyctaginaceae)

Notable Examples

Several genera represent the diversity within Petiveriaceae:

Petiveria alliacea

Guinea Hen Weed, Anamu

A perennial herb widely known for its strong garlic-like odor due to sulfur compounds. It bears slender spikes of small greenish-white flowers followed by distinctively ribbed achenes with 4 apical hooked bristles. It has a long history of use in traditional medicine across the Neotropics.

Rivina humilis

Pigeonberry, Bloodberry

A common herbaceous perennial, often found in shaded or disturbed areas. It features delicate racemes of small white or pinkish flowers followed by clusters of bright red, translucent berries that are attractive to birds. Often cultivated as an ornamental groundcover.

Trichostigma octandrum

Hoop Vine, Basket Vine

This species is often a woody liana or scrambling shrub found in tropical forests. It has racemes of small flowers, typically with 8 stamens (as the specific epithet suggests), followed by fleshy berries that mature from green to reddish or purplish-black. Its flexible stems are sometimes used locally.

Phylogeny and Classification

Petiveriaceae is firmly placed within the order Caryophyllales, a large and diverse group of core eudicots known for adaptations to extreme environments and unique biochemical pathways (like betalain pigments in many families, though not Petiveriaceae).

Within Caryophyllales, Petiveriaceae belongs to the core clade, often positioned near Phytolaccaceae and Nyctaginaceae. Its separation from Phytolaccaceae is primarily based on the consistent presence of a unicarpellate, uniovulate gynoecium in Petiveriaceae, contrasting with the typically multi-carpellate gynoecium of Phytolaccaceae sensu stricto. Molecular data strongly support this separation and its close relationship to other families previously grouped within or near Phytolaccaceae.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Clade: Angiosperms (Flowering plants)
  • Clade: Eudicots
  • Clade: Core Eudicots
  • Order: Caryophyllales
  • Family: Petiveriaceae

Related Families (Order Caryophyllales)

Petiveriaceae is part of the large Caryophyllales order, which includes diverse families such as:

  • Phytolaccaceae (Pokeweed family) - Closest relative, distinguished by gynoecium structure.
  • Nyctaginaceae (Four O'Clock family)
  • Amaranthaceae (Amaranth family - includes former Chenopodiaceae)
  • Cactaceae (Cactus family)
  • Aizoaceae (Ice Plant family)
  • Caryophyllaceae (Carnation family)
  • Polygonaceae (Buckwheat family)
  • And many others.

Evolutionary Significance

Petiveriaceae contributes to understanding the evolution within the core Caryophyllales:

  • Illustrates the phylogenetic refinement of families previously grouped together based on general similarities (like Phytolaccaceae sensu lato).
  • The consistent unicarpellate gynoecium represents a key morphological character defining this lineage within the order.
  • Showcases diversity in fruit types (achenes vs. berries) and dispersal syndromes within a relatively small, closely related group.
  • Represents a significant component of the Neotropical flora within the Caryophyllales.
