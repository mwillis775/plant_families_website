Phytolaccaceae

The Phytolaccaceae family, commonly known as the Pokeweed Family, belongs to the order Caryophyllales. Its circumscription has been significantly narrowed based on molecular phylogenetic studies; many genera formerly included are now placed in separate families like Petiveriaceae and Rivinaceae. As currently defined (sensu stricto), the family comprises mostly herbs, shrubs, and trees, with the genus Phytolacca being the largest and most well-known. The family has a nearly cosmopolitan distribution but is centered in the Neotropics and Africa.

Overview

Phytolaccaceae sensu stricto (in the strict sense) is now a much smaller family than historically conceived, primarily centered around the genus Phytolacca. It includes about 4-5 genera and roughly 30-35 species. These plants range from large, robust perennial herbs like the familiar American Pokeweed (Phytolacca americana) to shrubs, trees (like the massive Omb√∫, P. dioica), and even lianas (Ercilla).

Members typically have alternate, simple, entire leaves without stipules. The flowers are usually small, lack petals, but often have petal-like sepals (tepals), and are arranged in racemes or spikes. A key feature is the superior ovary composed of multiple carpels (often 5-16) typically arranged in a ring, each usually containing a single ovule. This structure develops into the characteristic fruit, which in Phytolacca is a fleshy, dark purple-black berry composed of the fused carpels, readily consumed by birds which disperse the seeds.

Many species, particularly in Phytolacca, contain toxic compounds (saponins, oxalates) and should be handled with caution, although young shoots of some species are consumed as 'poke sallet' after careful preparation. The family belongs to the core Caryophyllales and is related to families like Nyctaginaceae.

Key Characteristics (Sensu Stricto)

Growth Form and Habit

Mostly perennial herbs (often large and robust), shrubs, or trees; rarely lianas (Ercilla). Stems are often fleshy or semi-woody and may be tinged reddish or purple.

Leaves

Leaves are alternate, simple, with entire (smooth) margins, and usually petiolate. Stipules are absent or minute and inconspicuous.

Inflorescence

Flowers are typically arranged in terminal, axillary, or leaf-opposed racemes or spikes, sometimes forming panicles. Inflorescences are often elongated.

Flowers

Flowers are generally small, actinomorphic (radially symmetrical), and usually bisexual (though sometimes unisexual, e.g., P. dioica). Key features include:

  • Perianth: Consists of a single whorl of usually 5 (4-10) free or basally fused, sepal-like or often petal-like segments (tepals or sepals). These are typically greenish, white, or pinkish/purplish and are persistent, sometimes enlarging in fruit.
  • Petals: Absent.
  • Androecium: Stamens number from (5-)10-30 or more, often arranged in one or two whorls. Filaments are free or basally connate.
  • Gynoecium: Features a superior ovary composed of (3-)5-16(-many) carpels. The carpels are typically distinct or fused together, often arranged in a ring. Each carpel usually contains a single ovule. Styles are distinct or fused at the base, as many as the carpels. A nectar disc may be present or absent.

Fruits and Seeds

The most common fruit type is a fleshy berry, formed from the ring of fused carpels, often dark purple or black when mature (e.g., Phytolacca). Less commonly, fruits can be achenes or samaras (in smaller genera). Seeds are typically lens-shaped, shiny black, with one seed developing per carpel.

Chemical Characteristics

Contains various potentially toxic compounds, including triterpenoid saponins (like phytolaccatoxin), oxalates, alkaloids, and lectins. Ingestion of roots, mature leaves, stems, or unripe berries can cause severe gastrointestinal distress and other symptoms in humans and livestock. Young shoots ('poke sallet') of P. americana are sometimes eaten but require multiple changes of boiling water to remove toxins. The family lacks the betalain pigments found in many other core Caryophyllales families.

Field Identification (Sensu Stricto)

Identifying Phytolaccaceae, particularly the common genus Phytolacca, relies on recognizing a suite of characteristics:

Primary Identification Features

  • Habit: Robust perennial herbs, shrubs, or trees.
  • Leaves: Alternate, simple, entire, no stipules.
  • Stems: Often fleshy, smooth, sometimes reddish/purplish.
  • Inflorescence: Racemes or spikes of small flowers.
  • Flowers: Petals absent; usually 5 greenish, white, or pinkish persistent sepals/tepals.
  • Ovary: Superior, composed of multiple (5-many) carpels typically fused in a ring.
  • Fruit: Usually a fleshy, multi-lobed berry, dark purple/black when mature, formed from the ring of carpels.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Stamens often 10 or more.
  • Seeds shiny black, lens-shaped, 1 per carpel section of the berry.
  • Lack of milky latex.

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Flowers appear from spring through fall.
  • The dark purple berries ripening on reddish stalks in late summer and fall are very characteristic of Phytolacca americana.
  • Large herbaceous stems die back in winter in temperate zones but resprout from large taproots.

Common Confusion Points

  • Petiveriaceae (Petiveria, Rivina): Now separate; distinguished by usually having a single carpel/ovule, often achene fruits (Petiveria) or single-seeded red berries (Rivina), and sometimes a garlic odor (Petiveria). Phytolaccaceae s.s. has multiple carpels forming a compound berry.
  • Polygonaceae (Buckwheat Family): Often have swollen nodes and characteristic sheathing stipules (ocreae), which are absent in Phytolaccaceae. Flower and fruit structure also differs.
  • Other families with racemes: Check for presence/absence of petals, number of sepals/tepals, and especially the structure of the ovary (multiple carpels in a ring) and the resulting berry fruit.

Field Guide Quick Reference (Phytolaccaceae s.s. / Phytolacca)

Look For:

  • Robust herb/shrub/tree
  • Leaves: Alternate, simple, entire, no stipules
  • Racemes/spikes of small flowers
  • Flowers: Petals absent, 5 persistent tepals
  • Ovary: Superior, 5-many carpels in a ring
  • Fruit: Dark purple/black berry (compound)
  • Stems often fleshy/reddish

Key Distinctions:

  • Multi-carpellate ovary/berry (vs. 1 carpel in Petiveriaceae)
  • No ocreae (vs. Polygonaceae)
  • Lack of petals
  • Combination of features distinguishes from other families

Notable Examples (Sensu Stricto)

The family, in its strict sense, is dominated by the genus Phytolacca:

Phytolacca americana

American Pokeweed

A large, fast-growing perennial herb native to eastern North America but widely naturalized elsewhere. Known for its thick reddish stems, large leaves, racemes of white/pinkish flowers, and drooping clusters of dark purple berries. All parts are considered toxic, though young shoots are sometimes eaten after careful preparation.

Phytolacca dioica

Omb√∫

Native to the Pampas of South America, this species grows into a massive, tree-like perennial herb with a very thick, succulent base and trunk (technically herbaceous). It is dioecious and provides significant shade in open grasslands. Culturally important in Argentina and Uruguay.

Ercilla spp.

(No common name)

A small genus of evergreen climbing vines (lianas) native to Chile. They climb using adventitious roots and bear dense spikes or racemes of small flowers followed by berries.

Phylogeny and Classification

Phytolaccaceae belongs to the order Caryophyllales, within the Core Eudicots. The family's definition (circumscription) has been substantially revised based on molecular phylogenetic data. The traditional, broad concept of Phytolaccaceae was found to be polyphyletic (containing unrelated groups).

Consequently, numerous genera were removed to form or populate other families within Caryophyllales, including Petiveriaceae, Rivinaceae, Lophiocarpaceae, Kewaceae, Microteaceae, Barbeuiaceae, and Gisekiaceae (though Gisekiaceae is sometimes placed outside the core Caryophyllales). Phytolaccaceae sensu stricto (in the strict sense) now includes primarily the genus Phytolacca along with a few smaller related genera like Ercilla, Anisomeria, and Nowickea. This core group forms a monophyletic lineage within the core Caryophyllales clade, often showing relationships with Nyctaginaceae.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Clade: Angiosperms (Flowering plants)
  • Clade: Eudicots
  • Clade: Core Eudicots
  • Order: Caryophyllales
  • Family: Phytolaccaceae (sensu stricto)

Related Families (Order Caryophyllales)

Phytolaccaceae s.s. is part of the large core Caryophyllales clade, related to families such as:

  • Nyctaginaceae (Four O'Clock family) - Often considered closely related.
  • Petiveriaceae - Formerly included, now separate.
  • Rivinaceae - Formerly included, now separate.
  • Caryophyllaceae (Carnation family)
  • Amaranthaceae (Amaranth family)
  • Cactaceae (Cactus family)
  • Aizoaceae (Ice Plant family)
  • Polygonaceae (Buckwheat family)
  • And many others within the core Caryophyllales.

Evolutionary Significance

Phytolaccaceae sensu stricto is significant for:

  • Illustrating the impact of molecular systematics in refining family boundaries and revealing true evolutionary relationships within large orders like Caryophyllales.
  • Containing the genus Phytolacca, species of which are often aggressive colonizers and play ecological roles as pioneer plants in disturbed habitats.
  • Showcasing specific morphological traits within Caryophyllales, such as the multi-carpellate gynoecium forming a compound berry.
  • Its widespread distribution, particularly of Phytolacca, which provides opportunities for studying dispersal and adaptation.
