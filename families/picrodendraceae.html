Picrodendraceae

The Picrodendraceae family is a medium-sized family of flowering plants belonging to the order Malpighiales. Like Phyllanthaceae, it largely consists of genera previously classified within the broadly defined Euphorbiaceae. The family includes about 24-25 genera and 80-95 species of shrubs and trees. Picrodendraceae has a wide but notably disjunct pantropical distribution, occurring in the Neotropics, Africa, Madagascar, Southeast Asia, Australia, and Pacific islands. Leaves are often palmately compound.

Overview

Picrodendraceae represents another lineage segregated from the traditional Euphorbiaceae based on molecular phylogenetic evidence. The family comprises woody plants (shrubs and trees) characterized by typically alternate leaves that are often palmately compound (though sometimes simple or unifoliolate), presence of stipules, small unisexual flowers lacking petals, a superior ovary usually with 3 carpels, and fruit that is typically a drupe or sometimes a schizocarp. Like Phyllanthaceae, they generally lack the milky latex common in Euphorbiaceae sensu stricto.

The family exhibits a fascinating pantropical distribution pattern, with different genera occurring in widely separated tropical regions across the globe, suggesting an ancient origin and subsequent fragmentation or long-distance dispersal events. Notable genera include Picrodendron from the Caribbean (known for its toxic fruits), Oldfieldia and Androstachys from Africa and Madagascar (sources of valuable timber), and Austrobuxus found in Australasia and the Pacific.

Phylogenetically, Picrodendraceae is placed within the large order Malpighiales and is considered closely related to Phyllanthaceae.

Quick Facts

  • Common Name: (None widely established)

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Plants are shrubs or small to large trees. Often resinous.

Leaves

Leaves are usually alternate (rarely opposite) and typically palmately compound with 3 or more leaflets radiating from a single point (though sometimes unifoliolate or simple, e.g., Androstachys). Leaflets usually have entire margins. Stipules are present, though often small and sometimes falling early.

Inflorescence

Flowers are typically borne in axillary clusters, cymules, racemes, or spikes; sometimes flowers are solitary.

Flowers

Flowers are small, actinomorphic (radially symmetrical), and unisexual, with plants usually being dioecious (separate male and female individuals), less commonly monoecious. Key features include:

  • Perianth: Usually consists of a single whorl of (2-)3-6(-8) small, free or basally fused, sepal-like segments (tepals or sepals), often overlapping (imbricate).
  • Petals: Absent.
  • Androecium (Male Flowers): Features (2-)4-many stamens (often numerous). Filaments are free or sometimes fused at the base. Anthers dehisce via longitudinal slits. A rudimentary ovary (pistillode) is often present.
  • Gynoecium (Female Flowers): Staminodes (sterile stamens) may be present or absent. Features a superior ovary composed of (2-)3(-4) fused carpels. The ovary is typically 3-locular with 1 or 2 ovules per locule on axile placentas. There are (2-)3(-4) styles, which are free or fused only at the base and often deeply bifid (forked) or multifid. A nectar disc is usually absent.

Fruits and Seeds

The fruit is typically a drupe (fleshy or leathery with 1-3 hard inner stones/pyrenes containing the seeds) or sometimes a schizocarp that splits into segments (cocci). Seeds usually number 1 per locule or fruit segment, are often relatively large, and may possess a caruncle (fleshy appendage). Endosperm is usually present. Milky latex is absent.

Chemical Characteristics

The family contains various secondary metabolites, including tannins, alkaloids, and terpenoids. Some species (Androstachys, Oldfieldia) are known for durable, valuable timber. The fruits/seeds of Picrodendron are reported to be toxic.

Field Identification

Identifying members of Picrodendraceae requires considering their woody habit, leaf type, floral characteristics, and geographic location:

Primary Identification Features

  • Habit: Shrubs or trees.
  • Leaves: Usually alternate and typically palmately compound (check for simple/unifoliolate exceptions); stipules present.
  • Latex: Milky latex absent.
  • Sexual System: Usually dioecious (separate male/female plants).
  • Flowers: Small, unisexual, petals absent, sepals/tepals (2-)3-6.
  • Stamens (Male Fls): Often numerous (4-many).
  • Ovary (Female Fls): Superior, usually 3-carpellate.
  • Styles: Usually 3, often bifid.
  • Fruit: Typically a drupe or sometimes a schizocarp.
  • Distribution: Pantropical but disjunct.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Often resinous.
  • Ovules usually 1 or 2 per locule.

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Flowering and fruiting times vary greatly depending on the genus and tropical location.
  • Leaf type (palmately compound vs. simple) and fruit type (drupe vs. schizocarp) are key diagnostic features when available.

Common Confusion Points

Distinguishing Picrodendraceae from other families:

  • Euphorbiaceae sensu stricto: Often has latex; typically 1 ovule per locule; Euphorbia has unique cyathia. Picrodendraceae lacks latex and usually has palmately compound leaves (less common in Euphorbiaceae s.s.).
  • Phyllanthaceae: Closely related, also lacks latex, often has 3-carpellate ovary and schizocarpic fruit. However, Phyllanthaceae typically has simple leaves (though phyllanthoid branching can mimic compound leaves) and usually 2 ovules per locule. Palmately compound leaves are rare in Phyllanthaceae.
  • Sapindaceae (Soapberry Family): Often has compound leaves (pinnate or palmate), but flowers usually have petals, often zygomorphic, and fruit is typically a capsule or schizocarp, not usually a simple drupe.
  • Araliaceae (Ginseng Family): Often has palmately compound leaves, but typically has umbellate inflorescences and an inferior ovary.
  • The combination of often palmately compound leaves, lack of latex, dioecy, apetalous flowers, superior 3-carpellate ovary, and drupe/schizocarp fruit is characteristic.

Field Guide Quick Reference (Picrodendraceae)

Look For:

  • Shrub or tree (Pantropical)
  • Leaves: Usually alternate, palmately compound (or simple/unifoliolate)
  • Stipules present
  • Latex absent
  • Dioecious (usually)
  • Flowers: Small, unisexual, petals absent
  • Ovary: Superior, usually 3-carpellate
  • Fruit: Drupe or schizocarp

Key Distinctions:

  • Palmately compound leaves common (vs. simple in most Phyllanthaceae)
  • Lack of latex (vs. many Euphorbiaceae)
  • Apetalous flowers
  • Drupe/schizocarp fruit (vs. legumes in Fabaceae, capsules in Sapindaceae)
  • Superior ovary (vs. inferior in Araliaceae)

Notable Examples

Picrodendraceae includes several genera with distinct characteristics and distributions:

Picrodendron baccatum

Jamaica Walnut

A tree native to the Caribbean islands (e.g., Jamaica, Cuba, Hispaniola). It has palmately compound leaves with 3 leaflets and produces fleshy drupes that are reportedly toxic.

Oldfieldia spp.

African Oak

A genus of trees found in tropical Africa. Species like Oldfieldia africana yield valuable, hard, durable timber sometimes referred to as African Oak or Dantou. They typically have palmately compound leaves.

Androstachys johnsonii

Lebombo Ironwood

A tree native to southeastern Africa and Madagascar, known for its extremely hard, heavy, and durable wood (ironwood). Unlike most of the family, it has simple, opposite leaves. Its placement is sometimes debated, occasionally segregated into its own family.

Austrobuxus spp.

(No common name)

A genus of shrubs and trees with species distributed across Australia, New Caledonia, Fiji, and Malesia. They typically have simple leaves, showcasing the morphological diversity within the family.

Phylogeny and Classification

Picrodendraceae is classified within the large order Malpighiales, belonging to the Rosid clade of eudicots. Like Phyllanthaceae, it represents a lineage that was traditionally included within a broadly defined Euphorbiaceae but is now recognized as a distinct family based on molecular phylogenetic evidence.

Within Malpighiales, Picrodendraceae is generally considered to be closely related to Phyllanthaceae. Together, these two families form a significant part of the diversity previously encompassed by Euphorbiaceae sensu lato. Some studies suggest Picrodendraceae might not be strictly monophyletic as currently defined, with certain genera like Androstachys potentially representing separate lineages, but it is usually treated as a single family in current classifications like APG IV.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Clade: Angiosperms (Flowering plants)
  • Clade: Eudicots
  • Clade: Rosids
  • Order: Malpighiales
  • Family: Picrodendraceae

Related Families (Order Malpighiales)

Picrodendraceae is part of the Malpighiales order, one of the largest orders of flowering plants. Closely related families include:

  • Phyllanthaceae (Leaf Flower family) - Often considered the sister group or very closely related.
  • Putranjivaceae
  • Euphorbiaceae (Spurge family - sensu stricto)
  • Ixonanthaceae
  • Other families within the vast Malpighiales order include Salicaceae, Violaceae, Passifloraceae, Rhizophoraceae, Linaceae, Malpighiaceae, etc.

Evolutionary Significance

Picrodendraceae is evolutionarily significant for:

  • Further illustrating the complex relationships within Malpighiales and the redefinition of family boundaries previously obscured within Euphorbiaceae sensu lato.
  • Its intriguing pantropical but highly disjunct distribution, suggesting ancient origins possibly dating back to Gondwana, followed by vicariance and/or long-distance dispersal.
  • Showcasing morphological diversity, particularly in leaf structure (palmately compound vs. simple/unifoliolate leaves within the same family).
  • Contributing to the understanding of diversification patterns in tropical woody plant lineages.
