Platanaceae

Plane Tree Family / Sycamore Family

Platanaceae is a small, essentially monogeneric family of basal eudicots (order Proteales), comprising only the genus Platanus with about 8-10 species. These are large, deciduous trees native to the Northern Hemisphere, instantly recognizable by their distinctive, patchy, exfoliating bark, large, alternate, palmately lobed leaves resembling maple leaves, and dense, spherical clusters ("buttonballs") of tiny, wind-pollinated flowers and fruits.

Overview

Plane trees or sycamores (genus Platanus) are among the largest temperate deciduous trees in the Northern Hemisphere. Their most striking feature is the bark, which flakes off in irregular patches, revealing lighter inner bark layers (often white, gray, greenish, or tan), creating a characteristic mottled or camouflage-like pattern. Their large, alternate, palmately lobed leaves strongly resemble those of maples (Acer), but maples have opposite leaves and different fruits (winged samaras).

Platanaceae are monoecious (having separate male and female flower clusters on the same tree) and wind-pollinated. The flowers themselves are tiny and lack showy petals, densely packed into spherical heads that hang on long stalks. The male heads disintegrate after releasing pollen in spring, while the female heads develop into dense, persistent, spherical clusters of small, hairy, single-seeded fruits (achenes). These "buttonballs" often hang on the tree through winter before breaking apart to release the seeds.

Several species and hybrids are widely planted as durable and fast-growing shade and street trees in cities worldwide due to their tolerance of urban conditions and pollution. The most common include the American Sycamore (Platanus occidentalis), native to eastern and central North America including stream banks and bottomlands in Oklahoma (current location as of March 27, 2025); the Oriental Plane (P. orientalis) from southeastern Europe and western Asia; and their hybrid, the London Plane (P. × acerifolia), which is particularly common in urban plantings.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Platanaceae T. Lestib.
  • Common Name: Plane Tree family, Sycamore family
  • Number of Genera: 1 (Platanus)
  • Number of Species: Approximately 8-10
  • Distribution: Native to temperate and subtropical regions of North America (USA, Mexico), southeastern Europe, and southern Asia. Widely planted elsewhere.
  • Evolutionary Group: Eudicots (Basal Eudicot)
  • Order: Proteales

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Large, deciduous trees with stout trunks and spreading crowns, capable of reaching massive sizes. Bark is highly characteristic, exfoliating (peeling off) in thin, irregular plates, revealing layers of lighter colored inner bark (white, pale gray, greenish, tan), creating a mottled appearance.

Leaves

Leaves are simple, large (often 15-30 cm wide or more), arranged alternately along often zigzag twigs. The blade is palmately lobed with typically 3, 5, or sometimes 7 major pointed lobes (resembling maple leaves), and palmately veined. Leaf margins can be entire or coarsely toothed. A key feature is the base of the petiole, which is swollen and cone-like, completely covering and protecting the axillary bud until the leaf falls. Large, leaf-like stipules encircle the twig at the base of the petiole; these are often shed early but leave a distinct scar ringing the twig.

Inflorescence

Plants are monoecious. Flowers are tiny, unisexual, and densely packed into tight, spherical heads (capitula). These heads hang pendulously on long stalks (peduncles), with usually 1-5 (sometimes more) heads per stalk.

Flowers

Flowers are highly reduced, adapted for wind pollination (anemophily). Both male and female flowers are actinomorphic and hypogynous.

  • Perianth: Rudimentary, consisting of usually 3-8 small, scale-like sepals and 3-8 even smaller petals (or tepals), often hidden by dense hairs.
  • Male Flowers (Staminate): Contain usually 3-8 stamens with very short filaments and long anthers. The connective tissue at the anther apex is often expanded into a small cap. A rudimentary pistil (pistillode) may be present or absent.
  • Female Flowers (Pistillate): Contain usually 5-9 distinct (apocarpous), superior pistils (simple carpels). Each pistil has an elongated style with a stigmatic surface along one side and contains 1 (rarely 2) ovule(s). Sterile stamens (staminodes) and/or numerous hairs are often present around the pistils.

Fruits and Seeds

The fruit is a dense, spherical aggregate head (a multiple fruit) composed of numerous individual achenes. Each achene is small, dry, single-seeded, elongated or wedge-shaped, and crowned by the persistent style. The base of the achene is surrounded by a dense tuft of long, stiff, yellowish-brown hairs. The entire head typically persists through winter and breaks apart in late winter or spring, with the hairy achenes dispersed by wind and water.

Chemical Characteristics

Contain tannins and various phenolic compounds. Hairs on young leaves and twigs can be irritating to skin or respiratory passages. The family isn't particularly noted for unique or potent alkaloids or glycosides found in some other plant groups.

Field Identification

Identifying Platanaceae (Platanus trees) is usually straightforward based on their distinctive bark, leaves, and fruit clusters.

Primary Identification Features

  • Exfoliating, Patchy Bark: Bark peels in irregular plates, revealing lighter inner layers (mottled white, gray, tan, green).
  • Large, Alternate, Palmately Lobed Leaves: Resemble maple leaves but are arranged alternately on the stem.
  • Petiole Base Covers Bud: The swollen base of the leaf stalk completely encloses the next year's bud.
  • Spherical Flower/Fruit Heads: Dense, round clusters ("buttonballs") hanging on long stalks.
  • Fruit an Aggregate of Hairy Achenes: The "buttonball" is composed of many small, dry, hairy, single-seeded fruits.
  • Large Tree Habit: Typically grow into large deciduous trees, often found near water.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Zigzag Twigs: Twigs often grow in a slight zigzag pattern between nodes.
  • Encircling Stipule Scar: A thin line completely circling the twig at each node marks where the large stipule was attached.
  • Wind Pollination Features: In spring, look for inconspicuous male (pollen-producing) and female flower heads.
  • Persistent Fruit Heads: The "buttonballs" often remain on the tree throughout winter.

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Year-round: The distinctive patchy, exfoliating bark is the most reliable feature at any season. Tree form and habitat (often near streams) are also helpful.
  • Spring: New leaves emerge; tiny flower heads appear on long stalks.
  • Summer: Large leaves fully developed; fruit heads forming.
  • Fall: Leaves turn yellow or brown and fall; mature fruit heads conspicuous.
  • Winter: Bare tree structure shows alternate branching and zigzag twigs; persistent "buttonballs" are prominent.
  • (Current Time Context: Late March in Oklahoma - Sycamores (Platanus occidentalis) are likely budding or beginning to leaf out. Old fruit balls may still be present or breaking apart).

Common Confusion Points

  • Maples (Acer, Sapindaceae): Have similar palmately lobed leaves. Key difference: Maples have opposite leaf arrangement and produce winged fruits (samaras), not spherical heads. Buds are visible in leaf axils. Bark is usually furrowed or ridged, not exfoliating in patches like Platanus.
  • Sweetgum (Liquidambar styraciflua, Altingiaceae): Also a large tree with alternate, palmately lobed leaves. Key differences: Fruit is a spiky, woody ball (multiple of capsules), not hairy achenes. Bark is typically dark and deeply furrowed, sometimes with corky wings on twigs. Buds are visible.
  • Some Oaks (Quercus species): Large trees with alternate leaves, but leaves are typically pinnately lobed (not palmately), and fruit is an acorn.

The combination of alternate, maple-like leaves and distinctive patchy bark is usually sufficient. Confirm with the petiole covering the bud or the presence of "buttonball" fruits.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Large deciduous tree
  • Patchy, exfoliating bark (mottled)
  • Alternate, palmately lobed leaves (maple-like)
  • Petiole base encloses bud
  • Spherical flower/fruit heads ("buttonballs")
  • Fruit head = aggregate of hairy achenes

Key Variations (Species):

  • Number of fruit heads per stalk
  • Depth of leaf lobing
  • Degree of bark whiteness
  • Native range

Notable Examples

The Platanaceae family consists of a single genus, Platanus, with several well-known species.

Platanus occidentalis

American Sycamore

A massive deciduous tree native to eastern and central North America, including Oklahoma, typically found in bottomlands and along streams. Known for its striking white upper bark revealed by exfoliation. Leaves are large with broad, shallow lobes (usually 3-5). Typically bears only one spherical fruit head per stalk.

Platanus orientalis

Oriental Plane

A large deciduous tree native to southeastern Europe and western Asia. Similar to American Sycamore but often has more deeply lobed leaves (usually 5 lobes) and typically bears 2-6 fruit heads per stalk. Bark tends to be more yellowish or greenish-gray when exfoliating. Long-lived and culturally significant in its native range.

Platanus × acerifolia

London Plane

A hybrid between P. occidentalis and P. orientalis, widely believed to have originated in cultivation in Spain or England. Extremely popular and widely planted as a street and park tree globally due to its tolerance of urban pollution and soil conditions. Morphologically intermediate between parents, often with 2-3 fruit heads per stalk and moderately lobed leaves. Bark often exfoliates to yellowish or greenish inner layers.

Platanus racemosa

California Sycamore / Western Sycamore

A deciduous tree native to California and Baja California, Mexico, typically found along streams and canyons. Has deeply lobed leaves (usually 5 lobes) that are often fuzzy underneath. Characteristically bears multiple (3-7) fruit heads on each stalk. Bark often shows striking white patches.

Phylogeny and Classification

Platanaceae belongs to the small order Proteales, which is positioned near the base of the Eudicot evolutionary tree (basal eudicots), separate from the large Asterid and Rosid clades. Within Proteales, molecular data firmly places Platanaceae as the sister group to the large and diverse family Proteaceae (primarily Southern Hemisphere). The order also includes the unique aquatic family Nelumbonaceae (sacred lotuses).

The genus Platanus represents an ancient lineage with a fossil record extending well back into the Cretaceous period, indicating that trees very similar to modern plane trees existed alongside dinosaurs. Their phylogenetic position highlights early divergences within the eudicots. The family's characteristic features, such as wind pollination with reduced flowers in dense heads, apocarpous gynoecium (distinct pistils), and unique stipule/bud structure, reflect its long and distinct evolutionary history.

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Clade: Angiosperms (Flowering plants)
  • Clade: Eudicots
  • Clade: (Basal Eudicot)

Related Families

Within the order Proteales, Platanaceae is related to:

  • Proteaceae (Protea / Macadamia family): Sister family. Large, diverse family mainly in Southern Hemisphere. Mostly woody shrubs/trees, often with complex or showy flower clusters (sometimes heads), superior ovary usually with 1 carpel, fruit often a follicle or nut.
  • Nelumbonaceae (Lotus family): Aquatic herbs with large, peltate leaves and large, solitary flowers with numerous parts; phylogenetically distinct but placed within Proteales.
  • (Sabiaceae is sometimes included in or placed near Proteales).

Evolutionary Significance

Platanaceae, essentially the genus Platanus, provides a window into early eudicot evolution. Its placement in Proteales links it to predominantly Southern Hemisphere families, suggesting ancient Gondwanan connections despite its current Northern Hemisphere distribution. The family showcases successful adaptation to riparian habitats and the evolution of wind pollination involving highly specialized inflorescences (dense unisexual heads) and fruit dispersal mechanisms (hairy achenes). Its distinctive exfoliating bark and the unique way the petiole base protects the axillary bud are other notable evolutionary traits. The ancient fossil record confirms the long-term persistence of this distinctive tree morphology.
