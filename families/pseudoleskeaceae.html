Pseudoleskeaceae

Pseudoleskea Moss Family (Informal)

Pseudoleskeaceae is a family of typically small to medium-sized, dull, often wiry pleurocarpous mosses belonging to the order Hypnales. Including genera like Pseudoleskea, Leskeella, and Pseudoleskeella, they are characterized by a strong single costa, small papillose leaf cells, and often the presence of paraphyllia. They are primarily found in Northern Hemisphere temperate to arctic/alpine regions, often on rocks or tree bases.

Overview

The Pseudoleskeaceae family, established by Schimper, encompasses several genera of primarily dull, often wiry pleurocarpous mosses, including Pseudoleskea, Leskeella, and Pseudoleskeella. The taxonomic boundaries of this family are sometimes debated, with close affinities to Leskeaceae (sometimes being included within it) and Thuidiaceae. These mosses typically form intricate mats or tufts, often appearing dark green, brownish, or blackish.

Key characteristics include the presence of a strong single leaf midrib (costa), small leaf cells that are typically papillose (usually with a single papilla per cell), and often the presence of small filamentous or leaf-like appendages (paraphyllia) on the stems, although these can be sparse or absent in some species. The overall appearance is generally dull rather than glossy.

Pseudoleskeaceae are predominantly found in Northern Hemisphere temperate, boreal, arctic, and alpine environments. They commonly grow on rocks (both calcareous and acidic), soil over rocks, tree bases, or occasionally decaying wood, inhabiting both exposed and shaded sites. Phylogenetically, the family belongs to the order Hypnales, within the complex of families often referred to as the Leskeineae.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Pseudoleskeaceae Schimp.
  • Common Name: Pseudoleskea Moss Family (Informal)
  • Number of Genera: Several (e.g., Pseudoleskea, Leskeella, Pseudoleskeella).
  • Number of Species: Numerous species across the genera.
  • Distribution: Primarily Northern Hemisphere temperate, boreal, arctic, alpine.
  • Evolutionary Group: Bryophytes - Mosses (Class Bryatae - Order Hypnales)

Key Characteristics (Moss Features)

Gametophyte (Leafy Plant)

The dominant stage. Plants small to medium-sized, often wiry, dull green, brownish, or blackish, pleurocarpous, forming intricate mats or tufts.

  • Stems: Creeping, typically irregularly or subpinnately branched. Branches often short, sometimes ascending.
  • Paraphyllia: Often present on stems (and sometimes branch bases), though variable in abundance and form (filamentous to leaf-like).
  • Leaves (Phyllids): Typically small, crowded, often appressed or secund (curved to one side) when dry, erect-spreading when moist. Shape ovate to ovate-lanceolate. Apex usually acute to acuminate. Margins often plane or recurved, entire or serrulate.
  • Costa (Midrib): Single and strong, usually extending near to the leaf apex, sometimes percurrent (reaching tip) or shortly excurrent (extending beyond).
  • Leaf Cells: Small, dense, typically isodiametric to shortly rhomboidal or rectangular, usually thick-walled. Cells characteristically papillose (usually with a single papilla over the cell lumen), contributing to the dull appearance. Alar cells often somewhat differentiated, forming small groups of quadrate or rectangular cells.

Reproductive Structures (Gametophyte)

Antheridia and archegonia are borne in lateral buds on the stems.

Sporophyte (Spore-Producing Structure)

Arises laterally from the gametophyte stem. Consists of:

  • Seta: Usually long, smooth or sometimes slightly roughened above, often reddish or brownish.
  • Capsule (Sporangium): Typically inclined to horizontal, ovoid to cylindrical, usually curved and asymmetric. Operculum usually conical.

Peristome

Typically double and well-developed (hypnoid type). Consists of 16 outer exostome teeth (often cross-striolate below, papillose above) and an inner endostome with a high basal membrane, well-developed segments, and often cilia.

Field Identification

Identifying Pseudoleskeaceae involves recognizing their dull, often wiry appearance and key structural features:

Primary Identification Features

  • Dull, Wiry Habit: Plants typically form non-glossy, often dark or brownish-green intricate mats.
  • Strong Single Costa: Leaves possess a prominent midrib extending far up the leaf (use hand lens).
  • Papillose Leaf Cells: Contribute to the dull appearance (microscope needed for confirmation).
  • Paraphyllia Often Present: Look for small appendages on the stems between leaves (use hand lens).
  • Habitat: Rocks/Tree Bases: Commonly found on rocks (calcareous or acidic) or at the base of trees.
  • Northern Hemisphere Distribution: Primarily found in temperate to arctic/alpine regions.

Common Confusion Points

  • Leskeaceae: Very closely related and similar. Distinction can be difficult and often relies on technical details like paraphyllia type, specific cell papillosity, or peristome features. Often treated together.
  • Thuidiaceae: Also have paraphyllia and papillose cells, but are typically more regularly and complexly pinnate (fern-like) and often larger.
  • Amblystegiaceae: Some genera grow in similar habitats but usually have smooth leaf cells and often different costa lengths or leaf shapes.
  • Anomodontaceae: Also dull and papillose, often on rocks/bark, but generally more robust with broader leaves and often multipapillose cells.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Small to medium, dull, wiry moss mats
  • Pleurocarpous habit
  • Strong single costa
  • Papillose leaf cells (dull look)
  • Paraphyllia often present on stems
  • Habitat: Rocks, tree bases
  • N. Hemisphere temperate/arctic/alpine

Key Variations:

  • Abundance/type of paraphyllia
  • Branching density
  • Leaf shape/apex
  • (Variation across genera like Pseudoleskea, Leskeella)

Notable Examples

The family includes several important genera found in Northern Hemisphere ecosystems:

Pseudoleskea incurvata

(No widely used common name)

A common species found on rocks, especially non-calcareous ones, in montane and alpine regions. Forms dense, often dark mats with incurved leaves when dry, papillose cells, and a strong costa.

Leskeella nervosa

Nerved Leskeella

A widespread species often found on tree bark (especially deciduous trees) or calcareous rocks. Forms slender, wiry mats, typically with abundant filamentous paraphyllia and leaves with a strong costa reaching near the apex.

Pseudoleskeella tectorum

Roof Thread-moss

A small species often found in exposed habitats on rocks (calcareous or not) or thin soil, sometimes even on old roofs. Forms very dense, intricate mats, typically dark colored, with small leaves, papillose cells, and a strong costa.

Phylogeny and Classification

Pseudoleskeaceae is classified within the Class Bryatae (true mosses) and belongs to the large pleurocarpous order Hypnales. It is considered part of the Leskeineae complex within Hypnales, a group characterized by features like papillose cells and often the presence of paraphyllia.

The distinction between Pseudoleskeaceae and Leskeaceae is particularly contentious, with some classifications merging the two families. Molecular studies generally show them to be very closely related, forming a clade together with Thuidiaceae. Whether Pseudoleskeaceae warrants recognition as a separate family or is better treated as part of Leskeaceae depends on the specific phylogenetic framework and taxonomic philosophy applied.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Division: Bryophyta (Mosses)
  • Class: Bryatae (or Bryopsida)
  • Order: Hypnales
  • Family: Pseudoleskeaceae (very close to/sometimes included in Leskeaceae)

Related Families (within Order Hypnales)

Pseudoleskeaceae is most closely related to:

  • Leskeaceae: Very similar morphologically and phylogenetically; often treated as a single family complex.
  • Thuidiaceae: Shares papillose cells and paraphyllia but typically differs in branching habit and leaf dimorphism. Forms a clade with Leskeaceae/Pseudoleskeaceae.
  • Amblystegiaceae: Related within Hypnales but generally differs in having smooth cells and often preferring wetter habitats.
  • Anomodontaceae: Also has papillose cells but differs in leaf shape, costa length, and often multipapillose condition.

Evolutionary Significance

Pseudoleskeaceae, together with Leskeaceae and Thuidiaceae, represents a major lineage within the Hypnales characterized by the presence of papillose leaf cells and often paraphyllia. These features are likely adaptations related to water retention and perhaps surface interactions in their typical habitats on rock or bark. The family showcases diversification in temperate, boreal, and arctic/alpine environments, particularly in the Northern Hemisphere. Understanding the relationships within this complex is key to resolving the phylogeny of the Leskeineae group within Hypnales.
