Pseudoleskeellaceae

Pseudoleskeella Moss Family (Informal)

Pseudoleskeellaceae is a family of small, dull, often wiry pleurocarpous mosses belonging to the order Hypnales, primarily including the genus Pseudoleskeella. Separated from the closely related Leskeaceae/Pseudoleskeaceae complex, they are characterized by a strong single costa, small papillose leaf cells, and typically the absence of paraphyllia. They are found mainly in Northern Hemisphere temperate to arctic/alpine regions, often on rocks or walls.

Overview

The Pseudoleskeellaceae family, established by Ignatov & Ignatova, represents a taxonomic refinement within the Leskeineae complex of the order Hypnales. It primarily accommodates the genus Pseudoleskeella, separating it from the very closely related Pseudoleskeaceae and Leskeaceae based largely on the consistent absence of paraphyllia (small appendages on the stem) and molecular data.

These are typically very small to small, slender, often wiry pleurocarpous mosses that form dense, intricate mats, usually appearing dull green, brownish, or blackish. Like their close relatives, they possess a strong single leaf midrib (costa) and small, dense, papillose leaf cells. The absence of paraphyllia is a key distinction from many species traditionally placed in Pseudoleskeaceae or Leskeaceae.

Pseudoleskeellaceae are predominantly found in Northern Hemisphere temperate, boreal, arctic, and alpine environments. They typically grow on rocks (calcareous or acidic), thin soil over rocks, walls, or occasionally tree bases, often favouring exposed or periodically dry sites. Their phylogenetic placement is firmly within the Hypnales, nested within the Leskeaceae/Pseudoleskeaceae/Thuidiaceae clade.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Pseudoleskeellaceae Ignatov & Ignatova
  • Common Name: Pseudoleskeella Moss Family (Informal)
  • Number of Genera: Primarily 1 (Pseudoleskeella Kindb.), others sometimes included.
  • Number of Species: Several species in Pseudoleskeella.
  • Distribution: Primarily Northern Hemisphere temperate, boreal, arctic, alpine.
  • Evolutionary Group: Bryophytes - Mosses (Class Bryatae - Order Hypnales)

Key Characteristics (Moss Features)

Gametophyte (Leafy Plant)

The dominant stage. Plants very small to small, slender, often wiry, dull green, brownish, or blackish, pleurocarpous, forming dense, intricate mats.

  • Stems: Creeping, typically irregularly branched. Branches short, often filiform (thread-like).
  • Paraphyllia: Characteristically absent.
  • Leaves (Phyllids): Very small, crowded, typically appressed when dry, erect-spreading when moist. Shape ovate to ovate-lanceolate, often concave. Apex usually acute to acuminate. Margins often plane or slightly recurved, entire or finely serrulate.
  • Costa (Midrib): Single and strong, usually extending near to the leaf apex or percurrent (reaching the tip).
  • Leaf Cells: Small, dense, typically isodiametric to shortly rhomboidal, usually thick-walled. Cells characteristically papillose (usually with a single, sometimes low or indistinct papilla over the cell lumen), contributing to the dull appearance. Alar cells usually not or only weakly differentiated, typically a few quadrate cells at the basal corners.

Reproductive Structures (Gametophyte)

Antheridia and archegonia are borne in lateral buds on the stems.

Sporophyte (Spore-Producing Structure)

Arises laterally from the gametophyte stem. Consists of:

  • Seta: Usually long, smooth, often reddish or brownish.
  • Capsule (Sporangium): Typically inclined to horizontal, ovoid to cylindrical, usually curved and asymmetric. Operculum usually conical.

Peristome

Typically double and well-developed (hypnoid type). Consists of 16 outer exostome teeth and an inner endostome with a high basal membrane, well-developed segments, and often cilia.

Field Identification

Identifying Pseudoleskeellaceae involves recognizing their small, dull, wiry nature and specific features, often requiring magnification:

Primary Identification Features

  • Small, Dull, Wiry Habit: Plants form dense, intricate, non-glossy mats, often dark-colored.
  • Absence of Paraphyllia: Stems lack the small filamentous or leaf-like appendages found in many related mosses (key distinction, needs hand lens).
  • Strong Single Costa: Leaves possess a prominent midrib extending far up the leaf.
  • Papillose Leaf Cells: Contribute to the dull appearance (microscope needed for confirmation).
  • Habitat: Rocks/Walls/Exposed Sites: Commonly found on rocks, thin soil, or walls, often in exposed or periodically dry locations.
  • Northern Hemisphere Distribution: Primarily found in temperate to arctic/alpine regions.

Common Confusion Points

  • Pseudoleskeaceae / Leskeaceae: Very similar in overall appearance, costa, and papillose cells. Distinguished primarily by the presence of paraphyllia in most Pseudoleskeaceae/Leskeaceae versus their absence in Pseudoleskeellaceae.
  • Amblystegiaceae: Some small species grow on rock but typically have smooth leaf cells.
  • Small Racomitriaceae: Can grow in similar exposed rocky habitats but usually have hyaline leaf tips (hair points) and different cell structure (often sinuose walls).
  • Grimmiaceae: Acrocarpous mosses also common on rocks, distinguished by terminal sporophytes and different leaf/cell structure.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Very small, dull, wiry moss mats
  • Pleurocarpous habit
  • Paraphyllia absent (key!)
  • Strong single costa
  • Papillose leaf cells (dull look)
  • Habitat: Rocks, walls, exposed sites
  • N. Hemisphere temperate/arctic/alpine

Key Variations:

  • Branching density
  • Leaf shape/apex details

Notable Examples

The family primarily includes species of Pseudoleskeella:

Pseudoleskeella tectorum

Roof Thread-moss

A characteristic species of the family, forming very dense, dark, wiry mats on exposed rocks, walls, or thin soil, sometimes even on roofs. It lacks paraphyllia and has small, ovate-lanceolate leaves with a strong costa and papillose cells.

Pseudoleskeella nervosa (syn. Leskeella nervosa)

Nerved Thread-moss

This species is often placed in Leskeella (Pseudoleskeaceae) due to its abundant paraphyllia. If treated strictly, Pseudoleskeellaceae would exclude species with paraphyllia, highlighting the close taxonomic relationship and differing circumscriptions between these families.

Phylogeny and Classification

Pseudoleskeellaceae is classified within the Class Bryatae (true mosses) and belongs to the large pleurocarpous order Hypnales, specifically within the Leskeineae complex.

Its recognition as a distinct family represents a finer taxonomic split within the group traditionally encompassing Leskeaceae and Pseudoleskeaceae. This separation, supported by molecular data and morphological distinctions (primarily the consistent absence of paraphyllia in Pseudoleskeella sensu stricto), highlights the evolutionary diversification within this lineage. Pseudoleskeellaceae, Pseudoleskeaceae, Leskeaceae, and Thuidiaceae form a well-supported clade within Hypnales.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Division: Bryophyta (Mosses)
  • Class: Bryatae (or Bryopsida)
  • Order: Hypnales
  • Family: Pseudoleskeellaceae (very close to Leskeaceae/Pseudoleskeaceae)

Related Families (within Order Hypnales)

Pseudoleskeellaceae is most closely related to:

  • Leskeaceae / Pseudoleskeaceae: Form a clade together; distinction based mainly on paraphyllia presence/absence and molecular data.
  • Thuidiaceae: Sister group to the Leskeaceae/Pseudoleskeaceae/Pseudoleskeellaceae complex; shares papillose cells and often paraphyllia but differs in habit.
  • Amblystegiaceae: Related within Hypnales but generally differs in having smooth cells.

Evolutionary Significance

Pseudoleskeellaceae highlights the ongoing refinement of bryophyte classification based on integrating molecular and morphological data. It represents a lineage within the Leskeineae complex characterized by adaptation to often harsh, exposed environments (rocks, walls) and the loss or absence of paraphyllia compared to its close relatives. Studying this family helps clarify the evolutionary history and character evolution within this important group of pleurocarpous mosses.
