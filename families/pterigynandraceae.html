Pterigynandraceae

Pterigynandrum Moss Family

Pterigynandraceae is a small family of slender pleurocarpous mosses that typically form intricate, often dull-colored mats or wefts. They commonly grow on tree bark (epiphytic) or rocks (epilithic) in temperate and boreal regions. The family is often characterized by its julaceous (smoothly cylindrical or worm-like) stems and branches when dry, due to tightly appressed leaves, and frequently possesses papillose leaf cells.

Overview

The Pterigynandraceae family is placed within the large order Hypnales. It is a small family, often considered nearly monotypic with the main genus Pterigynandrum (containing the widespread species P. filiforme), although sometimes genera like Heterocladium or parts of Myurella are associated or included, depending on the classification system. These mosses are primarily found in the Northern Hemisphere across temperate, boreal, and arctic-alpine zones, but also occur in mountains further south.

They are characterized by their slender, creeping primary stems giving rise to ascending or spreading secondary stems and branches that often appear smoothly cylindrical (julaceous) when dry because the small, concave leaves are tightly appressed. The leaves typically lack a costa or have a very short double one, and the cells are short and often papillose. Asexual reproduction via specialized deciduous branchlets or other propagules is common, while sporophytes are relatively infrequent.

Ecologically, Pterigynandraceae are common components of epiphytic and epilithic communities, particularly on the bark of deciduous trees and on non-calcareous rocks in shaded or exposed habitats.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Pterigynandraceae
  • Common Name: Pterigynandrum Moss Family
  • Number of Genera: 1-3 (Mainly Pterigynandrum)
  • Number of Species: Approximately 5
  • Distribution: Widespread Northern Hemisphere (temperate, boreal, arctic-alpine), mountains southward.
  • Evolutionary Group: Bryophytes - Mosses - Hypnales

Key Characteristics (Moss Morphology)

Gametophyte: Growth Form and Habit

Plants are pleurocarpous, slender, forming intricate, often interwoven mats or thin wefts, typically dull green, yellowish-green, or brownish. Primary stems are creeping; secondary stems are ascending, prostrate, or arched, usually freely and irregularly to subpinnately branched. Stems and branches often appear julaceous (smoothly cylindrical and catkin-like) when dry due to tightly appressed leaves.

Gametophyte: Stems

Stems are slender. A central strand is usually absent or poorly developed. Paraphyllia are typically absent, but pseudoparaphyllia are present.

Gametophyte: Leaves

Leaves are small, crowded, typically strongly appressed when dry, erect-spreading when moist. They are usually broadly ovate, concave, not or only weakly plicate. Leaf apices are typically acute or shortly acuminate. Margins are often plane or slightly recurved below, usually serrulate or crenulate (scalloped) in the upper part. The costa is short and double, or absent. Leaf cells are typically short (isodiametric, rhombic, or rounded-quadrate), relatively thick-walled, and often papillose, either over the cell lumens or prorate (with papillae projecting from the upper cell ends).

Asexual Reproduction

Asexual reproduction is common via deciduous (easily detached) flagelliform (whip-like) branchlets or specialized multicellular propagula produced in leaf axils.

Sporophyte: Seta, Capsule, and Peristome

Sporophytes are relatively infrequent. Sexual condition is dioicous. The seta is elongated, slender, and smooth. The capsule is exserted, typically erect to slightly inclined, symmetrical, ovoid to cylindric, and smooth. The operculum is conical to rostrate. The peristome is double, of the hypnoid type, though sometimes imperfectly developed; exostome teeth lanceolate, papillose; endostome with a basal membrane, keeled segments, and often rudimentary or absent cilia.

Field Identification (Mosses)

Identifying Pterigynandraceae often involves recognizing its slender, mat-forming habit and julaceous branches:

Primary Identification Features (Field/Hand Lens)

  • Habit: Slender pleurocarpous moss forming intricate mats or wefts.
  • Julaceous Stems/Branches: Stems and branches often appear smooth, cylindrical, or catkin-like when dry due to small, tightly appressed leaves.
  • Habitat: Typically on tree bark (especially deciduous trees) or non-calcareous rock.
  • Small, Appressed Leaves: Leaves are small, ovate, concave, and hug the stem when dry.
  • Lack of Strong Costa: Leaves appear nerveless or with a very faint short double nerve under a hand lens.
  • Deciduous Branchlets: Look for easily detached, slender branch tips (asexual propagules).

Secondary Identification Features (Microscope often needed)

  • Cell Shape and Papillosity: Confirm short, often papillose leaf cells (papillae location can be diagnostic).
  • Costa Details: Verify short/double or absent costa.
  • Propagule Structure: Examine specialized asexual propagules if present.
  • Sporophyte Features: Erect capsule on long seta, hypnoid peristome (if found).

Common Confusion Points (Other Mosses)

  • Leskeaceae (e.g., Leskea, Pseudoleskeella): Also form intricate mats on bark/rock, often have papillose cells, but typically possess a distinct single costa.
  • Hypnaceae (small species, e.g., Hypnum cupressiforme var. julaceum, Pylaisia): Some small Hypnaceae can be julaceous and grow on bark, but often have different leaf shapes (e.g., falcate-secund), smooth cells, or different alar cell development.
  • Anomodontaceae (Anomodon): Often on bark/rock, can form mats, but typically more robust, not strongly julaceous, possess a single costa, and often have different papillosity.
  • Myurellaceae (Myurella): Very similar julaceous habit and small concave leaves, often with papillose cells; sometimes included in or near Pterigynandraceae. Often found on calcareous substrates.
  • Heterocladium (Thuidiaceae or own family): Can be somewhat julaceous, often has papillose cells, but typically shows more differentiation between stem and branch leaves and possesses paraphyllia.

Field Guide Quick Reference (Mosses)

Look For:

  • Slender pleurocarpous mats/wefts
  • Julaceous (smooth, cylindrical) branches when dry
  • Habitat on bark or rock
  • Small, appressed, concave leaves
  • Costa short/double or absent
  • Often papillose cells (use lens/microscope)

Key Variations:

  • Branching density
  • Degree of julaceous appearance
  • Cell papillosity details
  • Presence/absence of propagules/sporophytes

Notable Genera

The family is centered around the genus Pterigynandrum:

Pterigynandrum

(Genus)

The main genus, represented by the widespread species P. filiforme. Forms slender, intricate mats with characteristic julaceous branches when dry. Leaves are small, ovate, concave, appressed, ecostate or nearly so, with papillose cells. Frequently produces deciduous branchlets for asexual reproduction. Sporophytes are uncommon.

Heterocladium

(Related Genus)

Sometimes associated with Pterigynandraceae (or placed in Thuidiaceae). Shares features like papillose cells and sometimes julaceous branches, but typically shows stronger differentiation between stem and branch leaves and possesses paraphyllia.

Phylogeny and Classification

Pterigynandraceae is classified within the order Hypnales, class Bryopsida. Its phylogenetic relationships have sometimes been debated due to morphological similarities (like papillose cells) with families traditionally placed elsewhere (e.g., Leskeaceae, Thuidiaceae in Leskeales/Hypnales sensu lato). However, molecular data generally supports its placement within the core Hypnales, potentially showing affinities with families like Hylocomiaceae or other groups within the Hypnineae suborder.

The family represents an adaptation to epiphytic and epilithic habitats, particularly in temperate and boreal zones. The julaceous habit (tightly appressed leaves) likely aids in desiccation tolerance or water management. The frequent reliance on asexual reproduction via specialized propagules (deciduous branchlets) is a significant aspect of its life history strategy, perhaps compensating for the relative rarity of sporophyte production in some populations.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Division: Bryophyta (Mosses)
  • Class: Bryopsida
  • Order: Hypnales
  • Family: Pterigynandraceae

Related Families (Order Hypnales)

Within the Hypnales, Pterigynandraceae may be related to or share similarities with:

  • Hylocomiaceae: Large forest floor mosses, some with pinnate branching, but generally much more robust and with different leaf/cell features.
  • Thuidiaceae: Often pinnately branched with papillose cells, but possess paraphyllia and typically single costae.
  • Leskeaceae: Often have papillose cells and grow in similar habitats, but usually possess single costae and lack the strongly julaceous habit.
  • Hypnaceae: A large, diverse family; some small epiphytic species might resemble Pterigynandrum but differ in cell structure, leaf shape, or alar cells.
  • Myurellaceae: Sometimes considered closely related or included; shares julaceous habit and papillose cells but often prefers calcareous substrates.

Evolutionary Significance

The Pterigynandraceae family is significant for:

  • Adaptation to Epiphytism/Epilithism: Shows successful colonization of bark and rock surfaces in temperate/boreal regions.
  • Julaceous Habit: Represents a morphological adaptation (appressed leaves) likely related to water retention or desiccation tolerance.
  • Asexual Reproduction Strategies: Highlights the importance of vegetative propagation (via deciduous branchlets) in moss life cycles.
  • Phylogenetic Placement: Its position within Hypnales, despite some morphological resemblance to Leskealean families, underscores the importance of molecular data in resolving bryophyte relationships.
