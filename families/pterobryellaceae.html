Pterobryellaceae

Large Treelet Moss Family (Informal)

Pterobryellaceae is a monotypic family of relatively robust, dendroid mosses belonging to the order Hypnales. It comprises the single genus Pterobryella with its sole species P. praenitens. This family is characterized by its distinct tree-like or frondose habit with dimorphic leaves and is endemic to Australasia.

Overview

The Pterobryellaceae family, established by Buck & Vitt, contains only the genus Pterobryella, which in turn contains only the species Pterobryella praenitens. This makes the family monotypic at both the generic and specific levels. These mosses are notable for their relatively large size and conspicuous dendroid (tree-like) growth form, where erect, branched secondary stems (fronds) arise from creeping primary stems (stolons).

Pterobryella exhibits distinct dimorphic leaves, with small, scale-like leaves on the primary stems and larger, more developed leaves on the erect fronds and their branches. The family is typically found in humid forest environments in Australasia (New Zealand, Tasmania, SE Australia), growing as epiphytes on tree trunks, branches, and tree ferns, or occasionally on logs and rocks.

While historically placed elsewhere, molecular data generally supports its inclusion within the large pleurocarpous order Hypnales, potentially close to families like Lembophyllaceae or Hypopterygiaceae.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Pterobryellaceae W.R. Buck & Vitt
  • Common Name: Large Treelet Moss Family (Informal)
  • Number of Genera: 1 (Pterobryella (Hook.f. & Wilson) Müll. Hal.)
  • Number of Species: 1 (Pterobryella praenitens (Hampe) Müll. Hal.)
  • Distribution: Australasia (New Zealand, Tasmania, SE Australia)
  • Evolutionary Group: Bryophytes - Mosses (Class Bryatae - Order Hypnales)

Key Characteristics (Moss Features)

Gametophyte (Leafy Plant)

The dominant stage, showing a clear differentiation in growth form:

  • Habit: Distinctly dendroid. Plants consist of:
    • Primary Stem (Stolon): Creeping, wiry, bearing reduced, scale-like leaves and rhizoids.
    • Secondary Stem (Frond): Erect, stipitate (having a stalk-like base), arising from the stolon, pinnately or bipinnately branched above, forming a tree-like or fern-like structure often several centimeters tall. Plants often glossy.
  • Leaves (Phyllids): Clearly dimorphic. Stolon leaves are small and scale-like. Frond leaves (on the erect stems and branches) are larger, typically ovate to lanceolate, often concave and somewhat asymmetric. Margins are usually entire or finely toothed.
  • Costa (Midrib): Single and relatively strong, usually extending beyond mid-leaf but ending below the apex.
  • Leaf Cells: Generally smooth, rhomboidal to linear. Alar cells usually not strongly differentiated.

Reproductive Structures (Gametophyte)

Antheridia and archegonia are borne in lateral buds, typically on the primary stems (stolons) or at the base of the secondary stems (fronds).

Sporophyte (Spore-Producing Structure)

Arises laterally from the stolons or base of fronds. Consists of:

  • Seta: Usually short to moderate in length, smooth.
  • Capsule (Sporangium): Erect to slightly inclined, ovoid to cylindrical, symmetric or nearly so. Operculum often rostrate (beaked).

Peristome

Located beneath the operculum. The peristome is double and typically well-developed, consistent with the Hypnales order (hypnoid type), with 16 outer teeth and a well-formed inner endostome with segments and cilia.

Field Identification

Identifying Pterobryellaceae (Pterobryella praenitens) involves recognizing its large, dendroid form and habitat:

Primary Identification Features

  • Large Dendroid Habit: Look for relatively large (often 3-10 cm tall), distinct tree-like or fern-like moss fronds arising from creeping stems.
  • Dimorphic Leaves: Observe the difference between the small, scale-like leaves on the creeping stems and the larger leaves on the erect fronds (may require careful observation or magnification).
  • Strong Single Costa: Frond leaves possess a noticeable midrib extending well into the leaf.
  • Epiphytic Habitat: Typically found growing on tree trunks, branches, or tree ferns in humid forests.
  • Location: Restricted to Australasia (New Zealand, Tasmania, SE Australia).
  • Glossy Appearance: Plants often have a distinct sheen, especially when dry.

Common Confusion Points

  • Hypopterygiaceae (e.g., Hypopterygium): Also dendroid and often epiphytic. Distinguished by typically having leaves in three rows (two lateral, one ventral row of smaller underleaves) and often broader, more strongly asymmetric lateral leaves.
  • Hypnodendraceae (e.g., Hypnodendron): Also large and dendroid, but often terrestrial or on logs, and sporophytes typically arise terminally on specialized branches from the top of the stipe, with very long setae.
  • Calomniaceae (Calomnion): Also dendroid epiphytes in the region, but are much smaller and more delicate, with a weak or absent costa and reduced peristome.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Large, tree-like (dendroid) moss
  • Erect fronds from creeping stolons
  • Dimorphic leaves (small on stolon, larger on frond)
  • Strong single costa in frond leaves
  • Glossy appearance
  • Epiphytic in Australasia

Key Variations:

  • Size and robustness
  • Degree of branching
  • Presence/abundance of sporophytes
  • (Family is monotypic - variations are within P. praenitens)

Notable Examples

The family is monotypic, containing only one genus and species:

Pterobryella praenitens

(No widely used common name)

The sole species in the family Pterobryellaceae. It is a relatively large and conspicuous dendroid moss found in humid forests in New Zealand, Tasmania, and southeastern Australia, typically growing as an epiphyte. Key features include the tree-like habit and dimorphic leaves.

Phylogeny and Classification

Pterobryellaceae is classified within the Class Bryatae (true mosses). Its ordinal placement has varied historically, but recent molecular phylogenetic studies generally place it within the large pleurocarpous order Hypnales. Within Hypnales, its relationships are still being clarified, but it may be closely allied with families such as Lembophyllaceae or Hypopterygiaceae, or represent a relatively isolated lineage.

The family's distinct dendroid morphology, shared with several other unrelated moss lineages, highlights convergent evolution in growth forms likely adapted to similar epiphytic or forest floor environments. Its monotypic nature and restricted Australasian distribution make it an interesting subject for evolutionary and biogeographic studies within the Hypnales.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Division: Bryophyta (Mosses)
  • Class: Bryatae (or Bryopsida)
  • Order: Hypnales
  • Family: Pterobryellaceae

Related Families (within Order Hypnales)

Potential close relatives or families with similar habits within Hypnales include:

  • Lembophyllaceae: A diverse family of pleurocarpous mosses, often robust, sometimes with somewhat dendroid tendencies.
  • Neckeraceae: Often complanate (flattened) mosses, typically epiphytic, some genera approaching a frondose habit.
  • Hypopterygiaceae: Distinctly dendroid or flabellate (fan-shaped) mosses, often with leaves in three rows (including underleaves).
  • (Exact relationships within the diverse Hypnales order are complex and actively researched).

Evolutionary Significance

Pterobryellaceae, represented by the single species Pterobryella praenitens, is significant as an example of the evolution of a large, complex dendroid growth form within the Hypnales. This morphology likely enhances light interception and potentially spore dispersal in its shaded forest habitat. As a monotypic family endemic to Australasia, it contributes to the unique bryophyte diversity of the region and provides insights into the evolutionary history of its lineage within the pleurocarpous mosses.
