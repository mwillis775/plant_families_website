Pulchrinodaceae

Beautiful Tooth Moss Family (Informal)

Pulchrinodaceae is a monotypic family of mosses (Class Bryatae), containing the single genus Pulchrinodus with its sole species P. inflatus. Established based on molecular data, this family is endemic to Australasia (New Zealand, Tasmania, SE Australia). It belongs to the order Bryales.

Overview

Pulchrinodaceae is a small, distinct family within the moss order Bryales. It was established by Quandt, Bell & Stech in 2007 to accommodate the unique genus Pulchrinodus, which itself was described by Allen in 1987. Both the family and genus are monotypic, represented solely by the species Pulchrinodus inflatus.

This moss family has a restricted distribution, found only in temperate parts of Australasia, including New Zealand, Tasmania, and southeastern Australia. It typically grows in moist, shaded habitats such as on soil, rock crevices, or rotting logs in forests.

The recognition of Pulchrinodaceae as a separate family stems primarily from molecular phylogenetic studies (e.g., Stech et al. 2003) that indicated Pulchrinodus was sufficiently distinct from other families within the Bryales. Key morphological features, particularly the inflated alar cells and peristome structure, also support its unique status.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Pulchrinodaceae D.Quandt, N.E.Bell & M.Stech
  • Common Name: Beautiful Tooth Moss Family (Informal)
  • Number of Genera: 1 (Pulchrinodus B.H.Allen)
  • Number of Species: 1 (Pulchrinodus inflatus (Hook.f. & Wilson) B.H.Allen)
  • Distribution: Australasia (New Zealand, Tasmania, SE Australia)
  • Evolutionary Group: Bryophytes - Mosses (Class Bryatae - Order Bryales)

Key Characteristics (Moss Features)

Gametophyte (Leafy Plant)

The dominant, photosynthetic stage. Plants typically form cushions or mats. Stems are erect or creeping, bearing leaves and rhizoids (anchoring structures, not true roots).

  • Leaves (Phyllids): Typically ovate to lanceolate, often crowded on the stem. A key feature of Pulchrinodus is the presence of distinctly inflated, hyaline (clear) alar cells at the basal corners of the leaves. The leaf margin may be toothed, and a midrib (costa) is usually present but may not reach the leaf tip.
  • Stem Structure: Moss stems have simpler anatomy than vascular plants, often with a central strand of conductive tissue.

Reproductive Structures (Gametophyte)

Sexual reproduction involves antheridia (producing sperm) and archegonia (producing eggs), often clustered at stem tips and sometimes surrounded by modified leaves (perichaetial or perigonial leaves).

Sporophyte (Spore-Producing Structure)

Develops after fertilization, remaining attached to the gametophyte. Consists of:

  • Foot: Embeds in the gametophyte tissue.
  • Seta: A stalk of varying length, elevating the capsule.
  • Capsule (Sporangium): Contains the spores. In Bryales, capsules are often inclined or pendulous and possess a specialized structure for spore release called the peristome.

Peristome

A ring of tooth-like appendages around the mouth of the capsule, aiding in spore dispersal. The peristome in Bryales is typically double (an outer and inner ring). The name Pulchrinodus ("beautiful tooth/node") likely refers to features of these teeth, which might be robust, colored, or possess distinct ornamentation or nodes (joints).

Field Identification

Identifying Pulchrinodaceae (Pulchrinodus inflatus) involves looking for key moss features in the appropriate habitat:

Primary Identification Features (Microscope often needed)

  • Inflated Alar Cells: The most distinctive feature is the group of large, clear, inflated cells at the basal corners of each leaf. This requires microscopic examination.
  • Habit and Habitat: Look for cushion-forming or mat-forming moss in moist, shaded environments (soil, rock, logs) within its Australasian range.
  • Leaf Shape and Costa: Observe the general leaf shape (ovate-lanceolate) and the presence and length of the midrib (costa).
  • Capsule and Seta: If sporophytes are present, note the capsule shape, orientation (likely inclined/pendulous), and seta length.
  • Peristome Features: Examining the peristome teeth (if capsule is mature and open) requires magnification but is crucial for confirming Bryales and potentially Pulchrinodaceae characteristics.

Common Confusion Points

  • Other Bryales Mosses: Many families in the Bryales share similarities (e.g., double peristome, similar habit). Distinguishing Pulchrinodaceae requires attention to the specific combination of features, especially the inflated alar cells. Families like Bryaceae or Mniaceae might occur in similar habitats.
  • Non-Bryales Mosses: Mosses from other orders might resemble Pulchrinodaceae superficially but will differ significantly in sporophyte and peristome structure (or lack thereof).

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For (Microscope needed):

  • Moss forming cushions/mats
  • Inflated, clear alar cells on leaves
  • Leaves ovate-lanceolate with costa
  • Capsule likely inclined/pendulous
  • Double peristome (Bryales type)
  • Australasian distribution, moist habitat

Key Variations:

  • Plant size and robustness can vary
  • Presence/absence of sporophytes
  • Specific peristome details
  • (Family is monotypic - variations are within P. inflatus)

Notable Examples

The family contains only one genus and one species:

Pulchrinodus inflatus

(No widely used common name)

The sole species in the family. Characterized by its distinctive inflated alar cells. Found in moist, temperate forests and shaded habitats in New Zealand, Tasmania, and southeastern Australia. Its classification in a separate family highlights its unique combination of morphological and molecular characteristics within the Bryales.

Phylogeny and Classification

Pulchrinodaceae is placed within the Class Bryatae (true mosses) and the large Order Bryales. Its establishment as a distinct family by Quandt et al. (2007) was based on molecular data (Stech et al. 2003) demonstrating that Pulchrinodus formed a separate lineage distinct from other established families within the Bryales.

The family's position within the Bryales is accepted in modern bryophyte classifications (e.g., Goffinet et al. 2009). It represents a relatively isolated lineage within this major order of mosses, endemic to the Australasian region.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Division: Bryophyta (Mosses)
  • Class: Bryatae (or Bryopsida)
  • Order: Bryales
  • Family: Pulchrinodaceae

Related Families (within Order Bryales)

Pulchrinodaceae is related to other families within the Bryales, though its exact sister group relationship may require further research. Some major families in the Bryales include:

  • Bryaceae: A large, cosmopolitan family including the genus Bryum. Often have pear-shaped capsules and a well-developed double peristome.
  • Mniaceae: Characterized by often large leaves, frequently with distinct borders. Includes genera like Mnium and Plagiomnium.
  • Leptostomataceae: Another Australasian family, sometimes associated with Bryales.
  • Phyllodrepaniaceae: (Note: This family, discussed previously, is actually a conifer family often included in Podocarpaceae, not related to Pulchrinodaceae despite the similar-sounding name ending).
  • (Relationships within Bryales are complex and subject to ongoing research).

Evolutionary Significance

Pulchrinodaceae represents a distinct evolutionary lineage within the Bryales, highlighting the diversification of this moss order in the Southern Hemisphere. Its unique morphological features, such as the highly specialized alar cells, combined with its molecular distinctiveness, make it significant for understanding bryophyte evolution and biogeography in Australasia.
