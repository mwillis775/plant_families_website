Rhizogoniaceae

Toothed Moss Family

The Rhizogoniaceae is a family of primarily acrocarpous mosses (though often appearing pleurocarpous) characterized by their often 2-ranked (distichous) leaves, which typically feature strong borders and sharp marginal teeth. They are predominantly found in humid forests of the Southern Hemisphere and tropics, growing on decaying wood, tree bases, soil, or rock.

Overview

Rhizogoniaceae belongs to the Bryopsida class, often placed in its own order, Rhizogoniales, or sometimes associated with the Bryales. This family comprises mosses that exhibit a mix of acrocarpous and apparently pleurocarpous traits (due to frequent lateral sporophytes). They are most diverse in the temperate rainforests of the Southern Hemisphere (like New Zealand, Australia, southern South America) and extend through tropical regions.

Members are often recognizable by their distinctive leaves, which are frequently arranged in two rows, giving the shoots a flattened, frond-like appearance. These leaves typically have strong borders made of different cells and are usually armed with sharp teeth along the margins. They thrive in moist, shaded conditions, commonly found on rotting logs, tree bases, humus, and damp rocks within forest ecosystems.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Rhizogoniaceae Broth.
  • Common Name: Toothed Moss Family (tentative)
  • Number of Genera: Approximately 8-10 (e.g., Rhizogonium, Pyrrhobryum, Cryptopodium, Hymenodon, Goniobryum)
  • Number of Species: Approximately 50-60
  • Distribution: Primarily Southern Hemisphere temperate regions and pantropical.
  • Evolutionary Group: Bryophytes - Mosses (Class: Bryopsida, Order: Rhizogoniales or Bryales)

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Plants are mostly considered fundamentally acrocarpous but often appear pleurocarpous because sporophytes frequently arise laterally from branches (cladocarpous) or below the apex due to innovation. Growth forms range from tufts and mats to somewhat dendroid (tree-like) habits (Pyrrhobryum). Stems are erect, ascending, or sometimes creeping with erect branches.

Leaves

Leaf arrangement is variable but often distinctly 2-ranked (distichous) and flattened in one plane (Rhizogonium, Pyrrhobryum), or sometimes multi-ranked (radially arranged). Leaves are typically lanceolate to linear-lanceolate, often tapering to a sharp point. Key features include the margins, which are characteristically sharply toothed (serrated), often with teeth composed of single or multiple cells (sometimes appearing as double teeth). Leaves also frequently possess a strong border of narrow, elongated, often thick-walled cells that contrasts with the inner laminal cells. The costa (midrib) is single, strong, and usually percurrent (reaching the apex) or shortly excurrent. Laminal cells are typically small, isodiametric (quadrate, rounded, or hexagonal) or shortly rectangular, firm-walled, and usually smooth (lacking papillae).

Sporophyte (Capsule)

Sporophytes usually arise laterally from stems or branches (appearing pleurocarpous), less commonly terminally. The seta (stalk) is typically long, slender or stout, and smooth. The capsule is usually inclined to horizontal or pendent (hanging), ovoid to cylindrical, often symmetric but sometimes slightly curved. The capsule wall may become ribbed or furrowed when dry. An operculum (lid), typically conical to rostrate, is present.

The peristome is double and well-developed, generally of the Bryoid type. The 16 outer exostome teeth are lanceolate, typically yellowish to brownish, often densely papillose or sometimes cross-striate below. The inner endostome consists of a high basal membrane, broad, keeled segments (often perforated along the keel), and usually well-developed, often nodose or appendiculate cilia.

Habitat

Typically found in humid, shaded forest environments. Common substrates include decaying wood (logs, stumps), the bases of trees, humus-rich soil, shaded rock faces, and occasionally tree ferns. They are particularly characteristic of temperate rainforests and tropical montane forests.

Field Identification

Identifying Rhizogoniaceae often relies on leaf characteristics and habitat:

Primary Identification Features

  • Sharply Toothed Leaf Margins: Often visible with a hand lens.
  • Distinct Leaf Border: Border of narrow, elongated cells usually present (use hand lens).
  • Strong Single Costa: Midrib typically prominent and reaching near or into the apex.
  • 2-Ranked Leaves (Commonly): Many genera have leaves flattened into two rows (Rhizogonium, Pyrrhobryum).
  • Habitat: Humid forests, often on wood, rocks, or tree bases, primarily Southern Hemisphere/tropics.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Acrocarpous/Cladocarpous Growth: Erect stems or branches bearing sporophytes laterally or terminally.
  • Small, Smooth Leaf Cells: Typically isodiametric or short-rectangular (requires magnification).
  • Inclined/Pendent Capsule: Capsule often nodding or hanging on a long seta.
  • Ribbed Capsule (when dry): Common feature.
  • Bryoid Peristome: Well-developed double peristome (microscopic).

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Year-Round Gametophytes: Plants are typically perennial and visible year-round in moist forests.
  • Sporophytes Often Present: Many species fruit readily and sporophytes can be found throughout the year in suitable climates.

Common Confusion Points

  • Mniaceae / Plagiomniaceae: Can have toothed and sometimes bordered leaves, and similar habitats. Differ in usually having broader, often elliptic or obovate leaves, larger leaf cells, typically multi-ranked leaf arrangement, and sometimes different peristome details.
  • Bryaceae: Some have borders or teeth, but leaf shape often different (narrower below, wider above), cells often larger/laxer, peristome details differ.
  • Fissidentaceae: Also has 2-ranked leaves, but the unique equitant leaf structure (with basal pocket) is completely different.
  • Plagiotheciaceae: Can have flattened shoots but leaves are usually asymmetric, lack strong borders and teeth, costa is short/double or absent, and peristome is different.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Leaves often 2-ranked (flattened shoots)
  • Leaves with strong border AND sharp teeth
  • Strong single costa to near apex
  • Habitat: Humid forests (logs, rocks, tree bases)
  • Distribution: Primarily S. Hemisphere / Tropics

Key Variations:

  • Genera Rhizogonium, Pyrrhobryum common
  • Acrocarpous/Cladocarpous
  • Capsule inclined/pendent, often ribbed
  • Peristome double (Bryoid)

Notable Examples

Genera like Pyrrhobryum and Rhizogonium are characteristic of the family.

Pyrrhobryum spiniforme

(Includes Rhizogonium spiniforme)

A widespread and common species found throughout tropical and Southern Hemisphere temperate regions. It often grows in a somewhat dendroid fashion, with erect stems arising from a creeping base. The leaves are distinctly 2-ranked, linear-lanceolate, with strong borders and sharp teeth. Sporophytes are common, borne laterally on the stems, with long setae and inclined, ribbed capsules.

Cryptopodium bartramioides

(No common name widely used)

Found in New Zealand and surrounding regions. Unlike the 2-ranked genera, this species has leaves arranged radially around the stem. It forms dense cushions, often on tree ferns or logs. Leaves are lanceolate with strong borders and teeth. The sporophyte has a very short seta, resulting in an immersed capsule hidden among the upper leaves (hence Cryptopodium).

Phylogeny and Classification

The placement of Rhizogoniaceae has been debated. It is often treated as its own distinct order, Rhizogoniales, reflecting its unique combination of features (like the frequent 2-ranked leaves with strong borders/teeth, specific sporophyte traits) that separate it from core Bryales or Hypnales lineages. Alternatively, it is sometimes included within a broadly defined Bryales.

Molecular studies generally support Rhizogoniaceae as a distinct lineage, potentially related to other orders like Aulacomniales and Meesiales, or representing an early branch within the larger Bryanae subclass (which includes Bryales, Orthotrichales, Hypnales etc.). It clearly represents a significant evolutionary line, particularly diverse in Gondwanan regions.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Division: Bryophyta (Mosses)
  • Class: Bryopsida
  • Order: Rhizogoniales (or Bryales)
  • Family: Rhizogoniaceae

Related Families

Families sometimes considered phylogenetically related or placed nearby include:

  • Aulacomniaceae: Often placed in its own order Aulacomniales, sometimes considered related to Rhizogoniales.
  • Meesiaceae: Placed in order Meesiales, also potentially related at a higher level.
  • Core Bryales families (e.g., Bryaceae, Mniaceae, Leptostomataceae): Share the Bryoid peristome type but differ in vegetative features.

Evolutionary Significance

The Rhizogoniaceae family is significant for:

  • Gondwanan Diversity: Representing a major component of bryophyte diversity in Southern Hemisphere temperate rainforests and tropical montane forests.
  • Morphological Diversity: Exhibiting variations in leaf arrangement (2-ranked vs. multi-ranked) and growth form (tufted vs. dendroid) within the family.
  • Distinctive Leaf Features: The combination of strong borders, sharp teeth, and strong costa is characteristic and represents a particular adaptive morphology.
  • Phylogenetic Position: Helping to resolve the relationships among major lineages of Bryopsida, particularly the Bryanae subclass.
