Saulomataceae

(No widely used common name)

Saulomataceae is a family of pleurocarpous mosses belonging to the Hookeriales complex. Established based on molecular and morphological data (Buck et al. 2005), it includes the core genus Sauloma along with the morphologically distinct and taxonomically debated Chilean genera Ancistrodes and Vesiculariopsis. The family primarily has a Southern Hemisphere distribution.

Overview Characteristics Identification Examples Phylogeny

Overview

The Saulomataceae family was formally described by Buck et al. (2005) to accommodate a distinct evolutionary lineage within the Hookerialean group of mosses, supported by molecular data from multiple DNA regions. The core genus, Sauloma, previously placed in a broad Hookeriaceae, forms the basis of the family. Molecular data suggest this group, including Sauloma and the Chilean genus Ancistrodes, is sister to a larger clade containing families like Daltoniaceae, Pilotrichaceae, and a narrowly defined Hookeriaceae.

Goffinet et al. (2009) retained Saulomataceae, including Sauloma, Ancistrodes, and another Chilean monotypic genus, Vesiculariopsis. However, significant morphological differences exist between these genera, particularly between the Australasian Sauloma and the Chilean taxa, leading to ongoing taxonomic debate about the inclusion and placement of Ancistrodes (sometimes placed in Meteoriaceae) and Vesiculariopsis (historically illustrated in Leucomiaceae).

Due to this heterogeneity and limited available material for the Chilean genera, a single unifying morphological description for the family is challenging. The characteristics often focus on the type genus, Sauloma. The family primarily occurs in the Southern Hemisphere, with Sauloma centered in Australasia and the other genera in Chile.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Saulomataceae W.R. Buck, C.J. Cox, A.J. Shaw & Goffinet
  • Common Name: (None widely used)
  • Number of Genera: 3 (Sauloma, Ancistrodes, Vesiculariopsis - inclusion debated)
  • Number of Species: Few (e.g., ~4 in Sauloma, 1 each in others)
  • Distribution: Southern Hemisphere (Australasia, Chile)
  • Evolutionary Group: Bryophytes - Mosses (Class Bryatae - Order Hookeriales complex)

Key Characteristics (Moss Features)

Note: Due to significant morphological differences between the included genera, characteristics are described primarily based on the core genus Sauloma, with notes on the others based on the provided text.

Gametophyte (Leafy Plant) - Genus Sauloma

Plants typically delicate, pleurocarpous, forming glossy mats or tufts on various substrates (soil, logs, rocks).

  • Stems: Creeping, irregularly branched.
  • Leaves (Phyllids): Often complanate (flattened) or spreading, typically ovate to lanceolate, sometimes asymmetric, often with a distinct border of narrower cells. Apex acute to acuminate.
  • Costa (Midrib): Variable; often short and double, single and weak, or absent entirely.
  • Leaf Cells: Typically large, lax, thin-walled, smooth, often hexagonal or rhomboidal, sometimes porose (with pores connecting adjacent cells).

Gametophyte Notes - Other Genera

  • Ancistrodes: Reportedly pendent epiphytes. Leaves unbordered, abruptly long-acuminate with hooked apical teeth. Cells firm-walled, ellipsoid, non-porose. Costa weak and double.
  • Vesiculariopsis: Much-branched shoots, ovate-lanceolate leaves suggested by illustrations.

Reproductive Structures (Gametophyte)

Antheridia and archegonia are borne in lateral buds.

Sporophyte (Spore-Producing Structure) - Genus Sauloma

Arises laterally from the gametophyte stem. Consists of:

  • Seta: Often relatively long, smooth.
  • Capsule (Sporangium): Typically inclined to horizontal, ovoid to cylindrical, sometimes curved. Operculum often rostrate (beaked).

Sporophyte Notes - Other Genera

  • Ancistrodes: Exothecial cells (outer capsule wall) firm-walled, weakly collenchymatous (thickened corners). Calyptra (cap covering capsule) mitrate (conical/cap-like).

Peristome

Typically double and well-developed (Hookerialean type) in Sauloma. Peristome details for Ancistrodes may differ (potentially resembling Meteoriaceae based on historical placement/illustrations).

Field Identification

Identifying Saulomataceae requires careful attention, especially given the internal variation. Focus on identifying the core genus Sauloma first:

Identifying Sauloma

  • Habit: Look for delicate, often glossy, pleurocarpous mosses forming mats in moist, shaded habitats (soil, logs, rocks).
  • Leaves: Often flattened (complanate), ovate-lanceolate, possibly bordered.
  • Costa: Check for a very weak/short double costa, weak single costa, or absence of a costa (use hand lens).
  • Cells: Large, lax cells may give a translucent appearance (requires microscope for confirmation).
  • Location: Primarily Australasia.

Identifying Ancistrodes / Vesiculariopsis (Use Caution)

  • Location: Chile.
  • Ancistrodes: Look for potentially pendent epiphytes with abruptly pointed leaves having hooked teeth at the tip; costa weak/double.
  • Vesiculariopsis: Look for much-branched epiphytes. (Identification difficult without specimens/detailed descriptions).

Common Confusion Points

  • Hookeriaceae / Daltoniaceae / Pilotrichaceae / Leucomiaceae: Other families in the Hookeriales complex share features like large cells or complanate habits. Distinguishing them often requires microscopic examination of cell details, costa structure, and peristome features. Sauloma often differs in its very weak or absent costa compared to many Hookeriaceae or Pilotrichaceae.
  • Plagiotheciaceae: Some genera have complanate leaves but usually differ in cell shape and sporophyte details.
  • Meteoriaceae (for Ancistrodes): If Ancistrodes belongs here, it would share features with pendent epiphytic mosses common in this family.

Field Guide Quick Reference (Focus on Sauloma)

Look For (Sauloma):

  • Delicate, glossy, mat-forming moss
  • Pleurocarpous habit
  • Leaves often complanate, possibly bordered
  • Costa weak/short/double or absent
  • Large, lax leaf cells (microscope)
  • Australasian distribution

Key Variations:

  • Leaf border presence/absence
  • Costa details
  • Chilean genera (Ancistrodes, Vesiculariopsis) differ significantly in morphology and potentially habitat (pendent epiphytes?).

Notable Examples

The family includes the core genus Sauloma and the controversially placed genera Ancistrodes and Vesiculariopsis.

Sauloma tenella

(No widely used common name)

A representative species of the core genus, found in Australasia. It forms delicate, often glossy mats, typically with complanate leaves that have large cells and a weak or absent costa.

Ancistrodes genuflexa

(No widely used common name)

A Chilean species placed in Saulomataceae by some classifications but morphologically distinct from Sauloma. Characterized by abruptly pointed leaves with hooked tips and firm-walled cells. Its placement remains debated.

Vesiculariopsis spirifolium

(No widely used common name)

A poorly known Chilean species also tentatively placed in Saulomataceae. Illustrated as having much-branched shoots and ovate-lanceolate leaves. Its systematic position requires further study.

Phylogeny and Classification

Saulomataceae is classified within the Class Bryatae (true mosses) and belongs to the diverse Hookerialean lineage (often broadly Order Hookeriales). The family was established by Buck et al. (2005) based primarily on molecular evidence from four DNA regions, which showed that Sauloma and Ancistrodes formed a clade sister to a large group including Daltoniaceae, Pilotrichaceae, Schimperobryaceae, and Hookeriaceae sensu stricto.

While molecular data provide strong support for recognizing this lineage, the inclusion of all three genera (Sauloma, Ancistrodes, Vesiculariopsis) based on morphology is problematic. Ancistrodes shows features potentially aligning it with Meteoriaceae, and the position of Vesiculariopsis (not included in the original molecular analysis) is uncertain. This highlights a common theme in modern bryophyte systematics where molecular data reveals relationships not obvious from morphology alone, sometimes leading to morphologically heterogeneous families.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Division: Bryophyta (Mosses)
  • Class: Bryatae (or Bryopsida)
  • Order: Hookeriales (complex)
  • Family: Saulomataceae

Related Families (within Hookeriales complex)

Molecular data suggest Saulomataceae is sister to a clade containing:

  • Daltoniaceae: Often characterized by bordered leaves and specific peristome features.
  • Pilotrichaceae: Mostly tropical epiphytes, often robust, includes genera like Pilotrichum.
  • Hookeriaceae: Now defined more narrowly, includes Hookeria, known for large, clear leaf cells.
  • Schimperobryaceae: A small family related to the above groups.
  • Leucomiaceae: Sometimes associated, includes pale, delicate mosses like Leucomium.

Evolutionary Significance

Saulomataceae represents a distinct evolutionary branch within the Hookerialean mosses, primarily characterized by the genus Sauloma. Its establishment underscores the power of molecular data in refining bryophyte classification, even when it results in families with considerable internal morphological variation (if Ancistrodes and Vesiculariopsis are included). The family contributes to understanding the diversification and biogeography of this major moss lineage, particularly in the Southern Hemisphere.
