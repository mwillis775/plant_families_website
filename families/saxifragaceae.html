Saxifragaceae

Saxifrage Family

The Saxifragaceae, or Saxifrage family, is a diverse group of flowering plants primarily consisting of herbaceous perennials, many adapted to alpine, arctic, or rocky environments. Belonging to the order Saxifragales, this family (in its strict sense) includes about 30-40 genera and 600-700 species, known for often forming basal rosettes or cushions and bearing flowers typically with 5 petals and 2 carpels.

Overview

The Saxifragaceae family is renowned for its hardy members, many of which thrive in challenging habitats like rock crevices (the name "Saxifraga" literally means "stone-breaker"), alpine meadows, tundra, and cool woodlands. The vast majority are perennial herbs, often characterized by leaves clustered in a basal rosette and flowers borne on a stalk (scape) rising above the leaves. Their distribution is predominantly in the temperate, arctic, and alpine regions of the Northern Hemisphere, with some representation in the Andes of South America.

While the circumscription of the family has varied historically, the core Saxifragaceae (sensu stricto under the APG classification) forms a well-supported clade within the large and diverse order Saxifragales. This order represents a significant lineage within the core eudicots.

Many species within Saxifragaceae are highly valued in horticulture, especially as rock garden plants, groundcovers, or woodland perennials. Genera like Saxifraga, Heuchera (Coral Bells), Bergenia, Astilbe, and Tiarella (Foamflower) are popular ornamentals appreciated for their foliage, flowers, and resilience.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Saxifragaceae
  • Common Name: Saxifrage family
  • Number of Genera: Approximately 30-40
  • Number of Species: Approximately 600-700
  • Distribution: Primarily Northern Hemisphere temperate, arctic, and alpine regions; some in South America.
  • Evolutionary Group: Core Eudicots - Saxifragales

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Most members are herbaceous perennials, often low-growing and forming mats, cushions, or distinct basal rosettes. Some possess rhizomes or stolons. While less common, a few annuals or small subshrubs exist within the family. They are frequently associated with rocky substrates, alpine tundra, or moist woodland environments.

Leaves

Leaves are highly variable but typically alternate or arranged in a basal rosette; opposite leaves occur less frequently. They are usually simple, though often lobed or toothed, and sometimes palmately divided or (rarely) compound. Leaf texture can range from thin to somewhat fleshy or leathery. Stipules may be present (often small) or absent. A notable feature in some genera (especially Saxifraga) is the presence of hydathodes (water pores) near the leaf margins, which can excrete calcium carbonate, leaving visible white crusts.

Inflorescence

The arrangement of flowers (inflorescence) varies greatly. Common types include cymes, panicles, or racemes. Often, the inflorescence is borne on a leafless or nearly leafless stalk called a scape, which arises directly from the basal rosette or rhizome. In some species, flowers are solitary.

Flowers

Flowers are typically regular (radially symmetrical, actinomorphic), bisexual, and often showy. Key floral features include:

  • Perianth: Usually differentiated into calyx and corolla, typically 5-merous (parts in fives).
  • Hypanthium: A cup-shaped structure formed from the fusion of the bases of sepals, petals, and stamens is often present, surrounding or fused to the ovary.
  • Calyx: Usually 5 sepals, distinct or fused at the base into the hypanthium.
  • Corolla: Usually 5 distinct petals, sometimes clawed (narrowed at the base), occasionally reduced or absent in some species. Petal shape and color are diverse.
  • Androecium: Stamens usually number 5 or 10 (often twice the number of petals), arising from the hypanthium rim or the base of the ovary.
  • Gynoecium: Typically composed of 2 carpels (sometimes 3-5), which are usually fused at the base but often diverge into separate styles above. The ovary position is variable: superior, semi-inferior, or fully inferior, often influenced by the degree of fusion with the hypanthium. Contains numerous ovules.

Fruits and Seeds

The fruit is most commonly a capsule that splits open along the septa (septicidal) or locules (loculicidal), or sometimes a pair of divergent follicles. The fruits contain numerous small seeds, typically dispersed by wind or gravity.

Chemical Characteristics

Unlike families in Piperales, Saxifragaceae are not typically known for strong essential oils. Tannins are common, and various flavonoids contribute to pigmentation. Some species accumulate metals or minerals, reflected in the lime encrustations from hydathodes in certain saxifrages.

Field Identification

Identifying members of the Saxifragaceae family often involves observing their growth habit, habitat, and key floral features:

Primary Identification Features

  • Habit: Look for herbaceous perennials, frequently forming basal rosettes, cushions, or mats.
  • Habitat: Often found in rocky areas (crevices, scree), alpine meadows, tundra, or cool, moist woodlands.
  • Flowers: Typically radially symmetrical with parts usually in fives (5 sepals, 5 petals, 5 or 10 stamens).
  • Carpels: Usually 2 carpels, often fused below but with distinct styles above.
  • Inflorescence: Frequently borne on a leafless scape rising from a basal rosette.
  • Leaves: Check for simple, often lobed or toothed basal leaves; look for lime-encrusted pores (hydathodes) on leaf margins in some Saxifraga species.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Hypanthium: Note the presence and development of a floral cup (hypanthium).
  • Ovary Position: Observe whether the ovary is superior, semi-inferior, or inferior relative to the floral parts.
  • Fruit Type: Look for dry capsules or follicles containing many small seeds.
  • Stipules: Check for the presence or absence of small stipules at the leaf base.

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Spring/Summer: Main flowering period for most species, varying with altitude and latitude. Basal rosettes are usually present before flowering.
  • Fall: Fruits (capsules/follicles) mature. Foliage may develop fall color in some deciduous species (like Darmera).
  • Winter: Evergreen basal rosettes persist in many species, especially cushion/mat forms. Dried scapes and fruit capsules may remain visible.

Common Confusion Points

  • Crassulaceae (Stonecrop family): Often share similar rocky habitats and succulent tendencies. However, Crassulaceae typically have flowers with carpels equal in number to petals (e.g., 5 petals, 5 distinct carpels) and usually superior ovaries without a prominent hypanthium.
  • Rosaceae (Rose family): Some alpine/rock garden genera (e.g., Potentilla, Geum) can resemble saxifrages. Rosaceae often have >10 stamens, prominent stipules fused to the petiole, and fruits that are achenes, drupes, or aggregates, rather than capsules/follicles.
  • Ranunculaceae (Buttercup family): Alpine buttercups or anemones might be confused. Ranunculaceae typically have numerous stamens, numerous simple pistils (developing into achenes or follicles), and lack a hypanthium.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Herbaceous perennial, often basal rosette/cushion
  • Rocky/alpine/woodland habitat
  • Flowers usually 5-merous
  • Usually 2 carpels, fused below
  • Usually 5 or 10 stamens
  • Inflorescence often on a scape
  • Fruit a capsule or follicles

Key Variations:

  • Ovary position (superior to inferior)
  • Hypanthium development
  • Leaf shape (entire, lobed, divided)
  • Presence of hydathodes (lime pores)
  • Petal shape/presence

Notable Examples

The Saxifragaceae family includes many well-known and horticulturally important plants:

Saxifraga oppositifolia

Purple Saxifrage

A classic example of an arctic-alpine cushion plant. It forms dense mats with small, opposite, scale-like leaves. In early spring, it produces relatively large, solitary, bright purple-pink flowers with 5 petals. It's one of the northernmost flowering plants, found in tundra and high mountain habitats across the Northern Hemisphere.

Heuchera sanguinea (and hybrids)

Coral Bells

Valued primarily for their foliage, Heuchera species and their numerous cultivars form mounds of attractive, often lobed, basal leaves in shades of green, purple, bronze, and silver. Tall, slender scapes bear airy panicles of small, bell-shaped flowers (often reddish or pink), notable for their prominent hypanthium. Widely grown in gardens.

Darmera peltata

Umbrella Plant, Indian Rhubarb

Native to western North America, this robust perennial grows along streams. It produces stout, hairy scapes topped with rounded clusters of pinkish-white flowers in early spring before the leaves emerge. Later, huge, round, centrally attached (peltate) leaves develop, resembling umbrellas up to 2 feet across. Spreads by thick rhizomes.

Bergenia crassifolia

Heartleaf Bergenia, Pigsqueak

Known for its large, glossy, leathery, rounded basal leaves that often turn bronze or red in winter (earning the name "Pigsqueak" from the sound made when leaves are rubbed). Produces clusters of pink to purplish flowers on thick scapes in spring. A very hardy and popular garden perennial, native to Siberia and Mongolia.

Phylogeny and Classification

Saxifragaceae is the namesake family of the large and diverse order Saxifragales, which occupies a significant position within the core eudicots. This order includes a wide array of plant forms, from succulent herbs (Crassulaceae) and woody shrubs (Grossulariaceae - currants, Hamamelidaceae - witch hazels) to trees (Altingiaceae - sweetgums) and even large-flowered herbs (Paeoniaceae - peonies).

The definition of Saxifragaceae itself has narrowed considerably based on molecular data. Many genera previously included (like Astilbe, Rodgersia, Darmera - sometimes placed in their own families or grouped differently within Saxifragales depending on the specific classification system) are closely related but distinct from the core Saxifragaceae (which strongly centers around the large genus Saxifraga and relatives like Heuchera, Tiarella, Mitella, Bergenia). This page generally refers to Saxifragaceae sensu stricto (in the APG IV system sense).

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Clade: Angiosperms (Flowering plants)
  • Clade: Eudicots
  • Clade: Core Eudicots
  • Order: Saxifragales
  • Family: Saxifragaceae

Related Families (within Saxifragales)

Saxifragaceae is part of the diverse Saxifragales order, which includes families such as:

  • Crassulaceae (Stonecrop family): Succulent herbs with distinct carpels usually equal in number to petals.
  • Grossulariaceae (Gooseberry/Currant family): Woody shrubs bearing berries, often with spines.
  • Hamamelidaceae (Witch Hazel family): Woody shrubs or trees, often with strap-like petals and woody capsules.
  • Paeoniaceae (Peony family): Herbaceous perennials or shrubs with large, showy flowers and distinct follicles or leathery berries.

Evolutionary Significance

The Saxifragaceae family and the broader Saxifragales order are significant for:

  • Basal Core Eudicot Lineage: Saxifragales represents one of the major early-branching lineages within the core eudicots, providing insights into the diversification of this massive group.
  • Adaptation to Stressful Environments: Many Saxifragaceae exhibit remarkable adaptations to cold, drought, and low-nutrient conditions found in alpine and arctic regions.
  • Diversity in Form: Despite a relatively conserved floral plan (often 5-merous), the family shows great diversity in vegetative forms (rosettes, cushions, mats) and inflorescence structures.
  • Floral Evolution: The variability in ovary position and degree of carpel fusion within the family offers models for studying floral evolutionary pathways.
