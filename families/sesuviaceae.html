Sesuviaceae

Sea Purslane Family

Sesuviaceae is a small family of flowering plants in the order Caryophyllales, comprising approximately 4-5 genera and 25 species of succulent herbs and subshrubs. These plants are characterized by their succulent, often prostrate growth habit and adaptation to saline or arid environments. Members of this family typically have opposite, simple leaves that are fleshy and often covered with small bladder cells (papillae). The flowers are small but showy, with 5 perianth segments that are often colorful inside, and numerous stamens. The family has a pantropical and subtropical distribution, with species occurring in coastal areas, salt marshes, and arid regions worldwide, where they play important ecological roles in stabilizing soils and providing habitat in challenging environments.

Overview

The Sesuviaceae family comprises a small group of succulent flowering plants that are highly adapted to saline and arid environments. The family name is derived from the genus Sesuvium, which includes the widely distributed sea purslane (Sesuvium portulacastrum), a common coastal species in tropical and subtropical regions worldwide.

Sesuviaceae has a pantropical and subtropical distribution, with species occurring in coastal areas, salt marshes, desert margins, and other saline or arid habitats across Africa, Asia, Australia, and the Americas. Many species are particularly associated with coastal environments, where they form important components of beach and dune vegetation, helping to stabilize soils and providing habitat for other organisms.

Members of this family are notable for their ecological adaptations to challenging environments, including various mechanisms for salt tolerance, water conservation, and specialized photosynthetic pathways (C4 and CAM) that enhance their efficiency in hot, dry conditions. Some species, particularly Sesuvium portulacastrum, have been studied for their potential in phytoremediation of saline soils and coastal restoration projects.

  • Number of Species: Approximately 25
  • Distribution: Pantropical and subtropical regions worldwide
  • Evolutionary Group: Angiosperms - Eudicots - Caryophyllales

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Members of Sesuviaceae are typically prostrate or decumbent herbs or subshrubs with succulent stems and leaves. Some species form extensive mats that spread across the ground, while others have a more upright or bushy habit. The plants often have reddish or purplish stems, particularly when exposed to strong sunlight or stress conditions.

Leaves

The leaves of Sesuviaceae are opposite, simple, and succulent. They are typically oblong, spatulate, or linear in shape, with entire margins. The leaf surface often has small bladder cells (papillae) that give it a slightly rough texture and may function in salt secretion or water storage. The leaves are often glaucous (covered with a waxy bloom) and may have a reddish or purplish tinge, particularly at the margins or when under stress.

Reproductive Structures

The flowers are solitary or in small clusters, arising from the leaf axils. Each flower has 5 perianth segments (tepals) that are often green on the outside and colorful (white, pink, or purple) on the inside. The tepals may have a small appendage or "horn" near the apex. The flowers typically have numerous stamens arranged in 1-5 groups and a superior ovary with 2-5 carpels and an equal number of styles. The fruit is a circumscissile capsule (opening by a horizontal split) containing numerous small seeds with a curved embryo.

Ecological Adaptations

Sesuviaceae species have evolved various adaptations to their challenging environments:

  • Succulence: Water storage in leaves and stems to survive drought periods
  • Salt tolerance: Mechanisms for excluding, secreting, or compartmentalizing salt
  • Specialized photosynthesis: Many species use C4 or CAM photosynthetic pathways, which are more efficient in hot, dry conditions
  • Prostrate growth: Reduces exposure to wind and creates a favorable microclimate
  • Rapid colonization: Ability to quickly establish in disturbed or newly available habitats

Distinguishing Features

Sesuviaceae can be distinguished from other plant families by a combination of:

  • Succulent habit with opposite leaves
  • Flowers with 5 tepals, often colorful inside
  • Numerous stamens
  • Circumscissile capsule fruits
  • Adaptation to saline or arid environments

Identification

Field Identification Tips

When trying to identify members of the Sesuviaceae family in the field, look for:

  • Habitat: Plants growing in coastal areas, salt marshes, or arid environments
  • Growth form: Prostrate or spreading succulent herbs or subshrubs
  • Leaves: Opposite, fleshy, often with a glaucous appearance
  • Flowers: Small but showy, with 5 tepals that are often pink or purple inside
  • Fruits: Capsules that split horizontally (circumscissile dehiscence)

Similar Families

Sesuviaceae may be confused with several other plant families:

  • Aizoaceae: Very closely related family with similar succulent habit, but often with different floral structure and fruit dehiscence
  • Portulacaceae: Contains succulent plants with similar habit but typically with 2 sepals and 4-5 petals
  • Molluginaceae: Similar habit in some genera but typically less succulent and with different floral structure
  • Amaranthaceae: Some members have succulent habit but typically with different floral structure and fruit type

Distinguishing Features

The most reliable features for distinguishing Sesuviaceae from similar families include:

  • Floral structure: 5 tepals that are often colorful inside, numerous stamens
  • Fruit type: Circumscissile capsule (splitting horizontally)
  • Leaf arrangement: Opposite, succulent leaves
  • Habitat preference: Strong association with saline or arid environments

For definitive identification, examination of the flower structure and fruit dehiscence is often necessary, as these features are the most diagnostic for the family.

Notable Examples

The family Sesuviaceae contains approximately 25 species across 4-5 genera. Here are some notable representatives:

Sesuvium portulacastrum

Sea Purslane

A widespread coastal species found on tropical and subtropical shores worldwide. It forms extensive mats with succulent, spatulate leaves and small, pink to purple flowers. This species is highly salt-tolerant and plays an important ecological role in stabilizing beach and dune systems. It has been studied for its potential in coastal restoration and phytoremediation of saline soils. The leaves are edible and have been used in traditional cuisines in some regions, particularly in Southeast Asia and the Pacific Islands.

Trianthema portulacastrum

Desert Horse Purslane

An annual herb with a prostrate to ascending habit, native to tropical and subtropical regions worldwide but now considered a cosmopolitan weed. It has succulent, obovate leaves with a distinctive pink to red margin and small, pinkish-white flowers that are partially enclosed by the leaf bases. This species is adapted to arid and semi-arid environments and can be a problematic weed in agricultural settings. However, it also has traditional medicinal uses in various cultures for treating liver disorders, edema, and other conditions.

Cypselea humifusa

Panal

A small, inconspicuous annual herb native to the Caribbean region but naturalized in parts of the southeastern United States. It has a prostrate growth habit with tiny, succulent, spatulate leaves and minute, greenish-white flowers. This species typically grows in moist, sandy soils, often in areas that are seasonally wet. Despite its small size (often less than 5 cm tall), it can form dense mats in suitable habitats. It is of interest to botanists due to its specialized ecological niche and limited distribution.

Phylogenetic Note

Sesuviaceae has undergone significant taxonomic revisions based on molecular phylogenetic studies. The genera now included in this family were previously placed in Aizoaceae, and some classification systems still include them there. However, molecular evidence supports the recognition of Sesuviaceae as a distinct family within the Caryophyllales order. The family is part of the "core Caryophyllales" clade and is most closely related to Aizoaceae, with which it shares many morphological features. The succulent habit and adaptations to saline and arid environments seen in Sesuviaceae represent convergent evolution with similar adaptations in other Caryophyllales families, reflecting the remarkable ecological versatility of this plant order.
