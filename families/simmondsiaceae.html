Simmondsiaceae

Jojoba Family

Simmondsiaceae is a monotypic family represented by a single species, Simmondsia chinensis, commonly known as Jojoba. This evergreen desert shrub, native to southwestern North America, belongs to the order Caryophyllales. It is highly valued economically for the unique liquid wax esters (jojoba oil) extracted from its seeds and is characterized by its leathery, opposite leaves and reduced, unisexual, apetalous flowers.

Overview

The Simmondsiaceae family contains only one species, Simmondsia chinensis (Jojoba), making it monotypic. This remarkable plant is an evergreen shrub native to the arid environments of the Sonoran and Mojave Deserts in Arizona, California, and northwestern Mexico. Despite its specific epithet "chinensis" (due to a misinterpretation of early collections), it is exclusively a North American native.

Phylogenetically, Simmondsiaceae belongs to the large and diverse order Caryophyllales, which includes families as varied as cacti (Cactaceae), amaranths (Amaranthaceae), pinks (Caryophyllaceae), and pitcher plants (Nepenthaceae). Jojoba is well-adapted to its desert habitat, exhibiting drought tolerance through features like its thick, leathery leaves and deep taproot system. The plants are strictly dioecious, meaning individual shrubs are either male or female.

Jojoba is most famous for the substance extracted from its seeds, commonly called "jojoba oil." Chemically, this is not a typical oil (triglyceride) but rather a liquid wax ester, unique in the plant kingdom for its similarity to sperm whale oil (spermaceti). This stable liquid wax has numerous applications in cosmetics, personal care products, lubricants, and pharmaceuticals, making jojoba an economically important crop cultivated in arid regions worldwide.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Simmondsiaceae
  • Common Name: Jojoba family
  • Number of Genera: 1 (Simmondsia)

  • Distribution: Endemic to SW North America (Sonoran/Mojave deserts)

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Simmondsia chinensis is a woody, multi-stemmed, densely branched evergreen shrub, typically growing 1-2 meters tall but capable of reaching 3 meters. It is exceptionally drought-tolerant, possessing a long taproot that can reach deep water sources, and is well-adapted to arid and semi-arid desert conditions.

Leaves

Leaves are arranged oppositely on the stems. They are simple, entire (smooth-margined), ovate to oblong in shape, and typically 2-4 cm long. A key feature is their thick, leathery (coriaceous) texture and grayish-green color, often covered with a waxy cuticle. The leaves are typically held in a vertical orientation, which helps reduce heat load from direct sunlight. Stipules are absent (exstipulate).

Inflorescence

Flowers are borne in axillary or terminal positions, typically in dense clusters (fascicles or short cymes) for male flowers, while female flowers are often solitary or in smaller clusters.

Flowers

Flowers are small, inconspicuous, radially symmetrical (actinomorphic), unisexual (plants are dioecious), and lack petals (apetalous).

  • Male Flowers: Possess 4-6 (usually 5) yellowish-green, sepal-like perianth parts (sepals). They lack petals but have numerous stamens (typically 8-12) with very short filaments and prominent yellow anthers that produce abundant pollen. They are wind-pollinated.
  • Female Flowers: Possess 4-6 (usually 5) greenish sepals that are somewhat larger than those of male flowers. They lack petals and stamens but have a single pistil composed of 3 fused carpels. The ovary is superior and typically 3-locular (though often only one locule develops fully), with one ovule per locule. There are 3 separate, linear styles/stigmas.

Fruits and Seeds

The fruit is an ovoid or acorn-shaped, leathery capsule, about 1.5-2 cm long. Although the ovary has 3 locules/ovules, typically only one develops, resulting in a single large seed per capsule (sometimes 2 or 3). The capsule splits open (loculicidal dehiscence) upon maturity. The seeds are relatively large, brownish, and contain about 50% by weight of the unique liquid wax esters known commercially as jojoba oil.

Chemical Characteristics

The most distinctive chemical feature is the high concentration of liquid wax esters in the seeds, composed mainly of long-chain fatty acids and fatty alcohols, rather than the triglycerides found in most vegetable oils. The plant also contains cyanogenic glycosides like simmondsin, particularly in the meal left after wax extraction, making it unpalatable or toxic if ingested raw in quantity.

Field Identification

Identifying Jojoba (Simmondsia chinensis) relies heavily on its unique vegetative features and desert habitat:

Primary Identification Features

  • Habit & Habitat: An evergreen shrub found in the Sonoran and Mojave deserts of SW North America (Arizona, California, NW Mexico).
  • Leaves: Look for the distinctive opposite, simple, entire, thick, leathery, grayish-green leaves that are often oriented vertically. They lack stipules.
  • Flowers: Small, inconspicuous, and lacking petals (apetalous). Flowers are unisexual, found on separate male and female plants (dioecious). Male flowers are yellowish clusters with numerous stamens; female flowers are greenish, often solitary, with 3 distinct styles.
  • Fruit: An ovoid, leathery capsule, typically containing a single large seed.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Leaf Orientation: Vertical leaf orientation is a helpful clue.
  • Dioecious Nature: Observing only male flowers or only female flowers/fruits on a single plant confirms its dioecious status.
  • Seed Contents: While not a field character, knowledge that the seeds produce liquid wax is characteristic.

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Year-round: The distinctive leathery, opposite, vertically oriented leaves make it identifiable throughout the year.
  • Winter/Spring: Flowering typically occurs during cooler months, triggered by rainfall. Look for the small, apetalous male or female flowers.
  • Spring/Summer: Fruits (capsules) develop and mature.

Common Confusion Points

  • Larrea tridentata (Creosote Bush): Another dominant desert shrub with opposite leaves. Distinguished by having compound (bifoliolate) leaves, resinous texture with a strong creosote smell (especially after rain), small but conspicuous yellow flowers with 5 petals, and fuzzy spherical fruits.
  • Garryaceae (Silk Tassel family): Shrubs with opposite, simple, often leathery leaves. Differ in having flowers borne in distinctive pendulous catkins (wind-pollinated), inferior ovaries, and berry-like fruits.
  • Other Desert Shrubs: Many other shrubs inhabit the same region. Jojoba is distinguished by the combination of opposite, simple, entire, thick/leathery, vertically oriented, exstipulate leaves and apetalous, unisexual flowers.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Desert shrub (SW North America)
  • Leaves opposite, simple, entire, leathery, gray-green, vertical
  • Leaves exstipulate
  • Flowers small, apetalous (no petals)
  • Plants dioecious (separate male/female)
  • Fruit an ovoid capsule (usually 1-seeded)

Key Distinctions:

  • Opposite simple leaves (vs. compound in Creosote)
  • No petals (vs. Creosote, many others)
  • Superior ovary (vs. Garryaceae)
  • Capsule fruit (vs. drupes, berries, etc.)
  • Unique liquid wax in seeds

Notable Examples

The Simmondsiaceae family is monotypic, containing only one species:

Simmondsia chinensis

Jojoba

The sole member of its family, Jojoba is an evergreen shrub endemic to the Sonoran and Mojave deserts of SW North America. It is highly adapted to arid conditions with thick, leathery, opposite leaves often held vertically. Plants are dioecious, bearing small, apetalous, wind-pollinated flowers. Its large seeds are the source of jojoba oil, a unique liquid wax ester prized for cosmetics and other industries.

Phylogeny and Classification

Simmondsiaceae is placed within the large and exceptionally diverse order Caryophyllales, a major lineage of core eudicots. This order includes families adapted to a vast range of environments and exhibiting remarkable morphological and physiological diversity, such as Cactaceae (cacti), Amaranthaceae (amaranths, beets, spinach), Caryophyllaceae (pinks, carnations), Polygonaceae (buckwheats, rhubarb), Aizoaceae (ice plants), Nepenthaceae (tropical pitcher plants), and Droseraceae (sundews).

The exact position of Simmondsiaceae within Caryophyllales was historically uncertain due to its unique combination of features (woody habit, opposite simple leaves, apetalous unisexual flowers, unique seed chemistry). However, molecular phylogenetic studies consistently place it firmly within the order, although often as a somewhat isolated lineage, not nesting clearly within any other major family group. It represents a distinct evolutionary path within the Caryophyllales radiation.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Clade: Angiosperms (Flowering plants)
  • Clade: Eudicots
  • Clade: Core Eudicots
  • Order: Caryophyllales
  • Family: Simmondsiaceae

Related Families (within Caryophyllales)

As a member of Caryophyllales, Simmondsiaceae is related to numerous other families in this diverse order, including:

  • Cactaceae (Cactus family): Succulent stems, spines, often showy flowers with inferior ovaries.
  • Amaranthaceae (Amaranth family): Often herbs, small inconspicuous flowers, includes former Chenopodiaceae (beets, spinach, quinoa).
  • Caryophyllaceae (Pink family): Herbs with often opposite leaves, swollen nodes, flowers usually 5-merous with notched petals, free-central placentation.
  • Polygonaceae (Buckwheat family): Herbs/shrubs/vines with often swollen nodes and a characteristic papery sheath (ocrea) at the base of the leaves; small flowers, fruit usually an achene.
  • Aizoaceae (Ice Plant family): Often succulent herbs/shrubs, showy flowers resembling daisies (but petals are staminodes), fruit usually a capsule.
  • (Plus Nepenthaceae, Droseraceae, Plumbaginaceae, Nyctaginaceae, Portulacaceae, etc.)

Evolutionary Significance

The Simmondsiaceae family, represented by Jojoba, is significant for:

  • Unique Lineage in Caryophyllales: Represents a distinct evolutionary branch within a highly diverse order, showcasing a unique combination of morphological traits.
  • Biochemical Specialization: The evolution of liquid wax ester storage in seeds, rather than typical triglycerides, is a remarkable biochemical adaptation with significant ecological and economic implications.
  • Adaptation to Aridity: Provides an excellent example of plant adaptations (leathery leaves, vertical orientation, deep roots) to survive in extreme desert environments.
  • Dioecy and Wind Pollination: Exhibits a reproductive strategy (dioecy with wind pollination) found in various plant groups but notable in this specific context.
