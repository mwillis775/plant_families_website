Strychnaceae

Strychnine Family

Strychnaceae is a family of flowering plants in the order Gentianales, primarily comprising the genus Strychnos with approximately 200 species. These plants are characterized by their woody habit as trees, shrubs, or lianas, often with hooked tendrils in climbing species. Members of this family typically have opposite, simple leaves with 3-5 prominent longitudinal veins and interpetiolar stipules. The flowers are small, with a tubular corolla, and develop into berries or drupes with a hard or leathery rind. The family is notable for producing potent alkaloids, particularly strychnine and curare, which have been used in traditional medicine, arrow poisons, and modern pharmaceuticals. Strychnaceae has a pantropical distribution, with species occurring in tropical regions of Africa, Asia, Australia, and the Americas.

Overview

The Strychnaceae family primarily comprises the genus Strychnos, which includes approximately 200 species of trees, shrubs, and lianas. The family name is derived from the Greek word "strychnos," which was used by ancient Greek physicians to refer to various nightshade plants with toxic properties. In modern taxonomy, Strychnaceae is sometimes treated as a distinct family and sometimes included within the broader Loganiaceae family.

Strychnaceae has a pantropical distribution, with species occurring in tropical regions of Africa, Asia, Australia, and the Americas. Different species have adapted to various tropical habitats, including rainforests, dry forests, savannas, and coastal areas. The greatest diversity is found in tropical Africa and the Amazon basin.

Members of this family are notable for their production of potent alkaloids, particularly strychnine and curare compounds, which have played significant roles in human history as medicines, poisons, and scientific tools. Some species have been used in traditional medicine systems around the world, while others have been the source of arrow and dart poisons used by indigenous peoples for hunting. In modern times, compounds derived from Strychnaceae have been used in pharmaceuticals, scientific research, and historically as rodenticides.

  • Scientific Name: Strychnaceae
  • Common Name: Strychnine Family

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Members of Strychnaceae are woody plants that may be trees, shrubs, or lianas (woody climbers). The climbing species often have characteristic hooked tendrils that develop from modified branches or inflorescences. The bark is typically smooth to slightly fissured, and the wood is hard and dense. Many species have thorns or spines, particularly on the stems and branches.

Leaves

The leaves of Strychnaceae are simple, entire, and opposite (rarely whorled). A distinctive feature is the presence of 3-5 prominent longitudinal veins that run from the base to the apex of the leaf (acrodromous venation). The leaves are typically leathery in texture and have short petioles. Interpetiolar stipules (small structures between the leaf petioles) are present but often reduced to a line or ridge connecting the petiole bases.

Reproductive Structures

The flowers are arranged in axillary or terminal cymes (sometimes appearing as clusters or panicles). Each flower is small, typically with a 5-lobed calyx and a tubular corolla with 4-5 lobes. The corolla is usually white, cream, or greenish. The androecium consists of 4-5 stamens attached to the corolla tube, and the gynoecium has a superior ovary with 2 carpels. The fruit is a berry or drupe with a hard or leathery rind, containing 1 to many seeds embedded in a pulp that is often bitter or toxic. The seeds are typically flattened and disc-shaped or irregularly angular.

Chemical Characteristics

A defining characteristic of Strychnaceae is the production of indole alkaloids, particularly strychnine, brucine, and various curare compounds. These alkaloids are concentrated in the seeds, bark, and roots, and serve as chemical defenses against herbivores and pathogens. The alkaloids have complex molecular structures and potent pharmacological effects, particularly on the nervous system. Different species produce different alkaloid profiles, which has led to their diverse uses in traditional medicine and as arrow poisons.

Distinguishing Features

Strychnaceae can be distinguished from other plant families by a combination of:

  • Woody habit (trees, shrubs, or lianas) often with hooked tendrils in climbing species
  • Opposite leaves with 3-5 prominent longitudinal veins
  • Interpetiolar stipules (often reduced)
  • Small flowers with tubular corollas
  • Berry or drupe fruits with a hard or leathery rind
  • Production of indole alkaloids, particularly strychnine and curare compounds

Identification

Field Identification Tips

When trying to identify members of the Strychnaceae family in the field, look for:

  • Growth habit: Woody trees, shrubs, or lianas, often with hooked tendrils in climbing species
  • Leaves: Opposite, simple, with 3-5 prominent longitudinal veins
  • Stipules: Interpetiolar stipules (often reduced to a line between petioles)
  • Flowers: Small, with tubular corollas, arranged in cymes
  • Fruits: Berries or drupes with a hard or leathery rind

Similar Families

Strychnaceae may be confused with several other plant families:

  • Loganiaceae: Very closely related (and often including Strychnaceae), but typically with different venation patterns and fruit types
  • Apocynaceae: Similar growth habits and sometimes similar fruits, but typically with milky latex and different floral structures
  • Rubiaceae: Also has opposite leaves with interpetiolar stipules, but typically with different venation patterns and inferior ovaries
  • Gelsemiaceae: Similar growth habits and floral structures, but with different leaf venation and fruit types

Distinguishing Features

The most reliable features for distinguishing Strychnaceae from similar families include:

  • Leaf venation: 3-5 prominent longitudinal veins (acrodromous venation)
  • Tendrils: Hooked tendrils in climbing species (absent in non-climbing species)
  • Fruit structure: Berry or drupe with a hard or leathery rind
  • Chemical tests: Presence of strychnine and related alkaloids (requires laboratory testing)

For definitive identification, examination of the leaf venation pattern and, if present, the tendrils is often most helpful, as these features are distinctive for the family.

Notable Examples

The family Strychnaceae contains approximately 200 species, primarily in the genus Strychnos. Here are some notable representatives:

Strychnos nux-vomica

Poison Nut

A medium-sized tree native to India, Sri Lanka, and Southeast Asia. It has a smooth, gray bark and glossy, oval leaves with distinctive 3-5 longitudinal veins. The tree produces small, greenish-white flowers and round, orange fruits containing flat, disc-shaped seeds. These seeds are the primary source of strychnine, a potent alkaloid that has been used in traditional medicine, as a rodenticide, and in small doses as a stimulant. Despite its toxicity, extracts from this tree have been used in Ayurvedic and Chinese medicine for centuries to treat various conditions.

Strychnos toxifera

Curare Vine

A woody liana native to the Amazon basin in South America. It has a climbing habit with characteristic hooked tendrils and opposite leaves with the typical venation pattern. This species is famous as one of the primary sources of curare, a complex mixture of alkaloids that indigenous peoples of the Amazon have traditionally used as an arrow poison for hunting. Curare causes muscle paralysis by blocking neuromuscular junctions. Derivatives of these compounds have been used in modern medicine as muscle relaxants during surgery and for other medical applications.

Strychnos spinosa

Spiny Monkey Orange

A small tree or shrub native to tropical Africa, characterized by its spiny branches and round, woody fruits. The leaves show the typical opposite arrangement and venation pattern of the family. The fruits are large (5-10 cm in diameter), with a hard, woody shell and a pulp containing numerous seeds. Unlike many other Strychnos species, the fruit pulp of S. spinosa is edible when ripe, with a tangy flavor somewhat reminiscent of citrus. The fruits are eaten by various animals, including monkeys (hence the common name), and are also consumed by humans in parts of Africa. The seeds, however, contain alkaloids and should not be eaten.

Phylogenetic Note

Strychnaceae has undergone significant taxonomic revisions based on molecular phylogenetic studies. Traditionally, the genus Strychnos and its relatives were included within the Loganiaceae family. However, molecular evidence has suggested that Loganiaceae in the broad sense is polyphyletic, leading some taxonomists to split it into several families, including Strychnaceae. Other classification systems still maintain a broader concept of Loganiaceae that includes Strychnos. The distinctive morphological features of Strychnos and related genera, particularly their unique alkaloid chemistry, leaf venation patterns, and fruit structure, support their recognition as a separate family. However, the close relationship with Loganiaceae sensu stricto is clear from both morphological and molecular evidence. This taxonomic debate illustrates the ongoing refinement of our understanding of plant relationships as new evidence emerges.
