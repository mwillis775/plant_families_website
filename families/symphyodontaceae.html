Symphyodontaceae

No widely recognized common name

Symphyodontaceae is a monotypic family of pleurocarpous mosses within the order Hookeriales, containing only the genus Symphyodon. These mosses are characterized by their strongly flattened (complanate) foliage, typically absent or very short costa, and often glossy appearance. The family has a primarily Paleotropical distribution (Asia, Malesia, Oceania) and its members are usually found as epiphytes in humid forests.

Overview

The Symphyodontaceae comprises a single genus, Symphyodon, making it a relatively small and well-defined family within the Hookeriales. Its members are distinctive pleurocarpous mosses, readily recognized by their neat, strongly complanate mats or sometimes pendent growths, often exhibiting a yellowish-green to golden-brown hue and a noticeable gloss.

These mosses are primarily inhabitants of moist, shaded forest environments in tropical and subtropical Asia, extending through Malesia to Oceania, with some presence in Africa and Madagascar. They are typically epiphytic, growing on tree trunks, branches, and logs. The family is defined by the combination of its flattened habit, lack of a significant costa, smooth linear leaf cells, and specific details of the double peristome, which sometimes exhibits fusion between endostome segments or to the exostome teeth (inspiring the name Symphyodon, meaning "fused teeth").

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Symphyodontaceae
  • Common Name: None widely recognized
  • Number of Genera: 1 (Symphyodon)
  • Number of Species: Approximately 10-15
  • Distribution: Primarily Paleotropical (Asia, Malesia, Oceania), also Africa/Madagascar
  • Evolutionary Group: Bryophytes - Mosses (Class Bryopsida, Order Hookeriales, Habit: Pleurocarpous)

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Plants are pleurocarpous, forming dense, often glossy mats, or sometimes pendent festoons. Stems are creeping, with branches strongly flattened (complanate). They are typically epiphytic on trees or logs in humid forests.

Leaves

Leaves are strongly complanate and often asymmetric. Leaf shape is typically oblong-lanceolate or ovate-lanceolate, sometimes slightly curved (subfalcate). The apex is usually acute to acuminate. Margins are typically serrate or denticulate, especially in the upper half. A key feature is the costa, which is usually absent, or very short and double. Laminal cells are typically linear, smooth, and often thick-walled. Alar cells are generally not strongly differentiated or forming distinct groups.

Gametangia and Sporophytes

Sporophytes arise laterally from stems. Plants are often dioicous.

  • Seta: Usually elongate, smooth or sometimes papillose above, often reddish.
  • Capsule: Typically erect to inclined, symmetric or slightly asymmetric, ovoid to cylindrical. The operculum is usually conic to rostrate.
  • Peristome: Distinctly double (Hookeriales type).
    • Exostome: 16 lanceolate teeth, often pale yellowish, typically papillose.
    • Endostome: Consists of a basal membrane, well-developed, keeled segments (often similar in height to exostome teeth), and cilia that can be well-developed, rudimentary, or absent. Endostome segments are sometimes fused at their tips in pairs or adhere somewhat to the exostome teeth, though this feature can be variable.
  • Calyptra: Cucullate (hood-shaped), usually smooth, sometimes sparsely hairy, shed early.

Spores

Spores are typically small, spherical, and finely papillose.

Field Identification

Identifying Symphyodontaceae involves recognizing the combination of its pleurocarpous, complanate growth habit, the lack of a significant costa, and its typical epiphytic habitat in Paleotropical regions.

Primary Identification Features

  • Pleurocarpous Mats/Pendent Growth: Plants creep or hang, forming flattened structures.
  • Strongly Complanate Foliage: Leaves and branches are distinctly flattened in one plane.
  • Costa Absent or Short/Double: Lack of a prominent midrib is a key feature.
  • Linear Leaf Cells: Cells are long and narrow, smooth (requires magnification).
  • Habitat and Location: Epiphytic in humid forests, primarily in Asia/Malesia/Oceania.
  • Hookeriales Peristome (Microscopic): Double peristome with specific structure confirms placement.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Glossy Appearance: Often yellowish-green to golden-brown and shiny.
  • Serrate Upper Leaf Margins: Leaf edges are usually toothed towards the tip.
  • Acute/Acuminate Leaf Apex: Tips are generally pointed.
  • Erect to Inclined Capsules: Capsules are held on long setae.

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Gametophyte: The flattened, leafy plant is present year-round.
  • Sporophytes: Look for capsules on long stalks, produced seasonally.

Common Confusion Points

  • Stereophyllaceae (Hypnales): Similar complanate habit, but belongs to a different order (Hypnales) and typically differs in costa (often more variable, sometimes single/short), cell shape (sometimes shorter), alar cells (often more distinct), and peristome details (hypnoid type).
  • Plagiotheciaceae (Hypnales): Also complanate, but different order, often has decurrent leaf bases, and differs in peristome structure.
  • Hookeriaceae (Hookeriales): Often complanate but usually possess a single costa (sometimes double) and may have different leaf cell shapes (sometimes larger, laxer).
  • Daltoniaceae (Hookeriales): Can be complanate but often have different leaf shapes, costa presence, and distinct peristome features.
  • Leucomiaceae (Hookeriales): Often paler green or whitish, very complanate, typically ecostate, but usually have larger, laxer leaf cells.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Pleurocarpous, complanate, glossy mats/pendent
  • Costa absent or very short/double
  • Linear, smooth leaf cells
  • Serrate upper leaf margins
  • Acute/acuminate leaf apex
  • Epiphytic habitat
  • Paleotropical distribution
  • Erect/inclined capsules on long setae

Key Variations:

  • Degree of glossiness and color
  • Presence/absence of sporophytes
  • Papillosity of seta (smooth vs. papillose above)

Notable Examples

As a monotypic family, all examples belong to the genus Symphyodon.

Symphyodon Mont.

(Genus; species include S. complanatus, S. pygmaeus, S. perrottetii)

The sole genus of the family, Symphyodon species are recognized by their strongly flattened, often glossy mats, leaves lacking a significant costa, linear cells, and serrated upper margins. They are characteristic epiphytes of humid forests throughout tropical Asia and surrounding regions. Species differentiation relies on subtle details of leaf shape, cell size, seta ornamentation, and peristome structure.

Phylogeny and Classification

Symphyodontaceae is placed within the order Hookeriales, a large order of primarily tropical pleurocarpous mosses. It is a monotypic family, representing a distinct evolutionary lineage characterized by its specific combination of morphological traits, particularly the complanate habit combined with the absence of a substantial costa.

Molecular studies confirm its position within Hookeriales, distinguishing it from superficially similar complanate mosses in other orders like Hypnales (e.g., Stereophyllaceae, Plagiotheciaceae). Its relationships to other families within Hookeriales (like Hookeriaceae, Daltoniaceae, Leucomiaceae) are continually being refined through phylogenetic analyses.

  • Division: Bryophyta (Mosses)
  • Class: Bryopsida
  • Subclass: Bryidae
  • Superorder: Hypnanae
  • Order: Hookeriales

Related Families

Within the Hookeriales order, Symphyodontaceae is related to other families such as:

  • Hookeriaceae: Often complanate but usually differ in costa presence/strength and cell shape.
  • Daltoniaceae: Diverse family, some complanate, but differ in peristome and leaf details.
  • Pilotrichaceae: Often robust, pendent mosses, typically with strong costae.
  • Saulomataceae: Distinguished by specific peristome and leaf characters.
  • Leucomiaceae: Often pale, ecostate, and complanate, but typically with larger, laxer cells.
  • Schimperobryaceae: Small family distinguished by specific gametophytic and sporophytic features.
  • Regmatodontaceae: Another monogeneric family in the order, but differs in leaf costa (short single) and peristome details.

Evolutionary Significance

Symphyodontaceae highlights several evolutionary points:

  • Evolution of Complanate Habit: Represents one lineage within Hookeriales that has strongly adopted a flattened growth form, likely an adaptation for light capture or water relations in epiphytic habitats.
  • Costa Reduction: The consistent absence or near-absence of a costa is a significant derived trait within the family.
  • Peristome Variation: While fitting the general Hookeriales pattern, the potential for fused endostome tips represents a specific modification within this lineage.
  • Paleotropical Biogeography: Its distribution pattern contributes to understanding the historical connections and diversification of mosses across tropical Asia, Oceania, and Africa.
