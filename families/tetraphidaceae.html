Tetraphidaceae

Four-tooth Moss Family

The Tetraphidaceae is a small but highly distinctive family of mosses, notable for possessing a peristome consisting of only four large teeth, a unique feature among mosses. Often placed in its own class (Tetraphidopsida), its members typically grow on acidic decaying wood, peat, or shaded rock, and frequently produce specialized asexual gemmae cups.

Overview

Tetraphidaceae represents a unique and phylogenetically isolated lineage within the mosses. Comprising only two genera, Tetraphis and Tetrodontium, this family stands apart due to its remarkable peristome structure â€“ just four large, solid teeth, contrasting sharply with the more complex, typically 16-toothed peristomes found in the vast majority of other mosses (Bryidae).

These small, acrocarpous mosses are primarily found in temperate and boreal regions of the Northern Hemisphere, often inhabiting moist, shaded environments. They characteristically grow on acidic substrates like rotting logs and stumps, peat banks, or shaded sandstone rocks. Asexual reproduction via gemmae produced in specialized cup-like structures is common, particularly in Tetraphis pellucida.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Tetraphidaceae Schimp.
  • Common Name: Four-tooth Moss Family
  • Number of Genera: 2 (Tetraphis, Tetrodontium)
  • Number of Species: Approximately 5
  • Distribution: Primarily Northern Hemisphere temperate and boreal regions; some disjunct populations in the Southern Hemisphere and tropics.
  • Evolutionary Group: Bryophytes - Mosses - Class: Tetraphidopsida (or Bryopsida: Subclass Tetraphidae)

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Plants are acrocarpous and small, forming loose to dense tufts or mats. Stems are erect, simple or sparsely branched, often reddish-brown, especially below. Specialized flagelliform (slender, whip-like) shoots bearing gemmae cups are common in Tetraphis.

Leaves

Leaves are small, typically ovate to lanceolate or lingulate, often somewhat distant on lower stems but more crowded towards the apex and around reproductive structures. Margins are usually entire. The costa (midrib) is single, relatively strong, but typically ends well below the leaf apex. Leaf cells are generally smooth, not papillose, relatively firm-walled, and mostly isodiametric (quadrate to hexagonal) or shortly rectangular above, becoming longer rectangular towards the base.

Asexual Reproduction

Asexual reproduction is prominent, especially in Tetraphis pellucida. Specialized, erect, slender shoots bear terminal gemmae cups. These cups are formed by clusters of modified, broader leaves and contain numerous small, multicellular, lenticular (lens-shaped) gemmae, which are dispersed by rain splash.

Sporophyte (Capsule)

Sporophytes are terminal on fertile stems. The seta (stalk) is long, slender, straight, and smooth. The capsule is erect, symmetric, ovoid to cylindrical, and smooth. An operculum (lid), typically conical to short-rostrate, is present. The calyptra (covering the capsule) is often mitrate (cap-like, unslit or evenly lobed at base), thin, and sometimes plicate (pleated).

The most defining feature is the peristome: it consists solely of 4 large, triangular-lanceolate, non-articulated teeth. These teeth are solid (not composed of cell remnants like typical peristomes) and are thought to be derived from different tissue layers than the peristomes of Bryidae mosses. They are hygroscopic, opening when dry and closing when moist.

Habitat

Characteristically found on acidic substrates in moist, shaded locations. Common habitats include decaying wood (logs, stumps), humus-rich soil over rock, peat banks, and shaded acidic rock faces (e.g., sandstone). They are typical inhabitants of coniferous or mixed forests, bogs, and shaded ravines.

Field Identification

Identifying Tetraphidaceae often relies on habitat and unique reproductive structures:

Primary Identification Features

  • 4 Large Peristome Teeth: Absolutely diagnostic if capsules with peristomes are present (requires hand lens or microscope). Unlike any other moss.
  • Gemmae Cups: Presence of terminal cup-like structures on specialized stalks (common in Tetraphis pellucida).
  • Habitat: Characteristic growth on acidic decaying wood, peat, or shaded acidic rock.
  • Acrocarpous Growth: Forming tufts or mats with terminal sporophytes.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Small Size: Plants are generally small.
  • Leaf Shape: Ovate to lanceolate leaves with costa ending below the apex.
  • Smooth Leaf Cells: Cells lack papillae.
  • Long Seta / Erect Capsule: Typical sporophyte posture.

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Gemmae Cups: Often present year-round on specialized shoots, making Tetraphis recognizable even when sterile.
  • Sporophytes: Capsules often mature in summer or fall. The unique 4-toothed peristome persists on old, empty capsules.
  • Gametophytes: Visible year-round in suitable moist habitats.

Common Confusion Points

  • Vegetative Plants: Without gemmae cups or sporophytes, sterile tufts might resemble other small acrocarpous mosses found on wood or rock (e.g., small Dicranella, Pohlia, Bryum). Habitat (acidic wood/peat) can be a clue.
  • Gemmae Cups vs. Male Plants: Gemmae cups can superficially resemble the perigonia (structures surrounding antheridia) of some male moss plants, but close inspection reveals the cup shape and presence of gemmae inside.
  • With Sporophytes: The 4-toothed peristome is unmistakable and prevents confusion with any other moss family once observed.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Small acrocarpous tufts
  • Habitat: Decaying wood, peat, acid rock
  • Gemmae cups on stalks (Tetraphis)
  • Capsule with only 4 large teeth (if fertile)
  • Leaves ovate/lanceolate, costa ending below apex

Key Variations:

  • Genera Tetraphis, Tetrodontium
  • Unique phylogenetic position (Tetraphidopsida)
  • Smooth leaf cells

Notable Examples

The family contains only two small genera.

Tetraphis pellucida

Pellucid Four-tooth Moss

The most common species in the family, widespread in Northern Hemisphere forests. It forms green to brownish tufts on rotting logs, stumps, and peat banks. It is easily recognized by the frequent presence of specialized stalks bearing terminal gemmae cups. Fertile plants produce erect capsules on long setae with the characteristic 4 peristome teeth.

Tetrodontium brownianum

Brown's Four-tooth Moss

A much smaller, inconspicuous species, often overlooked. It typically grows in deep shade on the undersides of acidic rock overhangs (like sandstone) or in crevices. The gametophyte is highly reduced, consisting mostly of persistent protonema (filamentous stage) with tiny leaves. The sporophyte, however, is relatively conspicuous with a long seta, erect capsule, and the typical 4 large peristome teeth.

Phylogeny and Classification

Tetraphidaceae occupies a unique and isolated position within the phylogeny of mosses. Its distinctive peristome, composed of only four solid teeth derived differently from the articulated peristomes of most mosses, along with other features, indicates an early divergence from the main line of moss evolution (the Bryidae).

Consequently, it is often placed in its own class, Tetraphidopsida, or at least its own subclass (Tetraphidae) within the Bryopsida, separate from the vast majority of familiar mosses. It represents one of the few distinct lineages outside the Bryidae, alongside groups like Sphagnopsida (Peat Mosses), Andreaeopsida (Lantern Mosses), and Polytrichopsida (Haircap Mosses).

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Division: Bryophyta (Mosses)
  • Class: Tetraphidopsida (or Bryopsida: Subclass Tetraphidae)
  • Order: Tetraphidales
  • Family: Tetraphidaceae

Related Families

Due to its isolated phylogenetic position, Tetraphidaceae has no close relatives among common moss families. Comparisons are best made with representatives of other major moss classes/subclasses to highlight its distinctiveness:

  • Bryidae (Subclass): The largest group of mosses, characterized by articulated peristomes (typically 16 teeth, single or double), diverse life forms (acrocarpous/pleurocarpous). Includes families like Bryaceae, Hypnaceae, Dicranaceae, Pottiaceae etc.
  • Polytrichopsida (Class): Haircap mosses; robust, often with complex leaf structure (lamellae) and peristomes with teeth connected to an epiphragm. (e.g., Polytrichaceae)
  • Sphagnopsida (Class): Peat mosses; unique plant structure (fascicles of branches), hyaline cells in leaves, capsule elevated on pseudopodium, no peristome. (e.g., Sphagnaceae)
  • Andreaeopsida (Class): Lantern mosses; small, dark mosses on rock, capsules open by longitudinal slits, no peristome. (e.g., Andreaeaceae)

Evolutionary Significance

The Tetraphidaceae family is significant for:

  • Unique Peristome Structure: The 4-toothed peristome provides crucial evidence for understanding the evolution and homology of spore dispersal mechanisms in mosses.
  • Isolated Lineage: Representing an early diverging branch in moss evolution, helping to reconstruct the phylogeny of Bryophyta.
  • Asexual Reproduction Strategies: The specialized gemmae cups in Tetraphis illustrate effective mechanisms for clonal propagation and dispersal in specific habitats.
  • Ecological Niche: Occupying specific niches on acidic decaying wood and rock, contributing to decomposition processes and microhabitat diversity.
