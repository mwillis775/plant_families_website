Timmiellaceae

Timmiella Moss Family

The Timmiellaceae is a small family of acrocarpous mosses, usually placed within the order Pottiales. It contains the single genus Timmiella. These mosses typically grow on soil or rock, often calcareous, and are recognized by their leaves that are strongly crisped when dry, lingulate shape when moist, unique costa structure, and long, twisted peristome teeth.

Overview Characteristics Identification Examples Phylogeny

Overview

Timmiellaceae is a monogeneric family (containing only Timmiella) within the large and diverse order Pottiales. These mosses form tufts or cushions, often in habitats similar to many Pottiaceae species, such as bare soil banks, soil over rock, and rock crevices, with a preference for base-rich or calcareous substrates.

They are distributed widely across temperate, subtropical, and tropical mountainous regions of the world. While vegetatively they can resemble some Pottiaceae due to their crisped dry leaves and habitat, Timmiella is distinguished by its unique costa anatomy (lacking a dorsal stereid band) and its very distinctive peristome of long, thread-like, twisted teeth.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Timmiellaceae Y. Inoue & H. Tsubota
  • Common Name: Timmiella Moss Family
  • Number of Genera: 1 (Timmiella)
  • Number of Species: Approximately 10-15
  • Distribution: Widespread in temperate, subtropical, and tropical montane regions worldwide.
  • Evolutionary Group: Bryophytes - Mosses (Class: Bryopsida, Order: Pottiales)

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Plants are acrocarpous, forming low to moderately tall, often dense tufts or cushions. Stems are erect, simple or sparsely branched, often appearing reddish.

Leaves

Leaves are highly characteristic in their posture: strongly crisped, curled, and contorted when dry, becoming erect-spreading to spreading when moist. The leaf shape is typically lingulate (tongue-shaped) to lanceolate, usually tapering from an oblong, somewhat sheathing base to a broadly acute or obtuse apex. Margins are generally plane or weakly recurved below, often crenulate or finely serrulate near the apex due to projecting cell walls. The costa (midrib) is strong, single, ending in or near the apex (percurrent) or extending slightly beyond (shortly excurrent). A key diagnostic feature, visible only in cross-section, is the costa anatomy: it possesses guide cells and a distinct ventral stereid band (thick-walled supportive cells on the upper/inner side) but lacks a dorsal stereid band (unlike most Pottiaceae). Upper leaf cells are typically small, quadrate to hexagonal, often thick-walled, and usually smooth (lacking papillae) or sometimes slightly bulging (mammillose). Basal cells are usually rectangular, thinner-walled, and more transparent.

Sporophyte (Capsule)

Sporophytes are terminal on the stems. The seta (stalk) is long, slender, erect, and often reddish. The capsule is erect, symmetric, typically cylindrical, and smooth. An operculum (lid), usually conical to rostrate (beaked), is present. The calyptra is cucullate (hood-shaped, split on one side).

The peristome is very distinctive: it is single, consisting of 16 teeth that are long, filiform (thread-like), densely papillose, and typically spirally twisted together, often significantly so. This twisted, thread-like peristome is a key feature of the family.

Habitat

Typically found growing on soil, especially bare soil on banks, trailsides, roadsides, soil overlying rock, or in rock crevices. They often show a preference for calcareous or base-rich substrates but can sometimes be found in more neutral conditions. Common in disturbed or open sites from lowlands to montane elevations.

Field Identification

Identifying Timmiellaceae involves observing both vegetative and reproductive features:

Primary Identification Features

  • Leaves Strongly Crisped/Contorted Dry: This is a very noticeable feature in the field.
  • Lingulate/Lanceolate Leaves (Moist): Tongue-shaped or lance-shaped leaves spreading from the stem when wet.
  • Habitat: Typically on soil or soil over rock, often calcareous or disturbed sites.
  • Twisted Filiform Peristome: Long, thread-like, twisted peristome teeth are diagnostic (requires magnification).
  • Acrocarpous Growth: Forming tufts or cushions.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Sheathing Leaf Base: The base of the leaf often clasps the stem somewhat.
  • Smooth Upper Leaf Cells: Cells generally lack papillae (unlike many Pottiaceae).
  • Long Seta and Erect Cylindrical Capsule: Characteristic sporophyte shape and posture.
  • Reddish Stems: Often visible within the tuft.
  • Costa Anatomy: Lack of dorsal stereids (microscopic feature).

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Dry Season: The strongly crisped leaves are a key identification clue even without moisture.
  • Sporophyte Maturity: Capsules with the distinctive peristome are often present in spring or summer.
  • Year-Round Gametophytes: Vegetative tufts persist year-round.

Common Confusion Points

  • Pottiaceae (e.g., Tortella, Trichostomum, Weissia): Many Pottiaceae share the calcareous/soil habitat and have leaves crisped when dry. However, Pottiaceae often have distinctly papillose leaf cells, different costa structure (usually with dorsal stereids), and different peristome types (often shorter, straight teeth, sometimes rudimentary or absent).
  • Dicranaceae / Ditrichaceae: Usually have subulate leaves (not lingulate/lanceolate) and peristome teeth that are split into forks, not long, filiform, and twisted.
  • Encalyptaceae: Also on calcareous substrates, but has lingulate leaves that are less crisped and the unmistakable large extinguisher calyptra.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Acrocarpous tufts on soil/rock (often calcareous)
  • Leaves strongly crisped/contorted dry
  • Leaves lingulate/lanceolate with sheathing base moist
  • Costa strong, ending near apex
  • Peristome teeth long, thread-like, twisted (if fertile)

Key Variations:

  • Single genus Timmiella
  • Leaf cells usually smooth
  • Lacks dorsal stereid band in costa
  • Long seta, erect capsule

Notable Examples

The family contains only the genus Timmiella.

Timmiella anomala

(No common name widely used)

A widespread species found in temperate and tropical regions, often on calcareous soil banks or rocks. It forms dense, dark green tufts. The leaves are strongly crisped when dry, lingulate when moist, with a sheathing base and crenulate margins near the apex. Fertile plants show the characteristic long seta, erect capsule, and twisted, filiform peristome teeth.

Timmiella barbuloides

(No common name widely used)

Another common species, particularly in warmer temperate and subtropical areas, often found on soil over limestone or on mortar. Similar in habit to T. anomala, forming tufts with strongly crisped leaves when dry. Distinctions from other Timmiella species often rely on subtle differences in leaf shape, cell size, or geographical distribution.

Phylogeny and Classification

Timmiellaceae is placed within the large order Pottiales, which contains many mosses adapted to dry or calcareous environments. While sharing habitat preferences and the acrocarpous growth form with many Pottiaceae, Timmiellaceae (genus Timmiella) is distinguished by its unique combination of costa anatomy (lacking dorsal stereids) and peristome structure (long, twisted, filiform teeth).

Molecular studies confirm its placement within Pottiales but as a distinct lineage separate from the core Pottiaceae family. It represents an interesting morphological variation within the order, particularly regarding the peristome development.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Division: Bryophyta (Mosses)
  • Class: Bryopsida
  • Order: Pottiales
  • Family: Timmiellaceae

Related Families (within Pottiales)

Families within the Pottiales order considered related include:

  • Pottiaceae: The largest family in the order, sharing similar habitats but differing in costa structure, cell papillosity (often), and peristome details.
  • Cinclidotaceae: Often aquatic or riparian mosses on calcareous substrates, with different leaf structures (often strongly bordered, multistratose).
  • Serpotortellaceae: A small family sometimes recognized within Pottiales.
  • Encalyptaceae: Sometimes placed within Pottiales, distinguished by the extinguisher calyptra.

Evolutionary Significance

The Timmiellaceae family is significant for:

  • Unique Peristome Structure: The long, twisted, filiform peristome represents a distinct morphology within the Pottiales and mosses generally.
  • Costa Anatomy Variation: Demonstrating variation in the internal structure of the costa (absence of dorsal stereids) compared to closely related groups like Pottiaceae.
  • Convergent Evolution?: The crisped leaves when dry are a feature shared with many Pottiaceae, possibly representing convergent adaptation to similar environments.
  • Phylogenetic Relationships: Helping to resolve the complex relationships within the large and ecologically important Pottiales order.
