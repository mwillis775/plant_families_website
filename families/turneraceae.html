Turneraceae

Turnera Family

Turneraceae is a family of herbs, shrubs, and occasionally trees in the order Malpighiales, containing approximately 10 genera and 120 species. These plants are characterized by their alternate leaves with serrate or dentate margins, 5-merous flowers with a distinctive hypanthium, and heterostylous reproductive system (with different flower morphs having styles and stamens of different lengths). Primarily distributed in tropical and subtropical regions of Africa, Madagascar, and the Americas, they typically grow in diverse habitats from savannas to forest margins. The family is notable for its contorted corolla, three free styles, and parietal placentation. Closely related to Passifloraceae (and sometimes included within it), Turneraceae is best known for Turnera diffusa (damiana), which has traditional medicinal uses.

Overview

The Turneraceae family comprises a diverse group of flowering plants that are primarily distributed in tropical and subtropical regions of the Americas and Africa. These plants range from small herbs to shrubs and occasionally small trees, and they occupy a variety of habitats from savannas and grasslands to forest margins and disturbed areas.

The family is named after the genus Turnera, which was named in honor of William Turner (c. 1508-1568), an English naturalist and botanist often referred to as the "Father of English Botany." Turnera is the largest genus in the family, containing approximately half of the total species.

Turneraceae has had a complex taxonomic history. Traditionally recognized as a distinct family closely related to Passifloraceae (the passion flower family) and Malesherbiaceae, molecular phylogenetic studies have shown that these three families form a monophyletic group. As a result, some classification systems, including APG IV (Angiosperm Phylogeny Group), now include Turneraceae as a subfamily (Turneroideae) within an expanded Passifloraceae.

The family is notable for its heterostylous reproductive system, in which different plants produce flowers with styles and stamens of different lengths. This mechanism promotes outcrossing and genetic diversity. Many species in the family also have extrafloral nectaries that attract ants, which may provide protection against herbivores.

Several members of the family have economic importance, particularly Turnera diffusa (damiana), which has been used in traditional medicine for its purported aphrodisiac properties and as a tonic for various conditions. Some species, such as Turnera ulmifolia, are cultivated as ornamentals for their attractive yellow flowers.

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Members of Turneraceae exhibit a range of growth forms:

  • Herbs: Many species are annual or perennial herbs
  • Shrubs: A significant number of species are shrubby in habit
  • Trees: A few species develop into small trees

The plants are typically erect, sometimes with woody bases, and can range from a few centimeters to several meters in height. They often have simple or branched stems that may be smooth or hairy, with various types of trichomes (hairs) including glandular and eglandular forms.

Leaves

The leaves of Turneraceae have several distinctive characteristics:

  • Arrangement: Alternate and spirally arranged along the stems
  • Type: Simple, but sometimes dissected or pinnatifid
  • Margins: Typically serrate or dentate, often with glandular teeth
  • Petioles: Usually present, sometimes with extrafloral nectaries at the base of the blade
  • Stipules: Usually absent, but present in some genera (e.g., Erblichia)
  • Venation: Pinnate with cross-venulation

Many species have glands or extrafloral nectaries at the base of the leaf blade or on the petiole, which secrete nectar that attracts ants. These ants may provide protection against herbivores, forming a mutualistic relationship with the plant.

Flowers

The flowers of Turneraceae are highly distinctive and provide key diagnostic features for the family:

  • Arrangement: Solitary in the leaf axils or aggregated in racemose inflorescences; sometimes epiphyllous (borne on the leaf surface) in some species of Turnera
  • Structure: Regular (actinomorphic), hermaphroditic, 5-merous, with a distinctive hypanthium (floral tube)
  • Hypanthium: Short to tubular, often with a hemispherical swelling inside
  • Perianth: Distinct calyx and corolla, each with 5 segments
  • Calyx: Gamosepalous (fused sepals), toothed, imbricate, not persistent
  • Corolla: Polypetalous (separate petals), contorted in bud, typically yellow or sometimes red, with clawed petals
  • Corona: Some genera have a membranous corona inserted below the throat of the calyx or at the base of the sepals
  • Androecium: 5 stamens, free from the perianth and inserted low in the hypanthium
  • Gynoecium: Syncarpous (fused carpels), with 3 carpels forming a single-chambered ovary
  • Styles: 3, free, opposite the placentas
  • Stigmas: 3, fringed, commissural (aligned with the placentas)

A key feature of many Turneraceae species is heterostyly, a polymorphism in which different plants produce flowers with styles and stamens of different lengths. This promotes outcrossing by making self-pollination less likely and encouraging pollen transfer between different flower morphs.

Fruits and Seeds

After pollination, the flowers develop into:

  • Fruit type: A dehiscent capsule
  • Dehiscence: Valvular, with each of the three valves bearing a median placenta
  • Seeds: Numerous (typically 25-100 per fruit), small, with a reticulate or pitted surface
  • Arils: Present, derived from the funicle (the stalk attaching the seed to the placenta)
  • Endosperm: Copious, oily
  • Embryo: Well-differentiated, straight, with two planoconvex cotyledons

The arillate seeds are a feature shared with Passifloraceae and contribute to seed dispersal, often by ants or birds attracted to the nutritious aril.

Chemical Characteristics

Turneraceae plants have several notable chemical characteristics:

  • Cyanogenic compounds: Many species contain cyanogenic glycosides, particularly of the gynocardin group
  • Alkaloids: Present in some species, contributing to their medicinal properties
  • Proanthocyanidins and flavonols: Generally absent
  • Ellagic acid: Absent

The presence of cyanogenic compounds may serve as a defense mechanism against herbivores, while the alkaloids found in some species, particularly Turnera diffusa, contribute to their traditional medicinal uses.

Distinguishing Features

Turneraceae can be distinguished from related families by a combination of:

  • Alternate leaves with serrate or dentate margins
  • 5-merous flowers with a distinctive hypanthium
  • Contorted corolla
  • Three free styles opposite the placentas
  • Commissural stigmas
  • Parietal placentation
  • Arillate seeds in capsular fruits
  • Heterostylous reproductive system in many species

Identification

Field Identification Tips

Identifying members of the Turneraceae family in the field can be facilitated by looking for:

  • Habit: Herbs, shrubs, or occasionally small trees.
  • Leaves: Alternate, simple (sometimes dissected), with serrate or dentate margins; often with extrafloral nectaries at the base of the blade or on the petiole.
  • Flowers: Solitary in leaf axils or in racemose inflorescences; sometimes borne on the leaf surface (epiphyllous) in some Turnera species.
  • Floral structure: Regular, 5-merous, with a distinctive hypanthium; typically with yellow petals (sometimes red).
  • Reproductive features: Three free styles with fringed stigmas; heterostyly (different flower morphs with styles and stamens of different lengths) in many species.
  • Fruits: Dehiscent capsules with numerous small, arillate seeds.

Similar Families

Turneraceae may be confused with several related families:

  • Passifloraceae (Passion Flower Family): The closest relative, but typically has tendrils, stipules, and a distinctive corona of filaments in the flowers. Most Passifloraceae are climbers rather than herbs or shrubs.
  • Malesherbiaceae (Malesherbia Family): Similar in some floral features but has lateral styles (not opposite the placentas) and foetid leaves. Now often included in Passifloraceae.
  • Loasaceae (Stickleaf Family): Can have similar growth forms but differs in having often stinging hairs, complex floral structures, and different fruit types.
  • Cistaceae (Rock Rose Family): Similar in having 5-merous flowers with yellow petals, but differs in having opposite leaves (at least the lower ones) and different placentation.

Distinguishing Features

The most reliable features for distinguishing Turneraceae from similar families include:

  • Floral structure: The combination of a hypanthium, 5-merous flowers, contorted corolla, and three free styles opposite the placentas is distinctive.
  • Reproductive system: Heterostyly is common in Turneraceae and provides a useful diagnostic feature.
  • Leaf characteristics: Alternate leaves with serrate or dentate margins, often with extrafloral nectaries.
  • Fruit type: Capsules with arillate seeds and parietal placentation.

For definitive identification, examination of the floral structure, particularly the position of the styles relative to the placentas and the presence of heterostyly, is often necessary.

Notable Examples

The Turneraceae family contains approximately 10 genera and 120 species. Here are some notable representatives:

Turnera ulmifolia

Yellow Alder or West Indian Holly

A widely distributed species native to the Caribbean, Mexico, Central America, and northern South America, but now naturalized in many tropical regions worldwide. It is a perennial herb or small shrub growing to about 1 meter tall, with serrate, elm-like leaves (hence the specific epithet "ulmifolia").

The flowers are bright yellow, about 2-4 cm in diameter, and open for only a few hours in the morning. The species exhibits heterostyly, with different plants producing flowers with either long or short styles. T. ulmifolia is often cultivated as an ornamental and has naturalized in many tropical regions. It also has traditional medicinal uses for treating various ailments.

Turnera diffusa

Damiana

A small shrub native to southwestern Texas, Mexico, Central America, and the Caribbean, growing to about 1-2 meters tall. It has small, aromatic leaves and small yellow flowers that bloom in summer and are followed by small fruits with a sweet smell.

T. diffusa is best known for its traditional medicinal uses. The leaves have been used for centuries as an aphrodisiac and to treat various conditions including anxiety, depression, and digestive disorders. The plant contains alkaloids, flavonoids, and other compounds that may contribute to its reported effects. It is commercially harvested and sold as a herbal supplement and is also used as a flavoring in some liqueurs.

Piriqueta cistoides

Pitted Stripeseed

A perennial herb native to the southeastern United States, the Caribbean, and parts of Central and South America. It typically grows to about 30-60 cm tall and has simple, alternate leaves with serrate margins and stellate (star-shaped) hairs.

The flowers are yellow, about 1-2 cm in diameter, and are borne singly in the leaf axils. Like many members of the family, P. cistoides exhibits heterostyly. The genus Piriqueta differs from Turnera in having a narrow, fringed corona in the flowers. The species grows in a variety of habitats, including pine flatwoods, sandhills, and disturbed areas.

Phylogenetic Note

The phylogenetic position of Turneraceae has been clarified through molecular studies, which have shown that it forms a monophyletic group with Passifloraceae and Malesherbiaceae. This close relationship has led to taxonomic revisions in recent classification systems.

In the APG IV classification system (Angiosperm Phylogeny Group), Turneraceae is treated as a subfamily (Turneroideae) within an expanded Passifloraceae, along with Malesherbiaceae (as subfamily Malesherbioideae) and the traditional Passifloraceae (as subfamily Passifloroideae). This treatment reflects the close evolutionary relationships among these groups.

Despite this taxonomic reclassification, Turneraceae remains distinctive in several morphological and ecological aspects:

  • Unlike most Passifloraceae, Turneraceae lacks tendrils and typically has a shrubby or herbaceous habit rather than a climbing habit.
  • The contorted corolla and ephemeral calyx are distinctive features not found in typical Passifloraceae.
  • The three free styles positioned opposite the placentas and the commissural stigmas are characteristic of Turneraceae.
  • Heterostyly is common in Turneraceae but rare in Passifloraceae.

These distinctive features, combined with its specialized reproductive biology and diverse ecological adaptations, make Turneraceae an interesting case study in plant evolution. The family's heterostylous reproductive system, in particular, has been the subject of extensive research as a model for understanding the evolution of plant breeding systems and the mechanisms that promote genetic diversity through outcrossing.

The family also provides insights into plant-animal interactions, particularly through the extrafloral nectaries that attract ants and the arillate seeds that facilitate seed dispersal by animals. These adaptations have likely contributed to the successful diversification and widespread distribution of the family in tropical and subtropical regions.
