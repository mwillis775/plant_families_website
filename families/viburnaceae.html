Viburnaceae

Viburnum Family

The Viburnaceae family comprises the single large genus Viburnum, consisting of shrubs and small trees. They are recognized by their typically opposite, simple leaves, clusters of small white or pinkish flowers, and fleshy drupe fruits. Members of this family are widely cultivated as ornamentals and are found primarily in temperate regions of the Northern Hemisphere.

Overview

Viburnaceae is a family of flowering plants in the order Dipsacales. Recent phylogenetic studies have led to the recognition of this family, separating the genus Viburnum from related families like Adoxaceae or Caprifoliaceae where it was previously placed. The family now contains only the genus Viburnum, but this is a large and diverse genus with around 150-200 species.

Viburnums are popular horticultural plants, valued for their attractive foliage (sometimes with good fall color), showy flower clusters, and often colorful fruits that provide food for wildlife. They are typically woodland or thicket plants, found across temperate North America, Europe, and Asia, with some species extending into mountainous regions of the tropics in Southeast Asia and South America.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Viburnaceae Raf.
  • Common Name: Viburnum Family
  • Number of Genera: 1 (Viburnum)
  • Number of Species: Approximately 150-200
  • Distribution: Primarily Northern Hemisphere temperate regions, also mountains in Southeast Asia and South America.
  • Evolutionary Group: Angiosperms - Eudicots - Asterids - Dipsacales

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Plants are typically multi-stemmed shrubs or occasionally small trees. They can be deciduous or evergreen depending on the species and climate.

Leaves

Leaves are usually arranged oppositely along the stem (rarely in whorls of three). They are typically simple, although some species have palmately lobed leaves (resembling maple leaves). Leaf margins are variable: often toothed (dentate or serrate), sometimes entire or lobed. Small stipules may be present at the base of the petiole. Leaf surfaces can be smooth or hairy, and some species have distinctive glands (extrafloral nectaries) on the petioles or leaf bases.

Inflorescence

Flowers are borne in terminal, compound inflorescences, typically corymbs (often flat-topped or umbel-like) or sometimes panicles (more branched and pyramidal). In some species (e.g., Viburnum opulus, V. plicatum), the inflorescence has enlarged, showy, sterile flowers around the margin, surrounding smaller, fertile flowers in the center, similar to lacecap hydrangeas.

Flowers

Individual flowers are generally small and radially symmetrical (actinomorphic). The calyx is small, with 5 persistent lobes. The corolla is typically rotate (wheel-shaped) or broadly campanulate (bell-shaped), with 5 lobes (petals), usually white or pinkish, sometimes fragrant. There are 5 stamens, alternating with the corolla lobes and attached to the corolla tube; anthers typically yellow or purplish. The ovary is inferior, usually formed from 3 carpels but typically only one locule develops fully, containing a single ovule. The style is short and thick, topped by a 3-lobed stigma.

Fruits and Seeds

The fruit is a drupe (a fleshy fruit containing a single hard stone or pit which encloses the seed). Drupes are typically globose to ovoid or ellipsoid, and mature to shades of red, yellow, blue, purple, or black. The single stone inside is often flattened and may be grooved. Fruits are often attractive to birds, which aid in seed dispersal.

Field Identification

Identifying Viburnums (members of Viburnaceae) usually relies on a combination of vegetative and reproductive features:

Primary Identification Features

  • Shrub or Small Tree Habit: Woody plants, not herbs or large trees.
  • Opposite Leaves: Check leaf arrangement along the stem (most are opposite).
  • Simple Leaves: Leaves are typically simple (not compound), though often toothed or lobed.
  • Flower Clusters: Look for terminal clusters (corymbs or panicles) of small, 5-petaled, white or pinkish flowers.
  • Drupe Fruits: Presence of fleshy fruits (red, blue, black) each containing a single hard stone.

Secondary Identification Features

  • Toothed/Lobed Leaf Margins: Common in many species.
  • Inferior Ovary: Calyx and corolla arise from the top of the ovary (visible in flower or as remnants on fruit).
  • 5 Stamens: Consistently 5 stamens per flower.
  • Sterile Marginal Flowers: Some species have showy, enlarged sterile flowers around the edge of the inflorescence.
  • Stipules/Glands: Presence of small stipules or glands on petioles can be helpful.

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Spring/Early Summer: Look for characteristic flower clusters.
  • Late Summer/Fall: Look for maturing drupe fruits; many species also have distinctive fall foliage color.
  • Growing Season: Opposite, simple leaves are key vegetative features. Check for stipules or glands.
  • Winter: Opposite branching pattern, persistent fruits (on some), and distinctive buds can aid identification.

Common Confusion Points

  • Cornus (Dogwoods): Also shrubs/trees with opposite simple leaves and drupes. Differ in having flowers typically with 4 petals/stamens (vs. 5 in Viburnum) and often distinctive arcuate leaf venation (veins curving towards tip).
  • Sambucus (Elderberries - Adoxaceae): Have opposite leaves, but they are pinnately compound (vs. simple in Viburnum). Flower clusters and berry-like drupes can look similar.
  • Acer (Maples): Have opposite leaves, often lobed like some Viburnums, but have very different flowers and distinctive winged fruits (samaras).
  • Other Caprifoliaceae sensu lato: Families like Lonicera (Honeysuckles) have opposite leaves but are often vines or have tubular/bilabiate flowers and berry fruits. Diervilla (Bush Honeysuckle) has opposite leaves and capsules (not drupes).

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Shrub or small tree
  • Leaves opposite, simple (often toothed/lobed)
  • Terminal clusters (corymbs/panicles) of small flowers
  • Flowers with 5 petals, 5 stamens
  • Fruit a drupe (red, blue, black) with one stone

Key Variations:

  • Single genus Viburnum
  • Some species with showy sterile marginal flowers
  • Leaf margins entire, toothed, or lobed
  • Deciduous or evergreen

Notable Examples

The genus Viburnum includes many well-known horticultural and wild species.

Viburnum dentatum

Arrowwood Viburnum

A common deciduous shrub native to eastern North America. It has broadly ovate leaves with coarse teeth (dentate) and produces flat-topped clusters of white flowers in late spring, followed by blue-black drupes. Named "arrowwood" because Native Americans reportedly used its straight young stems for arrow shafts.

Viburnum opulus

Guelder Rose / European Cranberrybush

Native to Europe, North Africa, and Central Asia, widely cultivated. Known for its maple-like lobed leaves and distinctive flower clusters featuring large, sterile white flowers surrounding small fertile flowers. Produces bright red, translucent drupes that persist into winter. (Note: Not a true cranberry).

Viburnum acerifolium

Mapleleaf Viburnum

Another eastern North American native shrub, typically found in dry woodlands. Its leaves strongly resemble maple leaves with 3 distinct lobes. Produces flat-topped clusters of white flowers followed by black drupes. Known for its attractive pinkish to purplish fall foliage.

Phylogeny and Classification

Viburnaceae is placed within the order Dipsacales in the Asterid clade of Eudicots. The recognition of Viburnaceae as a separate family containing only Viburnum is a relatively recent change based on molecular phylogenetic analyses. Previously, Viburnum was often included in a broadly defined Caprifoliaceae or, more recently, within Adoxaceae.

Current understanding suggests that Viburnum represents a distinct lineage that diverged early within the Dipsacales, sister to the Adoxaceae family. Recognizing it as its own family, Viburnaceae, better reflects its phylogenetic position and distinct morphology compared to other members of Adoxaceae (like Sambucus and Adoxa).

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Clade: Angiosperms
  • Clade: Eudicots
  • Clade: Asterids
  • Order: Dipsacales
  • Family: Viburnaceae

Related Families (within Dipsacales)

The closest relative and other families within the Dipsacales order include:

  • Adoxaceae (Moschatel Family): Now typically includes Elderberry (Sambucus) and Moschatel (Adoxa). Considered the sister family to Viburnaceae.
  • Caprifoliaceae (Honeysuckle Family): In its stricter sense, includes Honeysuckles (Lonicera), Snowberries (Symphoricarpos), Weigela, etc.
  • Other smaller families in Dipsacales like Linnaeaceae (e.g., Linnaea, Abelia) and Morinaceae.

Evolutionary Significance

The Viburnaceae family (and the genus Viburnum) is significant for:

  • Early Divergence in Dipsacales: Representing a distinct lineage within the order, helping to understand the evolutionary relationships among families like Adoxaceae and Caprifoliaceae.
  • Biogeography: Its wide distribution across the Northern Hemisphere provides insights into historical plant migrations and diversification.
  • Floral Diversity: Exhibiting variations in inflorescence structure, including the evolution of showy sterile marginal flowers in some species, likely related to pollinator attraction.
  • Ecological Role: Providing important food resources (fruits) for birds and other wildlife, contributing to ecosystem function.
