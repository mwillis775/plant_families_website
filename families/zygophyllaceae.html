Zygophyllaceae

Caltrop Family / Creosote Bush Family

Zygophyllaceae is a family of flowering plants typically found in arid or saline environments in warm temperate and tropical regions worldwide. Members include herbs, shrubs, and small trees, often characterized by opposite, pinnately compound leaves which are frequently fleshy or resinous. Flowers are usually radially symmetrical, 5-merous, often yellow, and possess a prominent nectar disc. Fruits are commonly capsules or schizocarps that split into segments, sometimes spiny or winged.

Overview

The Zygophyllaceae family comprises about 22-30 genera and 250-300 species, well-adapted to thrive in harsh conditions like deserts, scrublands, and saline soils. Its distribution is widespread across arid and semi-arid zones of the world, including the Americas, Africa, the Mediterranean, central Asia, and Australia. Notable members include Creosote Bush (Larrea tridentata), dominant in North American deserts, Caltrop or Puncture Vine (Tribulus terrestris), a widespread weed known for its spiny fruits, and Lignum Vitae (Guaiacum spp.), known for its extremely dense wood.

Many species exhibit xerophytic (drought-adapted) or halophytic (salt-adapted) characteristics, such as small or fleshy leaves, resinous coatings, deep root systems, and specialized physiology. The family is placed in its own order, Zygophyllales, within the rosid clade, considered sister to the large nitrogen-fixing clade (including Fabales, Rosales, etc.).

Economically, the family includes sources of valuable timber (Guaiacum - Lignum Vitae, one of the hardest and densest woods), medicinal plants (Peganum harmala, Guaiacum), potential biofuel sources (Balanites), noxious weeds (Tribulus), and ecologically dominant shrubs (Larrea). They also contain diverse secondary metabolites like resins, saponins, and alkaloids.

Quick Facts

  • Scientific Name: Zygophyllaceae
  • Common Name: Caltrop family, Creosote Bush family
  • Number of Genera: Approximately 22-30
  • Number of Species: Approximately 250-300
  • Distribution: Warm temperate and tropical arid/saline regions worldwide.
  • Evolutionary Group: Eudicots - Rosids - Fabids - Order Zygophyllales

Key Characteristics

Growth Form and Habit

Annual or perennial herbs, subshrubs, shrubs, or small trees. Stems are sometimes jointed at the nodes. Plants are often adapted to dry (xerophytic) or salty (halophytic) conditions.

Leaves

Leaves are usually opposite (less commonly alternate), typically pinnately compound (usually even-pinnate, meaning leaflets in pairs with no terminal leaflet), sometimes simple or 2-foliolate. Leaflets are often fleshy or leathery, entire. Stipules are usually present, sometimes modified into spines, or fleshy.

Inflorescence

Flowers are often borne solitary or in few-flowered clusters (cymes), typically in axillary or terminal positions.

Flowers

Flowers are usually bisexual and typically radially symmetrical (actinomorphic). They are commonly 5-merous (based on a pattern of five), sometimes 4-merous.

  • Sepals: Usually 5 (or 4), typically free or slightly fused at the base, often persistent.
  • Petals: Usually 5 (or 4, sometimes absent), free, often yellow, white, or orange, sometimes clawed (narrowed at the base).
  • Androecium: Stamens typically twice the number of petals, commonly 10 (or 8) arranged in two whorls, often of unequal length. Filaments frequently bear scale-like appendages at their base.
  • Nectar Disc: A prominent nectar-producing disc is usually present at the base of the ovary, inside the stamens.
  • Gynoecium: Ovary is superior, typically composed of (2-)5 fused carpels, usually with the same number of locules as carpels (sometimes more due to false septa). Placentation is usually axile, with one to many ovules per locule. There is usually a single style, which may be simple or lobed at the stigma.

Fruits and Seeds

Fruit types are diverse but often dry and splitting. Common forms include:

  • Capsule: Splitting open along locule lines (loculicidal) or septa (septicidal).
  • Schizocarp: A fruit that splits into multiple (typically 5) one-seeded segments (mericarps). These segments may be spiny (e.g., Tribulus), winged, or unadorned.

Less commonly, the fruit may be a berry or a drupe. Seeds usually contain endosperm, though it is sometimes absent; the embryo can be straight or curved.

Chemical Characteristics

The family is known for producing a variety of secondary metabolites, including resins (notably in Larrea), steroidal saponins (common), alkaloids (such as harmala alkaloids in Peganum), lignans (like NDGA in Larrea), and essential oils. These compounds contribute to medicinal properties, toxicity, and adaptations to harsh environments.

Field Identification

Identifying Zygophyllaceae often involves recognizing their adaptation to dry/saline habitats, opposite compound leaves with stipules, and characteristic 5-merous flowers with 10 stamens.

Primary Identification Features

  • Habitat: Typically arid, semi-arid, or saline environments.
  • Leaves: Usually opposite, pinnately compound (often even-pinnate), frequently fleshy or leathery.
  • Stipules: Usually present, sometimes spiny or fleshy.
  • Flowers: Regular (actinomorphic), usually 5 sepals, 5 petals (often yellow), typically 10 stamens (often unequal), prominent nectar disc.
  • Ovary: Superior.
  • Fruit: Often a capsule or a schizocarp splitting into segments (sometimes spiny or winged).

Secondary Identification Features

  • Stamen Filaments: Often with basal appendages.
  • Stems: Sometimes jointed.
  • Resinous/Aromatic: Some species have resinous coatings or distinct scents (e.g., Creosote Bush).

Seasonal Identification Tips

  • Flowering: Often occurs opportunistically after rainfall in desert regions.
  • Fruiting: Fruits, especially the persistent spiny schizocarps of Tribulus or capsules, can aid identification long after flowering.
  • Vegetative: Leaf arrangement, compound nature, fleshiness, and stipules are useful year-round features.

Common Confusion Points

Plants with compound leaves or adapted to arid conditions can cause confusion:

  • Fabaceae (Pea family): Often have compound leaves and stipules, common in arid regions. However, Fabaceae flowers are typically zygomorphic (papilionaceous) and the fruit is a legume (pod).
  • Geraniaceae (Geranium family): Often herbs with stipules, 5-merous flowers, and fruits that split, but leaves are usually palmately lobed or compound, and the fruit is a distinctive schizocarp with a persistent, often elastic style ('stork's bill').
  • Rutaceae (Citrus family): Can have compound leaves, but typically possess pellucid dots (oil glands visible when held to light) and flowers/fruits have different structures (e.g., hesperidium in citrus, samaras, capsules).
  • Simaroubaceae (Quassia family): Often trees/shrubs with pinnately compound leaves, but flowers usually lack petals or have different stamen numbers/arrangements, and fruits are typically samaras or drupes. Often very bitter.

The combination of usually opposite, pinnately compound leaves (often fleshy, with stipules), regular 5-merous flowers with typically 10 stamens and a nectar disc, and capsule/schizocarp fruits in arid/saline habitats strongly suggests Zygophyllaceae.

Field Guide Quick Reference

Look For:

  • Herb/shrub/tree of arid/saline areas
  • Leaves usually opposite, pinnately compound
  • Leaflets often fleshy
  • Stipules present (sometimes spiny)
  • Regular flowers, 5 sepals, 5 petals (often yellow)
  • Usually 10 stamens
  • Prominent nectar disc
  • Fruit: capsule or schizocarp (sometimes spiny/winged)

Key Variations:

  • Leaflet number/size
  • Presence/type of spines (stipular or fruit)
  • Fruit details (capsule vs. schizocarp segments)
  • Habit (herb vs. shrub vs. tree)

Notable Examples

Includes ecologically dominant desert plants, notorious weeds, and sources of valuable wood.

Larrea tridentata

Creosote Bush

An evergreen shrub dominating large areas of North American deserts (Mojave, Sonoran, Chihuahuan). Recognizable by its small, resinous, opposite leaves (each with two leaflets), yellow 5-petaled flowers, and fuzzy white spherical schizocarp fruits. Famous for its characteristic "smell of rain" (creosote aroma) released after precipitation.

Tribulus terrestris

Puncture Vine / Caltrop / Goathead

A prostrate annual herb, now a cosmopolitan weed of warm, dry, disturbed areas. Features opposite, pinnately compound leaves and small yellow flowers. Notorious for its fruit, a schizocarp that splits into five hard, spiny segments (mericarps) capable of puncturing tires and injuring feet.

Guaiacum spp.

Lignum Vitae / Guayacan

A genus of slow-growing trees and shrubs native to the tropical Americas. Known for producing Lignum Vitae, one of the heaviest, hardest, and most durable woods known, historically used for bearings, ship parts, and carvings. Features opposite, even-pinnate leaves and often showy blue or purple flowers. Fruits are typically capsules. Several species are endangered due to overharvesting.

Zygophyllum spp.

Twinleaf

A large genus (~100 species) of herbs and shrubs found in arid and saline regions of Africa, Asia, the Mediterranean, and Australia. Typically have opposite, fleshy leaves, usually with only two leaflets (bifoliolate), or sometimes simple. Flowers are often yellow or white. Fruit is usually a capsule. Many species are well-adapted halophytes (salt-tolerant).

Phylogeny and Classification

Zygophyllaceae is placed in its own order, Zygophyllales, within the fabid clade (eurosids I) of the rosids. Molecular phylogenetic studies have robustly supported this placement, revealing that Zygophyllales is the sister group to the large "nitrogen-fixing clade," which comprises the orders Fabales, Rosales, Fagales, and Cucurbitales.

The order Zygophyllales primarily consists of the family Zygophyllaceae itself. The small family Krameriaceae (containing only the genus Krameria, hemiparasitic shrubs) is often included within Zygophyllales as its sister group, or sometimes treated as a separate, closely related order (Krameriales). The recognition of Zygophyllales highlights a distinct evolutionary lineage separate from other major rosid groups, characterized by adaptations to arid environments.

Position in Plant Phylogeny

  • Kingdom: Plantae
  • Clade: Angiosperms (Flowering plants)
  • Clade: Eudicots
  • Clade: Rosids
  • Clade: Fabids (Eurosids I)
  • Order: Zygophyllales
  • Family: Zygophyllaceae

Related Families

The only other family placed within or immediately sister to the order Zygophyllales is:

  • Krameriaceae: Contains the single genus Krameria (~18 species). Hemiparasitic shrubs of the Americas with alternate simple leaves and highly modified zygomorphic flowers.

The order Zygophyllales as a whole is sister to the large nitrogen-fixing clade, which includes major orders and families such as:

  • Fabales: Fabaceae (Pea/Legume family), Polygalaceae, Surianaceae, Quillajaceae
  • Rosales: Rosaceae (Rose family), Rhamnaceae, Ulmaceae, Moraceae, Urticaceae, Cannabaceae
  • Fagales: Fagaceae (Oak/Beech family), Betulaceae (Birch family), Juglandaceae (Walnut family), Casuarinaceae
  • Cucurbitales: Cucurbitaceae (Gourd family), Begoniaceae

Evolutionary Significance

Zygophyllaceae holds considerable evolutionary interest:

  • Arid/Saline Adaptation: Represents a major lineage highly specialized for survival in extreme desert and saline environments worldwide, showcasing diverse xerophytic and halophytic adaptations.
  • Phylogenetic Position: Its placement as sister to the massive nitrogen-fixing clade provides a key reference point for understanding the evolution and diversification of rosids.
  • Chemical Diversity: Produces a wide array of secondary metabolites (resins, alkaloids, saponins, lignans) with ecological and pharmacological significance.
  • Economic and Ecological Roles: Includes ecologically dominant plants (Larrea), valuable resources (Guaiacum), and problematic weeds (Tribulus), impacting ecosystems and human activities.
